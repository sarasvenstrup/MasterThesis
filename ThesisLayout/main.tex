\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
The term structure of interest rates is a high-dimensional object, yet empirical evidence suggests that a small number of latent factors captures most of its variation. In this thesis, neural network methods are combined with no-arbitrage theory to address this dimensionality reduction problem. The affine-inspired encoder-decoder framework of Matin et al. (2025) is extended and empirically evaluated. The model is trained on par swap rate data across nine currencies and compresses swap rate curves into a low-dimensional latent representation through a linear encoder, reconstructed through a decoder subject to a hard no-arbitrage constraint.

Two latent factors are found to be optimal out-of-sample. Out-of-sample performance is driven not alone by the volume of training data but by the diversity of curve shapes present in it. The model further struggles to reconstruct inverted and negative-rate curves, and its latent dynamics are found to be unsuitable for simulation. Two extensions are proposed to address these limitations. Augmenting the input with slope and curvature features improves in-sample fit but fails to generalise out-of-sample. Imposing mean-reverting constraints on the latent dynamics proves more effective, stabilising simulation and improving performance on atypical curve shapes. Finally, reconstruction accuracy alone is found not to guarantee dynamics suitable for derivative pricing.
