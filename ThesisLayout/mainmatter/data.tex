\chapter{Data Description and Sample Construction}
\label{chap:datadescription}

This chapter describes the panel data of par swap rates used throughout the 
thesis. The dataset is used for both model training and evaluation, and its 
characteristics are important for interpreting the results in later chapters. 
We begin by presenting the data source and coverage, before describing how 
the dataset is constructed.

\section{Data Description}

All data are obtained from Bloomberg and consist of quoted par swap rates reported in percentage terms. Prior to use, the rates are converted to decimal form. The data cover nine currencies,
\[
\{\text{AUD},\text{CAD},\text{DKK},\text{EUR},\text{GBP},\text{JPY},\text{NOK},\text{SEK},\text{USD}\},
\]
observed at monthly frequency over the period 2004--2024, although the exact sample period differs across currencies. Table \ref{tab:data_summary} summarizes the sample period and number of observations for each currency.

The panel is unbalanced across currencies, with start and end dates varying considerably. Most currencies span the full period from March 2004 to February 2024, while JPY and USD begin in 2009 and GBP covers a notably shorter period from May 2011 to November 2021, yielding only 127 observations. NOK, CAD and DKK have a small number of missing months within their respective sample periods. Importantly, the missing observations correspond to absent monthly dates rather than incomplete swap curves, since all available curves contain the full set of tenors. Therefore, missing observations are excluded and the remaining data is considered sufficient for training and evaluation across all nine currencies.

Having established the data coverage, we now turn to the main time-series characteristics of the swap rate data. The 10-year swap rates in Figure~\ref{fig:D2b_10y_timeseries} show a broadly declining trend across currencies from the beginning of the sample until around 2020, followed by a sharp increase from 2022 onwards. Four macroeconomic regimes are particularly visible in the sample.
% The economy following the GFC was still weak as demand was depressed, as households and businesses were paying down debt and saving rather than spending and investing, also resulting in less people being hired. Banks where not lending after suffering massive losses trying to rebuild capital. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Regime 1} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The Global Financial Crisis in September 2008 triggered a sharp and synchronized decline in swap rates, as central banks responded by lowering policy rates to support economic activity \citep{RBA2024}. 
    \item \textbf{Regime 2} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; Central banks in several advanced economies launched large-scale asset purchase programmes from 2015 onwards, pushing long-term swap rates toward and below zero, most notably for EUR, DKK, and SEK. \citep[p.~6]{BorioZabai2016}. 
    \item \textbf{Regime 3} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The COVID-19 shock in March 2020 marked a trough for many currencies, as central banks cut policy rates to near zero and launched large-scale asset purchase programmes \citep{Cantu2021}. 
    \item \textbf{Regime 4} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; A surge in inflation following the COVID-19 pandemic, amplified by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, caused a sharp and synchronized tightening of monetary policy across all currencies from 2022 onwards, reversing the prolonged low-rate environment \citep{IMF2022}. 
\end{itemize}

These periods are relevant for the model evaluation in later chapters, as they represent changing interest-rate regimes and structural shifts in the swap-rate environment. Figure~\ref{fig:D2a_swap_curves} illustrates the cross-sectional term structure on 31 August 2016, a date representative of the prolonged low-rate environment between the Quantitative Easing regime and the COVID-19 shock.

Most currencies display upward-sloping term structures while SEK, JPY, DKK and EUR show negative rates at the short end, reflecting the negative rate environment prevailing at the time. DKK closely tracks EUR across all maturities, consistent with the Danish krone's exchange-rate peg to the euro.

\section{Dataset Construction}

The dataset used throughout the thesis follows the sample construction of 
\citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}. Although swap rates are available at nine 
maturities
\[
\{1\text{Y},2\text{Y},3\text{Y},5\text{Y},7\text{Y},10\text{Y},15\text{Y},20\text{Y},30\text{Y}\},
\]
we work with the eight-maturity subset obtained by dropping the 7-year tenor. 
The sample is further restricted to swap curves from 2010 onwards.
