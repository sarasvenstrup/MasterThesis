\chapter{Data}
\label{chap:datadescription}

This chapter describes the panel of par swap rates used throughout the thesis. The dataset is used for both model training and evaluation, and its characteristics are essential for interpreting the results in later chapters. The chapter proceeds by first presenting the data source and coverage, before outlining the modeling choices made prior to application.

\section{Data Description}

All data are obtained from Bloomberg and consist of quoted par swap rates reported in percentage terms. Prior to use, the rates are converted to decimal form. The data covers nine currencies,
\[
\{\text{AUD},\text{CAD},\text{DKK},\text{EUR},\text{GBP},\text{JPY},\text{NOK},\text{SEK},\text{USD}\},
\]
observed at monthly frequency over the period 2004--2024, although the exact sample period differs across currencies. Table \ref{tab:data_summary} summarizes the sample period and number of observations for each currency.

The panel is unbalanced across currencies, with start and end dates varying considerably. Most currencies span the full period from March 2004 to February 2024, while JPY and USD begin in 2009 and GBP covers a notably shorter period from May 2011 to November 2021, yielding only 127 observations. NOK, CAD and DKK have a small number of missing months within their respective sample periods. Missing observations are simply excluded and the remaining data is considered sufficient for training and evaluation across all nine currencies.

Having established the data coverage, we now turn to the main time-series characteristics of the swap rate data. The 10-year swap rates in Figure~\ref{fig:D2b_10y_timeseries} show a broadly declining trend across currencies from the beginning of the sample until around 2020, followed by a sharp increase from 2022 onwards. Four macroeconomic events are particularly visible in the sample.

% The economy following the GFC was still weak as demand was depressed, as households and businesses were paying down debt and saving rather than spending and investing, also resulting in less people being hired. Banks where not lending after suffering massive losses trying to rebuild capital. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Event 1} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The Global Financial Crisis in September 2008 triggered a sharp and synchronized decline in swap rates, as central banks responded by lowering policy rates to support economic activity \citep{RBA2024}. 
    \item \textbf{Event 2} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The ECB quantitative easing announcement in January 2015 pushed European swap rates further toward and below zero, most notably for EUR, DKK and SEK, as long-term interest rates declined following the launch of the asset purchase programme \citep{Gern2015}. 
    \item \textbf{Event 3} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The COVID-19 shock in March 2020 marked a trough for many currencies, as central banks cut policy rates to near zero and launched large-scale asset purchase programmes \citep{Cantu2021}. 
    \item \textbf{Event 4} \textcolor{UCPHred}{\textbf{|}} \; The global rate-hiking cycle beginning in 2022, driven by inflationary pressures and amplified by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, caused a sharp and synchronized reversal upward across all currencies \citep{IMF2022}. 
\end{itemize}

These events are relevant for the model evaluation in later chapters, as they represent periods of changing interest-rate regimes and structural shifts in the swap-rate environment. Figure~\ref{fig:D2a_swap_curves} illustrates the cross-sectional term structure on 31 August 2016, a date representative of the prolonged low-rate environment between the ECB QE launch and the COVID-19 shock. Most currencies display upward-sloping term structures while SEK, JPY, DKK and EUR show negative rates at the short end, reflecting the negative rate environment prevailing at the time. DKK closely tracks EUR across all maturities, consistent with the Danish krone's exchange-rate peg to the euro.

\section{Modelling Choices}

Having described the data, we outline two modelling choices prior to application.

\begin{description}
    \item[Maturity Grid]
    For each observation date, swap rates are available on the maturity grid
    \[
    \{1\text{Y},2\text{Y},3\text{Y},5\text{Y},7\text{Y},10\text{Y},15\text{Y},20\text{Y},30\text{Y}\}.
    \]
    Although the raw data contain swap rates at nine maturities, the model is fitted to the subset
    \[
    \{1\text{Y},2\text{Y},3\text{Y},5\text{Y},10\text{Y},15\text{Y},20\text{Y},30\text{Y}\},
    \]
    
    Thus, the 7-year maturity is excluded. This is not because the 7-year rate is considered unreliable, but to maintain consistency with \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025} and to ensure comparability with the existing autoencoder literature on yield curves.

    \item[Training Period]
    Following \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, the model is trained on data from 2010 onwards. \textcolor{red}{[Argumenter for hvorfor vi vælger at gøre det samme udover blot for at være sammenlignlig]}

    Following \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, the model is trained on observations from 2010 onwards. This choice also improves the balance of the pooled cross-currency sample. Since JPY and USD only enter the dataset in 2009 and GBP begins in 2011, including the full pre-2010 sample would add observations primarily for the currencies with longer histories. In a pooled encoder--decoder model, this could give disproportionate weight to the early histories of a subset of currencies. Starting the training sample in 2010 therefore provides a more comparable cross-currency panel while retaining the main interest-rate regimes relevant for the empirical analysis, including the post-crisis low-rate period, the negative-rate environment, the COVID-19 shock, and the subsequent rate-hiking cycle.
    
\end{description}
