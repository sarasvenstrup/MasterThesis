\section{Derivatives Used in the Bond-Pricing PDE}
\label{app:pde_derivatives}

Define for convenience
\[
\Phi(z,\tau):=A_z(\tau)-B_z(\tau)\,G(z,\tau),
\qquad\text{so that}\qquad
P(z,\tau)=e^{\Phi(z,\tau)}.
\]

\textbf{(i) $\tau$-derivative.}
By the chain rule,
\[
\partial_\tau P = (\partial_\tau \Phi)\,P.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\partial_\tau \Phi
=
\partial_\tau A_z(\tau) - \partial_\tau\!\big(B_z(\tau)\,G(z,\tau)\big)
=
\partial_\tau A_z(\tau)
-
(\partial_\tau B_z(\tau))\,G(z,\tau)
-
B_z(\tau)\,\partial_\tau G(z,\tau).
\]
Hence,
\[
\boxed{
\partial_\tau P
=
\Big(
\partial_\tau A_z
-
(\partial_\tau B_z)\,G
-
B_z\,\partial_\tau G
\Big)P.
}
\]

\textbf{(ii) Gradient in $z$.}
Following the state-wise derivation used in the main text, \(A_z(\tau)\) and \(B_z(\tau)\) are treated as maturity-dependent coefficient paths for a fixed state \(z\). Hence their \(z\)-derivatives are not included in this calculation.
The $z$-dependence of $\Phi$ comes only through $G(z,\tau)$:
\[
\nabla_z \Phi = -B_z(\tau)\,\nabla_z G(z,\tau).
\]
Thus,
\[
\boxed{
\nabla_z P
=
(\nabla_z \Phi)\,P
=
\big(-B_z\,\nabla_z G\big)P.
}
\] 

\textbf{(iii) Hessian in $z$.}
A standard identity for $P=e^{\Phi}$ is
\[
H_z P = P\big(H_z\Phi + (\nabla_z\Phi)(\nabla_z\Phi)^\top\big).
\]
We have
\[
H_z\Phi = -B_z(\tau)\,H_z G(z,\tau),
\qquad
(\nabla_z\Phi)(\nabla_z\Phi)^\top
=
B_z(\tau)^2\,\nabla_z G(z,\tau)\,\nabla_z G(z,\tau)^\top.
\]
Therefore,
\[
\boxed{
H_z P
=
P\Big(
-B_z\,H_z G
+
B_z^2\,\nabla_z G\,\nabla_z G^\top
\Big).
}
\] 



\section{Cholesky Parameterization of the Volatility Matrix}
\label{sec:cholesky_volatility}

To ensure that the instantaneous covariance matrix is positive semidefinite, we parameterize it through a lower-triangular Cholesky factor. Let
\[
\Sigma = L L^\top,
\]
where \(L\) is lower triangular. We further write
\[
\Sigma = D R D,
\qquad
D = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\dots,\sigma_d),
\]
where \(\sigma_i > 0\) are factor volatilities and \(R\) is the correlation matrix. Hence, if
\[
R = C C^\top,
\]
then
\[
L = D C.
\]

Below we derive the explicit form of \(L\) for dimensions \(d=2,3,4\).

\subsection{Two-dimensional case}

In dimension \(2\), the correlation matrix is
\[
R =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \rho_{12} \\
\rho_{12} & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
We seek a lower-triangular Cholesky factor
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
c_{11} & 0 \\
c_{21} & c_{22}
\end{bmatrix}
\]
such that
\[
R = C C^\top.
\]
Matching entries gives
\[
c_{11}^2 = 1,
\qquad
c_{11}c_{21} = \rho_{12},
\qquad
c_{21}^2 + c_{22}^2 = 1.
\]
Choosing the positive diagonal gives
\[
c_{11}=1,
\qquad
c_{21}=\rho_{12},
\qquad
c_{22}=\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}.
\]
Therefore,
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
\rho_{12} & \sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
Multiplying by \(D=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\sigma_2)\) yields
\[
L = D C =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_1 & 0 \\
\rho_{12}\sigma_2 & \sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

\subsection{Three-dimensional case}

In dimension \(3\), the correlation matrix is
\[
R =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \rho_{12} & \rho_{13} \\
\rho_{12} & 1 & \rho_{23} \\
\rho_{13} & \rho_{23} & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
We write
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
c_{21} & c_{22} & 0 \\
c_{31} & c_{32} & c_{33}
\end{bmatrix}
\]
and impose \(R = C C^\top\). Matching entries row by row:

From the \((2,1)\)-entry,
\[
c_{21} = \rho_{12}.
\]
From the \((2,2)\)-entry,
\[
c_{21}^2 + c_{22}^2 = 1
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
c_{22} = \sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}.
\]
From the \((3,1)\)-entry,
\[
c_{31} = \rho_{13}.
\]
From the \((3,2)\)-entry,
\[
c_{31}c_{21} + c_{32}c_{22} = \rho_{23}.
\]
Substituting \(c_{21}=\rho_{12}\), \(c_{22}=\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}\), and \(c_{31}=\rho_{13}\), we obtain
\[
c_{32}
=
\frac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}}.
\]
Finally, from the \((3,3)\)-entry,
\[
c_{31}^2 + c_{32}^2 + c_{33}^2 = 1,
\]
hence
\[
c_{33}
=
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\frac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}.
\]
Thus,
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
\rho_{12} & \sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2} & 0 \\
\rho_{13} &
\dfrac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
Therefore,
\[
L = D C =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_1 & 0 & 0 \\
\rho_{12}\sigma_2 & \sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2} & 0 \\
\rho_{13}\sigma_3 &
\sigma_3\dfrac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sigma_3\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

\subsection{Four-dimensional case}

In dimension \(4\), the correlation matrix is
\[
R =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \rho_{12} & \rho_{13} & \rho_{14} \\
\rho_{12} & 1 & \rho_{23} & \rho_{24} \\
\rho_{13} & \rho_{23} & 1 & \rho_{34} \\
\rho_{14} & \rho_{24} & \rho_{34} & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
We write
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
c_{21} & c_{22} & 0 & 0 \\
c_{31} & c_{32} & c_{33} & 0 \\
c_{41} & c_{42} & c_{43} & c_{44}
\end{bmatrix}
\]
and again impose \(R=C C^\top\).

The first three rows follow exactly as in the three-dimensional case:
\[
c_{21}=\rho_{12},
\qquad
c_{22}=\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2},
\]
\[
c_{31}=\rho_{13},
\qquad
c_{32}=\frac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}},
\]
\[
c_{33}
=
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\frac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}.
\]

For the fourth row, the \((4,1)\)-entry gives
\[
c_{41}=\rho_{14}.
\]
The \((4,2)\)-entry gives
\[
c_{41}c_{21}+c_{42}c_{22}=\rho_{24},
\]
hence
\[
c_{42}
=
\frac{\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}}.
\]
The \((4,3)\)-entry gives
\[
c_{41}c_{31}+c_{42}c_{32}+c_{43}c_{33}=\rho_{34},
\]
so
\[
c_{43}
=
\frac{\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}-c_{32}c_{42}}{c_{33}}.
\]
Substituting the expressions for \(c_{32}\) and \(c_{42}\),
\[
c_{43}
=
\frac{
\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}{
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
}.
\]
Finally, the \((4,4)\)-entry gives
\[
c_{41}^2+c_{42}^2+c_{43}^2+c_{44}^2=1,
\]
hence
\[
c_{44}
=
\sqrt{1-\rho_{14}^2-c_{42}^2-c_{43}^2}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
c_{44}
=
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{14}^2
-\frac{(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
-\left(
\frac{
\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}{
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
}
\right)^2
}.
\]

Therefore,
\[
C =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\[0.4em]
\rho_{12} & \sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2} & 0 & 0 \\[0.6em]
\rho_{13} &
\dfrac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
} & 0 \\[1em]
\rho_{14} &
\dfrac{\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\dfrac{
\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}{
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
} &
c_{44}
\end{bmatrix},
\]
with \(c_{44}\) given above. Hence,
\[
L = D C.
\]

\subsection{Final expressions for the volatility matrix}

Collecting the results, the Cholesky factor \(L\) used in the model is:

\paragraph{Dimension \(2\):}
\[
L =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_1 & 0 \\
\rho_{12}\sigma_2 & \sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

\paragraph{Dimension \(3\):}
\[
L =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_1 & 0 & 0 \\
\rho_{12}\sigma_2 & \sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2} & 0 \\
\rho_{13}\sigma_3 &
\sigma_3\dfrac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sigma_3\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

\paragraph{Dimension \(4\):}
\[
L =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\[0.4em]
\rho_{12}\sigma_2 & \sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2} & 0 & 0 \\[0.6em]
\rho_{13}\sigma_3 &
\sigma_3\dfrac{\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sigma_3\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
} & 0 \\[1em]
\rho_{14}\sigma_4 &
\sigma_4\dfrac{\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14}}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} &
\sigma_4\dfrac{
\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}{
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
} &
\sigma_4 c_{44}
\end{bmatrix},
\]
where
\[
c_{44}
=
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{14}^2
-\frac{(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
-\left(
\frac{
\rho_{34}-\rho_{13}\rho_{14}
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})(\rho_{24}-\rho_{12}\rho_{14})}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}{
\sqrt{
1-\rho_{13}^2
-\dfrac{(\rho_{23}-\rho_{12}\rho_{13})^2}{1-\rho_{12}^2}
}
}
\right)^2
}.
\]

\subsection{Remark}

The formulas above give the Cholesky factor associated with a valid correlation matrix \(R\). In dimension \(2\), \(|\rho_{12}|<1\) is sufficient for positive definiteness. In dimensions \(3\) and \(4\), the pairwise bounds \(\rho_{ij}\in(-1,1)\) are not sufficient; the full matrix \(R\) must also be positive definite. Thus, for \(\ell>2\), additional validation or a positive-definite parameterization is required.

\section{Hyperspherical Cholesky Parameterisation}
\label{app:hyperspherical_cholesky}

The standard Cholesky factorisation constructs a lower-triangular factor from a matrix that is already positive definite. In the stable model, we instead need a parameterisation that guarantees positive definiteness by construction. This can be achieved by constructing the Cholesky factor directly using hyperspherical coordinates, following the correlation-matrix representation of \citet{PourahmadiWang2015}.

Let \(R\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) denote the matrix constructed from the
hyperspherical Cholesky factor. We construct a lower-triangular matrix \(L_R\)
and define
\[
R=L_RL_R^\top.
\]
The rows of \(L_R\) are parameterised as unit vectors. For \(\ell=3\), for
example, we set
\[
L_R
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
\cos\theta_{21} & \sin\theta_{21} & 0 \\
\cos\theta_{31} &
\sin\theta_{31}\cos\theta_{32} &
\sin\theta_{31}\sin\theta_{32}
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where
\[
\theta_{21},\theta_{31},\theta_{32}\in(0,\pi).
\]
Each row has Euclidean norm one. Moreover, since all diagonal entries of
\(L_R\) are strictly positive, \(L_R\) has full rank.

More generally, for \(i=1,\ldots,\ell\), the \(i\)-th row of \(L_R\) is
constructed using \(i-1\) angles. The first row is
\[
(L_R)_{11}=1.
\]
For \(i\geq2\), the nonzero entries are
\[
(L_R)_{i1}=\cos\theta_{i1},
\]
\[
(L_R)_{ij}
=
\left(\prod_{k=1}^{j-1}\sin\theta_{ik}\right)
\cos\theta_{ij},
\qquad
j=2,\ldots,i-1,
\]
and
\[
(L_R)_{ii}
=
\prod_{k=1}^{i-1}\sin\theta_{ik}.
\]
All entries above the diagonal are zero. If all angles satisfy
\[
\theta_{ij}\in(0,\pi),
\qquad
i=2,\ldots,\ell,\quad j=1,\ldots,i-1,
\]
then all sine terms are strictly positive. Hence the diagonal entries of
\(L_R\) are strictly positive, so \(L_R\) is full rank.

In the neural implementation, the network outputs a flat vector. The first
\(\ell\) outputs are used for the volatility levels, while the remaining
\(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) outputs are used for the correlation structure. We assign
these correlation outputs to the triangular angle indices by defining
\[
q(i,j)=\frac{(i-1)(i-2)}{2}+j,
\qquad
i=2,\ldots,\ell,\quad j=1,\ldots,i-1.
\]
Thus the unconstrained network output corresponding to angle \(\theta_{ij}\)
is
\[
g_{ij}(z)
=
[\mathcal{H}(z)]_{\ell+q(i,j)}.
\]
The corresponding angle is generated by
\[
\theta_{ij}(z)
=
\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_{ij}(z)).
\]
Since \(\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_{ij}(z))\in(0,1)\), this maps every
unconstrained network output into
\[
\theta_{ij}(z)\in(0,\pi).
\]
Thus all angles used in the hyperspherical construction lie in \((0,\pi)\), so
the diagonal entries of \(L_R(z)\) are strictly positive for every latent state
\(z\).

\subsection{Validity of the constructed correlation matrix}
\label{app:hyperspherical_cholesky_validity}

We now verify that the matrix constructed above is a valid correlation matrix.
Recall that
\[
R=L_RL_R^\top.
\]
First, \(R\) is symmetric, since
\[
R^\top
=
\left(L_RL_R^\top\right)^\top
=
L_RL_R^\top
=
R.
\]

Second, the diagonal entries of \(R\) are equal to one. The \(i\)-th diagonal
entry of \(R\) is the inner product of the \(i\)-th row of \(L_R\) with itself:
\[
R_{ii}
=
(L_R)_{i,\cdot}(L_R)_{i,\cdot}^\top
=
\|(L_R)_{i,\cdot}\|^2.
\]
For \(i=1\), this is immediate because the first row is
\[
(1,0,\ldots,0).
\]
For \(i\geq2\), the hyperspherical construction gives
\[
\|(L_R)_{i,\cdot}\|^2
=
\cos^2\theta_{i1}
+
\sum_{j=2}^{i-1}
\left(
\prod_{k=1}^{j-1}\sin^2\theta_{ik}
\right)
\cos^2\theta_{ij}
+
\prod_{k=1}^{i-1}\sin^2\theta_{ik}.
\]
This expression equals one by repeated use of the identity
\[
\cos^2 x+\sin^2 x=1.
\]
Equivalently, it can be written recursively as
\[
\cos^2\theta_{i1}
+
\sin^2\theta_{i1}
\left[
\cos^2\theta_{i2}
+
\sin^2\theta_{i2}
\left[
\cdots
\left[
\cos^2\theta_{i,i-1}
+
\sin^2\theta_{i,i-1}
\right]
\cdots
\right]
\right]
=
1.
\]
Hence each row of \(L_R\) has unit norm, and therefore
\[
R_{ii}=1,
\qquad
i=1,\ldots,\ell.
\]

Third, \(R\) is positive definite. Since all angles satisfy
\[
\theta_{ij}\in(0,\pi),
\]
we have
\[
\sin\theta_{ij}>0.
\]
The diagonal entries of \(L_R\) are therefore strictly positive, because
\[
(L_R)_{ii}
=
\prod_{k=1}^{i-1}\sin\theta_{ik}
>
0,
\qquad
i=2,\ldots,\ell,
\]
and
\[
(L_R)_{11}=1.
\]
Since \(L_R\) is lower triangular with nonzero diagonal entries, \(L_R\) has
full rank.

Now take any nonzero vector \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\). Then
\[
x^\top R x
=
x^\top L_RL_R^\top x
=
\left(L_R^\top x\right)^\top
\left(L_R^\top x\right)
=
\|L_R^\top x\|^2.
\]
Because \(L_R\) has full rank, \(L_R^\top x\neq 0\) whenever \(x\neq 0\).
Therefore
\[
x^\top R x
=
\|L_R^\top x\|^2
>
0.
\]
Thus \(R\) is positive definite.

Combining these properties, \(R\) is symmetric, positive definite, and has unit
diagonal entries. Hence \(R\) is a valid correlation matrix by construction.

\section{Numerical Solution of the ODE System Using the Runge--Kutta 3/8 Method}
\label{app:rk38}

In the affine-inspired encoder--decoder framework, the bond pricing functions are obtained as the solution of a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). Closed-form solutions are generally not available once the coefficient functions are represented by neural networks. Consequently, the system must be solved numerically.

To this end, we employ a fourth-order explicit Runge--Kutta scheme, specifically the \emph{Runge--Kutta 3/8 method}. This method offers a good balance between numerical accuracy and computational efficiency, while remaining straightforward to implement within automatic differentiation frameworks used for neural network training.

\subsection{General ODE formulation}

Consider an ODE of the form
\[
\frac{dy}{dt} = f(t,y),
\qquad
y(t_0) = y_0,
\]
where \(y(t)\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(f\) is a sufficiently smooth function. The goal is to compute an approximation of the solution at discrete time points \(t_n = t_0 + nh\), where \(h\) denotes the step size.

\subsection{Runge--Kutta methods}

Runge--Kutta methods approximate the solution by evaluating the derivative function \(f(t,y)\) at several intermediate points within each step. The classical fourth-order Runge--Kutta family achieves local truncation error of order \(O(h^5)\) and global error of order \(O(h^4)\).

Among these schemes, the Runge--Kutta 3/8 rule is a particular fourth-order method characterized by a symmetric weighting structure. It is defined by four intermediate slope evaluations.

\subsection{Runge--Kutta 3/8 scheme}

Let \(y_n\) denote the approximation to \(y(t_n)\). The Runge--Kutta 3/8 update from \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1}=t_n+h\) is given by

\[
k_1 = f(t_n, y_n),
\]

\[
k_2 = f\left(t_n+\frac{h}{3},\; y_n + \frac{h}{3}k_1\right),
\]

\[
k_3 = f\left(t_n+\frac{2h}{3},\; y_n + h\left(-\frac{1}{3}k_1 + k_2\right)\right),
\]

\[
k_4 = f\left(t_n+h,\; y_n + h\left(k_1 - k_2 + k_3\right)\right).
\]

The solution is then updated according to

\[
y_{n+1}
=
y_n
+
\frac{h}{8}
\left(
k_1 + 3k_2 + 3k_3 + k_4
\right).
\]

This scheme achieves fourth-order accuracy while requiring only four evaluations of the function \(f\) per step.

\subsection{Application in the model}

In the implementation of the affine-inspired encoder--decoder model, the Runge--Kutta 3/8 scheme is used to numerically integrate the ODE system governing the bond pricing functions. The maturity variable plays the role of the time variable in the ODE, and the integration proceeds along the maturity dimension.

Because the scheme is explicit and differentiable, it integrates naturally with modern deep learning frameworks such as PyTorch. Automatic differentiation can therefore propagate gradients through the numerical solver during model training.

The Runge--Kutta 3/8 method provides a fourth-order explicit approximation to the ODE solution while remaining compatible with gradient-based optimization.


\section{Well-posedness of the Latent Dynamics}
\label{app:sde_wellposed}

This appendix examines whether the two latent SDEs studied in the
thesis --- the baseline of \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025} and the stable
variant proposed here --- admit a unique non-explosive solution in the
sense of Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}. The proposition requires
three conditions to hold: the drift is globally Lipschitz, the diffusion
matrix is globally Lipschitz, and their joint norm satisfies a linear
growth bound. We check the three conditions in turn for each variant.


\subsection{Baseline Latent Dynamics}
\label{app:baseline_sde_wellposed}

The baseline latent dynamics are given by
\begin{equation}
    dz_t
    =
    \mu_{\mathrm{base}}(z_t)\,dt
    +
    L_{\mathrm{base}}(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
    \label{eq:app_baseline_sde}
\end{equation}
where \(z_t\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\), \(W^{\mathbb Q}\) is an
\(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion, and the drift is the affine map
\[
    \mu_{\mathrm{base}}(z) = Mz + N,
\]
with \(M\in\mathbb R^{\ell\times\ell}\) and \(N\in\mathbb R^{\ell}\) free
learned parameters. The diffusion matrix is built from raw neural-network
outputs \(a_i(z)\) and \(c_{ij}(z)\), for \(1\leq j<i\leq\ell\), through
the unbounded transformations
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_i(z) = \exp\bigl(a_i(z)\bigr),
    \qquad
    \rho_{ij}(z) = \tanh\bigl(c_{ij}(z)\bigr),
    \label{eq:app_baseline_vol}
\end{equation}
which define the diagonal matrix
\(D(z)=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1(z),\ldots,\sigma_\ell(z))\) and the
entries of the correlation matrix \(R(z)\). A Cholesky factor \(L_R(z)\) of
\(R(z)=L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top\) gives
\[
    L_{\mathrm{base}}(z) = D(z)\,L_R(z).
\]
Neither \(\sigma_i\) nor the correlation entries \(\rho_{ij}\) are
constrained by any hyperparameter.

\paragraph{Drift Lipschitz condition.}
The drift is affine in \(z\) with finite learned parameters \(M\) and
\(N\). For any \(z,y\in\mathbb R^{\ell}\),
\[
    \|\mu_{\mathrm{base}}(z)-\mu_{\mathrm{base}}(y)\|
    =
    \|M(z-y)\|
    \leq
    \|M\|\,\|z-y\|,
\]
so the first condition of Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence} holds with
\(K=\|M\|\).

\paragraph{Diffusion Lipschitz condition.}
The raw network output \(a_i(z)\) is itself globally Lipschitz, since it is
a finite composition of affine maps and the centred soft-step activation,
which has bounded derivative. The volatility map in
\eqref{eq:app_baseline_vol} is then the composition
\(\sigma_i = \exp\circ a_i\). The exponential function has unbounded
derivative on \(\mathbb R\), so this composition is not globally Lipschitz
on \(\mathbb R^{\ell}\). Concretely, along any ray \(z=tv\) with
\(\|v\|=1\) and \(t\to\infty\), \(\sigma_i(tv)\) generally grows
exponentially in \(t\). The product \(L_{\mathrm{base}}(z)=D(z)L_R(z)\)
therefore inherits this unboundedness, and the second condition of
Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence} fails.

\paragraph{Linear growth condition.}
By the same argument, \(\|L_{\mathrm{base}}(z)\|\) is not bounded by
\(C(1+\|z\|)\) for any finite constant \(C>0\), since \(\sigma_i(z)\) can
grow exponentially in \(\|z\|\). The third condition of
Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence} therefore also fails.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
The drift satisfies the Lipschitz condition, but the diffusion fails both
the Lipschitz and linear growth conditions. Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}
therefore cannot be invoked to guarantee a unique non-explosive solution of
\eqref{eq:app_baseline_sde}. A local strong solution exists under the
standard local Lipschitz continuity of the coefficients, but finite-time
explosion cannot be ruled out a priori. This is consistent with the
empirical observation in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation} that simulated
baseline paths can grow without bound over multi-year horizons, particularly
for higher latent dimensions.

In addition to the obstruction above, the parameterisation
\(\rho_{ij}(z)=\tanh(c_{ij}(z))\) only constrains each individual entry to
\((-1,1)\) and does not constrain \(R(z)\) to be positive semidefinite for
\(\ell\geq 3\). Although this is not a hypothesis of
Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, it is a separate structural concern,
because at states where \(R(z)\) is not positive semidefinite the Cholesky
factor \(L_R(z)\) is not real-valued and the diffusion matrix is not
well-defined by the construction above.


\subsection{Stable Latent Dynamics}
\label{app:stable_sde_wellposed}

The stable latent dynamics are given by
\begin{equation}
    dz_t
    =
    \mu_{\mathrm{st}}(z_t)\,dt
    +
    L_{\mathrm{st}}(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
    \label{eq:app_stable_sde}
\end{equation}
where
\[
    \mu_{\mathrm{st}}(z)
    =
    Mz+N,
    \qquad
    M=-(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell),
    \qquad
    \varepsilon>0.
\]
The diffusion matrix is constructed from bounded volatility levels and a
positive-definite correlation matrix. The volatility levels are defined by
\begin{equation}
    \log \sigma_i(z)
    =
    \log \sigma_{\min}
    +
    \bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
    \operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(a_i(z)\bigr),
    \label{eq:app_stable_vol}
\end{equation}
where \(0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}<\infty\). The raw correlation
outputs are mapped to hyperspherical angles
\(\theta_{ij}(z)=\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(c_{ij}(z))\in(0,\pi)\), which
define the hyperspherical Cholesky factor \(L_R(z)\) and the correlation
matrix \(R(z)=L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top\). The diffusion matrix is
\[
    L_{\mathrm{st}}(z)
    =
    D(z)L_R(z),
    \qquad
    D(z)=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1(z),\ldots,\sigma_\ell(z)).
\]

\paragraph{Drift Lipschitz condition.}
The drift has the same affine form as in the baseline. Since \(V\) and
\(N\) are finite learned parameters and \(\varepsilon>0\), the matrix
\(M=-(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell)\) is finite and
\[
    \|\mu_{\mathrm{st}}(z)-\mu_{\mathrm{st}}(y)\|
    \leq
    \|M\|\,\|z-y\|.
\]
The first condition of Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence} holds.

\paragraph{Diffusion Lipschitz condition.}
The raw outputs \(a_i(z)\) and \(c_{ij}(z)\) are globally Lipschitz as
compositions of affine maps and smooth activations with bounded
derivatives. The sigmoid in \eqref{eq:app_stable_vol} takes values in
\((0,1)\), so
\[
    \log\sigma_i(z)
    \in
    (\log\sigma_{\min},\log\sigma_{\max})
\]
for all \(z\in\mathbb R^{\ell}\). The exponential function has bounded
derivative on the compact interval
\([\log\sigma_{\min},\log\sigma_{\max}]\), so each map
\(z\mapsto\sigma_i(z)\) is globally Lipschitz and uniformly bounded in
\((\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\). The hyperspherical angles
\(\theta_{ij}(z)\) lie in \((0,\pi)\) and are globally Lipschitz by the
same composition argument. The entries of \(L_R(z)\) are finite products
of sine and cosine functions evaluated at these angles, so they are
bounded and globally Lipschitz. The product \(L_{\mathrm{st}}(z)=D(z)L_R(z)\)
is therefore globally Lipschitz, and the second condition of
Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence} holds.

\paragraph{Linear growth condition.}
Since the volatility levels are bounded above by \(\sigma_{\max}\) and the
entries of \(L_R(z)\) are bounded by construction, there exists a constant
\(C_L>0\) such that
\[
    \|L_{\mathrm{st}}(z)\|\leq C_L\leq C_L(1+\|z\|)
    \qquad
    \text{for all }z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]
Combined with the linear growth of the affine drift, this gives the third
condition of Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
The drift and diffusion coefficients satisfy all three conditions of
Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}. The stable latent SDE
\eqref{eq:app_stable_sde} therefore admits a unique strong solution on
every finite horizon \([0,T]\), with continuous sample paths and the
Markov property. The linear growth condition rules out finite-time
explosion.

The stable drift also has an explicit mean-reverting structure. For any
non-zero \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\),
\[
    x^\top(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell)x
    =
    \|Vx\|^2+\varepsilon\|x\|^2
    >
    0,
\]
so \(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\) is symmetric positive definite and all
eigenvalues of \(M=-(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell)\) are strictly negative.
The deterministic part of the stable dynamics is therefore globally
mean-reverting around the fixed point \(z^*=-M^{-1}N\).

The short-rate function in the stable model is bounded by construction. It
is specified as
\[
    r(z)
    =
    r_{\mathrm{center}}
    +
    r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh\bigl(f(z)\bigr),
    \qquad
    r_{\mathrm{scale}}
    =
    \operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+s_{\min}>0,
\]
so \(r(z)\in(r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},\,r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}})\)
for all \(z\). Consequently, for every finite horizon \(T\), the simulated
discount factor
\[
    D_T = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^T r(z_s)\,ds\right)
\]
is finite and strictly positive. The stable latent paths and the associated discount factors used in the simulation experiment are therefore mathematically well-defined.

\section{Relation to Operator-Learning Theory}
\label{app:operator_learning_relation}
%--------------------------------------------------------

This section summarises how the arbitrage-free affine-inspired encoder--decoder model proposed by \cite{MatinWanPoulsen2025} relates to the operator-learning framework of \citet{Benth2024}, and in which ways the two frameworks differ.

\subsection{Conceptual Similarities}

The decoder in \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025} can be related conceptually to the operator-learning framework of \citet{Benth2024}, which concerns the learning of solution maps for backward parabolic Cauchy problems. The connection is relevant because the no-arbitrage bond-pricing PDE, together with its terminal condition, belongs to the same class of backward parabolic Cauchy problems considered by \citet{Benth2024}. In the encoder--decoder model, a fixed latent state \(z\) is mapped to a bond-price curve as a function of time to maturity,
\[
    z \mapsto P_\theta(z, \cdot), 
    \qquad 
    \tau \mapsto P_\theta(z, \tau).
\]
\citet{Benth2024} provides mathematical motivation for treating such pricing functions as learnable objects, although the framework does not directly apply to the specific architecture used in the encoder--decoder model. The main differences are discussed below.


\subsection{Technical Differences}

The conceptual connection above should not be interpreted as a direct application of the theorem in \citet{Benth2024}. There are several important differences between the two frameworks.

\paragraph{Input space and operator type.}
In \citet{Benth2024}, the input is typically an element of an infinite-dimensional function space, such as a coefficient or terminal condition, and the object of interest is a function-to-function solution operator. In the encoder--decoder model, the input is finite-dimensional. At the full model level, the input is the observed swap-rate vector \(\mathbf S_t\in\mathbb R^M\). At the decoder level, the input is the latent state \(z_t\in\mathbb R^\ell\). The decoder is therefore better viewed as learning a finite-dimensional-input, function-valued map,
\[
    D_\theta:\mathbb R^\ell \to C([0,T]),
    \qquad
    z \mapsto P_\theta(z,\cdot),
\]
rather than a full function-to-function operator.

\paragraph{Output variable.}
The output functions are also defined on different domains. In the operator-learning framework, the solution is typically a function of the state variable \(x\). In the encoder--decoder model, the output is a function of time to maturity \(\tau\). Thus, both frameworks produce function-valued outputs, but the functions depend on different variables.

\paragraph{Network formalism.}
The framework of \citet{Benth2024} is formulated on Banach or Fréchet spaces. The encoder--decoder used in this thesis is implemented as a finite-dimensional neural network combined with an ODE-based no-arbitrage decoder. It therefore does not use the same neural-network formalism.

\paragraph{Role of the short rate.}
In an operator formulation of the pricing PDE, the short-rate function may be treated as an input to the pricing operator. In the encoder--decoder model, the short rate is learned internally as a function of the latent state,
\[
    r(t)=\widetilde r(z_t).
\]
Thus, the short rate is external in one formulation and internal in the other.

\paragraph{Finite maturity grid and numerical implementation.}
Although the decoder can be evaluated as a curve over time to maturity, the empirical loss is computed only on finitely many observed maturities. Training therefore controls the error on the maturity grid, not automatically on the full continuous maturity interval. In addition, the bond-price curve is generated by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system numerically. The implemented curve is therefore more accurately viewed as \(P_{\theta,h}(z,\tau)\), where \(h\) denotes the ODE step size.

\subsection{Conclusion}

The affine-inspired encoder--decoder should therefore be interpreted as
conceptually related to, but not directly covered by, the operator-learning
theory of \citet{Benth2024}. The comparison is nevertheless useful because it
places the decoder within a broader view of pricing as the learning of
structured function-valued maps.
