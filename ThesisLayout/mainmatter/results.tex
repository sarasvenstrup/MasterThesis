This chapter presents the empirical results for the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model. 
We first examine the in-sample training performance across factor dimensions, and then evaluate out-of-sample reconstruction accuracy relative to the EKF DNS benchmark. 
Finally, we discuss arbitrage diagnostics based on Sharpe-ratio calculations and the economic interpretation of the extracted latent factors and risk-neutral parameters.


After implementation and training of the arbitrage-free encoder-decoder model, we evaluate the cross-sectional reconstruction accuracy of the two- and three-factor specifications. Following \cite{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, performance is measured by the in-sample root mean square error (RMSE).

The results show a clear monotonic improvement in in-sample fit as the latent dimension increases. 
At the end of training, the average RMSE is approximately 32.71 bps for the one-factor model, 10.13 bps for the two-factor model, 6.08 bps for the three-factor model, and 4.33 bps for the four-factor model. 
Thus, the one-factor specification is clearly too restrictive, while the addition of second, third, and fourth factors progressively improves cross-sectional reconstruction accuracy.

An important feature of Table~\ref{tab:avg_rmse_comparison} is that the higher-dimensional models converge more slowly. 
In particular, the three- and four-factor models continue to improve materially after the two-factor model has largely stabilized. 
This suggests that higher-dimensional specifications require longer training horizons in order to exploit their additional flexibility. 
Consequently, comparisons across factor dimensions should be based on sufficiently long training runs; otherwise the performance of the richer models may be understated.

The three-factor specification achieves a final average training RMSE of roughly 6 bps, with some heterogeneity across currencies. 
The reconstruction errors are lowest for CAD, GBP and USD, while JPY remains somewhat harder to fit. 
This pattern is plausible given that the Japanese term structure exhibits prolonged low-rate and negative-rate regimes that differ substantially from the other markets in the sample.

Several conclusions emerge from Table~\ref{tab:rmse_summary_comparison}. 
First, for the autoencoder, out-of-sample performance improves materially as the latent dimension increases from two to four factors. 
Second, at the reported training horizon, the EKF DNS benchmark attains lower out-of-sample RMSE than the autoencoder for the comparable factor specifications. 
Thus, while the arbitrage-free autoencoder is capable of achieving a good in-sample fit, its out-of-sample performance remains somewhat weaker than the benchmark in the present implementation.

This gap should, however, be interpreted with caution. 
The training results in Table~\ref{tab:avg_rmse_comparison} indicate that the higher-dimensional autoencoder models continue to improve substantially beyond 2,500 epochs. 
It is therefore likely that the out-of-sample comparison understates the eventual performance of the three- and four-factor autoencoder specifications. 
A more definitive benchmark comparison should therefore be based on longer training runs for the out-of-sample experiments as well.

\section{Arbitrage diagnostics}
%========================================================

Reconstruction accuracy alone is not sufficient for evaluating the model, since the main contribution of the proposed framework is that the decoder is designed to be arbitrage-free by construction. 
To verify this numerically, we evaluate the model-implied approximate bond Sharpe ratios across maturities. 
Under exact no-arbitrage, these quantities should be zero up to numerical approximation error.

This diagnostic is closely related to the checks used by Andreasen, who evaluates whether the decoded curves are dynamically close to arbitrage-free through bond Sharpe ratios. 
In that framework, the curves are only approximately arbitrage-free, whereas in the present model the no-arbitrage condition is imposed directly through the ODE-based decoder. 
This difference is central to the interpretation of the empirical results.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/...}
    \caption{Approximate model-implied bond Sharpe ratios across maturities for a representative date and all currencies. Values remain close to zero across the maturity spectrum, supporting the numerical arbitrage-free property of the decoder.}
    \label{fig:sharperatioplot}
\end{figure}

The Sharpe-ratio calculations confirm that the reconstructed bond curves are numerically consistent with the no-arbitrage restrictions built into the model. 
Any remaining deviations from zero are attributable to numerical discretization and solver tolerance rather than to an economically meaningful arbitrage signal. 
This finding is important because it shows that the gain in theoretical consistency is achieved without rendering the reconstruction error unacceptably large.

To assess whether this result is stable across the sample rather than specific to a single date, it is also useful to summarize the Sharpe-ratio diagnostics over time and across currencies, for example by reporting mean absolute Sharpe ratios by tenor.

\section{Latent factors and parameter dynamics}
%========================================================

Beyond reconstruction accuracy, the model delivers economically interpretable state variables and risk-neutral parameters. 
The encoded latent factors summarize the cross-sectional shape of the swap curve, while the networks for the short rate, drift, and diffusion translate these latent states into a full arbitrage-free term-structure representation.

%========================================================
\section{Summary of findings}
%========================================================

Overall, the empirical evidence suggests three main conclusions. 
First, the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder achieves a strong in-sample fit, and reconstruction accuracy improves substantially as the latent dimension is increased. 
Second, the higher-dimensional models require longer training horizons, so short training runs may understate their performance, particularly out of sample. 
Third, the model delivers reconstructions that are numerically consistent with no-arbitrage, which is the key advantage of the proposed framework relative to more flexible but only approximately arbitrage-free alternatives.

The results therefore indicate that the hard no-arbitrage restriction is not excessively costly in terms of fit, while it delivers a structurally coherent term-structure representation that can be interpreted dynamically through the extracted latent factors and risk-neutral parameters.
