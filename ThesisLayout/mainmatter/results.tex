This chapter evaluates the empirical performance of the arbitrage-free autoencoder introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, fitted to the swap rate curves described in Chapter~\ref{chap:datadescription}. The evaluation considers three criteria: in-sample reconstruction accuracy, numerical satisfaction of the no-arbitrage restriction, and out-of-sample generalization. \textcolor{red}{Here, the no-arbitrage restriction means that the zero-coupon bond prices generated by the decoder must satisfy the risk-neutral bond-pricing PDE derived in Section~\ref{subsec:no_arbitrage_ode_system}.} Reconstruction accuracy is assessed across latent factor specifications $\ell \in \{1,2,3,4\}$, while out-of-sample performance is evaluated using a rolling window scheme across different market environments.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{In-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

In-sample evaluation serves as the first diagnostic in assessing the empirical performance of the model. Before considering out-of-sample reconstruction, the model must demonstrate that it can accurately reconstruct the swap rate curves used for estimation and that the implied zero-coupon bond prices satisfy the no-arbitrage restriction in practice.

Following \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, reconstruction accuracy is measured by the root mean squared error (RMSE) in basis points. Compliance with the no-arbitrage restriction is evaluated numerically using the residual of the bond-pricing PDE in \eqref{eq:bond_pricing_pde}. Since the decoder constructs zero-coupon bond prices by solving the ODE system derived from this PDE, the residual should be close to zero up to numerical approximation error.

\subsection{Reconstruction Accuracy}

We begin by examining how reconstruction error evolves over 5\,000 training epochs. Figure~\ref{fig:training_loss} shows the average training RMSE across all currencies for $\ell \in \{1,2,3,4\}$. The largest reduction in reconstruction error occurs early in training, after which the loss curves flatten considerably. For $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$, the curves exhibit noticeable spikes around 2\,500 epochs, suggesting a temporary optimizer transient or movement through a less stable region of the loss surface. After approximately 3\,500 epochs, however, all specifications appear to enter a more stable training regime, with only limited improvements thereafter. The final in-sample reconstruction accuracy is therefore assessed after the full 5\,000 training epochs.

The table confirms that the one-factor specification is too restrictive, with an average RMSE of 36.91 bps compared with 12.11 bps for $\ell=2$. Among the remaining specifications, reconstruction accuracy improves with the number of latent factors, but with diminishing gains: the average RMSE falls from 12.11 bps for $\ell=2$ to 9.94 bps for $\ell=3$ and 8.23 bps for $\ell=4$. The improvement in reconstruction accuracy comes at a computational cost. Training time increases only moderately from $\ell=1$ to $\ell=3$, from 54.0 to 63.5 minutes, but rises sharply to 126.1 minutes for $\ell=4$. This is consistent with the higher cost of the no-arbitrage decoder in larger latent dimensions. The bond-pricing PDE contains the Hessian-trace term
\[
\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[\sigma(z_t)\sigma(z_t)^\top H_z \mathcal{G}(z_t,\tau)\right],
\]
where \(H_z \mathcal{G}\) is an \(\ell\times \ell\) Hessian. A direct derivative calculation therefore involves \(\mathcal{O}(\ell^2)\) second-order terms, making the decoder increasingly expensive as the latent dimension grows. Given the substantially higher reconstruction error of $\ell=1$, the remaining diagnostic analysis focuses on $\ell\in\{2,3,4\}$.

In the calmer period, the observed curves are generally reconstructed well, although JPY already displays some short-end mismatch. During the COVID-19 shock, the reconstruction task becomes more challenging, especially for EUR and JPY. For these currencies, the observed curves have negative short-end rates and rise steeply with maturity, whereas the fitted curves remain closer to zero and are too flat. This suggests that part of the elevated reconstruction error for EUR and JPY is related to the model's difficulty in representing low- and negative-rate curves within the pooled low-dimensional decoder.

The in-sample results show that the autoencoder reconstructs swap rate curves with reasonable accuracy once at least two latent factors are used. Increasing the latent dimension improves the fit, but with diminishing marginal gains. The remaining reconstruction errors are concentrated at the short and long ends of the curve, during periods of abrupt market movements, and especially for low- or negative-rate curves such as EUR and JPY during the COVID-19 shock. Having assessed reconstruction accuracy, we now turn to the second in-sample criterion: whether the implied zero-coupon bond prices satisfy the no-arbitrage restriction.

\subsection{No-Arbitrage PDE Residual Diagnostic}

Reconstruction accuracy alone is not sufficient if the implied zero-coupon bond prices violate the no-arbitrage restriction. We therefore evaluate the residual of the arbitrage-free bond-pricing PDE in \eqref{eq:bond_pricing_pde}. As shown in Section~\ref{subsec:no_arbitrage_ode_system}, the decoder constructs zero-coupon bond prices by solving the ODE system derived from this PDE. The residual should therefore be close to zero, up to numerical approximation error, if the no-arbitrage restriction is satisfied numerically.

Figure~\ref{fig:Q7_pde_residual} reports the in-sample PDE residual for the $\ell=3$ specification, averaged by currency over all in-sample dates. Two features of the figure indicate that the no-arbitrage restriction is satisfied numerically. First, the scale of the residuals is on the order of $10^{-9}$, so deviations from zero are negligible in magnitude. Second, the residuals remain centered around zero across maturities and currencies, with no visible systematic pattern. This is consistent with numerical approximation error rather than systematic violations of the bond-pricing PDE.

\section{Out-of-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Accurate in-sample reconstruction is necessary, but not sufficient. A flexible model may fit the training sample closely while failing to reconstruct unseen swap rate curves. To assess whether the arbitrage-free autoencoder generalizes beyond the estimation sample, we evaluate its out-of-sample reconstruction performance using a rolling window scheme. Based on the convergence analysis in Figure~\ref{fig:training_loss}, each model is trained for 3\,500 epochs. The rolling-window in-sample RMSEs are therefore not directly identical to the full-sample in-sample results reported above, which are based on 5\,000 training epochs.

A rolling window scheme repeatedly estimates the model on a fixed-length training period and evaluates it on the subsequent hold-out period, before moving the entire window forward in time. This preserves the time ordering of the data and mimics a setting in which only past information is available when reconstructing new observations. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_window_diagram}, each window consists of a five-year training period followed by a six-month test period. The window is advanced by six months at a time, yielding 18 non-overlapping test periods spanning January 2015 to December 2023.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/ExtraFigures/rolling_window_diagram.png}
    \caption{Rolling window evaluation design. Each window consists of a five-year training period followed by a six-month test period. The window is advanced by six months at a time, yielding non-overlapping out-of-sample test periods.}
    \label{fig:rolling_window_diagram}
\end{figure}

For each rolling window, the in-sample RMSE is computed on the five-year training period, while the out-of-sample RMSE is computed on the subsequent six-month test period. Comparing these errors provides a direct measure of the model's generalization gap.

Figure~\ref{fig:Q2b} reports the average rolling out-of-sample RMSE across the 18 test periods for each latent dimension, with the corresponding in-sample RMSE included as a reference.

\begin{figure}[H]

The results reveal a substantial gap between in-sample and out-of-sample reconstruction performance. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Q2b}, the autoencoder fits the rolling training windows well across all latent dimensions, with average in-sample RMSEs between 9.31 and 11.61 bps. However, out-of-sample performance deteriorates markedly, especially for the higher-dimensional specifications. The $\ell=2$ model increases from an average in-sample RMSE of 10.46 bps to an out-of-sample RMSE of 19.00 bps, while the $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$ models increase to 66.53 and 100.53 bps, respectively. Thus, although additional latent factors reduce reconstruction error on the estimation sample, they do not translate into better out-of-sample performance in the rolling-window setting.
