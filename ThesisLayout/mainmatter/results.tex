\label{chap:evaluation}

First, the in-sample analysis assesses whether the model can accurately reconstruct the swap rate curves used for estimation. This chapter evaluates the empirical performance of the arbitrage-free autoencoder introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, trained on the swap rate curves described in Chapter~\ref{chap:datadescription}. The model is evaluated across four latent specifications, $\ell \in \{1,2,3,4\}$, proceeding in two stages. First, in-sample performance is assessed across all four specifications. Second, out-of-sample generalization is evaluated using a rolling window scheme, examining performance across different market regimes.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{In-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Two criteria are used to assess in-sample performance. First, the model must accurately reconstruct the swap rate curves used for training. Following \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, reconstruction accuracy is measured by the root mean squared error (RMSE) in basis points. Second, the implied zero-coupon bond prices must satisfy the no-arbitrage restriction, which is evaluated numerically via the residual of the bond-pricing PDE in \eqref{eq:bond_pricing_pde}. By construction, the decoder solves the ODE system derived from this PDE, so the residual should be close to zero up to numerical precision.

\subsection{Reconstruction Accuracy}
\label{subsec:reconstruction_accuracy} 

We begin by examining how reconstruction error evolves over 5\,000 training epochs. As seen in Figure~\ref{fig:training_loss}, the largest reduction in reconstruction error occurs early in training, after which the loss curves flatten considerably. For $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$, the loss curves exhibit brief upward spikes around 2\,500 epochs before stabilising. By 3\,500 epochs, all specifications have settled into a stable training regime with only marginal improvements thereafter. Notably, the $\ell=1$ specification settles at a markedly higher reconstruction error than the remaining specifications.

Table \ref{tab:q1a_is_rmse} confirms this, with $\ell=1$ yielding an average RMSE of 36.91 bps compared with 12.11 bps for $\ell=2$, indicating that a single latent factor is insufficient to capture the full shape of the swap rate curve. Given the relatively poor reconstruction accuracy of $\ell=1$, the remainder of the evaluation focuses on $\ell\in\{2,3,4\}$.

Among the remaining specifications, reconstruction accuracy improves with the number of latent factors, though with diminishing gains. The average RMSE decreases from 12.11 bps for $\ell=2$ to 9.94 bps for $\ell=3$ and 8.23 bps for $\ell=4$. However, these gains come at a computational cost. Training time increases moderately from $\ell=2$ to $\ell=3$, from 58.6 to 63.5 minutes, but nearly doubles for $\ell=4$. This is largely driven by the no-arbitrage decoder, which becomes increasingly expensive in larger latent dimensions. Specifically, the bond-pricing PDE contains the Hessian-trace term
\[
\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[\sigma(z_t)\sigma(z_t)^\top H_z \mathcal{G}(z_t,\tau)\right],
\]
where \(H_z \mathcal{G}\) is an \(\ell\times \ell\) Hessian, so a direct derivative calculation involves \(\mathcal{O}(\ell^2)\) second-order terms. 


The distribution of in-sample residuals, defined as the difference between the observed and reconstructed swap rates and pooled across all currencies, dates and tenors, provides further insight beyond the aggregate RMSE. As seen in Figure~\ref{fig:q1d_residual_histograms}, all three distributions are centred close to zero, indicating little systematic bias.

Nevertheless, this pooling may mask systematic errors within specific currencies, tenors, or time periods, which is addressed in what follows.


Focusing on $\ell=3$, reconstruction error is not evenly distributed over time. Around the time of the COVID-19 outbreak in March 2020, the error spikes notably for EUR and DKK, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Q6b}. This coincides with the sharp reversal in global swap rates observed around 2020 (Figure~\ref{fig:D2b_10y_timeseries}, Chapter~\ref{chap:datadescription}), suggesting that the model may struggle to reconstruct curves during periods of regime change, particularly given that it was trained on a predominantly globally declining rate environment.

Beyond the time dimension, reconstruction error also varies systematically across maturities. Figure~\ref{fig:Q6a} shows that the short and long ends of the swap rate curve are consistently more difficult to reconstruct across all specifications, while mid-range tenors exhibit lower errors. This may reflect the fact that the model has less structural information to draw on at the boundaries of the maturity spectrum, where the curve shape is more sensitive to short-term monetary policy expectations at the short end and to long-run term premium dynamics at the long end. The effect is most pronounced at the short end, with EUR and JPY exhibiting the largest errors at these extremes, consistent with their higher aggregate RMSE in Table~\ref{tab:q1a_is_rmse}.

In the calmer period, the observed curves are generally reconstructed well across all specifications, although JPY already displays some short-end mismatch. During the COVID-19 shock, the reconstruction task becomes more challenging, especially for EUR and JPY, where all three specifications struggle similarly. For these currencies, the observed swap rates reach low and negative levels, with the short end turning negative and the curve sloping upward with maturity. The fitted curves, however, remain closer to zero and fail to capture this shape across all three specifications. This suggests that the difficulty in reconstructing low- and negative-rate curves is not resolved by increasing the number of latent factors, pointing to a more fundamental limitation of the model. It is therefore of interest to examine whether reconstruction accuracy varies more broadly with the shape and level of the curve, not only for negative-rate curves, but also for structurally different shapes such as inverted curves.


Figure~\ref{fig:q6e_scatter_regime}plots the per-observation RMSE over time, coloured by interest rate regime, where each observation is classified by whether the curve is normal or inverted and whether rates are positive or negative. Normal, non-negative curves are reconstructed most accurately, while both inverted and negative rate curves exhibit notably higher errors. This is particularly evident for EUR and JPY, which experienced prolonged negative-rate periods and show the largest aggregate errors. The higher errors suggest that the model finds structurally atypical curve shapes harder to represent, particularly given that is was trained predominantly on a globally declining rate environment.

The in-sample reconstruction results show that the autoencoder captures swap rate curves with reasonable accuracy once at least two latent factors are used. Increasing the latent dimension improves reconstruction accuracy, though with diminishing gains. Figure~\ref{fig:Q7_pde_residual} reports the in-sample PDE residual for the $\ell=3$ specification, averaged by currency over all in-sample dates. Two features of the figure indicate that the no-arbitrage restriction is satisfied numerically. First, the residuals are on the order of $10^{-9}$, so deviations from zero are negligible in magnitude. Second, the values fluctuate randomly around zero across all maturities and currencies, consistent with numerical noise rather than a systematic arbitrage violation. The no-arbitrage restriction is therefore satisfied numerically.

\section{Out-of-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Accurate in-sample reconstruction is necessary, but not sufficient. A flexible model may fit the training sample closely while failing to reconstruct unseen swap rate curves. To assess whether the arbitrage-free autoencoder generalises beyond the training sample, we evaluate its out-of-sample reconstruction performance using a rolling window scheme. Based on the convergence analysis in Figure~\ref{fig:training_loss}, each model is trained for 3\,500 epochs. The rolling-window in-sample RMSEs are therefore not directly identical to the full-sample in-sample results reported above, which are based on 5\,000 training epochs.

The rolling window scheme used here re-estimates the model on a fixed five-year training period and evaluates it on the subsequent six-month test period. This mimics a setting where only past information is available at each point in time. The window is then advanced by six months, yielding 18 non-overlapping test periods spanning January 2015 to December 2023, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_window_diagram}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/ExtraFigures/rolling_window_diagram.png}
    \caption{Rolling window evaluation design. Each window consists of a five-year training period followed by a six-month test period. The window is advanced by six months at a time, yielding 18 non-overlapping out-of-sample test periods.}
    \label{fig:rolling_window_diagram}
\end{figure}

For each rolling window, the in-sample RMSE is computed on the five-year training period and the out-of-sample RMSE on the subsequent six-month test period, providing a direct measure of how well the model generalises beyond the training sample. Figure~\ref{fig:Q2b} compares this generalization gap across model specifications $\ell\in\{2,3,4\}$.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.80\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/AutoencoderPerformance/Q2b_rolling_oos_vs_dim.png}
    \caption{Average in-sample (IS) and rolling out-of-sample (OOS) RMSE (bps) for the autoencoder across latent dimensions $\ell \in \{2, 3, 4\}$. For each $\ell$, the left bar shows the average IS RMSE and the right hatched bar shows the average OOS RMSE across all 18 rolling windows. Bars are coloured by dimension: $\ell=2$ ({\color[HTML]{843C39}\rule[0.5ex]{1em}{1.5pt}}), $\ell=3$ ({\color[HTML]{393B79}\rule[0.5ex]{1em}{1.5pt}}), $\ell=4$ ({\color[HTML]{D6616B}\rule[0.5ex]{1em}{1.5pt}}).}
    \label{fig:Q2b}
\end{figure}

The results reveal a substantial gap between in-sample and out-of-sample reconstruction performance. The autoencoder fits the rolling training windows well across all specifications, with average in-sample RMSEs between 9.31 and 11.61 bps. Out-of-sample performance worsens markedly, especially for the higher-dimensional specifications. The $\ell=2$ model increases from an average in-sample RMSE of 10.46 bps to 19.00 bps out-of-sample, while the $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$ models increase to 66.53 and 100.53 bps, respectively. Thus, although additional latent factors reduce in-sample reconstruction error, they do not translate into better out-of-sample performance.

Table~\ref{tab:Q4a} shows that the out-of-sample loss in accuracy is uneven across currencies. For $\ell=2$, the increase in RMSE is moderate for most currencies, whereas $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$ exhibit severe currency-specific errors, particularly for JPY, EUR, CAD, and AUD. Average OOS RMSE is therefore driven largely by a few currencies rather than by uniform loss in accuracy across all markets.

The shaded regions further indicate that the instability is localised both across time and across currencies. The $\ell=2$ specification, though less accurate in-sample, exhibits more stable out-of-sample behaviour.

The regime-dependent pattern documented in Section~\ref{subsec:reconstruction_accuracy} raises the question of whether it persists out-of-sample. Focusing on $\ell=3$, Figure~\ref{fig:q4c_oos_scatter_regime} shows that it does, but is considerably more pronounced. Inverted curves are by far the most challenging, with an average OOS RMSE of 89.66 bps compared to 12.31 bps for normal, non-negative curves (Table~\ref{tab:q4c_oos_combined}). This represents a far more severe loss in accuracy than was observed in-sample, where inverted and non-negative curves were reconstructed at comparable accuracy. Negative-rate curves also lose accuracy out-of-sample, averaging 59.71 bps, though less severely than inverted curves. Notably, this is despite the rolling windows using only 3,500 training epochs compared to 5,000 for the in-sample results, suggesting that the difficulty with inverted curves is not primarily a function of training length but rather the structural shift in curve shape.

Overall, the out-of-sample results indicate that $\ell=2$ offers the most stable performance across rolling windows, while higher dimensional specifications are prone to instability in specific market environments. The errors are concentrated in periods of structural change, particularly the low-rate environment of 2016 and the rate-hiking cycle of 2022, and are most sever for inverted curves, which where rarely observed in the preceding training periods.
