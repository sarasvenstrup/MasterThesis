\chapter{Evaluation of the Arbitrage-Free Autoencoder}


This section evaluates the arbitrage-free autoencoder model described in Section \ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, fitted to swap rate curves across nine currencies (Section \ref{chap:datadescription}). The evaluation proceeds in three stages. First, in-sample performance is assessed across 2, 3, and 4 factor specifications. Second, out-of-sample reconstruction is benchmarked against the EKF-DNS model using a rolling window scheme, and robustness to random initialization is assessed across multiple seeds. Finally, the model output is analyzed, including latent factors, risk-neutral parameters, and the implied yield curve.

The evaluation is carried out using multiple tests. First of all we are interested in checking if the model does in fact work, and are we able to extend the model from only 2-factors as done in \cite{MatinWanPoulsen2025} and \cite{Andreasen2023}, to both 3 and 4 factors. Here the criteria for working is having reasonable reconstruction errors and arbitrage free results. After establishing this conclusion, we then want to evaluate the performance of the model out-of-sample. How well can the model reconstruct the swap rates on unseen data, and further how robust is it over different time periods. In order to do so we do this with a rolling window scheme, benchmarking against the Extended Kalman Filter Dynamic Nelson--Siegel (EKF-DNS) model. We also want to test the general robustness of the model by performing a fixed-window scheme and testing over different seeds, meaning we initialize the model parameters differently, to see how the model performs all else equal. After these assessment of the models performance, testing different aspects, we lastly want to analyze the resulting model output. Firstly, how the latent factors compare to classical PCA results, secondly the time series of the risk-neutral parameters, and lasty the zero-coupon bond price curve and the corresponding yield curve and how well this matches with what we see in the bond market.

\section{In-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

In-sample evaluation serves as the first checkpoint toward establishing whether the model is practically useful. Before assessing out-of-sample reconstruction, the model must demonstrate that it can accurately reconstruct the swap rate curves it was trained on, and that the implied zero-coupon bond prices satisfy the no-arbitrage constraint. Following \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, the precision of the reconstruction is measured by the RMSE in basis points, and the compliance with no-arbitrage is assessed through \textcolor{red}{the approximate Sharpe ratio (REFERENCE)} of the implied zero-coupon bond prices, which should be negligible if the constraint is satisfied by construction.

\section{Out-of-Sample Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────


While the in-sample results demonstrate that the autoencoder reconstructs the swap rate curve closely, and suggest that models with more latent factors achieve better reconstruction, a good in-sample fit alone does not guarantee that the model generalizes to new data. In particular, a model with many parameters may overfit the training period, failing to preserve its reconstruction accuracy when applied to independent observations. \textcolor{red}{(Reference to RegressionbyR Introduction - 1.2)} To assess generalization, two experiments are conducted. First, a rolling window evaluation tests out-of-sample reconstruction. Second, a robustness analysis repeats a fixed train/test split across multiple random seeds, assessing the sensitivity of out-of-sample performance to the random initialization of the model. All experiments in this section use 2,500 training epochs, which Figure~\ref{fig:training_loss} confirms is sufficient for convergence.

\subsection{Rolling Window Evaluation}

To evaluate generalization and identify which latent dimension yields the best out-of-sample reconstruction, a rolling window design is used (Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_window_diagram}), yielding 18 non-overlapping test periods spanning January 2015 to December 2023.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/ExtraFigures/rolling_window_diagram.png}
    \caption{Rolling window evaluation design: 18 windows each consisting of a five-year training period followed by a six-month test period, stepping forward six months at a time.}
    \label{fig:rolling_window_diagram}
\end{figure}



The results of the rolling window scheme is summed up in \ref{fig:Q2b} and we clearly see that the in-sample RMSE is consistently lower than the OOS RMSE across all model dimensions. Interestingly the autoencoder with four latent factors to describe the swap rate curve, has not only the worst perfomance relative to the in sample RMSE, but also overall the worst model OOS. From these it seems that our model with three latent factor performs best OOS.

An average for the whole model is not so telling when wanting to understand the basis of this errors. IF we look on how the models perform on a currency level, we see that the model with 4 dimensions actually performs better in some case but much worse in other cases.

For $\ell = 2$ and $\ell = 3$, the IS--OOS gap is modest (12.11 vs.\ 15.57 bps and 9.96 vs.\ 12.44 bps, respectively), indicating that both models generalise well to unseen data. In contrast, $\ell = 4$ achieves the lowest IS RMSE of 4.33 bps but degrades sharply out-of-sample to 17.38 bps --- nearly four times its training error --- with a particularly large EUR OOS deviation of 70.53 bps. This pattern is characteristic of overfitting: the additional latent factor captures noise in the training data rather than genuine curve structure. Together, these results suggest that $\ell = 2$ or $\ell = 3$ offers the best balance between reconstruction accuracy and generalisation.

\section{Latent Factor Interpretation}

The swap rate curve is observed at eight maturities, but its variation may be governed by far fewer underlying factors. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) identifies these by finding the orthogonal directions of maximum variance, yielding a low-dimensional representation that retains the greatest possible proportion of total variation. Since the encoder is linear, its learned directions can be directly compared to the PCA eigenvectors, telling us whether a model trained purely on reconstruction error recovers the same structure as a method designed explicitly to capture variance.


PCA is applied to decompose the swap rate curve into $M=8$ principal components. Figure \ref{fig:pca_pve} shows the proportion of variance explained by $PC_j$, $\mathrm{PVE}_j=\lambda_j/\sum_{k=1}^M\lambda_k$, where the first two principal components account for over $99\%$ of the total variation in the swap curve data. \textcolor{red}{The remaining components are negligible, motivating the use of a low-dimensional latent representation with $\ell = \{2,3,4\}$ factors.}

Figure~\ref{fig:pca_loadings} shows the evolution of the principal
component scores over time alongside their eigenvector loadings, revealing
that the three dominant components capture the classical level, slope and
curvature structure of the swap rate curve. Having established this structure, we now ask whether the encoder has independently learned to compress the data along the same directions.




Let $\mathbf{a}_k\in\mathbb{R}^M$ denote the $k$-th row of the encoder weight matrix $A_1$, representing the direction along which factor $k$ compresses the input. Following \cite{MatinWanPoulsen2025}, we assess how much this direction aligns with each principal component $\mathbf{v}_j$ by decomposing $\textbf{a}_k$ in the PCA basis:
\begin{equation}
    w_{k,j} = \frac{(\mathbf{a}_k^\top \mathbf{v}_j)^2}{\|\mathbf{a}_k\|^2}
    \times 100, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_{k,j} = 100.
    \label{eq:weight_proj}
\end{equation}

The projection weights $w_{i,j}$ are purely geometric; they compare fixed directions and say nothing about whether the latent factors actually move with the PC scores through time. To assess this co-movement, we compute the Pearson correlation between factor $z_k(t)$ and the $j$-th PC score
$\mathrm{PC}_{j,t} = \mathbf{v}_j^\top \mathbf{S}_t$ over all in-sample observations:
\begin{equation}
\rho_{k,j} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^n\bigl(z_k(t)-\bar{z}_k\bigr)
  \bigl(\mathrm{PC}_{j,t}-\overline{\mathrm{PC}}_j\bigr)}
  {\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n\bigl(z_k(t)-\bar{z}_k\bigr)^2
  \cdot\sum_{t=1}^n\bigl(\mathrm{PC}_{j,t}-\overline{\mathrm{PC}}_j\bigr)^2}}
\label{eq:pearson}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{z}_k$ and $\overline{\mathrm{PC}}_j$ are the respective sample
means, computed for all models $\ell \in \{2, 3, 4\}$.
