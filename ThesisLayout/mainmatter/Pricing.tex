%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Joint Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter examined whether the trained encoder--decoder model can be used as a stable forward scenario generator. We now use the same simulation framework for derivative pricing. In fixed-income applications, accurate reconstruction of the current swap curve is not sufficient: the value of an interest-rate derivative depends on the distribution of future discount factors, swap rates and short rates under the pricing measure.

The encoder--decoder framework provides a natural simulation-based pricing setup. Starting from an observed swap curve, the encoder gives an initial latent state. The learned risk-neutral dynamics are then simulated forward, and the decoder maps each simulated latent state into an arbitrage-free zero-coupon bond curve. This makes it possible to compute pathwise swap rates, annuities and discounted payoffs.

The purpose of this chapter is therefore twofold. First, we describe how European swaptions are priced by Monte Carlo using the simulated latent paths and decoded bond curves. Second, we introduce a joint training step, in which the model is trained simultaneously on swap-curve reconstruction and swaption pricing, allowing the diffusion and drift components to be calibrated to observed swaption normal volatilities while maintaining reconstruction quality.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Swaption payoff and pathwise swap rate}
\label{subsec:swaption_payoff}

Having generated simulated latent paths and decoded zero-coupon bond curves, we can price interest-rate derivatives by Monte Carlo under the risk-neutral measure. This section presents the methodology for European swaptions.

Let \(T_e\) denote the option expiry, let \(T_s=n\delta\) denote the tenor of the underlying swap, and let \(\delta>0\) denote the fixed-leg accrual period. In the implementation, we use annual accrual, so \(\delta=1\). The fixed-leg payment dates are therefore
\[
T_e+\delta,\; T_e+2\delta,\; \dots,\; T_e+n\delta.
\]

The strike is determined from the time-zero discount curve. Given the decoded bond prices \(P(0,T)\), the swap annuity at time \(0\) is
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(0,T_e+j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding forward swap rate is
\[
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n\delta)}{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
For an at-the-money swaption, the strike is chosen as
\[
K = F_0.
\]

For each simulated path \(m=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the decoder delivers a full zero-coupon bond curve at expiry. Since the model is expressed in terms of time-to-maturity, this curve is written as
\[
\tau \mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau).
\]
The payment dates of the underlying swap correspond to the maturities
\[
\delta,\; 2\delta,\; \dots,\; n\delta
\]
measured from \(T_e\). The pathwise annuity at expiry is therefore
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P^{(m)}(T_e,j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding spot-starting swap rate is
\[
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]

Let \(\mathcal N>0\) denote the notional. The payer swaption payoff on path \(m\) is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K,0\bigr),
\]
while the receiver swaption payoff is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e),0\bigr).
\]

\subsection{Monte Carlo estimator and implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:mc_estimator_implied_vol}

The payoff is discounted back to time \(0\) using the pathwise bank account discount factor computed during simulation. Denoting this factor on path \(m\) by
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\left(-\int_0^{T_e} r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
the discounted present value on path \(m\) is
\[
V_0^{(m)}
=
D^{(m)}_{T_e} X^{(m)}_{T_e}.
\]
The time-zero swaption price is then estimated by the Monte Carlo average
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}} V_0^{(m)}.
\]

To assess numerical uncertainty, we also report the sample standard deviation
\[
\widehat \sigma_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}-1}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\left(V_0^{(m)}-\widehat V_0\right)^2
}
\]
and the associated Monte Carlo standard error
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\frac{\widehat \sigma_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}}.
\]

In the implementation, the notional \(\mathcal N\) is set to 1 for simplicity. The time-zero quantities \(F_0\) and \(\mathcal A_0\) are computed directly from the decoded initial discount curve, while the pathwise quantities \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) are computed from the decoded discount curve at expiry on each simulated path. Because the pricing routine extracts bond prices directly from the model grids, the option expiry \(T_e\) must lie on the simulation time grid, while the payment dates \(j\delta\) must lie on the decoder maturity grid. In the numerical examples below, we consider in particular at-the-money payer swaptions, for which the strike is set equal to the time-zero forward swap rate.

We can finally summarise the pricing pipeline schematically as
\[
S_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
z_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
z^{(m)}_{T_e}
\;\longrightarrow\;
P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)
\;\longrightarrow\;
\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e),\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigr)
\;\longrightarrow\;
X^{(m)}_{T_e}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\widehat V_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
\widehat\sigma_N.
\]

\section{Bachelier Normal Volatility Framework}
\label{sec:bachelier_model}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Bachelier swaption formula}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

A European payer swaption with option expiry \(T_e\) and swap tenor \(T_s\) gives the holder the right to enter a fixed-for-floating swap at expiry. Under the annuity measure \(\mathbb{Q}^{\mathcal A}\), the forward swap rate \(F(t)\) is a martingale. In the Bachelier normal model \citep{Bachelier1900}, it evolves as
\begin{equation}
    dF(t) = \sigma_N\, dW^{\mathcal A}(t),
    \label{eq:bachelier_sde}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_N>0\) is the normal volatility. The normal model is convenient in low- and negative-rate environments because it allows the forward swap rate to take negative values.

Under~\eqref{eq:bachelier_sde}, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with notional \(\mathcal N\), strike \(K\), expiry \(T_e\), forward swap rate \(F_0\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{pay}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (F_0 - K)\,\Phi(d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr],
    \qquad
    d = \frac{F_0 - K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:bachelier_payer}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) and \(\phi\) denote the standard normal distribution function and density, respectively. For a receiver swaption, the corresponding price is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{rec}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (K - F_0)\,\Phi(-d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr].
    \label{eq:bachelier_receiver}
\end{equation}

For an at-the-money swaption, \(K=F_0\), and hence \(d=0\). The payer and receiver prices coincide and reduce to
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:bachelier_atm}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Market price targets and implied-volatility reporting}
\label{subsec:bachelier_two_roles}

The Bachelier formula serves two distinct roles in the implementation.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pricing target for the joint calibration.}
    Market swaption data are quoted as at-the-money normal volatilities \(\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\), typically in basis points. Before applying the formula, these volatilities are converted to decimal rate units. The market volatility is then converted into a price using the ATM formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_atm}:
    \begin{equation}
        V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
        =
        \mathcal N \mathcal A^{(i)}_0
        \frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
        \label{eq:pricing_target}
    \end{equation}
    This gives a fixed price target computed once per market observation, using the annuity \(\mathcal A_0^{(i)}\) associated with the corresponding quote date. The model Monte Carlo price \(\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}^{(i)}\) is then trained to match this target.

    \item \textbf{Implied-volatility inversion for reporting.}
    After pricing, the model price is converted back into a normal implied volatility for comparison with market quotes. In the at-the-money case, the inversion is explicit:
    \begin{equation}
        \widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}
        =
        \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
        {\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
        \label{eq:implied_vol}
    \end{equation}
    For off-ATM strikes, the Bachelier formula can instead be inverted numerically. Reporting prices in basis-point normal-volatility space makes model errors directly comparable to market quoting conventions.
\end{enumerate}

For a payer swaption, the model-implied Monte Carlo price is estimated by
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{V}^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
    \sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
    D^{(m)}_{T_e}\,
    \mathcal N\,
    \mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
    \max\!\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e) - K,\; 0\bigr).
    \label{eq:mc_price}
\end{equation}
Here \(D^{(m)}_{T_e}\) is the pathwise money-market discount factor, while \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) are the annuity and swap rate decoded from the bond curve at expiry. The implied normal volatility \(\widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}\) is then obtained by inserting \(\widehat{V}^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}\) into~\eqref{eq:implied_vol}.

\subsection{Analytic Greeks}
\label{sec:bachelier_greeks}

The following proposition collects the closed-form sensitivities of the Bachelier payer price~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. These Greeks are useful both for interpretation and as analytic benchmarks for numerical pricing diagnostics.

\begin{proposition}[Bachelier Greeks]
\label{prop:bachelier_greeks}
Let \(V=V^{\mathrm{pay}}\) be given by~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}, and define
\[
d=\frac{F_0-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
With the remaining variables fixed in each partial derivative, the payer swaption Greeks are
\begin{align}
    \Delta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial F_0}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d),
    \label{eq:delta}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vega}
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma_N}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d),
    \label{eq:vega}
    \\
    \Gamma
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F_0^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:gamma}
    \\
    \Theta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial T_e}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\sigma_N\,\phi(d)}
    {2\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:theta}
    \\
    \mathrm{DV01}
    &=
    \Delta \times 10^{-4}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d)}{10\,000},
    \label{eq:dv01}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vanna}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F_0\,\partial\sigma_N}
    =
    -\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
    \label{eq:vanna}
    \\
    \mathrm{Volga}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial\sigma_N^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N}.
    \label{eq:volga}
\end{align}
For the receiver swaption,
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0(\Phi(d)-1)
=
-\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\Phi(-d),
\]
while Vega, Gamma, Theta, Vanna and Volga are the same as for the payer. Put-call parity implies
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{pay}}-\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}=\mathcal N \mathcal A_0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The result follows by direct differentiation of~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. For Delta, differentiating with respect to \(F_0\) and noting that \(\partial d/\partial F_0 = 1/(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e})\), the term involving \(\phi(d)\cdot\partial d/\partial F_0\) cancels exactly across the two summands, leaving \(\Delta = \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\Phi(d)\). For Vega, \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N = -d/\sigma_N\), giving
\[
\frac{\partial V}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\!\left[\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d) + \bigl((F_0-K)\Phi'(d) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\phi'(d)\bigr)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}\right].
\]
Using \(\Phi'(d)=\phi(d)\), \(\phi'(d)=-d\phi(d)\) and \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N = -d/\sigma_N\), the bracket in the parentheses is
\(\phi(d)(-d/\sigma_N)(F_0-K) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}(-d\phi(d))(-d/\sigma_N) = 0\),
leaving \(\mathrm{Vega} = \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)\). Gamma and Theta follow from differentiating Delta and from \(\partial d/\partial T_e = -d/(2T_e)\), respectively. Vanna and Volga use the same two identities:
\[
\mathrm{Vanna}
= \frac{\partial\Delta}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
= -\frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
\qquad
\mathrm{Volga}
= \frac{\partial\,\mathrm{Vega}}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi'(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}{\sigma_N}.
\]
\end{proof}

\paragraph{ATM simplifications.}
At the money, \(K=F_0\), so \(d=0\). Since \(\Phi(0)=1/2\) and \(\phi(0)=(2\pi)^{-1/2}\), the payer Greeks simplify to
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac12\mathcal N \mathcal A_0,
\qquad
\mathrm{Vega}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{\frac{T_e}{2\pi}},
\qquad
\Gamma^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0}{\sigma_N\sqrt{2\pi T_e}},
\qquad
\Theta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sigma_N}{2\sqrt{2\pi T_e}}.
\label{eq:atm_greeks}
\end{equation}
Vanna and Volga vanish at the money.

\paragraph{Theta--Gamma identity.}
Combining~\eqref{eq:gamma} and~\eqref{eq:theta} gives
\begin{equation}
    \Theta = \frac12\sigma_N^2\Gamma.
    \label{eq:theta_gamma}
\end{equation}
This identity holds for the partial derivative with respect to time to expiry, holding \(F_0\), \(\mathcal A_0\), \(K\), and \(\sigma_N\) fixed. It provides a useful analytic consistency check for the Bachelier formulas, although it is not a full calendar-time theta for a dynamic term-structure model, where the annuity and forward swap rate also evolve.

\section{Why Recon-Only Training Cannot Price}
\label{sec:why_recon_only_fails}
%=========================================================

The encoder--decoder checkpoints from Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} were trained to minimise reconstruction error on observed swap curves. Reconstruction is necessary but not sufficient for derivative pricing: pricing requires that the decoder produce sensible discount curves not just at encoded points but at every latent state the simulated SDE visits. This section assembles the empirical evidence that recon-only training fails this requirement, and that the failure has two distinct mechanisms which a naive reading would conflate.

\paragraph{The pricing pipeline as a probe.}
The pricing pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology} can be read as a stress test of the trained model. From an encoded $z_0$, Euler--Maruyama is run forward to expiry to obtain $z_T$, and the decoder maps $z_T$ to a discount curve. Recon-only training optimises the decoder only on the manifold $\{\mathrm{encoder}(x) : x \in \mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{train}}\}$, which is a measure-zero subset of $\mathbb{R}^\ell$. Anything outside this manifold is uncharted territory for the decoder, and Brownian increments push $z_T$ off the manifold immediately.

\paragraph{Decoder robustness to off-manifold inputs.}
We probe each decoder directly by perturbing encoded points $z = z_0 + \varepsilon \eta$, $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_\ell)$, and measuring the fraction of decoded curves $\mathcal{G}(z)$ that are fully finite. Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:decoder_robustness} show the result for all six recon-only checkpoints. Three observations stand out. First, every decoder handles $\varepsilon = 0$ perfectly, as it should --- on-manifold inputs are exactly the inputs the decoder was trained on. Second, robustness degrades sharply: by $\varepsilon \approx 0.5$, none of the decoders is reliable. Third, the stable variant is uniformly \emph{more} robust than its baseline counterpart of the same dimension, refuting the natural hypothesis that stable's tighter encoded manifold makes its decoder more brittle. The stable decoder generalises better off-manifold; it is simply that no recon-only decoder generalises far enough.

\paragraph{Where the SDE actually goes.}
Whether the recon-only decoder's failure regime is reached during real simulation depends on the SDE's displacement distribution. Simulating $1{,}000$ Euler--Maruyama paths to $T_e = 5$Y from a representative EUR curve reveals two qualitatively different pathologies in the dim4 case (Figure~\ref{fig:latent_displacement_cdf} and Table~\ref{tab:displacement_summary}). The baseline SDE diverges to $\|z_T - z_0\| \sim 10^{13}$ --- thirteen orders of magnitude larger than the stable SDE's $\|z_T - z_0\| \sim 2$. At those astronomical magnitudes the baseline decoder happens to extrapolate to finite-but-meaningless outputs, returning curves that yield the large pricing errors observed when the recon-only checkpoints are evaluated against market quotes. The stable decoder, by contrast, rejects its (well-behaved) inputs with NaNs roughly half the time, because $\|z_T - z_0\| \approx 2$ sits exactly in the regime where Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} predicts failure.

This is the central observation of the section: baseline and stable both fail at pricing, but for different reasons. Baseline fails by SDE divergence --- the latent state explodes to coordinates no curve ever reached --- and the decoder is permissive enough to return finite garbage. Stable fails by decoder rejection --- the latent state is well-behaved but the decoder has never been asked to handle the modest displacements the SDE produces. Two different broken things, one common consequence: recon-only training cannot price.

\paragraph{$H$ versus $K$: where the displacement comes from.}
Decomposing the full displacement into diffusion-only and drift-only contributions localises the simulation pathology in the baseline. With $H = 0$, the baseline drift alone grows exponentially over five years (Figure~\ref{fig:drift_only_displacement} and Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition}); the linear part of $K$ has positive eigenvalues, and the unstable eigenvector absorbs the entire trajectory. The stable variant, having all-negative eigenvalues by construction, keeps the drift-only displacement bounded. For stable the realised displacement is therefore essentially the diffusion contribution, partially damped by the mean-reverting drift acting as a restoring force.

\paragraph{An apparent paradox in the stable variant.}
The stable equilibrium $z^* = -M^{-1} N_K$ for dim4 sits roughly $\|z^* - z_0\| \approx 9.5$ from a typical encoded EUR curve, against $\approx 0.2$ for baseline. One would expect strong mean-reverting drift with such a large equilibrium gap to dominate the displacement, yet the drift-only contribution at five years is only $\approx 0.14$. The resolution is that $(z^* - z_0)$ is almost entirely aligned with the slowest eigenvector of $K$. Table~\ref{tab:eigenvector_alignment} shows the projection breakdown: the slow eigenvector has timescale $1/|\lambda| \sim 600$ years and absorbs essentially all of the misplacement, so over five years $|1 - e^{\lambda T}|$ in that direction is below $0.01$. The misplacement is structural --- it sits in the direction the time-series fit cannot constrain --- but its dynamic effect at pricing-relevant horizons is negligible.

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_eigenvector_alignment}

\paragraph{Implications for joint training.}
Three conclusions follow. First, the recon-only stable model is not pricing-ready, but the failure mechanism is decoder generalisation, not SDE dynamics: stable's SDE delivers exactly the bounded latent paths it was designed for, and the displacement it produces is modest. Second, both architectures fail at pricing with recon-only training, but the comparison is asymmetric --- baseline's failure is a simulation pathology dressed up as a partial success, while stable's failure is an explicit rejection that quantifies the gap remaining to be closed. Third, joint training closes precisely that gap. The pricing loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{price}}$ introduced in the next section backpropagates through $\mathcal{G}(z_T)$, forcing the decoder to extend its working domain to the displacement range the SDE actually visits. The eigenvector-alignment result additionally suggests that adding a regulariser pushing $|\lambda_K|$ away from zero is conceptually clean --- it eliminates the slow direction in which $z^*$ can drift unconstrained --- without being strictly necessary at pricing horizons of $5$ to $10$ years.

\section{Joint Training: Reconstruction and Pricing}
\label{sec:joint_training}
%=========================================================

The diagnostics of Section~\ref{sec:why_recon_only_fails} identify the gap that pricing requires the model to close: the decoder must extend its working domain from the encoded manifold to the displacement range the simulated SDE actually visits. We address this directly through a joint training objective in which the model is fine-tuned simultaneously on swap-curve reconstruction and swaption-vol fitting, starting from the recon-only stable checkpoint of Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}.

\subsection{Joint objective function}
\label{subsec:joint_objective}

The joint training loss combines three components:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}}(\Omega)
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{recon}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}(\Omega)
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{price}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{price}}(\Omega)
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{eig}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eig}}(\Omega),
    \label{eq:joint_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega$ denotes all trainable parameters. The three terms are defined as follows.

\paragraph{Reconstruction loss.}
For a mini-batch of observed swap curves $\{S_i\}_{i \in \mathcal B}$, the reconstruction loss is the mean squared error
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}(\Omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal B|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal B}
    \|\widehat S_i - S_i\|^2,
    \label{eq:recon_loss}
\end{equation}
with $\widehat S_i$ the decoded swap curve. This term preserves the reconstruction quality acquired during pre-training; without it, the pricing gradient would degrade the encoder--decoder pair's fit to observed curves.

\paragraph{Pricing loss.}
Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology} delivers a Monte Carlo price $\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}^{(j)}$ for each ATM swaption quote $(\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(j)}, T_e^{(j)}, T_s^{(j)}, t^{(j)})$. Inverting the ATM Bachelier formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_atm} gives the model-implied normal volatility
\begin{equation}
    \widehat\sigma_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{(j)}
    =
    \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}^{(j)}\sqrt{2\pi}}
         {\mathcal N\,\mathcal A_0^{(j)}\sqrt{T_e^{(j)}}},
    \label{eq:sigma_mod_inversion}
\end{equation}
which we compare directly to the market vol in basis points. Writing $e^{(j)} = 10^4 \cdot (\widehat\sigma_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{(j)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(j)})$ for the vol-space residual and $\rho_\delta$ for the Huber penalty
\begin{equation*}
    \rho_\delta(e) =
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac12 e^2 & |e| \le \delta, \\
        \delta\bigl(|e| - \tfrac12 \delta\bigr) & |e| > \delta,
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
the pricing loss is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{price}}(\Omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{\delta^2 |\mathcal B_{\mathrm{price}}|}
    \sum_{j \in \mathcal B_{\mathrm{price}}}
    \rho_\delta\!\left(e^{(j)}\right),
    \qquad
    \delta = 50\text{ bp}.
    \label{eq:pricing_loss}
\end{equation}
Three design choices warrant comment. First, fitting in vol space rather than price space avoids the implicit $\mathcal A_0^2 T_e$ weighting that a squared price residual would impose on long-dated swaptions: the squared price residual scales with $\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}$ pointwise and hence with $\mathcal A_0^2 T_e$ after squaring, giving a 5Y$\times$10Y swaption (with $\mathcal A_0 \approx 7.4$) roughly $13.7\times$ the loss weight of a 1Y$\times$5Y swaption (with $\mathcal A_0 \approx 4.5$). The vol-space formulation is dimensionless once expressed in basis points and is invariant to annuity scale. Second, the Huber form is used rather than a pure squared error because the warm-start vols can disagree with market vols by several hundred basis points; a squared loss would put the gradient in regions where individual residuals dominate the batch and cause exploding optimiser steps. The Huber penalty grows linearly beyond $\delta = 50$ bp, capping the per-swaption gradient. Third, the rescaling by $\delta^2$ keeps $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{price}}$ at order one when residuals are of order $\delta$, which simplifies the choice of $\lambda_{\mathrm{price}}$.

\paragraph{Eigenvalue-floor regulariser.}
The diagnostic of Section~\ref{sec:why_recon_only_fails} identified one near-zero eigenvalue of the linearised drift in the recon-only stable model ($|\lambda_{\min}| \approx 0.002$), corresponding to a mean-reversion timescale of order centuries. This direction is structurally underdetermined by the recon objective and lets $z^*$ drift unconstrained. We add a soft floor on the magnitude of $K$'s eigenvalues:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eig}}(\Omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{\ell}\sum_{i=1}^{\ell}
    \bigl(\,\max(0,\;\varepsilon_{\min} - |\Re\lambda_i|)\,\bigr)^2,
    \label{eq:eig_loss}
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon_{\min} = 0.05$ (timescale $\sim 20$ years). The penalty activates only when an eigenvalue's magnitude falls below the floor. At pricing horizons of $5$ to $10$ years this regulariser has limited effect on $\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}$ directly --- the alignment analysis showed the slow direction barely activates over those horizons --- but it eliminates the unconstrained drift direction, removing a long-horizon pathology that would otherwise resurface in scenario-generation applications.

\subsection{Trainable parameter groups and gradient flow}
\label{subsec:param_groups}

Training starts from the dim4 stable checkpoint of Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. The encoder and short-rate network $\mathcal R$ are frozen for the entirety of joint training, and the decoder $\mathcal G$, drift $\mathcal K$, and diffusion $\mathcal H$ are fine-tuned at separately tuned learning rates (Table~\ref{tab:joint_hyperparams}). The freezing decision is deliberate. The recon-only encoder already produces a high-quality latent representation, with reconstruction RMSE of $5.3$ bp on EUR; allowing it to drift during pricing fine-tuning risks degrading recon performance without compensating gain in pricing, since the pricing-loss gradient with respect to the encoder parameters would have to travel through both the simulated path and the time-zero anchors. Likewise, the short-rate network $\mathcal R$ is frozen because it received no useful pricing signal under the recon-only objective and we want recon to remain its supervisor.

Within the pricing loss, the encoder pass to obtain $z_0$ and the time-zero decoder pass to obtain $F_0$ and $\mathcal A_0$ are evaluated under \texttt{no\_grad}. These two quantities serve as fixed anchors for the strike $K = F_0$ and the market price target, and propagating gradients through them would couple the strike choice to the loss minimisation, producing meaningless updates. The pricing loss therefore propagates gradients only through the simulation $z_0 \to z_T$ (touching $\mathcal K$ and $\mathcal H$) and through the expiry-time decode $\mathcal G(z_T)$.

A subtlety arises from the decoder generalisation problem: at warm start, roughly half of simulated paths produce non-finite outputs from $\mathcal G$. Naively backpropagating through such a batch produces NaN gradients on the shared decoder parameters even when the bad paths are masked from the loss, because PyTorch propagates $0 \times \mathrm{NaN} = \mathrm{NaN}$ through the autograd graph for masked-out branches. We therefore use a two-pass procedure: a no-gradient probe identifies which simulated $z_T$ values decode to finite curves, and a second pass with gradient enabled is run only on the surviving paths. The Monte Carlo estimator is then averaged over the survivors. As $\mathcal G$ extends its working domain during training, the survivor fraction approaches one and the two passes converge; the procedure introduces no bias in the limit. We track the survivor fraction explicitly as a training diagnostic.

\subsection{Variance reduction}
\label{subsec:antithetic}

The pricing loss is simulation-based and therefore subject to Monte Carlo noise. To reduce variance, we use antithetic variates: the $N_{\mathrm{MC}}/2$ noise realisations $\varepsilon$ and $-\varepsilon$ are paired so each path has its mirror image in the same Monte Carlo sample. This introduces negative dependence between paired paths while preserving the marginal distribution of the Brownian increments, halving the variance of any payoff that is monotone in $\varepsilon$ at no cost.

The Euler step
\[
z_{t+\Delta t}
=
z_t
+
\mu(z_t;\mathcal K)\Delta t
+
L(z_t;\mathcal H)\varepsilon_t\sqrt{\Delta t}
\]
is, conditional on the sampled noise, a deterministic differentiable transformation of the model parameters. Gradients therefore propagate through the simulated latent path, through all unfrozen network components, and through the diffusion matrix $L(z_t;\mathcal H)$ at each time step. Gradient clipping with maximum norm $1.0$ is applied uniformly to stabilise training.

The relative magnitudes of the three loss terms after rescaling are designed to keep reconstruction in primary control while letting the pricing loss exert directional pressure on $\mathcal G$, $\mathcal K$, and $\mathcal H$. The reconstruction MSE at warm start is of order $10^{-7}$ in squared-rate units; the Huber-rescaled pricing loss for an at-warm-start residual of $480$ bp is of order $9$ per swaption, contributing $\lambda_{\mathrm{price}} \cdot 9 \approx 0.45$ per batch of four swaptions.
