%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter examined whether the autoencoder model can be used as a stable forward scenario generator. We now use the same simulation framework for derivative pricing. In fixed-income applications, accurate reconstruction of the current swap curve is not sufficient: the value of an interest-rate derivative depends on the distribution of future discount factors, swap rates and short rates under the risk-neutral measure.

The encoder--decoder framework provides a natural simulation-based pricing setup. Starting from an observed swap curve, the encoder gives an initial latent state. The learned risk-neutral dynamics are then simulated forward, and the decoder maps each simulated latent state into an arbitrage-free zero-coupon bond curve. This makes it possible to compute pathwise swap rates, annuities and discounted payoffs.


%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We now specialize the simulation-based pricing framework to European swaptions. The objective is to compute the option payoff on each simulated path and then average the discounted payoffs to obtain a time-zero price.

The section first defines the underlying swap and the at-the-money strike. It then derives the pathwise swap rate and payoff at expiry, before introducing the Monte Carlo estimator and the Bachelier normal-volatility convention used for reporting model prices.


\subsection{Swaption payoff and pathwise swap rate}
\label{subsec:swaption_payoff}

A European swaption gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to enter into an interest-rate swap at a future exercise date \(T_e\). The underlying swap starts at \(T_e\) and has length \(T_s=n\delta\), where \(n\in\mathbb N\) is the number of fixed-leg payment periods and \(\delta>0\) is the fixed-leg accrual period. In the implementation, we use annual accrual, so \(\delta=1\). The fixed-leg payment dates are therefore
\[
T_e+\delta,\; T_e+2\delta,\; \dots,\; T_e+n\delta.
\]

The fixed rate agreed in the swaption contract is called the strike and is denoted by \(K\). A swaption is said to be at the money if this strike is equal to the current forward swap rate of the underlying swap. The forward swap rate is the par fixed rate, observed at time zero, for a swap starting at \(T_e\) and ending at \(T_e+n\delta\). Given the time-zero discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the corresponding swap annuity is
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(0,T_e+j\delta),
\]
and the forward swap rate is
\[
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n\delta)}{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
For an at-the-money swaption, the strike is therefore chosen as
\[
K = F_0,
\]
so that the fixed rate in the option contract equals the par rate of the underlying forward-starting swap at inception.

For each simulated path \(m=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated forward to the option expiry \(T_e\). The decoder then produces a zero-coupon bond curve at expiry. Since the decoder is expressed in terms of time to maturity, the pathwise curve is written as
\[
\tau \mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau),
\]
where \(m\) indexes the Monte Carlo path and \(P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)\) denotes the price at time \(T_e\), on path \(m\), of a zero-coupon bond maturing \(\tau\) years later.

At expiry, the underlying swap is spot-starting: it starts immediately at \(T_e\) and has payment dates \(T_e+\delta,\dots,T_e+n\delta\). In time-to-maturity notation, these payment dates correspond to maturities
\[
\delta,\;2\delta,\;\dots,\;n\delta
\]
measured from \(T_e\). The pathwise swap annuity at expiry is therefore
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P^{(m)}(T_e,j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding pathwise par swap rate is
\[
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]
Here the numerator uses \(P^{(m)}(T_e,0)=1\), since the floating leg of a spot-starting swap has value \(1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)\) under the single-curve assumption.

Let \(\mathcal N>0\) denote the notional. A payer swaption gives the holder the right to enter a payer swap, meaning that the holder pays the fixed rate \(K\) and receives the floating rate. It is therefore valuable when the market swap rate at expiry exceeds the strike. The payer payoff on path \(m\) is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K,0\bigr).
\]
A receiver swaption gives the holder the right to enter a receiver swap, meaning that the holder receives the fixed rate \(K\) and pays the floating rate. It is therefore valuable when the market swap rate at expiry is below the strike. The receiver payoff is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e),0\bigr).
\]


\subsection{Monte Carlo estimator}
\label{subsec:mc_estimator}

The payoff is discounted back to time \(0\) using the pathwise bank account discount factor computed during simulation. Denoting this factor on path \(m\) by
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\left(-\int_0^{T_e} r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
the discounted present value on path \(m\) is
\[
V_0^{(m)}
=
D^{(m)}_{T_e} X^{(m)}_{T_e},
\]
where \(X^{(m)}_{T_e}\) denotes either the payer or receiver payoff defined above. In the implementation, the integral in the discount factor is approximated on the simulation grid using the trapezoidal rule, consistent with the discount-factor discretisation described in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}.

The time-zero swaption price is then estimated by the Monte Carlo average
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}} V_0^{(m)}.
\]

To assess numerical uncertainty, we also report the sample standard deviation of the discounted pathwise payoffs,
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}-1}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\left(V_0^{(m)}-\widehat V_0\right)^2
},
\]
and the associated Monte Carlo standard error
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\frac{\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}}.
\]

In the numerical examples below, the notional is set to \(\mathcal N=1\). The time-zero quantities \(F_0\) and \(\mathcal A_0\) are computed directly from the decoded initial discount curve, while the pathwise quantities \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) are computed from the decoded discount curve at expiry on each simulated path. Because the pricing routine extracts bond prices directly from the model grids, the option expiry \(T_e\) must lie on the simulation time grid. Moreover, the maturities needed for the initial forward swap rate, \(T_e\) and \(T_e+j\delta\), and the expiry-time maturities \(j\delta\), must lie on the decoder maturity grid.


\subsection{Bachelier swaption formula and implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

To report swaption prices in market-standard volatility units, we use the Bachelier normal-volatility framework. Under the annuity measure \(\mathbb{Q}^{\mathcal A}\) \textcolor{red}{what is that ???} associated with the underlying swap annuity, the forward swap rate is a martingale. In the Bachelier normal model \citep{Bachelier1900}, it evolves as
\begin{equation}
    dF(t) = \sigma_N\, dW^{\mathcal A}(t),
    \label{eq:bachelier_sde}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_N>0\) is the normal volatility \textcolor{red}{what is that????}. The normal model \textcolor{red}{what is the normal model????} is convenient in low- and negative-rate environments because it allows the forward swap rate to take negative values.

Under~\eqref{eq:bachelier_sde}, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with notional \(\mathcal N\), strike \(K\), option expiry \(T_e\), forward swap rate \(F\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{pay}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (F-K)\Phi(d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr],
    \qquad
    d =
    \frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:bachelier_payer}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) and \(\phi\) denote the standard normal distribution function and density, respectively. For a receiver swaption, the corresponding price is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{rec}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (K-F)\Phi(-d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr].
    \label{eq:bachelier_receiver}
\end{equation}

For a market at-the-money swaption, the strike is \(K=F_0\), where \(F_0\) is the market forward swap rate observed at time zero. In that case \(d=0\), and the Bachelier price reduces to
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:bachelier_atm}
\end{equation}
This formula is used to convert market ATM normal-volatility quotes into price targets:
\begin{equation}
    V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A^{(i)}_0
    \frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:pricing_target}
\end{equation}

After Monte Carlo pricing, the model price can also be converted back into an implied normal volatility. If the model distribution is centred at the same forward rate as the market, \(F=F_0=K\), the inversion is explicit:
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
    {\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
    \label{eq:implied_vol_atm}
\end{equation}

\textcolor{red}{maybe we should only introduce this when relevant and commented on later ????}

However, this ATM inversion is valid only when the option is at the money with respect to the forward rate used in the Bachelier formula. If the model-implied forward differs from the market forward, the market-ATM strike \(K=F_0\) is not necessarily at the money under the model.

To separate the model's volatility from any forward-rate bias, we also compute a model-implied forward using payer--receiver parity. Given model payer and receiver prices at the same strike \(K=F_0\),
\begin{equation}
    F_{\mathrm{mod}}
    =
    K
    +
    \frac{
        V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
        -
        V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
    }{
        \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    }.
    \label{eq:model_forward_from_parity}
\end{equation}
The bias-adjusted model normal volatility is then obtained by numerically inverting the full Bachelier payer formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer} with
\[
F = F_{\mathrm{mod}},
\qquad
K=F_0,
\qquad
V^{\mathrm{pay}}=\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}.
\]
This distinction is important in the diagnostics below: the ATM formula~\eqref{eq:implied_vol_atm} measures volatility correctly only when the model forward is close to the market forward, whereas the full inversion separates volatility from forward bias.


\subsection{Bachelier Greeks}
\label{subsec:bachelier_greeks}

The following proposition collects the closed-form sensitivities of the Bachelier payer price~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. These Greeks are useful both for interpretation and as analytic benchmarks for numerical pricing diagnostics.

\begin{proposition}[Bachelier Greeks]
\label{prop:bachelier_greeks}
Let \(V=V^{\mathrm{pay}}\) be given by~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}, and define
\[
d=\frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
With the remaining variables fixed in each partial derivative, the payer swaption Greeks are
\begin{align}
    \Delta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial F}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d),
    \label{eq:delta}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vega}
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma_N}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d),
    \label{eq:vega}
    \\
    \Gamma
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:gamma}
    \\
    \Theta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial T_e}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\sigma_N\,\phi(d)}
    {2\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:theta}
    \\
    \mathrm{DV01}
    &=
    \Delta \times 10^{-4}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d)}{10\,000},
    \label{eq:dv01}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vanna}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F\,\partial\sigma_N}
    =
    -\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
    \label{eq:vanna}
    \\
    \mathrm{Volga}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial\sigma_N^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N}.
    \label{eq:volga}
\end{align}
For the receiver swaption,
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0(\Phi(d)-1)
=
-\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\Phi(-d),
\]
while Vega, Gamma, Theta, Vanna and Volga are the same as for the payer. Put-call parity implies
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{pay}}-\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}=\mathcal N \mathcal A_0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The result follows by direct differentiation of~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. For Delta, differentiating with respect to \(F\) and noting that \(\partial d/\partial F = 1/(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e})\), the terms involving \(\partial d/\partial F\) cancel, leaving
\[
\Delta = \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\Phi(d).
\]
For Vega, using \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N = -d/\sigma_N\), \(\Phi'(d)=\phi(d)\), and \(\phi'(d)=-d\phi(d)\), the terms involving \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N\) again cancel, leaving
\[
\mathrm{Vega}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d).
\]
Gamma follows by differentiating Delta with respect to \(F\), while Theta follows by differentiating the price with respect to \(T_e\), using \(\partial d/\partial T_e=-d/(2T_e)\). Finally, Vanna and Volga follow from differentiating Delta with respect to \(\sigma_N\) and Vega with respect to \(\sigma_N\), respectively:
\[
\mathrm{Vanna}
=
\frac{\partial\Delta}{\partial\sigma_N}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
=
-\frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
\]
and
\[
\mathrm{Volga}
=
\frac{\partial\,\mathrm{Vega}}{\partial\sigma_N}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi'(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
=
\frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}{\sigma_N}.
\]
\end{proof}

At the money, \(K=F\), so \(d=0\). Since \(\Phi(0)=1/2\) and \(\phi(0)=(2\pi)^{-1/2}\), the payer Greeks simplify to
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac12\mathcal N \mathcal A_0,
\qquad
\mathrm{Vega}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{\frac{T_e}{2\pi}},
\qquad
\Gamma^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0}{\sigma_N\sqrt{2\pi T_e}},
\qquad
\Theta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sigma_N}{2\sqrt{2\pi T_e}}.
\label{eq:atm_greeks}
\end{equation}
Vanna and Volga vanish at the money.

Combining~\eqref{eq:gamma} and~\eqref{eq:theta} gives
\begin{equation}
    \Theta = \frac12\sigma_N^2\Gamma.
    \label{eq:theta_gamma}
\end{equation}
This identity holds for the partial derivative with respect to time to expiry, holding \(F\), \(\mathcal A_0\), \(K\), and \(\sigma_N\) fixed. It provides a useful analytic consistency check for the Bachelier formulas, although it is not a full calendar-time theta for a dynamic term-structure model, where the annuity and forward swap rate also evolve.

\section{Why the Stable Model Cannot Price Without Calibration}
\label{sec:why_recon_only_fails}
%=========================================================

The autoencoder model from Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} was trained to minimize reconstruction error on observed swap curves. This objective ensures that the model can reproduce curves close to the training data, but derivative pricing places an additional requirement on the model. For swaption pricing, the initial swap curve is encoded into a latent state, the latent SDE is simulated forward to the option expiry, and the decoder is then applied to the latent states reached by the simulated paths. These simulated latent states need not coincide with the encoded historical states seen during reconstruction training.

This section investigates whether reconstruction-only training is sufficient for this pricing task. We use the Monte Carlo swaption pricing setup as a diagnostic tool and ask where, if anywhere, the reconstruction-trained model breaks down when used for pricing \textcolor{red}{yeah well we dont price in this section so maybe a bit misleadning? }. 

\textcolor{red}{maybe we shouldnt list?}

The analysis proceeds in three steps. First, we test how robust the decoder is to perturbations away from encoded training states. Second, we examine where the simulated latent paths actually move. Third, we decompose the movement into drift and diffusion contributions in order to identify its source. The baseline model is included as a reference point, but the main focus is the stable model used for pricing.

\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]

\item[The pricing pipeline as an off-manifold test.] 
The Monte Carlo swaption pricing framework developed above can be viewed as a test of the trained autoencoder model away from the pure reconstruction setting. Starting from an observed swap curve, the encoder produces an initial latent state \(z_0\). The latent SDE is then simulated forward to the option expiry, producing a terminal latent state \(z_{T_e}^{(m)}\) on each Monte Carlo path. Finally, the decoder maps \(z_{T_e}^{(m)}\) into a zero-coupon bond curve, from which the pathwise swap rate, annuity and payoff are computed.

For reconstruction, the decoder is only required to behave well at encoded historical states of the form \(z=E(S)\), where \(S\) is an observed swap curve. For pricing, however, it must also behave well at simulated latent states reached by the SDE. This is a stronger requirement. If the simulated states lie outside the region where the decoder has learned to produce valid discount curves, the resulting swaption prices can become unreliable even if the reconstruction error on observed curves is small.

We first examine decoder robustness in isolation, before considering the SDE dynamics. For an encoded point \(z_0\), we generate perturbed latent states
\[
z = z_0 + \varepsilon \eta,
\qquad
\eta \sim \mathcal N(0,I_\ell),
\]
and measure the fraction of decoded curves that are fully finite. This gives a simple diagnostic of how far the decoder can be moved away from encoded historical states before numerical failures occur.

Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:decoder_robustness} show that all decoders handle the unperturbed encoded states, corresponding to \(\varepsilon=0\). As the perturbation scale increases, the fraction of finite decoded curves declines. The stable decoders are more robust than the baseline decoders of the same dimension, but robustness still deteriorates for moderate perturbations. This suggests that reconstruction training gives the decoder a limited reliable operating region around the encoded data, rather than a guarantee of valid decoding throughout latent space.

\item[Where the SDE actually goes.] 
The previous diagnostic shows how the decoder behaves under controlled perturbations. The next question is whether the simulated latent SDE reaches perturbation sizes where the decoder becomes unreliable. To test this, we simulate \(1{,}000\) paths to \(T_e=5\)Y and compute the terminal displacement
\[
\|z_{T_e}^{(m)}-z_0\|.
\]
This is the Euclidean distance in latent space between the initial encoded state \(z_0\) and the simulated state at option expiry on path \(m\). It measures how far the pricing simulation moves the latent state away from the starting curve.

    \centering

    \label{fig:latent_displacement_cdf}
\end{figure}

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_displacement_summary}

Figure~\ref{fig:latent_displacement_cdf} plots the cumulative distribution function of the terminal displacement. Thus, for any value on the horizontal axis, the curve shows the fraction of Monte Carlo paths with terminal displacement below that value. The baseline distribution lies far to the right, indicating extremely large latent movements. The stable distribution is much more controlled and concentrated at moderate distances from \(z_0\).

This distinction is important. The baseline model may still return finite numerical outputs for some very large latent inputs, but these outputs are produced by extreme extrapolation and should not be interpreted as reliable discount curves. The stable model avoids this explosive behaviour, which is the intended effect of the stability restriction. However, the stable paths still move far enough away from the encoded starting point that they enter the range where the decoder robustness test above showed frequent failures. Hence the stable model is dynamically well behaved, but the decoder is not necessarily reliable on all latent states visited during pricing.

\item[Drift and diffusion contributions.] 
To understand where the stable model's latent movement comes from, we decompose the simulated motion into drift-only and diffusion-only contributions. This separates the roles of the drift network \(\mathcal K\) and the diffusion network \(\mathcal H\).

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_hk_decomposition}

The baseline model is included in Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition} as a reference. Its large displacement is mainly a consequence of the instability already discussed in the simulation chapter. In particular, the drift can generate explosive movement along unstable eigendirections.

The stable model behaves differently. The stability restriction ensures that the linear drift has only negative eigenvalues, so the drift-only trajectory remains bounded. The full simulated displacement is therefore mainly driven by the diffusion term, with the mean-reverting drift acting as a restoring force. Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition} shows that the drift-only five-year displacement is small, while the full displacement is much larger. Thus, for the stable model, the pricing-relevant movement away from \(z_0\) is primarily diffusion-driven.

\item[An apparent paradox in the stable variant.]
One might expect the stable drift to dominate the simulation because the stable equilibrium
\[
z^* = -M^{-1}N_{\mathcal K}
\]
lies far from a typical encoded EUR curve. In the dim4 stable model, \(\|z^*-z_0\|\) is approximately \(9.5\). At first sight, this suggests that the mean-reverting drift should pull the latent state strongly toward the equilibrium, producing a large drift-driven displacement over five years.

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_eigenvector_alignment}

Table~\ref{tab:eigenvector_alignment} explains why this does not happen. The vector \(z^*-z_0\) is almost entirely aligned with the slowest eigenvector of \(\mathcal K\). The corresponding eigenvalue is very close to zero, giving a mean-reversion timescale of roughly 600 years. Over a five-year pricing horizon, this direction barely moves. Hence the large distance between \(z_0\) and \(z^*\) is structurally present, but dynamically almost inactive at swaption-pricing horizons. The realised drift-only displacement is therefore small, despite the equilibrium being far away.

\end{description}

The diagnostics above identify a mismatch between the region where the decoder was trained and the region visited by the simulated stable SDE. The stability restriction prevents the latent dynamics from exploding, but it does not ensure that the simulated latent states remain inside the decoder's reliable reconstruction region. The stable paths move by a moderate amount in latent space, while the decoder was trained primarily on encoded historical states and small neighbourhoods around them.

This is not a failure of stability itself. The bounded displacement of the stable SDE is precisely what the stability restriction was designed to achieve. The problem is that reconstruction-only training does not prepare the decoder for all off-manifold states reached during pricing. This motivates the calibration step introduced in the next section. Rather than retraining the full model, we calibrate a scalar multiplier on the diffusion output. This controls the size of simulated latent movements while matching the level of model-implied swaption volatilities to market quotes.

\section{Diffusion-Scale Calibration}
\label{sec:hscale_calibration}
%=========================================================

So now we want to actually try to price...


In the implementation, the notional \(\mathcal N\) is set to \(1\) for simplicity. The time-zero quantities \(F_0\) and \(\mathcal A_0\) are computed directly from the decoded initial discount curve, while the pathwise quantities \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) are computed from the decoded discount curve at expiry on each simulated path. Because the pricing routine extracts bond prices directly from the model grids, the option expiry \(T_e\) must lie on the simulation time grid. Moreover, the maturities needed for the initial forward swap rate, \(T_e\) and \(T_e+j\delta\), and the expiry-time maturities \(j\delta\), must lie on the decoder maturity grid. In the numerical examples below, we focus on at-the-money payer swaptions, for which the strike is set equal to the time-zero forward swap rate.


The Bachelier formula serves two distinct roles in the implementation. First, it converts market normal volatility quotes into price targets for calibration. Second, it converts model prices back into implied normal volatilities for reporting and comparison.

For the calibration step, market swaption data are quoted as at-the-money normal volatilities \(\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\), typically in basis points. Before applying the formula, these volatilities are converted to decimal rate units. The market volatility is then converted into a price using the at-the-money formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_atm}:
\begin{equation}
    V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A^{(i)}_0
    \frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:pricing_target}
\end{equation}
This gives a fixed price target computed once per market observation, using the annuity \(\mathcal A_0^{(i)}\) associated with the corresponding quote date, expiry and underlying swap tenor. The model Monte Carlo price \(\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}^{(i)}\), computed as in Section~\ref{subsec:mc_estimator}, is then trained to match this target.

After pricing, the model price is converted back into a normal implied volatility for comparison with market quotes. In the at-the-money case, the inversion is explicit:
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}
    =
    \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
    {\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
    \label{eq:implied_vol}
\end{equation}
For off-at-the-money strikes, the Bachelier formula can instead be inverted numerically. Reporting model outputs in basis-point normal-volatility units makes the pricing errors directly comparable to market quoting conventions.

\subsection{Calibration procedure}
\label{subsec:hscale_procedure}

For each swaption quote \(j\), let \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}\) denote the market normal
volatility and let \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\) denote the model-implied normal volatility
obtained from Monte Carlo pricing at diffusion scale \(s=1\). Since the at-the-money
Bachelier price is linear in the normal volatility, and since scaling the diffusion approximately
scales the spread of the terminal swap-rate distribution, we use the working approximation
\[
    \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(s)
    \approx
    s\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1).
\]
This approximation is not exact, because the decoder, the annuity and the swaption payoff
are nonlinear functions of the latent state. It nevertheless provides a simple and interpretable
first-order calibration rule.

The global scale is chosen by minimising squared errors in normal-volatility space:
\begin{equation}
    s^*
    =
    \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{s>0}
    \sum_{j:\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}>0}
    \bigl(
        s\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)
        -
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}
    \bigr)^2.
    \label{eq:hscale_objective}
\end{equation}
This is an origin-constrained ordinary least-squares problem with closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
    s^*
    =
    \frac{
        \sum_j
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\,
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}
    }{
        \sum_j
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)^2
    }.
    \label{eq:hscale_ols}
\end{equation}
The calibrated scale is applied through a \texttt{diffusion\_scale} argument in the simulator.
No weights in \(\mathcal H\) are modified when producing the calibrated pricing results.

\subsection{Per-cell calibration and out-of-sample validation}
\label{subsec:per_cell_calibration}

The global scale \(s^*=0.151\) corrects the average level of model-implied volatilities, but
the residual errors in Table~\ref{tab:hscale_per_cell} suggest that the required correction
differs across the expiry--tenor grid. As a more flexible alternative, we estimate one scale
factor per cell:
\begin{equation}
    s^*(e,t)
    =
    \frac{
        \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}}
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},d}(1;\,e,t)\,
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},d}(e,t)
    }{
        \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}}
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},d}(1;\,e,t)^2
    },
    \label{eq:per_cell_ols}
\end{equation}
where \(e\) denotes expiry, \(t\) denotes tenor, and \(\mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}\) is the
set of training dates. This is the same origin-constrained OLS formula as
\eqref{eq:hscale_ols}, applied independently to each expiry--tenor cell.

\paragraph{Train/test split.}
All available EUR quote dates are sorted chronologically. The first \(70\%\), covering
October 2010 to June 2019, form the training window. The remaining \(30\%\), covering
July 2019 to February 2024, are held out for out-of-sample testing. The test period includes
both the COVID-19 volatility episode and the 2022--23 rate-hiking cycle. The scale factors
\(s^*(e,t)\) are fitted exclusively on the training dates.

Table~\ref{tab:per_cell_scales} reports the nine fitted scale factors. They lie in the interval
\([0.098,0.183]\), close to the global estimate \(s^*=0.151\), but with noticeable variation
across cells. The smallest estimate is obtained for the 1Y\(\times\)10Y cell, while the largest
is obtained for the 10Y\(\times\)10Y cell. This confirms that some of the residual error under
the global scale is cell-specific.

\subsection{Expiry-level calibration and structural diagnosis}
\label{subsec:calibration_improvements}

The per-cell calibration improves flexibility, but it also introduces nine separate parameters.
Because the scale \(s\) multiplies the diffusion during the simulation over the option horizon,
it is more naturally associated with expiry than with the tenor of the underlying swap. This
motivates an intermediate specification with one scale per expiry row, pooling all tenors for
a given expiry.

The fitted expiry-level scales are
\[
  s^*(1\mathrm{Y}) = 0.129, \qquad
  s^*(5\mathrm{Y}) = 0.133, \qquad
  s^*(10\mathrm{Y}) = 0.141.
\]
These values are close to each other and to the global estimate, suggesting that most of the
calibration effect is a common reduction in diffusion scale. The expiry-level model achieves
an out-of-sample MAE of \(72\) bp, slightly better than the nine-parameter per-cell model
and substantially more parsimonious. Table~\ref{tab:expiry_comparison} gives the
cell-level comparison. This makes the expiry-level calibration the preferred practical
specification among the scale-based methods considered here.

\input{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_stable_hscale/expiry_scale/tab_expiry_comparison}

\paragraph{Why the residual error is structural.}
The expiry-level and per-cell calibrations both converge to an out-of-sample MAE around
\(72\)--\(73\) bp. Even a daily recalibration, which uses each out-of-sample date's own market
volatility as the calibration target and is therefore not implementable in practice, does not
materially improve the error. This suggests that the remaining pricing error is not primarily
caused by the choice of scale factor. Instead, it points to a structural limitation of the
reconstruction-trained dynamics.

The issue is that the drift network \(\mathcal K\) was identified through the reconstruction
objective, not through derivative prices. The model uses the risk-neutral latent dynamics
\[
  dz_t = \mathcal K(z_t)\,dt + \mathcal H(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
\]
and \(\mathcal K\) enters the no-arbitrage ODE coefficients that shape the decoded
bond-price curve. During reconstruction training, \(\mathcal K\) is selected to help fit the
cross-section of swap rates at each date. It is not directly calibrated to enforce the
annuity-measure martingale condition for the forward swap rate,
\[
\mathbb E^{\mathbb Q^{\mathcal A}}[S_{T_e}] = F_0.
\]
As a result, the simulated distribution of \(S_{T_e}\) need not be centred on the initial
forward swap rate. A scalar diffusion scale can adjust the amount of randomness, but it
cannot remove a drift-induced forward bias.

\paragraph{The drift floor as a diagnostic.}
We can measure this effect by pricing swaptions at a near-zero diffusion scale,
\(s=0.01\). This largely removes the stochastic contribution and leaves the deterministic
movement induced by \(\mathcal K\). Any non-zero implied volatility in this limit is referred
to as a drift floor. It indicates that the drift alone moves the swap rate away from the
at-the-money strike and thereby creates option value.

The 1Y\(\times\)10Y cell illustrates a different limitation. It achieves a low in-sample error

indicates that the fitted scale is not merely a noisy statistical artefact within the training
period; rather, the out-of-sample period represents a different volatility regime. Ridge
regularisation reduces within-sample variance, but it does not solve temporal regime
change.

\paragraph{A direct test of forward-rate bias.}
A further diagnostic is obtained by comparing payer and receiver pricing. If the model
distribution of \(S_{T_e}\) were centred correctly under the annuity measure, payer and
receiver at-the-money prices would be consistent through the Bachelier framework. If,
instead, the model has a directional drift bias, then the receiver and payer prices will be
affected asymmetrically.

To test this, we compute a straddle-implied volatility based on the average of the payer and
receiver prices,
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{str}}
  =
  \frac{V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}}}{2}
  \cdot
  \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
Under a correctly centred at-the-money distribution, this should be consistent with the
payer-implied volatility. In the experiment, however, the out-of-sample MAE rises sharply
when the straddle measure is used. The reason is that the model's forward bias is
systematically negative across the grid, as shown in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_diagnostics}.
The simulated swap rate distribution tends to lie below \(F_0\), inflating receiver payoffs.
Averaging payer and receiver prices therefore does not cancel the bias; instead, it
introduces the inflated receiver component into the reported volatility.

\section{Forward Bias and Effective Exercise Probabilities}
\label{sec:pricing_diagnostics}
%=========================================================

The Bachelier Greeks of Section~\ref{sec:bachelier_greeks} provide a useful lens for reading the model's pricing behaviour. At the money, the Bachelier Delta is
\[
  \Delta^{\mathrm{ATM}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathcal{N}\,\mathcal{A}_0,
\]
exactly, for any volatility level: the distribution of $S_T$ is symmetric around $F_0$, so the exercise probability is precisely $50\%$. This is the defining property of a model in which $F_0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}}[S_T]$ --- i.e.\ one that satisfies the annuity-measure martingale condition.

A model with $\mathcal{K}$ identified from cross-sectional reconstruction rather than from the martingale condition will in general have $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}}[S_T] \neq F_0$. This forward bias is directly measurable from put-call parity. Pricing both the payer and receiver at the expiry-level calibrated scales gives
\begin{equation}
  \text{forward bias} = \frac{V_{\mathrm{pay}} - V_{\mathrm{rec}}}{\mathcal{A}_0}
  = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}}[S_T] - F_0,
  \label{eq:forward_bias}
\end{equation}
and the effective ATM exercise probability follows by standardising relative to the model's own distribution width:
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{\text{forward bias}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{str}}\sqrt{T_e}},
  \qquad
  p_{\mathrm{eff}} = \Phi(d_{\mathrm{eff}}),
  \label{eq:p_eff}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{str}} = (V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}})/2 \cdot \sqrt{2\pi}/(\mathcal{A}_0\sqrt{T_e})$ is the straddle vol, which measures distribution width without directional contamination. Under a correctly specified model: $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$ and $p_{\mathrm{eff}} = 50\%$.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:delta_diagnostic} reports the per-cell forward bias and effective exercise probability at the expiry-level calibrated scales. Two findings stand out.

First, the forward bias is \emph{consistently negative} across all nine cells, ranging from $-18$ bp for the 10Y$\times$1Y cell to $-694$ bp for the 1Y$\times$5Y cell. The model's $\mathbb{Q}$ distribution of $S_T$ is systematically below $F_0$ at every expiry--tenor combination. This reflects $\mathcal{K}$ being identified on 2010--2019 EUR data, a period of persistently falling rates: the fitted drift encodes a downward-rate prior that is entirely appropriate for in-sample reconstruction but inconsistent with the martingale condition. Applied out-of-sample to the 2022--23 rate-hiking cycle, this prior is wrong in direction as well as magnitude.

Second, the effective exercise probabilities range from $24\%$ (1Y$\times$5Y) to $49\%$ (10Y$\times$1Y), with an overall average of $35\%$ versus the Bachelier benchmark of $50\%$. The 10Y$\times$1Y cell is closest to the benchmark ($p_{\mathrm{eff}} = 48.8\%$): over a ten-year horizon the drift's cumulative effect is still small relative to the diffusion, so $\mathcal{K}$'s mis-calibration matters less. The 1Y expiry cells are the most affected, since the one-year drift contribution is large relative to diffusion at the calibrated scale $s^* \approx 0.13$.

These results also explain why the straddle experiment of Section~\ref{subsec:calibration_improvements} fails: the receiver payoff is inflated at every cell, so the straddle average $(V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}})/2$ is dominated by the inflated receiver and produces a vol far above market. No symmetric averaging can improve the payer fit when the bias is uniformly negative.
