%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter examined whether the trained encoder--decoder can be used as a stable scenario generator. We now ask whether it can also be used for swaption pricing. This is a stronger requirement than curve reconstruction, since pricing depends not only on the current decoded curve, but also on the risk-neutral distribution of future swap rates, discount factors and annuities.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on the single-curve swap rates introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_e\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_e\) to \(T_e+n\delta\), with fixed-leg payment dates
\[
T_e+\delta,\;T_e+2\delta,\;\ldots,\;T_e+n\delta.
\]
In the implementation, we use annual accrual, so \(\delta=1\).

For an at-the-money swaption, the strike is the time-zero forward swap rate of the underlying forward-starting swap. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\delta\sum_{j=1}^{n}P(0,T_e+j\delta),
\qquad
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n\delta)}{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
The strike is therefore set to
\[
K=F_0.
\]

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_e\). The decoder then produces the time-\(T_e\) zero-coupon bond curve
\[
\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau).
\]
Since the underlying swap is spot-starting at expiry, the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\delta\sum_{j=1}^{n}P^{(m)}(T_e,j\delta),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]
The numerator uses \(P^{(m)}(T_e,0)=1\), as in the single-curve swap-pricing relation of Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}.

For notional \(\mathcal N\), the payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K\bigr)^+,
\]
and
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e)\bigr)^+.
\]

The payoff is discounted by the pathwise money-market discount factor
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\left(-\int_0^{T_e}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
where the integral is approximated on the simulation grid using the trapezoidal rule, as in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}. The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is therefore
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_e}X^{(m)}_{T_e}.
\]
We also report the Monte Carlo standard error
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\frac{\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}},
\]
where \(\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}\) is the sample standard deviation of the discounted pathwise payoffs.

\subsection{Bachelier implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

Market swaption quotes are reported as normal, or Bachelier \citep{Bachelier1900}, implied volatilities. Under the Bachelier model, the forward swap rate follows
\[
dF(t)=\sigma_N\,dW^{\mathcal A}(t)
\]
under the annuity measure \citep[Ch.~26]{Bjork2020}, where \(\sigma_N\) is the normal volatility. The normal model is convenient in low- and negative-rate environments because it allows rates to become negative \citep{Antonov2015}.

For an at-the-money swaption, \(K=F_0\), and the Bachelier payer and receiver prices coincide:
\[
V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\]
This formula is used to convert market ATM normal-volatility quotes into price targets,
\[
V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A^{(i)}_0
\frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}},
\]
and to convert Monte Carlo prices back into model-implied normal volatilities.

To avoid mixing volatility level with directional bias, the model-implied volatility is computed from the average of payer and receiver prices,
\[
\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}
=
\frac{1}{2}
\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\]
so that
\[
\widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
All normal volatilities are reported in basis points by multiplying decimal rate units by \(10^4\).

Finally, we use the payer--receiver difference as a forward-centering diagnostic. At the ATM forward strike, a correctly centred model should satisfy
\[
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
\approx
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}.
\]
We therefore define
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0}.
\]
A value close to zero indicates that the simulated forward swap-rate distribution is approximately centred at the ATM strike.

\section{Can the Base Model Price Swaptions?}
\label{sec:pricing_challenges}
%=========================================================

The base model was trained solely to reconstruct observed swap curves from a low-dimensional latent state. Having established in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation} that the latent dynamics are stable and well-behaved as a scenario generator, we now ask a harder question: can these same dynamics be used directly for swaption pricing?

This is not guaranteed. Swaption pricing requires the \emph{risk-neutral distribution} of future swap rates, which is a fundamentally different object from the historical distribution of curves. We first examine the theoretical reasons why a gap may arise, then investigate empirically what actually happens.

\subsection{Why problems may arise}
\label{subsec:recon_pricing_gap}

The encoder--decoder is trained to minimise the reconstruction loss
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}
= \mathbb{E}\!\left[\bigl\|S - \hat S(z)\bigr\|^2\right],
\]
which measures how accurately the decoder recovers today's observed swap curve from the latent state \(z\). This training objective contains no information about the option-implied risk-neutral distribution of future swap rates.

The latent SDE under the training measure \(\mathbb{P}\) is
\[
dz_t = K^{\mathbb{P}}(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{P}},
\]
where \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) is the drift estimated from historical curve dynamics and \(L(z_t)\) is the diffusion matrix. Both were calibrated to fit the time-series behaviour of the curve, not the cross-sectional behaviour of option prices.

Two distinct problems may arise. First, the \emph{vol level}: \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) and \(L\) together determine the distribution of \(F_{T_e}\) under the physical measure, but market swaption prices reflect the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\), which can differ substantially via the market price of risk. Second, the \emph{forward centering}: the physical drift \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) does not guarantee that the simulated distribution is centred at the ATM forward strike \(F_0\), which is required for the parity condition \(\hat b \approx 0\) to hold.

We investigate both concerns empirically below.

\subsection{Empirical investigation of the base model}
\label{subsec:base_model_evidence}

To assess the severity of these concerns, we apply the base model directly to swaption pricing without any adjustment, using the physical dynamics \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) and unit diffusion scaling \(\sigma_{\mathrm{vec}} = \mathbf{1}\). The per-cell errors are shown in Table~\ref{tab:base_per_cell_all} and in the left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:pricing_heatmap_comparison_all}.

The overall mean absolute error is \textbf{414.7 bp}, far exceeding typical swaption bid--offer spreads of 1--5 bp. The errors have a clear structure: short-expiry cells (1Y option) are the most severely mispriced, with the 1Yx5Y cell showing an MAE of over 1000 bp. Longer-expiry cells are somewhat better but still far from acceptable.

All cells show a large positive bias in the vol error, meaning the base model systematically overestimates swaption volatility. This is consistent with the physical measure having a larger diffusion than the risk-neutral measure: the drift \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) does not mean-revert towards \(F_0\), so simulated paths drift away from the forward, inflating both payer and receiver values.

\subsection{Regime dependence}
\label{subsec:regime_dependence}

Figure~\ref{fig:pricing_comparison_timeseries} (grey lines) shows the base-model vol error over the full sample period 2010--2024. The errors are not uniformly distributed across time; three distinct regimes are visible:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{2010--2015 (post-GFC low-rate environment):} errors are large and positive. The base model overestimates vol substantially, driven by the physical measure's larger diffusion scale relative to the suppressed risk-neutral volatility of the near-zero-rate period.
    \item \textbf{2016--2021 (ZIRP / negative rates):} errors are heterogeneous across cells. The latent state is compressed near the boundary of the training distribution, and the decoder Jacobian is steep in this region.
    \item \textbf{2022--2024 (rate normalisation):} errors are again large, now driven by the rapid repricing of the curve to a high-rate regime. The physical drift does not anticipate the speed of this transition.
\end{enumerate}

These findings confirm that a pricing adjustment is needed: one that can account for the gap between the physical and risk-neutral measures and adapt the vol level to match market quotes across different rate regimes.

\section{Constant Market Price of Risk Adjustment}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{subsec:cmpr_architecture}

We introduce a trainable pricing layer on top of the frozen base model. The layer modifies the latent SDE drift to produce a risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\) under which swaption prices match market quotes.

Specifically, we replace the physical drift \(K^{\mathbb{P}}(z)\) with an adjusted drift
\[
\boxed{
K^{\mathbb{Q}}(z) = K^{\mathbb{P}}(z) + L(z)\,\lambda_0,
}
\]
where \(\lambda_0 \in \mathbb{R}^d\) is a learnable constant vector. The full adjusted SDE is therefore
\[
dz_t = K^{\mathbb{Q}}(z_t)\,dt + \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_{\mathrm{vec}})\,L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
with per-factor diffusion scales \(\sigma_{\mathrm{vec}} \in \mathbb{R}^d_{>0}\) also learnable.

The correction \(L(z)\lambda_0\) is a constant vector rotated by the diffusion geometry \(L(z)\). Importantly, \(\lambda_0\) does \emph{not} depend on \(z\). This means the correction applies the same increment to the drift everywhere in the latent space, and cannot amplify deviations of \(z_t\) from its starting point.

\textbf{Contrast with a state-dependent correction.} A natural extension would be to use \(L(z)\Lambda z\) for a trainable matrix \(\Lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\). This correction grows linearly with \(\|z\|\): if \(z_t\) drifts from \(z_0\) due to diffusion noise, the correction term grows, which pushes \(z_t\) further still. In our setting, this creates an explosive feedback loop through the decoder's steep Jacobian, producing model-implied volatilities far in excess of market levels for short-expiry options. Section~\ref{sec:pricing_diagnostics} documents this instability in detail. The constant correction \(\lambda_0\) avoids this problem entirely.

\subsection{Trainable parameters}
\label{subsec:cmpr_params}

The pricing layer has \(2d = 8\) trainable parameters for \(d = 4\):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \(\lambda_0 \in \mathbb{R}^4\): constant drift bias in latent space. Initialised to zero.
    \item \(\log \sigma_{\mathrm{vec}} \in \mathbb{R}^4\): log of per-factor diffusion scales. Initialised to \(-1.8\), giving \(\sigma_{\mathrm{vec}} \approx 0.165 \cdot \mathbf{1}\).
\end{itemize}

The base model parameters (encoder, decoder, \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\), \(H\)) are fully frozen throughout calibration.

\subsection{Calibration objective}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

Calibration minimises a combination of two losses. Let \((T_e^{(i)}, n^{(i)}, \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)})\) denote the expiry, tenor, and market ATM normal vol for observation \(i\).

The \emph{straddle vol loss} matches model-implied and market volatilities:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}
= \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\hat\sigma_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled swaption observations.

The \emph{forward-centering penalty} enforces parity at the ATM strike:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}
=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(\frac{\hat b^{(i)}}{100}\right)^2.
\]

The total loss is
\[
\mathcal{L}
= \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}
+ 0.5\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}
+ 10^{-3}\,\|\lambda_0\|^2,
\]
with a light \(\ell_2\) penalty on \(\lambda_0\) to prevent unnecessary drift magnification.

Optimisation uses Adam with a linear warmup over 50 epochs followed by cosine annealing to \(10^{-7}\), over 1000 epochs. Each epoch samples 4 mini-batches of 8 swaptions drawn uniformly from the full EUR training set. Monte Carlo pricing uses \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 512\) antithetic paths per observation.

\section{Pricing Results}
\label{sec:pricing_results}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Overall accuracy}
\label{subsec:pricing_overall}

Table~\ref{tab:constant_mpr_per_cell_all} reports per-cell pricing errors for the Constant MPR model across all 1246 EUR observations. The overall MAE falls from 414.7 bp (base model) to \textbf{41.4 bp}, a reduction of over 90 per cent using only 8 trainable parameters.

The test-set MAE is 55.5 bp and the train-set MAE is 36.3 bp. The gap is modest and reflects the different rate regimes in each split: the training set (2010--2021) includes the ZIRP period which is structurally different from the test set (2021--2024). The forward bias is essentially eliminated: the overall mean forward bias is $+10.1$ bp, compared to $-84.6$ bp for the base model.

\subsection{Per-cell structure}
\label{subsec:pricing_per_cell}

Figure~\ref{fig:pricing_heatmap_comparison_all} shows MAE heatmaps for both models. The improvement is uniform across the surface, with larger absolute reductions in the cells that were most severely mispriced under the base model.

The remaining errors have a clear structure. Short-expiry cells (1Y option) carry higher MAE than long-expiry cells: the 1Yx10Y cell has a test MAE of 109 bp, while all 5Y and 10Y expiry cells are below 70 bp. This reflects a fundamental limitation of the constant correction: \(\lambda_0\) is a single vector applied identically to all dates and all cells, so it cannot simultaneously centre short- and long-expiry distributions that live at very different regions of the latent space.

\section{Path Diagnostics and the Stability of the Pricing Layer}
\label{sec:pricing_diagnostics}
%=========================================================

To understand the properties of the pricing layer, it is informative to examine the simulated path distributions directly. We decompose the total error into a \emph{drift displacement} component (how far the zero-noise trajectory moves from \(F_0\)) and a \emph{diffusion component} (how much variance is added by the noise term).

\subsection{State-dependent drift: the Lambda v2 instability}
\label{subsec:lambda_instability}

As a point of comparison, we also trained a more flexible pricing layer using a state-dependent drift correction:
\[
K^{\mathbb{Q}}(z) = K^{\mathbb{P}}(z) + L(z)\,\Lambda z,
\]
where \(\Lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\) is a trainable matrix. This model has 20 trainable parameters and is a strict generalisation of the Constant MPR. It converged to an overall MAE of 91.5 bp after 1000 training epochs.

Path diagnostics reveal the reason for the inferior performance. For the 1Yx1Y cell on 2022-03-31 (a representative high-error date), the zero-noise simulation produces a deterministic forward-rate displacement of $+267$ bp from \(F_0 = 88.5$ bp, while the market implied displacement (standard deviation of \(F_{T_e}\)) is only 106 bp. The full simulation, including diffusion noise, produces \(\mathrm{std}(F_{T_e}) = 711\) bp---nearly 7 times the market level.

The mechanism is as follows. The correction \(L(z)\Lambda z\) grows linearly with \(\|z\|\). Over the 12 Euler steps of the simulation, a small deviation of \(z_t\) from \(z_0\) (arising from a single noise draw) causes the correction term to grow, which pushes \(z_t\) further from \(z_0\), which further increases the correction. This \emph{position-dependent feedback loop} is explosive for short expiries where there is insufficient time for the base drift \(K^{\mathbb{P}}\) to mean-revert. The steep Jacobian of the decoder then maps the amplified latent deviation into enormous forward-rate swings.

\subsection{Why the constant correction is stable}
\label{subsec:constant_stable}

With \(\lambda_0\) constant, the drift correction is \(L(z)\lambda_0\). This term varies with \(z\) through \(L(z)\), but since \(\lambda_0\) does not depend on \(z\), the correction cannot amplify deviations of \(z_t\) from \(z_0\). Mathematically, the feedback loop is severed: a perturbation \(\delta z\) contributes \(L(z)\lambda_0\,\delta t\) to the next step regardless of \(\|\delta z\|\), so there is no compounding amplification.

In practice, the path-finite fraction remains at 100\% throughout training and evaluation, and no extreme paths are produced. The model-implied distribution is concentrated near the correct forward rate, with the vol error driven by the residual mismatch between the constant correction and the regime-specific vol level.

\section{The Model-Implied Volatility Smile}
\label{sec:pricing_smile}
%=========================================================

So far we have only evaluated the model at the at-the-money (ATM) strike. A richer characterisation of the model is given by the \emph{implied volatility smile}: how the Bachelier implied vol varies across strikes \(K = F_0 + \delta\) for \(\delta \in [-200, +200]\) bp.

The smile is not a direct calibration target — the model is trained only to match ATM vols — but it is determined implicitly by the shape of the simulated distribution of \(F_{T_e}\). Under the Bachelier model, \(F_{T_e}\) would be exactly normally distributed and the smile would be flat. Any skew or curvature in the model smile therefore reveals the degree to which the nonlinear latent dynamics deviate from a pure normal model.

\subsection{Computing the smile}
\label{subsec:smile_computation}

For each (date, expiry, tenor) triple, we reuse the same Monte Carlo paths generated for the ATM pricing. For each strike offset \(\delta\) we compute the payer swaption price
\[
V_{\mathrm{pay}}(K) = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}\sum_{m} D^{(m)}_{T_e}\,\mathcal{A}^{(m)}(T_e)\,\bigl(F^{(m)}_{T_e} - K\bigr)^+,
\]
and normalise by the effective annuity \(\bar{\mathcal{A}}_0 = N_{\mathrm{MC}}^{-1}\sum_m D^{(m)}_{T_e}\mathcal{A}^{(m)}(T_e)\). The normalised price is then inverted to a Bachelier implied vol \(\hat\sigma_N(K)\) by solving
\[
(F_0 - K)\,\Phi(d) + \hat\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
=
\frac{V_{\mathrm{pay}}(K)}{\bar{\mathcal{A}}_0},
\qquad
d = \frac{F_0 - K}{\hat\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
\]
numerically via Brent's method \citep{Glasserman2004}.

\subsection{Smile results}
\label{subsec:smile_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:pricing_smile} shows the smile for three rate regimes and three swaption cells. Figure~\ref{fig:pricing_smile_dist} shows the empirical distribution of \(F_{T_e}\) for the same selections, overlaid with the Bachelier normal distribution calibrated to the ATM vol.

Several features are noteworthy. First, the smile is not flat: the model produces modest skew and curvature even though it was trained only on ATM prices. This arises from the nonlinearity of the decoder, which maps the approximately Gaussian latent distribution into a non-Gaussian distribution of swap rates. Second, the skew is regime-dependent: in the low-rate period (2015) the smile is relatively flat, whereas in the rate-hike period (2022) short-expiry cells show a pronounced negative skew, reflecting the asymmetric response of the decoder to large upward moves in the latent state. Third, long-expiry cells (10Yx10Y) are closer to Gaussian at all dates, consistent with the central-limit-type averaging of the path integral over a longer horizon.

These results suggest that, while the model was not designed to match the swaption smile, it produces economically reasonable skew as a by-product of the nonlinear latent dynamics. A dedicated calibration to off-ATM strikes would be a natural extension.
