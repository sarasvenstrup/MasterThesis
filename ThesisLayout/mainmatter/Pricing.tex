\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, produces more controlled latent dynamics that are suitable for simulation. This chapter applies those dynamics to Monte Carlo pricing of swaptions, for which the relevant pricing theory is presented first.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on a standard interest rate swap introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_{\mu}\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_{\mu}\) to \(T_{\nu}\), with annual fixed-leg payment dates \(T_{\mu+1}, T_{\mu+2}, \ldots, T_{\nu}\).

For an at-the-money (ATM) swaption the strike equals the time-zero forward swap rate. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P(0,T_{k}),
\qquad
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)
=
\frac{P(0,T_{\mu})-P(0,T_{\nu})}{\mathcal A_0},
\]
so the ATM strike is \(K = R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\).

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_{\mu}\) with monthly steps.\footnote{Concretely, twelve steps for the 1Y expiry, sixty for 5Y, and one hundred and twenty for 10Y. During calibration training the step is loosened to \(dt=1/6\) for tractability.} The decoder then produces the time-\(T_{\mu}\) zero-coupon bond curve \(\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},\tau)\), from which the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},T_k-T_\mu),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_{\mu}, T_{\nu}-T_\mu)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})}.
\]
The payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})-K\bigr)^+,
\qquad
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigr)^+.
\]
Each payoff is discounted by the pathwise bank account
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^{T_{\mu}}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
approximated on the simulation grid by the trapezoidal rule (Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}). The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is
\[
\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}X^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}.
\]

The typical market convention is to quote swaptions in terms of normal implied volatility.\footnote{See \citet{Skovmand2025tsm7}, p.~60, lecture notes, week 7.} Under the Bachelier model, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with expiry \(T_{\mu}\), strike \(K\), forward swap rate \(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\[
V_0
=
\mathcal A_0
\left[
\bigl(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K\bigr)\Phi(d)
+
\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}\,\phi(d)
\right],
\qquad
d=
\frac{R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}},
\]
where \(\Phi(\cdot)\) and \(\phi(\cdot)\) denote the standard normal distribution and density functions, respectively. In the at-the-money case, \(K=R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), so \(d=0\), and the price reduces to
\begin{equation}
V_0^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\label{eq:atm_bachelier_price}
\end{equation}
A Monte Carlo estimate of the at-the-money swaption price can therefore be converted into a normal implied volatility by inverting this expression,
\[
\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\]
To reduce the effect of directional centering errors, we price both a payer and a receiver swaption at the ATM strike and average the two time-zero prices:
\begin{equation}
\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0
=
\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\qquad
\widehat\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\label{eq:straddle_normal_vol}
\end{equation}
This gives a \emph{straddle normal implied volatility}. Since payer and receiver prices move in opposite directions when the simulated forward swap rate deviates from the ATM strike, averaging removes the first-order directional effect of such centering errors.

We also track the \emph{forward centering bias}
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0},
\]
which should be close to zero at the ATM strike for a correctly centred model.

\section{A Pricing Layer on Top of the Stable Model}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

To test whether the latent dynamics trained on swap-rate data can also support derivative pricing, we add a small \emph{pricing layer} on top of the stable model. The learned weights and networks are kept fixed. Let \(\mu(z)\) and \(\sigma(z)\) denote the drift and diffusion of the frozen stable model. The pricing layer introduces two trainable parameter vectors: a constant drift shift \(\eta \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and a positive per-factor diffusion scale \(s \in \mathbb{R}^d_{>0}\). The adjusted dynamics are
\[
dz_t
=
\bigl(\mu(z_t) + \sigma(z_t)\eta\bigr)\,dt
+
\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t.
\]
The parameter \(\eta\) shifts the centre of the simulated forward distribution, while \(s\) adjusts its width. With \(2d\) parameters in total, namely \((\eta,\log s)\), the layer is calibrated to EUR ATM swaption straddle volatilities on the 2010--2021 training window and evaluated out of sample on 2022--2024.

This is an \emph{exploratory experiment, not a fully consistent joint model}. The bond-pricing ODE is re-solved under the adjusted drift and diffusion, so the simulation and the terminal bond decode at expiry \(T_\mu\) are internally consistent. However, the initial bond curve at \(t=0\) is read from the \emph{stable} decoder so that today's swap curve matches the market by construction. There is therefore a seam between the initial curve and the subsequent pricing dynamics. We return to this in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_limitation} as the main limitation of the experiment.

\subsection{Calibration}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

The pricing layer is calibrated by minimising a loss with three terms: a volatility error, a forward-centering penalty, and a small regularisation term on the drift shift:
\[
\mathcal{L}
=
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_{N}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}}
+
0.5
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat b^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}}
+
10^{-3}\|\eta\|^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled EUR swaption observations. Here \(\widehat{\sigma}_{N}^{(i)}\) and \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}\) are measured in basis points, and \(\widehat b^{(i)}\) is also expressed in basis points. The first term fits the level of the implied normal volatility. The second term penalises violations of the ATM centering condition
\[
\mathbb{E}^{\mathcal A}
\!\left[
S(T_\mu)
\right]
=
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0),
\]
so that the simulated forward swap rate remains centred around the initial ATM strike. The final term keeps the drift shift \(\eta\) from becoming too large.

Optimisation is done with Adam. The learning rate is first increased linearly during a short warm-up period and is then decreased using cosine annealing. We train for \(1000\) epochs using \(N_{\mathrm{MC}}=512\) antithetic paths per observation.\footnote{Antithetic variates are a standard Monte Carlo variance-reduction technique \citep{Glasserman2004}. For each Brownian increment vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), we also simulate a mirrored path with increment \(-\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\). The two payoffs are then averaged, which reduces Monte Carlo noise at little extra cost.}

The overall MAE across the surface is \(\mathbf{40.1}\) bp. The error is \(39.4\) bp on the training window and \(\mathbf{42.0}\) bp out of sample. The small train--test gap suggests that the eight-parameter pricing layer does not materially overfit the training data. The errors, however, are not evenly spread across the surface. \subsection{The residual 1Y expiry error and forward bias}
\label{subsec:cmpr_1y_residual}

The cross-sectional error pattern is informative. The 1Y expiry row has much larger errors than the 5Y and 10Y rows. The 1Y\(\times\)1Y, 1Y\(\times\)5Y and 1Y\(\times\)10Y cells have MAEs of \(148\), \(68\) and \(54\) bp, respectively. By contrast, all six cells at 5Y and 10Y expiry lie between \(18\) and \(24\) bp.

The main explanation is the forward bias \(\widehat b\). Across the full sample, the mean bias is \(+70\) bp at 1Y expiry, \(+68\) bp at 5Y expiry and \(+139\) bp at 10Y expiry. The largest mean bias is therefore at the longest expiry, even though this is the row with the smallest volatility error. This is because the same absolute bias matters more at short expiries. At short horizons, the diffusion has less time to spread the forward distribution, so a fixed shift in its centre is large relative to its total standard deviation.

A positive bias means that the simulated forward swap rate is shifted upward relative to the initial ATM strike. The payer swaption is then too valuable relative to the receiver swaption. The straddle-vol construction in equation~\eqref{eq:straddle_normal_vol} averages payer and receiver prices. This removes the first-order effect of the shift, but it does not remove the remaining nonlinear effect. When the bias is large relative to the total Bachelier standard deviation \(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_\mu}\), the straddle price is still inflated. This is most visible in the 1Y row, where biases of order \(50\)--\(140\) bp are large relative to market normal volatilities of \(48\)--\(90\) bp. At 10Y expiry, the absolute bias is similar or larger, but the diffusion has ten years to spread the distribution, so the same shift matters proportionally less.

This interpretation is also supported by the data. The cross-sectional correlation between \(|\widehat b|\) and the absolute volatility error is \(0.84\) at 1Y expiry, compared with \(0.49\) at 5Y expiry and \(0.60\) at 10Y expiry. The relationship is therefore strongest at 1Y expiry, indicating that the residual volatility error in this row is mainly driven by forward-centering error rather than by a pure volatility-level mismatch.

Figure~\ref{fig:constant_mpr_forward_bias} shows the forward bias over time, decomposed by expiry. The bias is present through much of the sample and is not only a feature of the 2022 tightening period. This suggests that it has a structural component, although its magnitude still varies over time.

Structurally, the residual bias comes from using a single global drift shift \(\eta\) to fit a heterogeneous swaption surface. The calibration loss in Section~\ref{subsec:cmpr_calibration} balances the straddle-volatility error against the forward-centering penalty. The optimiser therefore accepts some remaining bias in order to fit the average volatility level across the surface. This remaining bias is felt most strongly at the 1Y expiry because the total diffusion over one year is small. The 1Y residual is therefore a limitation of using a single-vector drift correction. Richer parameterisations, such as expiry-dependent or state-dependent drift adjustments, could reduce this error, but would also weaken the stability and parsimony of the present experiment.

\section{Internal Consistency of the Pricing Dynamics}
\label{sec:pricing_limitation}
%=========================================================

The pricing experiment makes one deliberate compromise, already noted in Section~\ref{sec:constant_mpr}. The pricing setup uses the stable model at the initial date, but the adjusted model during the pricing simulation. At \(t=0\), the initial swap curve is read from the \emph{stable} decoder, so that the model matches the observed market curve by construction. At expiry \(T_\mu\), however, bond prices are decoded using the adjusted dynamics
\[
\bigl(\mu+\sigma\eta,\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma\bigr).
\]
These two decoders correspond to two different sets of bond-pricing ODE coefficients. Each is internally consistent on its own, but the pricing setup switches from one to the other at \(t=0\). Hence, there is no single adjusted model that both matches the initial curve and generates the terminal bond prices used in the swaption payoff.

In a fully consistent version, the adjusted decoder would also be used at \(t=0\). This would require an additional anchoring step: for each initial market curve, one would solve
\[
z_0^\star
=
\arg\min_z
\left\|
S^*(z)-S_0^{\mathrm{mkt}}
\right\|^2,
\]
where \(S^*(z)\) denotes the swap curve produced by the adjusted decoder. The pricing simulation would then start from \(z_0^\star\), so that the adjusted model reproduces today's swap curve before simulating forward. In that case, the model ATM forward \(F_0^*\) would match the market ATM forward by construction, and the forward-centering diagnostic should be close to zero up to numerical and Monte Carlo error.

The persistent positive forward bias found in Section~\ref{subsec:cmpr_1y_residual} is consistent with this missing anchoring step. Since the initial curve is taken from the stable decoder but the terminal curve is produced by the adjusted decoder, the model can start from a curve that is not exactly the initial curve of the adjusted pricing dynamics. This mismatch can shift the simulated forward distribution and therefore show up as a non-zero value of
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
The observed bias of roughly \(60\)--\(140\) bp is therefore consistent with the remaining internal inconsistency of the pricing layer. It may also reflect the restricted form of the single-vector drift correction.

We do not implement the fully anchored version in this thesis for two reasons. First, it would make calibration much more expensive. Each pricing step would require an inner optimisation for \(z_0^\star\) for every date in the mini-batch. Second, the current experiment is intended as a small pricing layer on top of a frozen yield-curve model, not as a full joint calibration of curves and swaptions. The present setup is therefore useful as an exploratory test: it shows that a very small number of pricing parameters can fit most of the swaption surface, while also revealing the main limitation of the approach.

A fully consistent extension is a natural direction for future work. It would use the adjusted decoder both at \(t=0\) and at expiry, together with an inverse-decoder anchor for the initial state. Adding a soft reconstruction loss during training could also help ensure that the adjusted decoder remains close enough to the stable decoder for the inner anchoring problem to be solved quickly.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:pricing_summary}
%=========================================================

The pricing-layer experiment gives three main conclusions.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A small pricing layer with only \(8\) trainable parameters can fit the EUR ATM swaption surface reasonably well. The layer consists of a constant drift shift \(\eta\) and a per-factor diffusion scale \(s\), while the stable model itself is kept fixed. It reaches an overall MAE of \(40.1\) bp and an out-of-sample MAE of \(42.0\) bp. The small train--test gap suggests that the result is not mainly driven by overfitting.

    \item The main residual error is in the 1Y expiry row. This is driven by a persistent positive forward bias. At short expiries, the total diffusion scale \(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_\mu}\) is small, so a fixed shift in the forward distribution has a large effect on the implied volatility. This explains why the same type of bias is much more damaging at 1Y than at 5Y or 10Y. The strong correlation between \(|\widehat b|\) and absolute volatility error at 1Y supports this interpretation.

    \item The main limitation is internal consistency. The pricing setup uses the stable decoder for the initial curve and the adjusted decoder for the terminal payoff. A fully consistent version would instead use the adjusted decoder throughout and solve an inverse-decoder anchoring problem for the initial state \(z_0\). This extension is left for future work.
\end{enumerate}
