%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter established that the encoder--decoder model produces stable latent dynamics suitable for scenario generation. We now ask a harder question: can the same model be used to price swaptions? This requires the \emph{risk-neutral} distribution of future swap rates, which is a fundamentally different object from the historical cross-section the model was trained on.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on the single-curve swap rates introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_e\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_e\) to \(T_e+n\), with annual fixed-leg payment dates \(T_e+1, T_e+2, \ldots, T_e+n\).

For an at-the-money (ATM) swaption the strike equals the time-zero forward swap rate. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}P(0,T_e+j),
\qquad
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n)}{\mathcal A_0},
\]
so the ATM strike is \(K = F_0\).

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_e\) using a fine time grid with step \(dt \ll 1\). The decoder then produces the time-\(T_e\) zero-coupon bond curve \(\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)\), from which the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}P^{(m)}(T_e,j),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]
The payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K\bigr)^+,
\qquad
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e)\bigr)^+,
\]
where we set notional \(\mathcal N = 1\). Each payoff is discounted by the pathwise bank account
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^{T_e}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
approximated on the simulation grid by the trapezoidal rule (Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}). The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_e}X^{(m)}_{T_e}.
\]

\subsection{Bachelier implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

Market swaption quotes are reported as normal \citep{Bachelier1900} implied volatilities \(\sigma_N\). Under the Bachelier model the forward swap rate satisfies \(dF(t)=\sigma_N\,dW^{\mathcal A}(t)\) under the annuity measure \citep[Ch.~26]{Bjork2020}, giving the ATM price
\[
V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\]
The normal convention is standard in low- and negative-rate environments because it permits negative rates \citep{Antonov2015}. To convert a Monte Carlo price into an implied vol we invert this relation.

To decouple the vol level from any directional bias, we price both a payer and a receiver at the ATM strike and average:
\[
\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}
=
\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\qquad
\widehat{\sigma}_{N}
=
\frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
This \emph{straddle vol} eliminates the effect of the distribution being off-centre: if the model shifts the forward rate up or down, both payer and receiver prices move in opposite directions and the average is unchanged at first order. All vols are reported in basis points.

We also track the \emph{forward centering bias}
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0},
\]
which should be close to zero at the ATM strike for a correctly centred model.

\section{Pricing with the Stable Model}
\label{sec:pricing_stable_model}
%=========================================================

The stable model from Chapter~\ref{chap:model} is written entirely under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\),
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
and the no-arbitrage condition is enforced by construction through the bond-pricing ODE. In principle this is all that is needed to price options: simulate latent paths under the stable dynamics, decode to bond curves at expiry, and compute the Monte Carlo estimator of Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}. There is no measure change to perform. The natural first experiment is therefore to ask whether the stable model, used directly, prices swaptions accurately.

\subsection{Empirical results}
\label{subsec:stable_pricing_results}

We price all available EUR ATM swaptions using the stable model directly, without any adjustment to its dynamics. The model parameters --- the drift \(\mu\), diffusion \(\sigma\), short rate \(r\) and decoder \(G\) --- are held fixed at the values obtained from the reconstruction calibration in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}. We refer to this configuration as the \emph{frozen stable model}: only the stochastic Brownian increments vary across Monte Carlo paths, while all neural-network parameters remain constant. The simulation uses \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 512\) antithetic\footnote{Antithetic variates is a standard Monte Carlo variance-reduction technique \citep{Glasserman2004}. For each randomly drawn path with Brownian increment vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), a mirrored path with \(-\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) is also simulated. The two paths are then averaged. This eliminates the variance contribution from the linear part of the payoff and approximately halves the Monte Carlo standard error at unchanged computational cost.} paths per cell and the time grid described in Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}.

The overall MAE is \textbf{414.7 bp},\footnote{MAE denotes the mean absolute error between model-implied and market normal implied volatilities, reported in basis points.} far exceeding market bid--offer spreads, which are typically only a few basis points. Short-expiry cells perform worst, with the 1Yx5Y cell exceeding 1000 bp. All cells show a large positive vol error, indicating that the frozen stable model systematically overestimates implied volatility. The forward bias is mixed in sign, but negative on average and particularly pronounced for the 1Y expiry cells, showing that the simulated forward distributions are not consistently centred at \(F_0\). A latent-state model that reconstructs curves accurately is therefore not, by itself, a swaption-pricing model.

\subsection{How could that be?}
\label{subsec:stable_pricing_issue}

The size of this error is striking. It is worth pausing to understand why an arbitrage-free model that fits swap curves to within tens of basis points can produce option-pricing errors two orders of magnitude larger. The reason is a calibration mismatch. The model parameters were chosen to minimise the empirical curve-reconstruction risk
\[
\mathcal L(\Omega)
=
\frac{1}{N|\mathcal M|}
\sum_{n=1}^N
\sum_{\tau\in\mathcal M}
\left(
S_n(\tau)-\widetilde S_n(\tau;\Omega)
\right)^2,
\]
as defined in Section~\ref{sec:model_estimation_training_choices}. This objective measures how accurately the model reconstructs observed swap curves. It contains no information about the risk-neutral distribution of future swap rates and no information from option markets. Hence, a drift and diffusion that produce accurate curve reconstructions are not guaranteed to reproduce the option-implied distribution of \(F_{T_e}\).

Two distinct problems arise, both visible in the table above:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Wrong volatility level.} The diffusion \(\sigma(z)\) and drift \(\mu(z)\) together determine the spread of the distribution of \(F_{T_e}\) under \(\mathbb{Q}\). Since neither was calibrated against option prices, the resulting implied vol can differ substantially from market-observed levels. The large positive vol errors in the table are a direct expression of this mismatch.
    \item \textbf{Wrong forward centering.} The drift \(\mu(z)\) is calibrated to reconstruct the current curve, not to centre the simulated forward swap rate at \(F_0\). The forward bias \(\widehat b\) is therefore mixed in sign across cells, but large in magnitude, indicating that the simulated forward distributions are not consistently centred at \(F_0\).
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the chapter introduces a minimal trainable pricing layer that recalibrates the risk-neutral dynamics while keeping the stable model frozen, and shows that this single adjustment closes the bulk of the gap.

%=========================================================
\section{Constant Market Price of Risk}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{subsec:cmpr_architecture}

We address the two failures above by adding a minimal trainable pricing layer on top of the frozen stable model. The aim is not to relearn the curve representation, but only to recalibrate the risk-neutral dynamics that determine the swaption-implied distribution.

Specifically, we replace the reconstruction-calibrated drift by
\[
\mu^*(z) = \mu(z) + \sigma(z)\lambda_0,
\]
where \(\lambda_0 \in \mathbb{R}^d\) is a learnable constant vector and \(\mu^*(z)\) denotes the option-calibrated drift. The adjusted risk-neutral dynamics are
\[
dz_t
=
\mu^*(z_t)\,dt
+
\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
\]
where \(s\in\mathbb R^d_{>0}\) are learnable per-factor diffusion scales. All base model parameters are frozen, so the pricing layer has only \(2d=8\) trainable parameters for \(d=4\).

The two trainable components target the two errors identified above. The scale vector \(s\) primarily adjusts the width of the simulated distribution of \(F_{T_e}\), and therefore targets the volatility-level error. The drift correction \(\lambda_0\) primarily adjusts the centre of the simulated distribution, and therefore targets the forward-centering error.

The correction \(\lambda_0\) is analogous to a constant market-price-of-risk adjustment, but here it should be understood as a direct recalibration of the risk-neutral drift rather than as a change of measure. Multiplication by \(\sigma(z)\) maps the constant vector into the model's diffusion directions, so the drift is adjusted along the latent directions that generate uncertainty.

The key restriction is that \(\lambda_0\) is constant. A state-dependent correction could make the drift adjustment grow as simulated paths move away from the initial latent region, amplifying errors through the decoder and producing unstable short-expiry implied volatilities. A constant correction is therefore deliberately conservative: it can shift the centre of the simulated forward distribution, but it does not introduce additional state-dependent feedback.

\subsection{Calibration}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

Calibration minimises a straddle vol loss plus a forward-centering penalty:
\[
\mathcal{L}
= \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\hat\sigma_{N}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}}
+\;
0.5\underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(\frac{\hat b^{(i)}}{100}\right)^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}}
+\;
10^{-3}\,\|\lambda_0\|^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled EUR swaption observations. Optimisation uses Adam with linear warmup and cosine annealing \footnote{Linear warmup gradually increases the learning rate at the start of
training before the cosine annealing schedule begins to decrease it.} over \(1000\) epochs, with \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 512\) antithetic paths per observation.

\subsection{Results}
\label{subsec:cmpr_results}

Table~\ref{tab:constant_mpr_per_cell_merged} reports per-cell errors, with the mean absolute error broken out for the full sample, the training period (2010--2021) and the test period (2022--2024). The overall MAE falls from \(414.7\) bp (base model) to \(\mathbf{40.1}\) bp --- a reduction of over \(90\%\) using only \(8\) trainable parameters. The training- and test-period totals are \(39.4\) bp and \(42.0\) bp respectively.

The very small train--test gap at the total level is the headline observation. Looking inside the table, however, the per-cell pattern is more nuanced. The 1Yx1Y and 1Yx5Y cells, which dominate the all-dates MAE, are large primarily in the \emph{training} period (\(188\) and \(79\) bp respectively) and substantially smaller in the test period (\(25\) and \(22\) bp). The training-period 1Y blow-up reflects the negative-rate era from 2014--2019, where the model struggles to reproduce inverted and floor-pressed short-end curves. Conversely, the 5Y and 10Y rows are systematically worse in the test period than in the training period, reflecting the 2022 tightening cycle which stresses long-tenor cells. The two effects largely offset in the totals, and the model can be said to mis-fit different cells in different regimes with a roughly constant overall error budget rather than to overfit in the standard sense.

\subsection{The residual 1Y expiry error and forward bias}
\label{subsec:cmpr_1y_residual}

The cross-sectional pattern of the residual errors is informative. The 1Y expiry row carries substantially higher MAE than the 5Y and 10Y rows. Concretely, the 1Y\(\times\)1Y, 1Y\(\times\)5Y and 1Y\(\times\)10Y cells have MAEs of \(148\), \(68\) and \(54\) bp, while all six cells at 5Y and 10Y expiry lie between \(18\) and \(24\) bp.

The driver is the forward bias \(\widehat b\). Across the full sample, the mean bias is \(+70\) bp at 1Y expiry, \(+68\) bp at 5Y expiry and \(+139\) bp at 10Y expiry. The largest mean bias is therefore at the longest expiry, yet that is the row with the smallest MAE. A positive bias means the simulated forward distribution is shifted upward relative to \(F_0\), so the payer leg is systematically more valuable than the receiver leg. The straddle vol formula in Section~\ref{subsec:bachelier_formula} averages payer and receiver prices, which removes the bias to first order but not to second order: a non-zero bias inflates the resulting straddle vol estimate. Because the natural Bachelier vol level scales with \(\sqrt{T_e}\), the same absolute bias translates into a much larger inflation of the model's implied vol at short expiries than at long expiries. The 1Y row sees a bias of order \(50\)--\(140\) bp against market vol levels of \(48\)--\(90\) bp, and the bias dominates the implied-vol MAE. At 10Y expiry, the bias is similar in absolute magnitude but the diffusion has had ten times longer to spread, so the same absolute bias matters proportionally less.

This interpretation is confirmed empirically. The cross-sectional correlation between \(|\widehat b|\) and the absolute vol error is \(0.84\) at 1Y expiry, \(0.49\) at 5Y expiry and \(0.60\) at 10Y expiry. At 1Y the relationship is close to one-to-one, indicating that the residual vol error at this expiry is almost entirely explained by the forward bias rather than by a vol-level mismatch.

Figure~\ref{fig:constant_mpr_forward_bias} shows the forward bias over time, decomposed by expiry. The bias is persistent across the entire sample and is not a regime-specific artefact of the 2022 tightening cycle. Its sign and approximate magnitude are stable from 2010 onwards, which is what one would expect from a structural rather than a regime-driven origin.

    \caption{Forward bias \(\widehat b\) (bp) over time for the Constant MPR. Coloured by expiry (blue = 1Y, green = 5Y, red = 10Y). The bias is persistent across the entire sample and is not concentrated in any particular regime.}
    \label{fig:constant_mpr_forward_bias}

Structurally, the residual bias is the consequence of using a single global \(\lambda_0\) to fit a heterogeneous surface. The calibration loss in Section~\ref{subsec:cmpr_calibration} balances the squared straddle-vol error against the squared forward-bias penalty with weight \(0.5\). The optimiser tolerates some residual bias in order to fit the average vol level across the surface, and that residual bias is felt most strongly at the 1Y expiry by the \(\sqrt{T_e}\) mechanism above. This 1Y residual is the principal limitation of the Constant MPR architecture.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:pricing_summary}
%=========================================================

The pricing experiments establish three facts about the latent-state model when applied to swaption pricing:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The frozen stable model, used directly, gives implied-vol errors of order \(415\) bp --- two orders of magnitude above bid--offer spreads. A latent model that reconstructs swap curves accurately is not by itself a pricing model.
    \item A minimal trainable pricing layer with only \(8\) parameters, the Constant MPR (\(\lambda_0\) drift correction and a per-factor diffusion scale), reduces this to \(40\) bp overall and \(42\) bp out-of-sample. The very small train--test gap shows that the model does not overfit, and the cleanest demonstration that the latent space supports a Q-recalibration.
    \item The residual error concentrates in the 1Y expiry row, where a positive forward bias of order \(100\) bp inflates the implied-vol estimate disproportionately relative to longer expiries. This is the structural limit of a single global drift correction.
\end{enumerate}
