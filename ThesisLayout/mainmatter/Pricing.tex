\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, produces more controlled latent dynamics that are suitable for simulation. This chapter applies those dynamics to Monte Carlo pricing of swaptions, for which the relevant pricing theory is presented first.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on a standard interest rate swap introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_{\mu}\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_{\mu}\) to \(T_{\nu}\), with annual fixed-leg payment dates \(T_{\mu+1}, T_{\mu+2}, \ldots, T_{\nu}\).

For an at-the-money (ATM) swaption the strike equals the time-zero forward swap rate. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P(0,T_{k}),
\qquad
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)
=
\frac{P(0,T_{\mu})-P(0,T_{\nu})}{\mathcal A_0},
\]
so the ATM strike is \(K = R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\).

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_{\mu}\) with monthly steps.\footnote{Concretely, twelve steps for the 1Y expiry, sixty for 5Y, and one hundred and twenty for 10Y. During calibration training the step is loosened to \(dt=1/6\) for tractability.} The decoder then produces the time-\(T_{\mu}\) zero-coupon bond curve \(\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},\tau)\), from which the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},T_k-T_\mu),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_{\mu}, T_{\nu}-T_\mu)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})}.
\]
The payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})-K\bigr)^+,
\qquad
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigr)^+.
\]
Each payoff is discounted by the pathwise bank account
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^{T_{\mu}}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
approximated on the simulation grid by the trapezoidal rule (Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}). The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is
\[
\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}X^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}.
\]

The typical market convention is to quote swaptions in terms of normal implied volatility.\footnote{See \citet{Skovmand2025tsm7}, p.~60, lecture notes, week 7.} Under the Bachelier model, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with expiry \(T_{\mu}\), strike \(K\), forward swap rate \(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\[
V_0
=
\mathcal A_0
\left[
\bigl(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K\bigr)\Phi(d)
+
\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}\,\phi(d)
\right],
\qquad
d=
\frac{R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}},
\]
where \(\Phi(\cdot)\) and \(\phi(\cdot)\) denote the standard normal distribution and density functions, respectively. In the at-the-money case, \(K=R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), so \(d=0\), and the price reduces to
\begin{equation}
V_0^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\label{eq:atm_bachelier_price}
\end{equation}
A Monte Carlo estimate of the at-the-money swaption price can therefore be converted into a normal implied volatility by inverting this expression,
\[
\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\]
To reduce the effect of directional centering errors, we price both a payer and a receiver swaption at the ATM strike and average the two time-zero prices:
\begin{equation}
\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0
=
\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\qquad
\widehat\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\label{eq:straddle_normal_vol}
\end{equation}
This gives a \emph{straddle normal implied volatility}. Since payer and receiver prices move in opposite directions when the simulated forward swap rate deviates from the ATM strike, averaging removes the first-order directional effect of such centering errors.

We also track the \emph{forward centering bias}
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0},
\]
which should be close to zero at the ATM strike for a correctly centred model.

%=========================================================
\section{A Pricing Layer on Top of the Stable Model}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

To test whether the latent dynamics trained on swap-rate data can also support derivative pricing, we add a small \emph{pricing layer} on top of the stable model. The learned weights and networks are kept fixed. Let \(\mu(z)\) and \(\sigma(z)\) denote the drift and diffusion of the frozen stable model. The pricing layer introduces two trainable parameter vectors: a constant drift shift \(\eta \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and a positive per-factor diffusion scale \(s \in \mathbb{R}^d_{>0}\). The adjusted dynamics are
\[
dz_t
=
\bigl(\mu(z_t) + \sigma(z_t)\eta\bigr)\,dt
+
\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t.
\]
The parameter \(\eta\) shifts the centre of the simulated forward distribution, while \(s\) adjusts its width. In the calibration we optimise \(\log s\) rather than \(s\) directly, which ensures that the diffusion scales remain positive. With \(2d\) trainable parameters in total, namely \((\eta,\log s)\), the layer is calibrated to EUR ATM swaption straddle volatilities on the 2010--2021 training window and evaluated out of sample on 2022--2024.

This is an \emph{exploratory experiment, not a fully consistent joint model}. The bond-pricing ODE is re-solved under the adjusted drift and diffusion, so the simulation and the terminal bond decode at expiry \(T_\mu\) are internally consistent. However, the initial bond curve at \(t=0\) is read from the \emph{stable} decoder so that today's swap curve matches the market by construction. There is therefore a mismatch between the initial curve and the subsequent pricing dynamics. We return to this in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_limitation} as one limitation of the experiment.

\subsection{Calibration}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

The pricing layer is calibrated by minimising a loss with three terms: a volatility error, a forward-centering penalty, and a small regularisation term on the drift shift:
\[
\mathcal{L}
=
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_{N}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}}
+
0.5
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat b^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}}
+
10^{-3}\|\eta\|^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled EUR swaption observations. All volatilities and centering biases in the loss are measured in basis points, and the division by \(100\) normalises errors to units of \(100\) basis points. The first term fits the straddle-implied normal volatility, while the second penalises deviations from ATM centering. In a correctly centred model, the simulated forward swap rate should remain centred around the initial ATM strike,
\[
\mathbb{E}^{\mathcal A}
\!\left[
S(T_\mu)
\right]
\approx
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0).
\]
The final term keeps the drift shift \(\eta\) from becoming too large.

Optimisation is done with Adam. The learning rate is first increased linearly during a short warm-up period and is then decreased using cosine annealing. We train for \(1000\) epochs using \(N_{\mathrm{MC}}=512\) antithetic paths per observation.\footnote{Antithetic variates are a standard Monte Carlo variance-reduction technique \citep{Glasserman2004}. For each Brownian increment vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), we also simulate a mirrored path with increment \(-\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\). The two payoffs are then averaged, which reduces Monte Carlo noise at little extra cost.}

\subsection{Results}
\label{subsec:cmpr_results}

Table~\ref{tab:pricing_combined} summarises the results across all nine swaption cells for all dates, comparing the unadjusted base model, the calibrated pricing layer, and the market. The forward-centering bias is included as a diagnostic column alongside the mean absolute errors across all dates, the training window, and the test window.

\input{Figures/Pricing/comparison/tab_pricing_combined}

The results tell a two-part story. The first part is encouraging: the pricing layer is a dramatic improvement over the unadjusted base model. Without any calibration the base model produces straddle vols of 691 at the 1Y\(\times\)1Y cell against a market level of 47, and the overall MAE is 413. After adding the eight-parameter layer the overall MAE falls to \(\mathbf{49.4}\), a reduction of nearly ninety per cent. The layer therefore succeeds in its primary purpose of bringing the simulated volatility level into the right ballpark.

The second part is more sobering. The errors are not uniformly small. The 1Y expiry cells retain MAEs of 157, 93 and 47, while the 10Y cells show only 17--23. At first glance this looks like an expiry-dependent success. However, the forward bias column reveals that the apparently good long-expiry fit is partly misleading. The 10Y cells carry a forward bias of approximately \(+160\), meaning the simulated forward swap rate is shifted roughly 160 basis points above the initial ATM strike. A positive bias inflates the payer swaption price and deflates the receiver price. Although averaging payer and receiver prices removes the first-order directional effect, a large shift away from the strike can still increase the straddle value. The long-expiry straddle is therefore partly supported by centering error, not only by the model volatility. The small 10Y MAE is thus partly a spurious cancellation between under-volatility and over-centering, not a genuine match of the terminal distribution.

The training and test periods confirm this picture. On the training window the overall MAE is 43.7; on the 2022--2024 test period it rises to 62.3. The 19-point train-to-test increase is non-trivial for an eight-parameter model, and it is concentrated in the 5Y and 10Y cells, where the forward bias is largest. This suggests the optimiser found a local compromise that happens to work during low-volatility regimes but degrades when the vol surface moves sharply, as it did around 2022.

Figure~\ref{fig:constant_mpr_timeseries} shows the vol error over time for all nine cells. The errors are relatively stable until 2022, after which several cells deteriorate sharply. The 1Y expiry errors are persistently large throughout, while the 5Y and 10Y errors are smaller in the training period but widen in the test period.

\subsection{The term-structure trade-off and the masking effect}
\label{subsec:cmpr_term_structure}

The pattern in Table~\ref{tab:pricing_combined} has a structural explanation rooted in the shape of the model's volatility term structure.

\paragraph{The base model produces the wrong slope.}
Figure~\ref{fig:term_structure_vol} plots the model-implied forward-rate volatility and the market normal volatility against option expiry, for each of the three swap tenors. The base model (before any pricing adjustment) generates vol that is strongly \emph{decreasing} with expiry: roughly 690 at 1Y, falling to 285 at 5Y and 225 at 10Y. The market normal vol surface is nearly \emph{flat}: approximately 40--70 across all expiries. The two curves have opposite shapes.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_constant_mpr/term_structure_diag/term_structure_vol_by_tenor.png}
    \caption{Term structure of ATM normal volatility: base model (decreasing, left axis) versus market (flat, dashed). Each panel shows one swap tenor. The base model vol is roughly 10--16 times the market at 1Y expiry and 3--4 times at 10Y expiry, and its annualised vol falls strongly with expiry due to variance saturation in the mean-reverting latent SDE.}
    \label{fig:term_structure_vol}
\end{figure}

The source of the decreasing pattern is variance saturation. For a mean-reverting latent process the variance of the state tends to level off as the horizon grows, because mean reversion pulls paths back towards the centre. Since the reported normal volatility is annualised,
\[
\sigma_N(T_\mu)
\approx
\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{Var}(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(T_\mu))}{T_\mu}},
\]
a variance that saturates produces an annualised volatility that decreases with expiry. This is a property of the frozen stable model, not of the pricing layer: the latent dynamics were trained to match the historical swap-rate curve, not the risk-neutral term structure of swaption variance. The resulting vol shape is therefore structurally incompatible with the flat market surface.

\paragraph{A single scale \(s\) cannot fix the slope.}
The pricing layer introduces a per-factor diffusion scale \(s\) that multiplies the entire diffusion matrix. This changes the overall volatility level but leaves the ratio between expiries unchanged: if the base model produces twice as much vol at 1Y than at 5Y, the calibrated model does too. Figure~\ref{fig:scaling_sensitivity} makes this concrete by showing what happens to the model vol at each expiry as \(s\) varies.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_constant_mpr/term_structure_diag/scaling_sensitivity.png}
    \caption{Model volatility at each expiry as a function of the global diffusion scale \(s\), for tenor 1Y. The dashed horizontal lines are the market targets. No single value of \(s\) can simultaneously hit the 1Y target (requiring \(s \ll 1\)) and the 10Y target (requiring \(s < 1\) but much closer to 1). The term-structure mismatch is therefore locked in regardless of \(s\).}
    \label{fig:scaling_sensitivity}
\end{figure}

After calibration the optimiser settles on a scale that reduces the large 1Y over-volatility at the cost of introducing under-volatility at 5Y and 10Y. Table~\ref{tab:term_structure_trained} confirms this: the calibrated model produces 143 at 1Y (market 41--65, ratio 2--3.4$\times$), but only 49 at 5Y (market 68--69, ratio 0.72$\times$) and 52 at 10Y (market 67--69, ratio 0.76$\times$). The slope is inverted relative to the market: the model is over-vol at short expiries and under-vol at long expiries.

\input{Figures/Pricing/comparison/tab_term_structure_trained}

\paragraph{The masking effect at long expiries.}
Given that the model under-produces vol at 5Y and 10Y expiries (ratio 0.67--0.76), why do the MAEs in Table~\ref{tab:pricing_combined} appear so small for those cells (17--35)? The answer lies in the forward bias. At 10Y expiry the mean forward bias is approximately \(+160\), meaning the simulated forward swap rate sits 160 basis points above the initial ATM strike. A payer swaption struck at the initial ATM rate is therefore in the money on average, which raises the straddle price above what the vol level alone would predict. Although averaging payer and receiver prices removes the first-order directional effect, a large centering shift can still increase the straddle value. In other words, positive centering error compensates for negative vol error, and the two partially cancel in the straddle price. The resulting MAE is misleadingly small.

Figure~\ref{fig:constant_mpr_forward_bias} shows the forward bias over time for all nine cells. The bias is persistent across the full sample and not concentrated in any particular market regime, confirming that it is structural rather than incidental. The 10Y cells (red) carry a large positive bias throughout, while the 1Y cells (blue) show a smaller and more variable bias. This is consistent with the masking mechanism: the 10Y under-vol problem is largely hidden by the centering error, whereas the 1Y over-vol problem is not, because the forward distribution has had only one year to spread and a centering shift is small relative to the width.

    \centering

    \caption{Forward bias \(\widehat{b}\) in basis points for the calibrated pricing layer (all nine cells grouped by expiry). The 10Y cells (red lines) carry a persistent positive bias of around \(+160\) bp throughout the sample. This bias inflates straddle prices at long expiries, masking the model's under-volatility and producing a misleadingly small MAE.}
    \label{fig:constant_mpr_forward_bias}

The bias is partly related to the internal inconsistency of the pricing setup: the initial swap curve is decoded by the \emph{stable} model while the terminal curve is decoded by the adjusted model. However, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_limitation}, anchoring the initial state does not remove the problem. The bias therefore also reflects a deeper mismatch in the latent dynamics.

\paragraph{Variance saturation in the latent SDE.}
The source of the decreasing pattern is variance saturation. For a mean-reverting latent process the variance of the state tends to level off as the horizon grows, because mean reversion pulls paths back towards the centre. Since the reported normal volatility is annualised,
\[
\sigma_N(T_\mu)
\approx
\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{Var}(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(T_\mu))}{T_\mu}},
\]
a variance that saturates produces an annualised volatility that decreases with expiry. This is a property of the frozen stable model, not of the pricing layer: the latent dynamics were trained to match the historical swap-rate curve, not the risk-neutral term structure of swaption variance. The resulting vol shape is therefore structurally incompatible with the flat market surface.

Figure~\ref{fig:FT_distribution} shows the same effect directly in simulated forward-rate space. The distribution of \(F_{T_\mu}-F_0\) does not spread out proportionally to \(\sqrt{T_\mu}\). The annualised volatility therefore falls with expiry.

\section{Internal Consistency of the Pricing Dynamics}
\label{sec:pricing_limitation}
%=========================================================

The pricing experiment makes one deliberate compromise, already noted in Section~\ref{sec:constant_mpr}. At the initial date, the swap curve is read from the \emph{stable} decoder. During the pricing simulation, the model uses the adjusted dynamics \(\bigl(\mu+\sigma\eta,\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma\bigr)\), with the bond-pricing ODE re-solved under those adjusted parameters. The terminal bond prices are therefore decoded using the pricing-layer model. The initial and terminal curves are thus generated by two different sets of parameters, and the initial \(z_0\) is encoded using the stable model rather than the pricing-layer model.

In a fully consistent version, the adjusted decoder would also be used at \(t=0\). This requires an anchoring step: for each observed initial curve, one finds an initial latent state \(z_0^\star\) such that the adjusted decoder reproduces the observed swap curve,
\[
z_0^\star
=
\arg\min_z
\left\|
S^*(z)-S_0
\right\|^2,
\]
and the simulation then starts from \(z_0^\star\). In that case the forward distribution is centred consistently with the adjusted model, and the bias \(\widehat{b}\) should be small apart from numerical and Monte Carlo error.

We implemented this anchored variant. It did not remove the forward bias. The reason is that the stable model was trained to fit market swap rates at their natural scale, while the pricing layer calibrates \(s \ll 1\) to compensate for the base model's massive vol. As a result, the adjusted decoder maps the same observed swap curve to a latent \(z_0^\star\) that is very different from the stable encoder's \(z_0\). Starting from \(z_0^\star\) changes the initial point of the simulation, but the latent SDE dynamics --- and therefore the shape of the vol term structure --- remain fixed. The fundamental problem, a mean-reverting SDE with variance saturation, persists regardless of where in latent space the simulation begins.

The forward bias is therefore not simply an anchoring artefact. It partly reflects the structural mismatch between the stable model's mean-reverting dynamics and the risk-neutral vol term structure. When starting from the re-anchored \(z_0^\star\), the model still drifts toward a different long-run level than the market implies, and the centering error reappears.

The current setup is therefore best viewed as an exploratory experiment that probes how much a small parametric overlay can achieve on top of frozen yield-curve dynamics. A genuinely consistent joint model would require the pricing dynamics to be built into the training objective from the start.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:pricing_summary}
%=========================================================

The pricing-layer experiment shows that a small parametric overlay can fix the \emph{level} of the model's swaption volatility, but not its \emph{slope}. Starting from a base model that produces straddle vols of 691 at 1Y\(\times\)1Y (market: 47) and an overall MAE of 413, the eight-parameter layer --- a global drift shift \(\eta\) and per-factor diffusion scale \(s\) --- reduces the all-date overall MAE to \(\mathbf{49.4}\). On the 2010--2021 training window the MAE is 43.7; on the 2022--2024 out-of-sample period it rises to 62.3.

However, the fit is not uniformly genuine. The 1Y expiry cells retain large MAEs (47--157) because the model over-produces volatility at short expiries by a factor of 1.6--3.4 relative to the market, and no single global scale can eliminate this without simultaneously creating under-volatility at longer expiries. After calibration the model under-produces vol at 5Y and 10Y expiries by about 25--30 per cent. The mean-reverting latent SDE generates an annualised vol that decreases with expiry due to variance saturation, whereas the market normal vol surface is nearly flat. This shape mismatch is structural and cannot be resolved by the pricing layer.

The apparently small MAEs at 10Y expiry (17--23) should be interpreted with caution. The 10Y cells carry a persistent forward bias of approximately \(+160\), meaning the simulated forward swap rate is systematically above the initial ATM strike. The resulting inflation of the straddle price partly offsets the under-volatility, producing a misleadingly small error. When the bias is accounted for, the long-expiry fit is no better than the numbers suggest.

The forward bias is partly an internal consistency issue: the initial swap curve is decoded by the stable model while the terminal curve uses the adjusted model. An anchored version, which re-encodes the initial curve under the adjusted decoder, was tested but did not resolve the bias, because the mismatch is rooted in the dynamics rather than the initial condition.

Overall, the experiment demonstrates that a latent yield-curve model contains enough structure to support an approximate swaption pricing layer, but that a genuinely accurate calibration would require either a less mean-reverting latent dynamics, expiry-dependent diffusion scales, or a model trained jointly on swaption volatilities from the outset.
