%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter examined whether the trained encoder--decoder model can be used as a stable forward scenario generator. We now ask whether the same model can be used for swaption pricing. This is a stronger requirement than curve reconstruction: pricing depends not only on the current decoded curve, but also on the distribution of future swap rates, discount factors and annuities under the risk-neutral measure.

The chapter is structured as an investigation. We first define the Monte Carlo pricing pipeline and apply it to the pretrained checkpoints. We then test whether joint training can repair the pricing failure, and whether a simpler diffusion-scale calibration is sufficient. The remaining pricing error is diagnosed through the model-implied forward bias, which reveals a mismatch between the historical drift learned from encoded yield-curve dynamics and the risk-neutral drift required for pricing. This motivates a specification with separate historical and risk-neutral drifts, \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) and \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\). The chapter ends by evaluating the remaining errors and discussing volatility-regime dependence as future work.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We now specialise the simulation-based pricing framework to European swaptions. The objective is to compute the option payoff on each simulated path and then average the discounted payoffs to obtain a time-zero price.

The section first defines the underlying swap and the at-the-money strike. It then derives the pathwise swap rate and payoff at expiry, before introducing the Monte Carlo estimator and the Bachelier convention used to express prices as implied normal volatilities.

\subsection{Swaption payoff and pathwise swap rate}
\label{subsec:swaption_payoff}

A European swaption gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to enter into an interest-rate swap at a future exercise date \(T_e\). The underlying swap starts at \(T_e\) and has length \(T_s=n\delta\), where \(n\in\mathbb N\) is the number of fixed-leg payment periods and \(\delta>0\) is the fixed-leg accrual period. In the implementation, we use annual accrual, so \(\delta=1\). The fixed-leg payment dates are therefore
\[
T_e+\delta,\; T_e+2\delta,\; \dots,\; T_e+n\delta.
\]

The fixed rate agreed in the swaption contract is called the strike and is denoted by \(K\). In general, a swaption can be specified with any strike. In this thesis, we focus on at-the-money swaptions, following the market quoting convention for normal swaption volatilities. A swaption is at the money when the strike is equal to the current forward swap rate of the underlying swap.

The forward swap rate is the par fixed rate, observed at time zero, for a swap starting at \(T_e\) and ending at \(T_e+n\delta\). Given the time-zero discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the corresponding swap annuity is
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(0,T_e+j\delta),
\]
and the forward swap rate is
\[
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n\delta)}{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
For an at-the-money swaption, the strike is therefore chosen as
\[
K = F_0.
\]
This choice makes the underlying forward-starting swap have zero value at inception, but the swaption itself still has positive value due to the optionality.

For each simulated path \(m=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated forward to the option expiry \(T_e\). The decoder then produces a zero-coupon bond curve at expiry. Since the decoder is expressed in terms of time to maturity, the pathwise curve is written as
\[
\tau \mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau),
\]
where \(m\) indexes the Monte Carlo path and \(P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)\) denotes the price at time \(T_e\), on path \(m\), of a zero-coupon bond maturing \(\tau\) years later.

At expiry, the underlying swap is spot-starting: it starts immediately at \(T_e\) and has payment dates \(T_e+\delta,\dots,T_e+n\delta\). In time-to-maturity notation, these payment dates correspond to maturities
\[
\delta,\;2\delta,\;\dots,\;n\delta
\]
measured from \(T_e\). The pathwise swap annuity at expiry is therefore
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P^{(m)}(T_e,j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding pathwise par swap rate is
\[
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]
Here the numerator uses \(P^{(m)}(T_e,0)=1\), since the floating leg of a spot-starting swap has value \(1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)\) under the single-curve assumption.

Let \(\mathcal N>0\) denote the notional. A payer swaption gives the holder the right to enter a payer swap, meaning that the holder pays the fixed rate \(K\) and receives the floating rate. It is therefore valuable when the market swap rate at expiry exceeds the strike. The payer payoff on path \(m\) is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K,0\bigr).
\]
A receiver swaption gives the holder the right to enter a receiver swap, meaning that the holder receives the fixed rate \(K\) and pays the floating rate. It is therefore valuable when the market swap rate at expiry is below the strike. The receiver payoff is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e),0\bigr).
\]

\subsection{Monte Carlo estimator}
\label{subsec:mc_estimator}

The payoff is discounted back to time \(0\) using the pathwise bank account discount factor computed during simulation. Denoting this factor on path \(m\) by
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\left(-\int_0^{T_e} r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
the discounted present value on path \(m\) is
\[
V_0^{(m)}
=
D^{(m)}_{T_e} X^{(m)}_{T_e},
\]
where \(X^{(m)}_{T_e}\) denotes either the payer or receiver payoff defined above. In the implementation, the integral in the discount factor is approximated on the simulation grid using the trapezoidal rule, consistent with the discount-factor discretisation described in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}.

The time-zero swaption price is then estimated by the Monte Carlo average
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}} V_0^{(m)}.
\]

To assess numerical uncertainty, we also report the sample standard deviation of the discounted pathwise payoffs,
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}-1}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\left(V_0^{(m)}-\widehat V_0\right)^2
},
\]
and the associated Monte Carlo standard error
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\frac{\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}}.
\]

\subsection{Bachelier swaption formula and implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

To report swaption prices in market-standard volatility units, we use the Bachelier normal-volatility framework. The relevant numeraire for a forward swap rate is the swap annuity,
\[
\mathcal A(t)
=
\delta\sum_{j=1}^{n} P(t,T_e+j\delta).
\]
The probability measure associated with this numeraire is called the annuity measure and is denoted by \(\mathbb Q^{\mathcal A}\). Under this measure, the forward swap rate is a martingale. This is analogous to a single-period forward rate being a martingale under its corresponding forward measure, but with the swap annuity replacing the zero-coupon bond numeraire.

In the Bachelier normal model \citep{Bachelier1900}, the forward swap rate is modelled as an arithmetic Brownian motion under the annuity measure:
\begin{equation}
    dF(t) = \sigma_N\, dW^{\mathcal A}(t),
    \label{eq:bachelier_sde}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_N>0\) is the normal volatility. The term normal volatility refers to the volatility parameter in this arithmetic, normally distributed model for rate changes. It is quoted in absolute rate units, often basis points per square-root year. The model is convenient in low- and negative-rate environments because it allows the forward swap rate to take negative values.

Under~\eqref{eq:bachelier_sde}, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with notional \(\mathcal N\), strike \(K\), option expiry \(T_e\), forward swap rate \(F\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{pay}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (F-K)\Phi(d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr],
    \qquad
    d =
    \frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:bachelier_payer}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) and \(\phi\) denote the standard normal distribution function and density, respectively. For a receiver swaption, the corresponding price is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{rec}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (K-F)\Phi(-d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr].
    \label{eq:bachelier_receiver}
\end{equation}

For a market at-the-money swaption, the strike is \(K=F_0\), where \(F_0\) is the market forward swap rate observed at time zero. Taking \(F=F_0\), we have \(d=0\), and the Bachelier price reduces to
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:bachelier_atm}
\end{equation}
This formula is used to convert market ATM normal-volatility quotes into price targets:
\begin{equation}
    V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A^{(i)}_0
    \frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:pricing_target}
\end{equation}

Conversely, after Monte Carlo pricing, an at-the-money model price can be converted back into an implied normal volatility by
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
    {\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
    \label{eq:implied_vol_atm}
\end{equation}
This is the reporting convention used for the initial pricing experiments below. Later, when diagnosing forward bias, we return to the distinction between the market forward \(F_0\) and the forward level implied by the model.

The Bachelier Delta of a payer swaption is
\[
\Delta
=
\frac{\partial V^{\mathrm{pay}}}{\partial F}
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\Phi(d).
\]
At the money, \(d=0\), so
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac12\mathcal N\mathcal A_0.
\]
This identity is used later as a benchmark: in the Bachelier model, a correctly centred market at-the-money payer swaption has \(d=0\), so the annuity-measure exercise probability \(\Phi(d)\) is \(50\%\).

\section{Can the Pretrained Model Price?}
\label{sec:can_pretrained_model_price}
%=========================================================

The encoder--decoder model from Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} was trained to minimise reconstruction error on observed swap curves. This objective ensures that the model can reproduce curves close to the training data. Swaption pricing, however, places an additional requirement on the model. The pricing pipeline from Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology} encodes the initial swap curve into a latent state, simulates the latent SDE forward to the option expiry, and then decodes the simulated latent states into zero-coupon bond curves. These simulated latent states need not coincide with the encoded historical states seen during reconstruction training.

We begin by applying this pipeline directly to the pretrained checkpoints, without any additional calibration. This provides a diagnostic test of whether a reconstruction-trained model can be used for swaption pricing without further adjustment. The direct pricing attempt reveals two different failure modes. The baseline model returns finite numerical outputs, but the resulting swaption prices are economically unreasonable. The stable model avoids explosive latent dynamics, but a large fraction of simulated paths lead to non-finite decoded bond curves. The remainder of this section investigates why these failures occur.

\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]

\item[The pricing pipeline as an off-manifold test.]
The Monte Carlo swaption pricing framework can be viewed as a test of the trained encoder--decoder model away from the pure reconstruction setting. Starting from an observed swap curve, the encoder produces an initial latent state \(z_0\). The latent SDE is then simulated forward to the option expiry, producing a terminal latent state \(z_{T_e}^{(m)}\) on each Monte Carlo path. Finally, the decoder maps \(z_{T_e}^{(m)}\) into a zero-coupon bond curve, from which the pathwise swap rate, annuity and payoff are computed.

For reconstruction, the decoder is only required to behave well at encoded historical states of the form \(z=E(S)\), where \(S\) is an observed swap curve. For pricing, however, it must also behave well at simulated latent states reached by the SDE. This is a stronger requirement. If the simulated states lie outside the region where the decoder has learned to produce valid discount curves, the resulting swaption prices can become unreliable even if the reconstruction error on observed curves is small.

We first examine decoder robustness in isolation, before considering the SDE dynamics. For an encoded point \(z_0\), we generate perturbed latent states
\[
z = z_0 + \varepsilon \eta,
\qquad
\eta \sim \mathcal N(0,I_\ell),
\]
and measure the fraction of decoded curves that are fully finite. This gives a simple diagnostic of how far the decoder can be moved away from encoded historical states before numerical failures occur.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/Pricing/fig_decoder_robustness.png}
    \caption{Decoder finite-decoding fraction versus isotropic perturbation scale \(\varepsilon\).}
    \label{fig:decoder_robustness}
\end{figure}

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_decoder_robustness}

Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:decoder_robustness} show that all decoders handle the unperturbed encoded states, corresponding to \(\varepsilon=0\). As the perturbation scale increases, the fraction of finite decoded curves declines. The stable decoders are more robust than the baseline decoders of the same dimension, but robustness still deteriorates for moderate perturbations. This suggests that reconstruction training gives the decoder a limited reliable operating region around the encoded data, rather than a guarantee of valid decoding throughout latent space.

\item[Where the SDE actually goes.]
The previous diagnostic shows how the decoder behaves under controlled perturbations. The next question is whether the simulated latent SDE reaches perturbation sizes where the decoder becomes unreliable. To test this, we simulate \(1{,}000\) paths to \(T_e=5\)Y and compute the terminal displacement
\[
\|z_{T_e}^{(m)}-z_0\|.
\]
This is the Euclidean distance in latent space between the initial encoded state \(z_0\) and the simulated state at option expiry on path \(m\). It measures how far the pricing simulation moves the latent state away from the starting curve.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/Pricing/fig_latent_displacement_cdf.png}
    \caption{CDF of the terminal latent displacement \(\|z_{T_e}-z_0\|\) at \(T_e = 5\)Y for the dim4 baseline and stable models. The horizontal axis is shown on a symlog scale.}
    \label{fig:latent_displacement_cdf}
\end{figure}

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_displacement_summary}

Figure~\ref{fig:latent_displacement_cdf} plots the cumulative distribution function of the terminal displacement. For any value on the horizontal axis, the curve shows the fraction of Monte Carlo paths with terminal displacement below that value. The baseline distribution lies far to the right, indicating extremely large latent movements. The stable distribution is much more controlled and concentrated at moderate distances from \(z_0\).

This explains the different pricing failures. The baseline model sends the latent state far outside the region represented in the training data. Even if the decoder returns finite numerical outputs, these outputs are extreme extrapolations and should not be interpreted as reliable discount curves. The stable model avoids this explosive behaviour, which is the intended effect of the stability restriction. However, the stable paths still move far enough away from the encoded starting point that they enter the range where the decoder robustness test showed frequent failures. Hence the stable model is dynamically well behaved, but the decoder is not necessarily reliable on all latent states visited during pricing.

\item[Drift and diffusion contributions.]
To understand where the stable model's latent movement comes from, we decompose the
simulated motion into drift-only and diffusion-only contributions. This separates the roles
of the drift network \(\mathcal K\) and the diffusion network \(\mathcal H\).

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_hk_decomposition}

The baseline model is included in Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition} as a reference. Its large displacement is mainly a consequence of the instability already discussed in the simulation chapter. In particular, the drift can generate explosive movement along unstable eigendirections.

The stable model behaves differently. The stability restriction ensures that the linear drift has only negative eigenvalues, so the drift-only trajectory remains bounded. The full simulated displacement is therefore mainly driven by the diffusion term, with the mean-reverting drift acting as a restoring force. Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition} shows that the drift-only five-year displacement is small, while the full displacement is much larger. Thus, for the stable model, the pricing-relevant movement away from \(z_0\) is primarily diffusion-driven.

\item[An apparent paradox in the stable variant.]
One might expect the stable drift to dominate the simulation because the stable equilibrium
\[
z^* = -M^{-1}N_{\mathcal K}
\]
lies far from a typical encoded EUR curve. In the dim4 stable model, \(\|z^*-z_0\|\) is approximately \(9.5\). At first sight, this suggests that the mean-reverting drift should pull the latent state strongly toward the equilibrium, producing a large drift-driven displacement over five years.

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_eigenvector_alignment}

Table~\ref{tab:eigenvector_alignment} explains why this does not happen. The vector \(z^*-z_0\) is almost entirely aligned with the slowest eigenvector of \(\mathcal K\). The corresponding eigenvalue is very close to zero, giving a mean-reversion timescale of roughly 600 years. Over a five-year pricing horizon, this direction barely moves. Hence the large distance between \(z_0\) and \(z^*\) is structurally present, but dynamically almost inactive at swaption-pricing horizons. The realised drift-only displacement is therefore small, despite the equilibrium being far away.

\end{description}

The diagnostics identify two distinct barriers to using the pretrained checkpoints directly for pricing. The baseline model fails because the latent SDE can move explosively far away from the region covered by the training data. The stable model removes this instability, but the simulated latent states still move outside the decoder's reliable reconstruction region. The problem is therefore not only SDE stability, but also decoder coverage: reconstruction-only training does not prepare the decoder for all off-manifold states reached during pricing.

This motivates the next question. If the decoder fails on the simulated states reached by the pretrained dynamics, can this be fixed by training directly on a pricing objective, or is the difficulty structural?

%=========================================================
\section{Can Joint Training Fix the Pricing Failure?}
\label{sec:joint_training_attempt}
%=========================================================

The previous section showed that the pretrained stable model cannot be used directly for
pricing: the latent dynamics are stable, but the simulated states still move outside the
region where the reconstruction-trained decoder produces reliable discount curves. A
natural response is to include swaption prices directly in the training objective. The hope
is that the model can then adjust its latent dynamics so that simulated paths lead to
meaningful decoded bond curves and realistic implied volatilities.

This section investigates that possibility. Starting from the pretrained stable checkpoint, we
add a pricing loss to the reconstruction objective and examine whether joint training can
improve pricing without destroying the curve reconstruction.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:joint_training_setup}

For each training date, the latent SDE is simulated forward to the option expiry, the
terminal latent states are decoded into zero-coupon bond curves, and the resulting
model-implied normal volatility is compared to the market quote. The combined loss is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{recon}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{price}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{price}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{eig}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eig}},
    \label{eq:joint_loss}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}\) is the mean-squared reconstruction error on observed
swap curves, \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{price}}\) is a Huber loss on the error between
model-implied and market normal volatilities, and \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eig}}\) is an
eigenvalue floor penalty that keeps the real parts of the drift eigenvalues strictly negative.

The decoder network \(\mathcal G\) is frozen throughout the experiment. Without this
restriction, the pricing loss could reduce option values by reshaping the decoder response
at simulated terminal states, risking damage to the reconstruction map on encoded
historical states. With \(\mathcal G\) frozen, the pricing signal flows through the drift and
diffusion networks, \(\mathcal K\) and \(\mathcal H\). The experiment therefore tests whether
the latent dynamics can be adjusted to make the pricing pipeline work while preserving the
trained decoder structure.

\subsection{Finding: reconstruction collapses}
\label{subsec:joint_training_finding}

Table~\ref{tab:joint_training_result} reports the reconstruction RMSE and pricing outcomes
at selected checkpoints. At the pretrained checkpoint, the reconstruction RMSE is
approximately \(5\) bp, but the pricing experiment from the previous section showed that
the stable model does not yet produce reliable swaption prices. During joint training, the
reconstruction RMSE grows rapidly. The first run was stopped at epoch \(557\), where the
RMSE had already increased to \(210\) bp. A second run was allowed to continue to epoch
\(3{,}500\), at which point the reconstruction RMSE reached approximately \(2{,}200\) bp.
At this checkpoint, the pricing evaluation produced \(100\%\) non-finite outputs.

The two objectives fail together. The model that no longer reconstructs curves also cannot
price swaptions. Allowing training to continue does not recover either objective:
reconstruction continues to deteriorate, and the pricing pipeline produces no finite prices.
This suggests that the failure is not simply due to too few epochs or insufficient
optimisation.

\subsection{Why joint training fails}
\label{subsec:joint_training_diagnosis}

The failure has a structural cause. The drift network \(\mathcal K\) appears in two distinct
places in the model.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item In the simulation step,
    \[
        dz_t = \mathcal K(z_t)\,dt + \mathcal H(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q}.
    \]
    The pricing loss backpropagates through the simulated paths and updates
    \(\mathcal K\) through its effect on future latent states.

    \item In the no-arbitrage ODE, \(\mathcal K\) enters the coefficients used to compute
    \(A_{z_t}(\tau)\) and \(B_{z_t}(\tau)\). These functions determine the decoded
    zero-coupon bond prices. The reconstruction loss therefore also backpropagates
    through \(\mathcal K\), through its effect on the ODE solution and hence on the
    reconstructed swap curve.
\end{enumerate}

Both losses therefore write gradients to the same drift network, but through different
roles. The pricing loss pushes \(\mathcal K\) toward dynamics that produce realistic
risk-neutral swaption prices. The reconstruction loss pushes \(\mathcal K\) toward values
that help reproduce the cross-section of observed swap curves. These targets need not be
the same. In particular, reconstruction is based on historical curve observations, whereas
pricing requires risk-neutral dynamics.

Freezing \(\mathcal G\) does not remove this coupling. Even when \(\mathcal G\) is fixed, the
bond prices used for reconstruction are still computed from \(A_{z_t}(\tau)\) and
\(B_{z_t}(\tau)\), and these ODE solutions depend on \(\mathcal K\). Any meaningful pricing
update to \(\mathcal K\) therefore changes the reconstructed bond prices as well.

This is an architectural constraint rather than a simple tuning problem. Adjusting the
pricing-loss weight, the number of swaptions per batch, or the learning-rate schedule did
not change the qualitative outcome across runs: once the pricing loss meaningfully updates
\(\mathcal K\), reconstruction deteriorates.

The finding from this section is therefore that joint training with a single shared
\(\mathcal K\) cannot cleanly reconcile reconstruction and pricing. A different approach is
needed. Rather than asking only how to extend decoder coverage, we now ask why the drift
needed for pricing differs from the drift learned during reconstruction.


%=========================================================
\section{Can a Diffusion Scale Be Calibrated Instead?}
\label{sec:hscale_calibration}
%=========================================================

The previous section showed that joint training with a single shared drift network
\(\mathcal K\) does not provide a stable solution: once the pricing loss meaningfully updates
\(\mathcal K\), the reconstruction map deteriorates. We therefore consider a simpler and
less invasive calibration question. Instead of changing the trained networks, can we keep
the pretrained checkpoint fixed and calibrate only the scale of the simulated diffusion?

In the numerical experiments in this section, the notional is set to \(\mathcal N=1\). We
focus on at-the-money payer swaptions, so the strike is set equal to the time-zero forward
swap rate, \(K=F_0\). The quantities \(F_0\) and \(\mathcal A_0\) are computed from the
decoded initial discount curve, while the pathwise quantities \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) and
\(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) are computed from the decoded discount curve at expiry. Since
bond prices are extracted directly from the model grids, the option expiry \(T_e\) must lie
on the simulation time grid. The maturities needed for the initial forward swap rate,
\(T_e\) and \(T_e+j\delta\), and the expiry-time maturities \(j\delta\), must lie on the
decoder maturity grid.

\subsection{Calibration procedure}
\label{subsec:hscale_procedure}

We introduce a scalar diffusion scale \(s>0\), applied only at simulation time. The
Euler--Maruyama simulation uses
\[
dz_t
=
\mathcal K(z_t)\,dt
+
s\,\mathcal H(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
\]
instead of the unscaled diffusion \(\mathcal H(z_t)\). Equivalently, at each time step,
\[
z_{t+\Delta t}
=
z_t
+
\mathcal K(z_t)\Delta t
+
s\,\mathcal H(z_t)\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\varepsilon_t,
\qquad
\varepsilon_t\sim\mathcal N(0,I).
\]
The ODE system and decoder are left unchanged. Thus, the scale changes the amount of
stochastic variation along simulated paths, but it does not modify the trained bond-price
reconstruction for a given latent state.

For each swaption quote \(j\), let \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}\) denote the market normal
volatility and let \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\) denote the model-implied normal volatility
obtained from Monte Carlo pricing at the unscaled diffusion level \(s=1\). Since the
at-the-money Bachelier price is linear in the normal volatility, and since scaling the
diffusion approximately scales the spread of the terminal swap-rate distribution, we use
the working approximation
\[
    \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(s)
    \approx
    s\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1).
\]
This approximation is not exact, because the decoder, the annuity and the swaption payoff
are nonlinear functions of the latent state. It nevertheless gives a simple first-order
calibration rule.

The global scale is chosen by minimising squared errors in normal-volatility space:
\begin{equation}
    s^*
    =
    \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{s>0}
    \sum_{j:\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}>0}
    \bigl(
        s\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)
        -
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}
    \bigr)^2.
    \label{eq:hscale_objective}
\end{equation}
This is an origin-constrained ordinary least-squares problem with closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
    s^*
    =
    \frac{
        \sum_j
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\,
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}
    }{
        \sum_j
        \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)^2
    }.
    \label{eq:hscale_ols}
\end{equation}
The calibrated scale is applied through a \texttt{diffusion\_scale} argument in the simulator.
No weights in \(\mathcal H\) are modified.

\subsection{Calibration results}
\label{subsec:hscale_results}

The global calibration is estimated on the EUR at-the-money swaption quotes for which the
unscaled pricing run produces finite model-implied volatilities. This leaves \(1{,}246\)
observations. At \(s=1\), the reconstruction-trained diffusion produces model-implied
normal volatilities far above market levels, with a volatility MAE of \(315\) bp. The OLS
estimate is
\[
s^*=0.151.
\]
Thus, the volatility level implied by the unscaled diffusion is approximately
\(1/0.151\approx 6.6\) times larger than the level needed to match market swaption
volatilities on average. Applying this scale during simulation reduces the volatility MAE to
\(91\) bp.

The global scale corrects the average level, but not the shape of the volatility surface.
Because the diffusion scale acts over the option horizon, a natural refinement is to fit one
scale per expiry row, pooling all tenors for a given expiry. This gives
\[
  s^*(1\mathrm{Y}) = 0.129, \qquad
  s^*(5\mathrm{Y}) = 0.133, \qquad
  s^*(10\mathrm{Y}) = 0.141.
\]
The three values are close to one another and to the global estimate, suggesting that most
of the calibration effect is a common reduction in diffusion scale. The expiry-level
calibration reduces the out-of-sample MAE to \(72\) bp.

As a further check, we also fit one scale factor per expiry--tenor cell. This gives nine
parameters instead of three, but the out-of-sample MAE is \(73\) bp, essentially unchanged
relative to the expiry-level calibration. Thus, increasing the number of scale parameters
does not materially improve pricing accuracy.

expiry-level calibration suggests that the remaining error is not mainly a scale-selection
problem.

The conclusion from this section is therefore twofold. First, a scalar diffusion scale is
useful: it turns the pretrained stable model into a functioning pricing baseline and reduces
the volatility error substantially. Second, the error appears to have a floor around
\(72\)--\(74\) bp that is not removed by adding more scale parameters. The next section
investigates whether this remaining error reflects a structural bias in the model dynamics
rather than a miscalibrated diffusion level.


%=========================================================
\section{Forward Bias and Effective Exercise Probabilities}
\label{sec:pricing_diagnostics}
%=========================================================

The previous section showed that diffusion-scale calibration improves pricing substantially, but that the error appears to have a floor around \(72\)--\(74\) bp. This section investigates whether the remaining error is only a volatility-scale problem, or whether the model dynamics introduce a systematic bias in the simulated swap-rate distribution.

The Bachelier Delta identity from Section~\ref{subsec:bachelier_formula} provides a useful benchmark. In the Bachelier model, the payer Delta is
\[
\Delta
=
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\Phi(d),
\qquad
d=
\frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
For a correctly centred at-the-money swaption, \(F=K=F_0\), so \(d=0\). The corresponding exercise probability is therefore
\[
\Phi(0)=50\%.
\]
Equivalently, under the annuity measure, the forward swap rate should satisfy the martingale condition
\[
\mathbb E^{\mathbb Q^{\mathcal A}}[S_{T_e}] = F_0.
\]
If the simulated distribution of \(S_{T_e}\) is not centred at \(F_0\), then the market at-the-money strike \(K=F_0\) is no longer at the money from the model's perspective.

This forward bias can be estimated from payer--receiver parity. For payer and receiver prices computed at the same strike \(K=F_0\), the model-implied forward is
\[
F_{\mathrm{mod}}
=
F_0
+
\frac{
V_{\mathrm{pay}}-V_{\mathrm{rec}}
}{
\mathcal N\mathcal A_0
},
\]
so the forward bias is
\begin{equation}
  \text{forward bias}
  =
  F_{\mathrm{mod}}-F_0
  =
  \frac{
  V_{\mathrm{pay}}-V_{\mathrm{rec}}
  }{
  \mathcal N\mathcal A_0
  }.
  \label{eq:forward_bias}
\end{equation}
A negative value means that the model-implied distribution is centred below the market forward rate. In that case, a market at-the-money payer swaption is effectively out of the money under the model.

To express the size of the bias in probability terms, we standardise it by a volatility scale. We use the straddle-implied volatility
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{str}}
  =
  \frac{V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}}}{2}
  \cdot
  \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}},
\]
as a simple scale proxy for the model's dispersion around the strike. This gives
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{eff}}
  =
  \frac{\text{forward bias}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{str}}\sqrt{T_e}},
  \qquad
  p_{\mathrm{eff}}
  =
  \Phi(d_{\mathrm{eff}}).
  \label{eq:p_eff}
\end{equation}
Under a correctly centred at-the-money Bachelier model, \(d_{\mathrm{eff}}=0\) and
\(p_{\mathrm{eff}}=50\%\). Deviations from \(50\%\) therefore provide a diagnostic for whether the model prices the market at-the-money strike as truly at the money.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:delta_diagnostic} reports the per-cell forward bias and effective exercise probability at the expiry-level calibrated scales. Two findings stand out.

First, the forward bias is consistently negative across all nine cells, ranging from \(-18\) bp for the 10Y\(\times\)1Y cell to \(-694\) bp for the 1Y\(\times\)5Y cell. Thus, the model-implied distribution of \(S_{T_e}\) is systematically below \(F_0\) across the expiry--tenor grid. This is consistent with the interpretation that the drift \(\mathcal K\), estimated from historical yield-curve dynamics, carries information about the historical direction of rates rather than the risk-neutral martingale condition required for pricing.

Second, the effective exercise probabilities range from \(24\%\) for the 1Y\(\times\)5Y cell to \(49\%\) for the 10Y\(\times\)1Y cell, with an overall average of approximately \(35\%\). The Bachelier benchmark is \(50\%\). The 10Y\(\times\)1Y cell is closest to the benchmark, while the short-expiry cells are furthest away. This indicates that the residual pricing error after diffusion-scale calibration is not only a matter of volatility level. The model also shifts the swap-rate distribution relative to the market forward.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_stable_hscale/delta_diagnostic/fig_exercise_prob.png}
  \caption{Effective ATM exercise probability \(p_{\mathrm{eff}}\) per cell at the expiry-level calibrated scale. The Bachelier benchmark is \(50\%\). All cells are below \(50\%\), indicating a systematic downward bias in the model-implied swap-rate distribution.}

The diagnostic explains why adding more diffusion-scale parameters did not remove the remaining pricing error. A scalar diffusion scale can adjust the spread of the simulated distribution, but it cannot recenter the distribution around \(F_0\). The source of the remaining error is therefore structural: the same drift \(\mathcal K\) is being used both as the historical drift learned from encoded yield-curve dynamics and as the risk-neutral drift required for swaption pricing. The next section addresses this mismatch by introducing a separate risk-neutral drift.


%=========================================================
\section{A Separate Risk-Neutral Drift: the \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) Approach}
\label{sec:kq_training}
%=========================================================

The forward-bias analysis in the previous section suggests that the remaining pricing error is not only a diffusion-scale problem. The drift network \(\mathcal K\) was learned from historical yield-curve dynamics, but it is then used as the risk-neutral drift in both the pricing simulation and the no-arbitrage ODE. These two roles make different demands on the same parameters.

The joint-training experiment of Section~\ref{sec:joint_training_attempt} tried to resolve this by updating the shared \(\mathcal K\) with a pricing loss. That approach failed because any pricing-gradient update to \(\mathcal K\) also changes the ODE-derived bond prices, thereby corrupting reconstruction. The problem is architectural: a single shared drift cannot simultaneously act as the historical drift needed for reconstruction and the risk-neutral drift needed for pricing.

A natural modification is therefore to introduce a separate risk-neutral drift network, denoted \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\), used during pricing. The original drift, denoted \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\), remains the historical drift associated with the reconstruction-trained model. The pricing SDE becomes
\[
    dz_t
    =
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}(z_t)\,dt
    +
    \mathcal H(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q}.
\]
For pricing consistency, \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) is also used in the no-arbitrage ODE when decoding the simulated terminal states. Thus, the same risk-neutral drift governs both the simulated latent paths and the bond curves used to compute the swaption payoff.

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{subsec:kq_architecture}

The separation is implemented through a subclass \texttt{FullModelPrice}, which adds an optional \texttt{k\_override} argument to the decoding step. When \texttt{k\_override} \(= \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) is passed, the subclass temporarily replaces the model drift by \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) for the duration of the ODE solve and then restores the original \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\). The base model \texttt{FullModel} is not modified. The simulator is extended analogously: when a \texttt{k\_override} is provided, the Euler--Maruyama drift step uses \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}(z_t)\) instead of \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z_t)\).

The network \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) is initialised as a copy of the pretrained \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) and is then trained against the swaption pricing loss. The encoder and decoder \(\mathcal G\) are frozen throughout. In this experiment, the diffusion network \(\mathcal H\) is also unfrozen, so that the pricing loss can adjust the volatility level as well as the drift. Since the reconstruction-trained diffusion produced volatilities roughly six times above market levels, the output layer of \(\mathcal H\) is pre-scaled by a factor of \(0.1\) before training begins.

This experiment should therefore be read as an unconstrained pricing-training experiment with a separate risk-neutral drift, rather than as a final pricing architecture. It tests whether introducing \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) can reduce the forward-bias problem, while also revealing whether allowing \(\mathcal H\) to move creates new reconstruction or decoding issues.

\subsection{Training setup}
\label{subsec:kq_training_setup}

The pricing loss is a mean-squared error on normal-volatility errors:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L_{\mathrm{price}}
    =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal S|}
    \sum_{(e,t,d)\in\mathcal S}
    \left(
        \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},d}(e,t)-\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},d}(e,t)}
        {100\,\mathrm{bp}}
    \right)^2,
    \label{eq:kq_price_loss}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal S\) is a random subsample of expiry--tenor--date triples drawn at each step, and volatilities are expressed in basis points. At each epoch, \(N_{\mathrm{steps}}=8\) gradient steps are taken, each using \(N_{\mathrm{MC}}=512\) simulated paths and \(N_{\mathrm{sw}}=16\) randomly selected swaption quotes. The optimiser is Adam with an initial learning rate of \(10^{-3}\), warmed up linearly over the first \(200\) epochs.

\label{subsec:kq_results}

Table~\ref{tab:kq_training_progress} reports the key metrics at selected checkpoints over the \(1{,}000\)-epoch run.

The pricing loss decreases substantially over the first \(600\) epochs, from \(7.891\) to \(0.230\). This shows that the additional risk-neutral drift can learn a meaningful pricing correction. After epoch \(600\), the loss no longer improves and becomes noisier, suggesting that the run has reached the useful part of the optimisation. However, two problems emerge in parallel.

\paragraph{First failure mode: \(\mathcal H\)-ODE coupling.}
The reconstruction RMSE rises from \(49.1\) bp to \(66.0\) bp over the run. The initial RMSE is already far above the pretrained reconstruction level of \(5.3\) bp because the output layer of \(\mathcal H\) is pre-scaled before training begins. This matters because \(\mathcal H\) enters the no-arbitrage ODE through the diffusion covariance
\[
\Sigma(z)
=
\mathcal H(z)\mathcal H(z)^\top.
\]
Changing \(\mathcal H\) therefore changes the ODE coefficients and hence the reconstructed bond prices, even when \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) and \(\mathcal G\) are frozen. Subsequent pricing updates to \(\mathcal H\) push the reconstruction RMSE further upward. Thus, the same coupling problem identified for \(\mathcal K\) in Section~\ref{sec:joint_training_attempt} reappears through \(\mathcal H\).

\paragraph{Second failure mode: decoder coverage.}
The path finite fraction falls from \(52\%\) at the start of the run to \(32\%\) by epoch \(999\). As \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) changes, the simulated risk-neutral paths move into different regions of latent space. If these regions are outside the domain where the reconstruction-trained decoder produces valid curves, the decoded bond curves become non-finite. Thus, although \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) improves the pricing loss, it also moves the pricing distribution into latent regions where decoder coverage remains incomplete.

\paragraph{What \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) learned.}
Inspecting the final checkpoint gives insight into the correction learned by \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\). The smallest eigenvalue of the stability matrix associated with \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) increases from \(0.0016\) at initialisation to approximately \(0.120\) by the end of training. In mean-reversion terms, the slowest latent direction therefore changes from being nearly non-stationary to reverting on a horizon of roughly \(8\) years.

This suggests that the pricing loss pushes the risk-neutral drift toward stronger mean reversion in the slowest latent direction. Economically, this direction is naturally interpreted as a level-like factor of the yield curve. Under the historical measure, the EUR rate level in the training period behaves close to a random walk, whereas the pricing loss favours a risk-neutral drift that reduces the forward bias identified in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_diagnostics}. The learned \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) therefore moves in the expected direction: it changes the drift most strongly in the low-frequency component that dominates swap-rate levels.

\paragraph{Summary.}
The \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) experiment confirms that separating the historical and risk-neutral drifts is a meaningful direction. The pricing loss decreases substantially, and the learned drift correction targets the slowest, level-like latent direction where the forward-bias problem is most naturally located. However, the unconstrained version of the experiment does not yet provide a satisfactory pricing architecture.

Two limitations remain. First, allowing \(\mathcal H\) to move introduces reconstruction damage, because \(\mathcal H\) also enters the no-arbitrage ODE. Second, the learned \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) can move simulated paths into regions of latent space where the decoder was not trained to produce valid curves. Both limitations point toward a more constrained specification: keep \(\mathcal H\) fixed, and parameterise the risk-neutral drift as a controlled deviation from \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\). This motivates the structural framework discussed next.

%=========================================================
\section{A Structural Extension: Market Price of Risk Parameterisation}
\label{sec:lyashenko}
%=========================================================

The \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) approach of the previous section is pragmatic: it introduces
a second drift and trains it against observed swaption prices without imposing an explicit
relationship between \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) and \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\). A more
structured approach is suggested by Girsanov's theorem. If the historical and risk-neutral
dynamics have the same diffusion coefficient, then the difference between the two drifts is
determined by a market price of risk.

Let \(W^{\mathbb P}\) be a Brownian motion under the historical measure and let
\(\lambda_t\) denote the market price of risk. Under the change of measure,
\[
    W^{\mathbb Q}_t
    =
    W^{\mathbb P}_t
    +
    \int_0^t \lambda_s\,ds
\]
is a Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure. If the latent dynamics under
\(\mathbb P\) are
\begin{equation}
    dz_t
    =
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z_t)\,dt
    +
    \mathcal H(z_t)\,dW^{\mathbb P}_t,
    \label{eq:P_sde}
\end{equation}
then the corresponding \(\mathbb Q\)-measure dynamics are
\begin{equation}
    dz_t
    =
    \Bigl[
        \mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z_t)
        -
        \mathcal H(z_t)\lambda(z_t)
    \Bigr]dt
    +
    \mathcal H(z_t)\,dW^{\mathbb Q}_t.
    \label{eq:Q_sde}
\end{equation}
Hence the risk-neutral drift is
\[
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}(z)
    =
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z)
    -
    \mathcal H(z)\lambda(z),
\]
where \(\lambda\) is the market-price-of-risk function.

This decomposition is the natural theoretical counterpart to the separate-drift experiment.
Instead of estimating \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) freely, one keeps the historical drift
\(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) fixed and estimates the market-price-of-risk component. In this
form, the risk-neutral drift is not an arbitrary second network; it is a controlled adjustment
of the historical drift through the diffusion directions of the model. This is closely related
to the framework of \citet{Lyashenko2019}, where the separation between historical and
risk-neutral term-structure dynamics is imposed at the model-design level rather than
recovered by unconstrained pricing training.

\paragraph{Relation to the \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) experiment.}
The experiment in Section~\ref{sec:kq_training} estimated the composite drift
\(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) directly, without decomposing it into
\(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\) and \(\mathcal H\lambda\). The learned correction was
economically interpretable: the largest change occurred in the slowest, level-like latent
direction, increasing its mean-reversion speed under the pricing measure. In the
market-price-of-risk representation, this corresponds to a large risk-premium adjustment in
the level factor.

However, the unconstrained \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) experiment also revealed two
limitations. First, allowing \(\mathcal H\) to move contaminated the reconstruction ODE,
because \(\mathcal H\) enters the bond-pricing equations through the diffusion covariance
\[
    \Sigma(z)=\mathcal H(z)\mathcal H(z)^\top.
\]
Second, the freely trained \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}\) could move the risk-neutral latent
distribution into regions where the decoder was not trained to produce valid curves. This
reduced the finite-path fraction during pricing.

The market-price-of-risk parameterisation addresses both issues more directly. The diffusion
network \(\mathcal H\) can be kept fixed, avoiding reconstruction damage. The drift
correction is also constrained to lie in the directions spanned by \(\mathcal H(z)\). For
example, with an affine specification
\[
    \lambda(z)=\Lambda z,
\]
the risk-neutral drift becomes
\[
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}(z)
    =
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z)
    -
    \mathcal H(z)\Lambda z.
\]
The learnable object is then the matrix \(\Lambda\), rather than an entirely unrestricted
second drift network. With regularisation on \(\Lambda\), the risk-neutral correction is kept
close to the historical dynamics unless the swaption pricing loss provides evidence for a
larger adjustment.

\paragraph{Implementation as future work.}
A natural extension of the present framework is therefore to train only the market-price-of-risk
parameter \(\Lambda\). In this setup, all original network weights
\(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\), \(\mathcal H\), and \(\mathcal G\) remain frozen. The simulation and
pricing ODE use
\[
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}(z)
    =
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb P}(z)
    -
    \mathcal H(z)\Lambda z,
\]
while reconstruction continues to use the original \(\mathcal K^{\mathbb P}\). The volatility
level can still be controlled by the expiry-level diffusion scales estimated in
Section~\ref{sec:hscale_calibration}, since those scales are applied only at simulation time
and do not change any network weights.

This parameterisation is implemented in \texttt{Training\_lambda\_mpr.py}. For the
four-dimensional stable model, \(\Lambda\) is a \(4\times4\) matrix initialised at zero. Thus,
training starts from
\[
    \mathcal K^{\mathbb Q}=\mathcal K^{\mathbb P},
\]
with the original reconstruction fit preserved. The pricing loss then updates only
\(\Lambda\), pushing the risk-neutral drift away from the historical drift only through an
explicit market-price-of-risk channel. This provides a more constrained and interpretable
route to correcting the forward bias identified in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_diagnostics}.
A full empirical evaluation of this extension is left for future work.
