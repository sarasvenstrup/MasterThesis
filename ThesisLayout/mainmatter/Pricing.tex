\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, produces more controlled latent dynamics that are suitable for simulation. This chapter applies those dynamics to Monte Carlo pricing of swaptions, for which the relevant pricing theory is presented first.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on a standard interest rate swap introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_{\mu}\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_{\mu}\) to \(T_{\nu}\), with annual fixed-leg payment dates \(T_{\mu+1}, T_{\mu+2}, \ldots, T_{\nu}\).

For an at-the-money (ATM) swaption the strike equals the time-zero forward swap rate. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P(0,T_{k}),
\qquad
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)
=
\frac{P(0,T_{\mu})-P(0,T_{\nu})}{\mathcal A_0},
\]
so the ATM strike is \(K = R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\).

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_{\mu}\) with monthly steps.\footnote{Concretely, twelve steps for the 1Y expiry, sixty for 5Y, and one hundred and twenty for 10Y. During calibration training the step is loosened to \(dt=1/6\) for tractability.} The decoder then produces the time-\(T_{\mu}\) zero-coupon bond curve \(\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},\tau)\), from which the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\sum_{k=\mu + 1}^{\nu}P^{(m)}(T_{\mu},T_k-T_\mu),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_{\mu}, T_{\nu}-T_\mu)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})}.
\]
The payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})-K\bigr)^+,
\qquad
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_{\mu})\bigr)^+.
\]
Each payoff is discounted by the pathwise bank account
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^{T_{\mu}}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
approximated on the simulation grid by the trapezoidal rule (Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}). The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is
\[
\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}X^{(m)}_{T_{\mu}}.
\]

The typical market convention is to quote swaptions in terms of normal implied volatility.\footnote{See \citet{Skovmand2025tsm7}, p.~60, lecture notes, week 7.} Under the Bachelier model, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with expiry \(T_{\mu}\), strike \(K\), forward swap rate \(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\[
V_0
=
\mathcal A_0
\left[
\bigl(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K\bigr)\Phi(d)
+
\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}\,\phi(d)
\right],
\qquad
d=
\frac{R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}},
\]
where \(\Phi(\cdot)\) and \(\phi(\cdot)\) denote the standard normal distribution and density functions, respectively. In the at-the-money case, \(K=R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0)\), so \(d=0\), and the price reduces to
\begin{equation}
V_0^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\label{eq:atm_bachelier_price}
\end{equation}
A Monte Carlo estimate of the at-the-money swaption price can therefore be converted into a normal implied volatility by inverting this expression,
\[
\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{MC}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\]
To reduce the effect of directional centering errors, we price both a payer and a receiver swaption at the ATM strike and average the two time-zero prices:
\begin{equation}
\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0
=
\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\qquad
\widehat\sigma_{N}^{\mathrm{imp}}
=
\frac{\widehat V^{\mathrm{str}}_0\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_{\mu}}}.
\label{eq:straddle_normal_vol}
\end{equation}
This gives a \emph{straddle normal implied volatility}. Since payer and receiver prices move in opposite directions when the simulated forward swap rate deviates from the ATM strike, averaging removes the first-order directional effect of such centering errors.

We also track the \emph{forward centering bias}
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{0,\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0},
\]
which should be close to zero at the ATM strike for a correctly centred model.

\section{A Pricing Layer on Top of the Stable Model}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

To test whether the latent dynamics trained on swap-rate data can also support derivative pricing, we add a small \emph{pricing layer} on top of the stable model. The learned weights and networks are kept fixed. Let \(\mu(z)\) and \(\sigma(z)\) denote the drift and diffusion of the frozen stable model. The pricing layer introduces two trainable parameter vectors: a constant drift shift \(\eta \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and a positive per-factor diffusion scale \(s \in \mathbb{R}^d_{>0}\). The adjusted dynamics are
\[
dz_t
=
\bigl(\mu(z_t) + \sigma(z_t)\eta\bigr)\,dt
+
\operatorname{diag}(s)\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t.
\]
The parameter \(\eta\) shifts the centre of the simulated forward distribution, while \(s\) adjusts its width. In the calibration we optimise \(\log s\) rather than \(s\) directly, which ensures that the diffusion scales remain positive. With \(2d\) trainable parameters in total, namely \((\eta,\log s)\), the layer is calibrated to EUR ATM swaption straddle volatilities on the 2010--2021 training window and evaluated out of sample on 2022--2024.

This is an \emph{exploratory experiment, not a fully consistent joint model}. The bond-pricing ODE is re-solved under the adjusted drift and diffusion, so the simulation and the terminal bond decode at expiry \(T_\mu\) are internally consistent. However, the initial bond curve at \(t=0\) is read from the \emph{stable} decoder so that today's swap curve matches the market by construction. There is therefore a mismatch between the initial curve and the subsequent pricing dynamics. We return to this in Section~\ref{sec:pricing_limitation} as one limitation of the experiment.

\subsection{Calibration}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

The pricing layer is calibrated by minimising a loss with three terms: a volatility error, a forward-centering penalty, and a small regularisation term on the drift shift:
\[
\mathcal{L}
=
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_{N}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}}
+
0.5
\underbrace{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\widehat b^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2
}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}}
+
10^{-3}\|\eta\|^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled EUR swaption observations. All volatilities and centering biases in the loss are measured in basis points, and the division by \(100\) normalises errors to units of \(100\) basis points. The first term fits the straddle-implied normal volatility, while the second penalises deviations from ATM centering. In a correctly centred model, the simulated forward swap rate should remain centred around the initial ATM strike,
\[
\mathbb{E}^{\mathcal A}
\!\left[
S(T_\mu)
\right]
\approx
R_{\mathrm{swap}}(0).
\]
The final term keeps the drift shift \(\eta\) from becoming too large.

Optimisation is done with Adam. The learning rate is first increased linearly during a short warm-up period and is then decreased using cosine annealing. We train for \(1000\) epochs using \(N_{\mathrm{MC}}=512\) antithetic paths per observation.\footnote{Antithetic variates are a standard Monte Carlo variance-reduction technique \citep{Glasserman2004}. For each Brownian increment vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), we also simulate a mirrored path with increment \(-\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\). The two payoffs are then averaged, which reduces Monte Carlo noise at little extra cost.}

The overall MAE across the swaption surface is \(\mathbf{49.4}\) bp. The error is \(XX.X\) bp on the training window and \(\mathbf{XX.X}\) bp out of sample. The small train--test gap suggests that the eight-parameter pricing layer does not materially overfit the training data. However, the errors are not evenly distributed across the surface. The largest residuals come from the 1Y\(\times\)1Y and 1Y\(\times\)5Y swaptions, with all-date MAEs of \(157.4\) bp and \(93.1\) bp, respectively.

The large short-expiry errors are not surprising. At short expiries, small changes in the terminal forward-swap-rate distribution have a large effect on the annualised implied volatility. The 1Y expiry cells therefore provide a demanding test of whether the model has the right local volatility scale.

The forward-bias diagnostic helps separate two different effects. The volatility error measures whether the simulated terminal distribution has the right width. The forward bias measures whether the distribution is centred around the initial ATM strike. A small volatility error does not necessarily mean that both are correct: a model can underproduce volatility but still obtain a reasonable straddle price if the simulated forward rate is shifted away from the strike.

This is important for interpreting the longer-expiry results. Several 5Y and 10Y expiry swaptions have smaller volatility MAEs, but their forward biases are not always small. In particular, the 10Y expiry cells show large positive forward biases. This means that the simulated forward swap rate is shifted above the initial ATM strike. The shift increases the straddle price and can partly compensate for too little model volatility. The apparently better fit at long expiries should therefore be interpreted with caution: it is partly caused by cancellation between volatility error and centering error.

\subsection{Error decomposition: volatility level and forward bias}
\label{subsec:cmpr_error_decomposition}

The forward-bias diagnostic shows that the volatility MAE alone does not fully describe the pricing error. The straddle-implied volatility depends both on the width of the terminal swap-rate distribution and on how far its centre is from the ATM strike. A model can therefore obtain a reasonable straddle price even if the underlying terminal distribution is wrong.

The 1Y expiry swaptions have the largest volatility errors. This is mainly because the calibrated model still produces too much short-expiry volatility. At short expiries, the annualised implied volatility is very sensitive to the local width of the simulated forward distribution, so the 1Y cells are a demanding test of the diffusion scale.

The longer-expiry swaptions look better in the MAE table, but the forward-bias diagnostic shows that this fit is partly misleading. In particular, the 10Y expiry cells have large positive forward biases. This means that the simulated forward swap rate is shifted above the initial ATM strike. The shift increases the straddle value and partly compensates for the fact that the model produces too little volatility at long expiries.

Thus, the smaller 10Y errors are not a clean success. They partly reflect cancellation between two errors: too little volatility and too much positive forward bias. Conversely, the large 1Y errors reflect the fact that short-expiry volatility remains too high after calibration.

The residual errors therefore reflect two limitations of the pricing layer. The diffusion scale \(s\) cannot reshape the volatility profile across expiries, and the drift shift \(\eta\) cannot centre all expiry--tenor cells at the same time.

\subsection{Structural limitation: the model volatility term structure}
\label{subsec:model_vol_term_structure}

The main limitation of the pricing layer is not only the forward-centering bias. Even when the bias is removed, the model produces the wrong term structure of swaption volatility. Table~\ref{tab:model_market_vol_term_structure} compares the scale of the simulated forward-swap-rate volatility with the market normal volatility level.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Model-implied forward-rate volatility scale versus market normal volatility. 
Values are reported in basis points.}
\label{tab:model_market_vol_term_structure}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Expiry & Model forward-rate volatility & Market normal volatility \\
\midrule
1Y  & 84--157 & 47--68 \\
5Y  & 45--47  & 71--72 \\
10Y & 33--36  & 68--71 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The pattern is clear. The model produces too much short-expiry volatility and too little long-expiry volatility. In other words, the volatility generated by the latent SDE decreases strongly with option expiry, whereas the market normal volatility surface is much flatter.

This cannot be fixed by the global diffusion scale \(s\). The scale \(s\) can move the overall volatility level up or down, but it cannot change the shape across expiries. For example, reducing \(s\) would help the 1Y swaptions, but would make the 5Y and 10Y swaptions even too low. Increasing \(s\) would help the 10Y swaptions, but would make the 1Y swaptions much too high. Hence the calibration faces a locked trade-off between short-expiry over-volatility and long-expiry under-volatility.

This suggests that the problem is inherited from the frozen stable model rather than from the small pricing layer. The latent dynamics were trained to fit the historical swap-rate curve, not the risk-neutral term structure of swaption variance. A stable mean-reverting model naturally tends to generate variance that saturates with time, which translates into a decreasing annualised volatility as expiry increases. The market swaption data require a much flatter term structure of volatility.

Therefore, the pricing layer should be interpreted as a diagnostic experiment. It shows that the latent representation contains useful information for pricing, but it does not contain the right risk-neutral variance structure. Matching the market swaption surface would require a richer pricing model, for example expiry-dependent diffusion scales, a recalibration of the drift, or a model trained directly on swaption volatilities.

For a mean-reverting latent process, the variance of the state tends to level off as the expiry grows. Since the reported normal volatility is annualised, this leads approximately to
\[
\sigma_N(T_\mu)
\approx
\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{Var}(R_{\mathrm{swap}}(T_\mu))}{T_\mu}},
\]
so a variance that saturates over time produces a volatility that decreases with expiry.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:pricing_summary}
%=========================================================

The pricing-layer experiment shows that a small number of additional pricing parameters can capture part of the EUR ATM swaption surface, but the fit remains limited. The layer has only \(8\) trainable parameters, given by a constant drift shift \(\eta\) and a per-factor diffusion scale \(s\), while the stable model itself is kept fixed. It reaches an overall MAE of \(40.1\) bp and an out-of-sample MAE of \(42.0\) bp. The small train--test gap suggests that the result is not mainly driven by overfitting, but the absolute error level is still too large for a fully satisfactory pricing model.

The largest residual errors occur for the 1Y expiry swaptions, especially the 1Y\(\times\)1Y swaption. These errors are partly explained by a persistent positive forward bias. At short expiries, the total diffusion scale \(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_\mu}\) is small, so a shift in the centre of the forward distribution has a large effect on the implied volatility. The forward-centering problem is therefore more damaging at 1Y expiry than at longer expiries.

The main limitation is internal consistency. The pricing setup uses the stable decoder for the initial curve and the adjusted decoder for the terminal payoff. A fully consistent version would instead use the adjusted decoder throughout and solve an inverse-decoder anchoring problem for the initial state \(z_0\). This would make the method more expensive, but it is the natural next step if the pricing layer is to become a genuine joint model for curves and swaptions.
