%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter examined whether the trained encoder--decoder model can be used as a stable forward scenario generator. We now use the same simulation framework for derivative pricing. In fixed-income applications, accurate reconstruction of the current swap curve is not sufficient: the value of an interest-rate derivative depends on the distribution of future discount factors, swap rates and short rates under the pricing measure.

The encoder--decoder framework provides a natural simulation-based pricing setup. Starting from an observed swap curve, the encoder gives an initial latent state. The learned risk-neutral dynamics are then simulated forward, and the decoder maps each simulated latent state into an arbitrage-free zero-coupon bond curve. This makes it possible to compute pathwise swap rates, annuities and discounted payoffs.

The chapter proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology} describes how European swaptions are priced by Monte Carlo using the simulated latent paths and decoded bond curves. Section~\ref{sec:why_recon_only_fails} diagnoses why the stable model cannot price without calibration, tracing the failure to the gap between the decoder's training manifold and the states the SDE visits at pricing horizons. Section~\ref{sec:hscale_calibration} introduces the diffusion-scale calibration, derives the OLS estimator, extends the calibration to a per-cell scheme, evaluates both on a held-out test period, and closes with a structural diagnosis of the residual pricing error. Section~\ref{sec:pricing_diagnostics} provides analytic ATM Greeks as a reference benchmark for interpreting model-implied sensitivities.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Swaption payoff and pathwise swap rate}
\label{subsec:swaption_payoff}

Having generated simulated latent paths and decoded zero-coupon bond curves, we can price interest-rate derivatives by Monte Carlo under the risk-neutral measure. This section presents the methodology for European swaptions.

Let \(T_e\) denote the option expiry, let \(T_s=n\delta\) denote the tenor of the underlying swap, and let \(\delta>0\) denote the fixed-leg accrual period. In the implementation, we use annual accrual, so \(\delta=1\). The fixed-leg payment dates are therefore
\[
T_e+\delta,\; T_e+2\delta,\; \dots,\; T_e+n\delta.
\]

The strike is determined from the time-zero discount curve. Given the decoded bond prices \(P(0,T)\), the swap annuity at time \(0\) is
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(0,T_e+j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding forward swap rate is
\[
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n\delta)}{\mathcal A_0}.
\]
For an at-the-money swaption, the strike is chosen as
\[
K = F_0.
\]

For each simulated path \(m=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the decoder delivers a full zero-coupon bond curve at expiry. Since the model is expressed in terms of time-to-maturity, this curve is written as
\[
\tau \mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau).
\]
The payment dates of the underlying swap correspond to the maturities
\[
\delta,\; 2\delta,\; \dots,\; n\delta
\]
measured from \(T_e\). The pathwise annuity at expiry is therefore
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\delta \sum_{j=1}^{n} P^{(m)}(T_e,j\delta),
\]
and the corresponding spot-starting swap rate is
\[
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n\delta)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]

Let \(\mathcal N>0\) denote the notional. The payer swaption payoff on path \(m\) is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K,0\bigr),
\]
while the receiver swaption payoff is
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal N\,\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
\max\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e),0\bigr).
\]

\subsection{Monte Carlo estimator and implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:mc_estimator_implied_vol}

The payoff is discounted back to time \(0\) using the pathwise bank account discount factor computed during simulation. Denoting this factor on path \(m\) by
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\left(-\int_0^{T_e} r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
the discounted present value on path \(m\) is
\[
V_0^{(m)}
=
D^{(m)}_{T_e} X^{(m)}_{T_e}.
\]
The time-zero swaption price is then estimated by the Monte Carlo average
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}} V_0^{(m)}.
\]

To assess numerical uncertainty, we also report the sample standard deviation
\[
\widehat \sigma_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}-1}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\left(V_0^{(m)}-\widehat V_0\right)^2
}
\]
and the associated Monte Carlo standard error
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
=
\frac{\widehat \sigma_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}}.
\]

In the implementation, the notional \(\mathcal N\) is set to 1 for simplicity. The time-zero quantities \(F_0\) and \(\mathcal A_0\) are computed directly from the decoded initial discount curve, while the pathwise quantities \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) are computed from the decoded discount curve at expiry on each simulated path. Because the pricing routine extracts bond prices directly from the model grids, the option expiry \(T_e\) must lie on the simulation time grid, while the payment dates \(j\delta\) must lie on the decoder maturity grid. In the numerical examples below, we consider in particular at-the-money payer swaptions, for which the strike is set equal to the time-zero forward swap rate.

We can finally summarise the pricing pipeline schematically as
\[
S_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
z_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
z^{(m)}_{T_e}
\;\longrightarrow\;
P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)
\;\longrightarrow\;
\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e),\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigr)
\;\longrightarrow\;
X^{(m)}_{T_e}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\widehat V_0
\;\longrightarrow\;
\widehat\sigma_N.
\]

\section{Bachelier Normal Volatility Framework}
\label{sec:bachelier_model}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Bachelier swaption formula}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

A European payer swaption with option expiry \(T_e\) and swap tenor \(T_s\) gives the holder the right to enter a fixed-for-floating swap at expiry. Under the annuity measure \(\mathbb{Q}^{\mathcal A}\), the forward swap rate \(F(t)\) is a martingale. In the Bachelier normal model \citep{Bachelier1900}, it evolves as
\begin{equation}
    dF(t) = \sigma_N\, dW^{\mathcal A}(t),
    \label{eq:bachelier_sde}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_N>0\) is the normal volatility. The normal model is convenient in low- and negative-rate environments because it allows the forward swap rate to take negative values.

Under~\eqref{eq:bachelier_sde}, the time-zero price of a payer swaption with notional \(\mathcal N\), strike \(K\), expiry \(T_e\), forward swap rate \(F_0\), and swap annuity \(\mathcal A_0\) is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{pay}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (F_0 - K)\,\Phi(d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr],
    \qquad
    d = \frac{F_0 - K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:bachelier_payer}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) and \(\phi\) denote the standard normal distribution function and density, respectively. For a receiver swaption, the corresponding price is
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{rec}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \Bigl[
        (K - F_0)\,\Phi(-d)
        +
        \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)
    \Bigr].
    \label{eq:bachelier_receiver}
\end{equation}

For an at-the-money swaption, \(K=F_0\), and hence \(d=0\). The payer and receiver prices coincide and reduce to
\begin{equation}
    V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0
    \frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
    \label{eq:bachelier_atm}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Market price targets and implied-volatility reporting}
\label{subsec:bachelier_two_roles}

The Bachelier formula serves two distinct roles in the implementation.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pricing target for the joint calibration.}
    Market swaption data are quoted as at-the-money normal volatilities \(\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\), typically in basis points. Before applying the formula, these volatilities are converted to decimal rate units. The market volatility is then converted into a price using the ATM formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_atm}:
    \begin{equation}
        V^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}
        =
        \mathcal N \mathcal A^{(i)}_0
        \frac{\sigma^{(i)}_{\mathrm{mkt}}\sqrt{T_e^{(i)}}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
        \label{eq:pricing_target}
    \end{equation}
    This gives a fixed price target computed once per market observation, using the annuity \(\mathcal A_0^{(i)}\) associated with the corresponding quote date. The model Monte Carlo price \(\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}^{(i)}\) is then trained to match this target.

    \item \textbf{Implied-volatility inversion for reporting.}
    After pricing, the model price is converted back into a normal implied volatility for comparison with market quotes. In the at-the-money case, the inversion is explicit:
    \begin{equation}
        \widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}
        =
        \frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
        {\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
        \label{eq:implied_vol}
    \end{equation}
    For off-ATM strikes, the Bachelier formula can instead be inverted numerically. Reporting prices in basis-point normal-volatility space makes model errors directly comparable to market quoting conventions.
\end{enumerate}

For a payer swaption, the model-implied Monte Carlo price is estimated by
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{V}^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
    \sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
    D^{(m)}_{T_e}\,
    \mathcal N\,
    \mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
    \max\!\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e) - K,\; 0\bigr).
    \label{eq:mc_price}
\end{equation}
Here \(D^{(m)}_{T_e}\) is the pathwise money-market discount factor, while \(\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\) and \(S^{(m)}(T_e)\) are the annuity and swap rate decoded from the bond curve at expiry. The implied normal volatility \(\widehat{\sigma}_{N,\mathrm{mod}}\) is then obtained by inserting \(\widehat{V}^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}\) into~\eqref{eq:implied_vol}.

\subsection{Analytic Greeks}
\label{sec:bachelier_greeks}

The following proposition collects the closed-form sensitivities of the Bachelier payer price~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. These Greeks are useful both for interpretation and as analytic benchmarks for numerical pricing diagnostics.

\begin{proposition}[Bachelier Greeks]
\label{prop:bachelier_greeks}
Let \(V=V^{\mathrm{pay}}\) be given by~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}, and define
\[
d=\frac{F_0-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
With the remaining variables fixed in each partial derivative, the payer swaption Greeks are
\begin{align}
    \Delta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial F_0}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d),
    \label{eq:delta}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vega}
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma_N}
    =
    \mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d),
    \label{eq:vega}
    \\
    \Gamma
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F_0^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:gamma}
    \\
    \Theta
    &=
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial T_e}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\sigma_N\,\phi(d)}
    {2\sqrt{T_e}},
    \label{eq:theta}
    \\
    \mathrm{DV01}
    &=
    \Delta \times 10^{-4}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,\Phi(d)}{10\,000},
    \label{eq:dv01}
    \\
    \mathrm{Vanna}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial F_0\,\partial\sigma_N}
    =
    -\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
    \label{eq:vanna}
    \\
    \mathrm{Volga}
    &=
    \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial\sigma_N^2}
    =
    \frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}
    {\sigma_N}.
    \label{eq:volga}
\end{align}
For the receiver swaption,
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0(\Phi(d)-1)
=
-\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\Phi(-d),
\]
while Vega, Gamma, Theta, Vanna and Volga are the same as for the payer. Put-call parity implies
\[
\Delta^{\mathrm{pay}}-\Delta^{\mathrm{rec}}=\mathcal N \mathcal A_0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The result follows by direct differentiation of~\eqref{eq:bachelier_payer}. For Delta, differentiating with respect to \(F_0\) and noting that \(\partial d/\partial F_0 = 1/(\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e})\), the term involving \(\phi(d)\cdot\partial d/\partial F_0\) cancels exactly across the two summands, leaving \(\Delta = \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\Phi(d)\). For Vega, \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N = -d/\sigma_N\), giving
\[
\frac{\partial V}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\!\left[\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d) + \bigl((F_0-K)\Phi'(d) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}\phi'(d)\bigr)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}\right].
\]
Using \(\Phi'(d)=\phi(d)\), \(\phi'(d)=-d\phi(d)\) and \(\partial d/\partial\sigma_N = -d/\sigma_N\), the bracket in the parentheses is
\(\phi(d)(-d/\sigma_N)(F_0-K) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}(-d\phi(d))(-d/\sigma_N) = 0\),
leaving \(\mathrm{Vega} = \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi(d)\). Gamma and Theta follow from differentiating Delta and from \(\partial d/\partial T_e = -d/(2T_e)\), respectively. Vanna and Volga use the same two identities:
\[
\mathrm{Vanna}
= \frac{\partial\Delta}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,\phi(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
= -\frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\,d\,\phi(d)}{\sigma_N},
\qquad
\mathrm{Volga}
= \frac{\partial\,\mathrm{Vega}}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,\phi'(d)\frac{\partial d}{\partial\sigma_N}
= \frac{\mathcal N\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}\,d^2\phi(d)}{\sigma_N}.
\]
\end{proof}

\paragraph{ATM simplifications.}
At the money, \(K=F_0\), so \(d=0\). Since \(\Phi(0)=1/2\) and \(\phi(0)=(2\pi)^{-1/2}\), the payer Greeks simplify to
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac12\mathcal N \mathcal A_0,
\qquad
\mathrm{Vega}^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sqrt{\frac{T_e}{2\pi}},
\qquad
\Gamma^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0}{\sigma_N\sqrt{2\pi T_e}},
\qquad
\Theta^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\frac{\mathcal N \mathcal A_0\sigma_N}{2\sqrt{2\pi T_e}}.
\label{eq:atm_greeks}
\end{equation}
Vanna and Volga vanish at the money.

\paragraph{Theta--Gamma identity.}
Combining~\eqref{eq:gamma} and~\eqref{eq:theta} gives
\begin{equation}
    \Theta = \frac12\sigma_N^2\Gamma.
    \label{eq:theta_gamma}
\end{equation}
This identity holds for the partial derivative with respect to time to expiry, holding \(F_0\), \(\mathcal A_0\), \(K\), and \(\sigma_N\) fixed. It provides a useful analytic consistency check for the Bachelier formulas, although it is not a full calendar-time theta for a dynamic term-structure model, where the annuity and forward swap rate also evolve.

\section{Why the Stable Model Cannot Price Without Calibration}
\label{sec:why_recon_only_fails}
%=========================================================

The encoder--decoder checkpoints from Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} were trained to minimise reconstruction error on observed swap curves. Reconstruction is necessary but not sufficient for derivative pricing: pricing requires that the decoder produce sensible discount curves not just at observed inputs but at every latent state the simulated SDE visits. This section assembles the empirical evidence that recon-only training fails this requirement, and identifies the two mechanisms responsible.

\paragraph{The pricing pipeline as a probe.}
The pricing pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology} can be read as a stress test of the trained model. From an encoded $z_0$, Euler--Maruyama is run forward to expiry to obtain $z_T$, and the decoder maps $z_T$ to a discount curve. Recon-only training optimises the decoder only on the manifold $\{\mathrm{encoder}(x) : x \in \mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{train}}\}$, which is a measure-zero subset of $\mathbb{R}^\ell$. Anything outside this manifold is uncharted territory for the decoder, and Brownian increments push $z_T$ off the manifold immediately.

\paragraph{Decoder robustness to off-manifold inputs.}
We probe each decoder by perturbing encoded points $z = z_0 + \varepsilon \eta$, $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_\ell)$, and measuring the fraction of decoded curves that are fully finite. Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:decoder_robustness} show the result for the dim4 checkpoints. Every decoder handles $\varepsilon = 0$ perfectly; robustness degrades sharply by $\varepsilon \approx 0.5$. Notably, the stable variant is uniformly \emph{more} robust than its baseline counterpart of the same dimension --- the stable decoder generalises better off-manifold. But neither generalises far enough for pricing.

\paragraph{Where the SDE actually goes.}
Whether the failure regime is reached during simulation depends on the SDE's displacement distribution. Simulating $1{,}000$ Euler--Maruyama paths to $T_e = 5$Y reveals two qualitatively different pathologies (Figure~\ref{fig:latent_displacement_cdf} and Table~\ref{tab:displacement_summary}). The baseline SDE diverges to $\|z_T - z_0\| \sim 10^{13}$ --- at those magnitudes the decoder extrapolates to finite-but-meaningless outputs, producing the large pricing errors observed. The stable SDE, by contrast, stays at $\|z_T - z_0\| \approx 2$, which sits exactly in the regime where Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_robustness} predicts failure --- and the decoder rejects those inputs with NaNs roughly half the time.

\paragraph{$\mathcal{H}$ versus $\mathcal{K}$: where the displacement comes from.}
Decomposing the displacement into diffusion-only and drift-only contributions localises the pathology. With $\mathcal{H} = 0$, the baseline drift alone grows exponentially --- the linear part of $\mathcal{K}$ has positive eigenvalues, and the unstable eigenvector absorbs the entire trajectory. The stable variant, having all-negative eigenvalues by construction, keeps the drift-only displacement bounded. For stable, the realised displacement is therefore essentially the diffusion contribution, partially damped by the mean-reverting drift acting as a restoring force. Table~\ref{tab:hk_decomposition} reports the two contributions separately.

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_hk_decomposition}

\paragraph{An apparent paradox in the stable variant.}
The stable equilibrium $z^* = -M^{-1}N_{\mathcal{K}}$ for dim4 sits roughly $\|z^* - z_0\| \approx 9.5$ from a typical encoded EUR curve. One would expect strong mean-reverting drift to dominate the displacement, yet the drift-only contribution at five years is only $\approx 0.14$. The resolution is that $(z^* - z_0)$ is almost entirely aligned with the \emph{slowest} eigenvector of $\mathcal{K}$, which has timescale $1/|\lambda| \sim 600$ years. Over five years, $|1 - e^{\lambda T}|$ in that direction is below $0.01$. Table~\ref{tab:eigenvector_alignment} shows the projection breakdown: the large structural misplacement sits in the direction that the time-series fit cannot constrain, and its dynamic effect at pricing-relevant horizons is negligible.

\input{Figures/Pricing/tab_eigenvector_alignment}

\paragraph{Summary.}
The stable model fails at pricing for a concrete reason: the decoder was never trained to decode states at distance $\|z_T - z_0\| \approx 2$, which is where the well-behaved stable SDE sends simulated paths. The failure is not a flaw in the SDE design --- the bounded displacement is exactly what was intended --- but a gap between the reconstruction manifold and the simulation range. Closing this gap by retraining the diffusion is architecturally blocked, for reasons explained in Section~\ref{sec:hscale_calibration}. The solution is to calibrate a scalar multiplier on the diffusion output, which simultaneously brings simulated paths within the decoder's working domain and matches model-implied volatilities to market levels.

\section{Diffusion-Scale Calibration}
\label{sec:hscale_calibration}
%=========================================================

The encoder--decoder model from Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} was trained to minimise swap-curve reconstruction error. When the same model is used for swaption pricing without modification, it systematically over-prices: at $s=1$ the model-implied normal volatilities are $4$--$11\times$ market vols across the nine expiry--tenor cells, reflecting the fact that $\mathcal{H}$ was calibrated to match the yield-curve cross-section rather than market derivative prices. A direct fix --- retraining $\mathcal{K}$ or $\mathcal{H}$ jointly against swaption vols --- is architecturally blocked: both networks appear in the no-arbitrage ODE that computes $A_{z_t}(\tau)$ and $B_{z_t}(\tau)$, so any gradient update to them simultaneously changes the reconstructed bond prices. There is, however, one free parameter that enters the simulation without touching the ODE: a global scalar $s$ applied to the diffusion output at simulation time. If the Euler step uses $s \cdot \mathcal{H}(z_t)$ instead of $\mathcal{H}(z_t)$, the latent paths change while $A(\tau)$, $B(\tau)$, and all reconstructed bond prices remain exactly as trained. Calibrating this scalar to market swaption volatilities is the approach adopted here.

\subsection{Calibration procedure}
\label{subsec:hscale_procedure}

For a swaption with market normal volatility $\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}$ and model-implied normal volatility $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(s)$ at diffusion scale $s$, the at-the-money Bachelier formula~\eqref{eq:bachelier_atm} gives
\[
    \widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}(s) \propto s \cdot \widehat V_{\mathrm{MC}}(1),
\]
since scaling the diffusion by $s$ scales all increments $\Delta z$ by $s$, which scales the terminal distribution's spread by $s$, and hence scales the ATM swaption price linearly in $s$ to first order. The model vol at scale $s$ is therefore approximately $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(s) \approx s \cdot \sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)$.

This linearity motivates a simple ordinary least-squares calibration. We first price all available swaptions at $s = 1$ to obtain $\{\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\}$. We then estimate the optimal scale by minimising the sum of squared vol errors in basis points:
\begin{equation}
    s^* = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{s > 0}
    \sum_{j:\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}>0}
    \bigl(s \cdot \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1) - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}\bigr)^2,
    \label{eq:hscale_objective}
\end{equation}
which has the closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
    s^* = \frac{\sum_j \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},j}}
               {\sum_j \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},j}(1)^2}.
    \label{eq:hscale_ols}
\end{equation}
The scale $s^*$ is then applied as a \texttt{diffusion\_scale} argument to the Euler--Maruyama simulator; no weight in $\mathcal H$ is modified for pricing. For the saved calibrated checkpoint, the last linear layer of $\mathcal H$ is scaled by $s^*$ (equivalent to baking the scale into the weights), and the reconstruction RMSE with the scaled weights is measured explicitly as a diagnostic of the ODE coupling cost.

\subsection{Calibration results}
\label{subsec:hscale_results}

The calibration was run on $1{,}246$ EUR ATM swaptions with path survival $100\%$ at $s = 1$. The OLS estimator gives $s^* = 0.151$: the diffusion implied by the reconstruction-calibrated $\mathcal{H}$ weights is approximately $6.6\times$ larger than needed to match market swaption implied volatilities. Table~\ref{tab:hscale_results} summarises the vol errors before and after calibration, and Table~\ref{tab:hscale_per_cell} shows the breakdown by expiry and tenor.

Two observations follow from Table~\ref{tab:hscale_per_cell}. First, the model systematically over-prices volatility at every expiry--tenor cell: at $s = 1$, model vols are $2\times$ to $11\times$ market vols, confirming that the reconstruction-calibrated diffusion is far above the level implied by market swaption prices. Second, the ratio $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)/\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}$ is not constant across the grid: it is highest at short expiries (10.7$\times$ at 1Y$\times$1Y) and lowest at long expiries (4.2$\times$ at 10Y$\times$10Y). A single scale factor $s^*$ can correct the average level but not the shape of the volatility term structure. The residual errors after calibration therefore retain a systematic structure: the 1Y$\times$1Y cell has a $202$ bp error while the 10Y$\times$10Y cell has a $50$ bp error. This shape mismatch is a genuine limitation of the one-parameter calibration and reflects the fact that the model's volatility term structure --- determined by how $\mathcal H(z)$ varies across the simulated latent path --- does not match the market's term structure shape.

The reconstruction cost of baking $s^*$ into the $\mathcal H$ weights is $45.9$ bp RMSE (versus $5.3$ bp for the unscaled model), confirming the ODE coupling: scaling $\mathcal H$'s last layer by $0.151$ changes the ODE diffusion coefficient and hence every reconstructed bond price.

A further, initially unexpected consequence of the ODE coupling is that the calibrated checkpoint (with scaled $\mathcal H$ weights) does not reproduce the 91 bp vol MAE when used for pricing. When the scaled checkpoint is loaded and priced with \texttt{diffusion\_scale}$=1.0$, the vol MAE is $278$ bp rather than $91$ bp. The reason is that the scaled $\mathcal H$ changes the ODE coefficients used for both the time-$0$ bond prices (which determine the strike $K = F_0$) and the at-expiry bond prices (which determine $S^{(m)}(T_e)$). The strike and the payoff are therefore both shifted by the ODE change, and the resulting swaption prices no longer reflect a simple $s^*$-rescaling of the diffusion. The correct way to apply the calibration is to use the original (unscaled) checkpoint with \texttt{diffusion\_scale}$= s^*$ passed to the simulator at pricing time. This leaves all ODE computations unchanged and reproduces the 91 bp vol MAE exactly. The calibrated checkpoint with scaled weights is provided as a convenience artefact for inspection but should not be used directly for pricing without the corresponding ODE correction.

\subsection{Per-cell calibration and out-of-sample validation}
\label{subsec:per_cell_calibration}

The single global scale $s^* = 0.151$ corrects the average level of model-implied volatilities but cannot correct the \emph{shape}: the ratio $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)/\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}$ varies from roughly $11\times$ at the 1Y$\times$1Y cell to $4\times$ at the 10Y$\times$10Y cell. We therefore extend the calibration to nine independent scale factors, one per (expiry, tenor) cell:
\begin{equation}
    s^*(e,t)
    =
    \frac{\sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}} \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},d}(1;\, e,t)\,\sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},d}(e,t)}
         {\sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}} \sigma_{\mathrm{mod},d}(1;\, e,t)^2},
    \label{eq:per_cell_ols}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal D_{\mathrm{train}}$ is the training set of dates and the sum excludes dates with zero market vol (missing data). This is the same OLS formula as~\eqref{eq:hscale_ols}, applied independently to each cell using only training-period data.

\paragraph{Train/test split.}
All 170 available EUR quote dates are sorted chronologically. The first 70\% (105 dates, October 2010 -- June 2019) form the training window; the remaining 30\% (46 dates, July 2019 -- February 2024) are a held-out out-of-sample test. Crucially, the scale factors $s^*(e,t)$ are fitted \emph{exclusively} on training dates; the test period, which includes the COVID-19 volatility spike of 2020 and the rate-hiking cycle of 2022--23, was never seen during calibration.

\paragraph{Fitted scale factors.}
Table~\ref{tab:per_cell_scales} reports the nine fitted scale factors. All nine values lie in the narrow band $[0.10, 0.18]$, close to the global OLS estimate of $0.151$. The largest factor is at 10Y$\times$10Y ($0.183$) and the smallest at 1Y$\times$10Y ($0.098$). There is no clean monotone pattern across the grid: the correction required in each cell is driven by both the local vol level and the model's sensitivity in that region of state space.

\input{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_stable_hscale/per_cell/tab_per_cell_scales}

\paragraph{R-squared and explanatory power.}
The $R^2$ of the origin-constrained regression~\eqref{eq:per_cell_ols} ranges from $0.11$ to $0.33$ across the nine cells (Table~\ref{tab:per_cell_mae}). These values are low: the model's time-series variation in $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)$ explains only $11$--$33\%$ of the variance in market vols. The remainder reflects market volatility dynamics (regime changes, risk premia, etc.) that the encoder --- calibrated to cross-sectional swap-curve reconstruction rather than to derivative prices --- cannot capture. This is an important limitation: the per-cell scales correct the \emph{average level} of each cell, but the model cannot track day-to-day or year-to-year fluctuations in the market vol surface.

\paragraph{In-sample and OOS results.}
Table~\ref{tab:per_cell_mae} and Figures~\ref{fig:per_cell_train}--\ref{fig:per_cell_oos} report the vol MAE per cell for the training and test periods. The MC prices at cell-specific scales are computed honestly (not under the linearity approximation), so the table reflects actual pricing errors rather than a best-case linear fit.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The per-cell calibration addresses the shape limitation of the global scale, and the OOS test answers whether the fitted factors hold up post-calibration. The aggregate results are: in-sample MAE $83$ bp (RMSE $99$ bp), OOS MAE $73$ bp (RMSE $109$ bp), overall $81$ bp --- compared to $91$ bp for the global single-scale calibration. The per-cell extension yields only a modest $10$ bp aggregate improvement over the one-parameter approach, which already reflects the low $R^2$ (0.11--0.33): because the model's time-series variation in $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)$ explains only a small fraction of the market vol variance, adding nine cell-specific intercepts cannot buy much beyond what the single global scale provides.

The cell-level pattern is more revealing. Six of the nine cells show OOS MAE \emph{smaller} than or comparable to their training MAE, indicating that the fitted scale factors are stable across market regimes: 5Y$\times$5Y ($61$ bp train, $59$ bp OOS), 5Y$\times$10Y ($54$ vs.\ $56$ bp), 10Y$\times$1Y ($89$ vs.\ $45$ bp), 10Y$\times$5Y ($66$ vs.\ $43$ bp), and 10Y$\times$10Y ($67$ vs.\ $53$ bp) all show minimal degradation or even improvement on the held-out period, which covers COVID-19 and the 2022--23 rate-hiking cycle.

Two cells stand out as problematic. The 1Y$\times$1Y cell has MAE $209$ bp in-sample and $161$ bp out-of-sample: the per-cell correction reduces the error relative to the global scale ($202$ bp from Table~\ref{tab:hscale_per_cell}) but leaves a residual of more than one-and-a-half percentage points, indicating a structural limitation of the model for very short expiries. The 1Y$\times$10Y cell is the most concerning: it achieves the lowest in-sample MAE of any cell ($23$ bp) but the second-highest OOS MAE ($144$ bp, RMSE $229$ bp). The fitted scale $s^* = 0.098$ --- appreciably smaller than any other cell --- over-fitted to the training data, and the large OOS error is a textbook sign of this. The extremely low in-sample error combined with $R^2 = 0.24$ suggests that the OLS closed-form happened to fit the training observations well on average, while providing no useful signal about the underlying risk-neutral dynamics.

These findings clarify the appropriate use of the per-cell calibration. For mid- to long-expiry cells (5Y and 10Y), the calibrated scale factors appear robust and the pricing accuracy is sufficient for indicative use. For 1Y expiry cells, the framework has genuine limitations: the 1Y$\times$10Y cell requires close monitoring and possible recalibration as market conditions evolve, and the 1Y$\times$1Y cell may require a structural extension (e.g.\ a richer diffusion parameterisation) rather than a simple scale correction.

\subsection{Calibration limits and structural diagnosis}
\label{subsec:calibration_improvements}

\paragraph{A better calibration: expiry-level OLS.}
The diffusion scale $s$ enters the model at the \emph{simulation} stage: it multiplies $\mathcal{H}(z_t)$ in the SDE solved over the $T_e$-year option horizon. It does not enter the payoff calculation, which depends on the tenor of the underlying swap. This asymmetry motivates fitting one scale per expiry row --- pooling all three tenor columns --- rather than one per cell. The result is three parameters rather than nine:
\[
  s^*(1\mathrm{Y}) = 0.129, \qquad
  s^*(5\mathrm{Y}) = 0.133, \qquad
  s^*(10\mathrm{Y}) = 0.141.
\]
These are strikingly uniform: the scale varies by only $0.012$ across the three expiry horizons, compared with a range of $[0.098, 0.183]$ for the nine per-cell estimates, which reflects genuine pooling. The OOS MAE is $72$ bp (RMSE $105$ bp), matching or marginally improving on the nine-parameter per-cell result of $73$ bp, while also reducing in-sample MAE from $83$ bp to $78$ bp. Table~\ref{tab:expiry_comparison} gives the cell-level breakdown. This is the recommended calibration: physically motivated, parsimonious, and robust.

\input{Figures/TrainingResults/dim4_stable_hscale/expiry_scale/tab_expiry_comparison}

\paragraph{Why the residual error is structural, not a calibration problem.}
The expiry-level and per-cell scales both converge near $72$--$73$ bp OOS MAE, and even daily recalibration --- setting $s_t = \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt},t}/\sigma_{\mathrm{mod},t}(1)$ using each OOS date's \emph{own observed vol} as the target, an unrealisable lower bound --- yields an essentially identical $74$ bp. The flat ceiling across all these approaches points to a structural origin rather than a calibration one.

The origin lies in how $\mathcal{K}$ was identified. The model derives no-arbitrage bond prices from the $\mathbb{Q}$-measure SDE
\[
  dz_t = \mathcal{K}(z_t)\,dt + \mathcal{H}(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where $\mathcal{K}$ appears in the ODE coefficients $\alpha_{z_t}(\tau)$ and thereby shapes the term structure of decoded bond prices. In training, $\mathcal{K}$ is identified by minimising the cross-sectional swap-rate reconstruction loss~\eqref{eq:model_estimation_loss}. It is selected to make the yield curve \emph{shape} fit well at each date in isolation, not to satisfy the risk-neutral martingale condition $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_{\mathcal{A}}}[S_T] = F_0$ (the requirement that the forward swap rate is a martingale under the annuity numeraire). As a result, when the SDE is integrated forward to price a swaption, the distribution of $S_T$ under the model's $\mathbb{Q}$ is not centred on $F_0$: the model assigns non-zero expected rate changes over the option horizon that cannot be removed by any diffusion-scale correction.

\paragraph{The drift floor as a diagnostic.}
This mis-calibration of $\mathcal{K}$ is directly measurable. Pricing all swaptions at near-zero diffusion ($s = 0.01$) eliminates the stochastic component and leaves only the deterministic contribution of $\mathcal{K}$. Any non-zero implied vol at $s \to 0$ --- the \emph{drift floor} --- reveals that $\mathcal{K}$ alone moves $z$ in a direction that creates intrinsic swaption value. Table~\ref{tab:drift_floor} reports the average drift floor per cell over training dates.

The 1Y$\times$1Y cell is the most extreme case: $\mathcal{K}(z)$ moves $z$ so strongly toward its learned equilibrium $z^*$ over a one-year horizon that the implied vol from the drift alone averages $133$ bp, more than twice the market vol of $62$ bp, and exceeds the market on one in four training dates. For the remaining cells the drift floor is small relative to market vol, so the diffusion scale can bridge the gap; the persistent 1Y$\times$1Y error ($161$ bp OOS) is therefore a ceiling set by the drift, not a failure of the scale search. More broadly, across all cells, the $\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}$-martingale condition is not satisfied by the model: $\mathcal{K}$ was never calibrated to market derivative prices, only to yield-curve cross-sections.

\paragraph{Ridge regularisation and temporal over-fitting.}
The 1Y$\times$10Y cell achieves the lowest in-sample MAE ($23$ bp) but the second-highest OOS MAE ($144$ bp), a textbook over-fit. Ridge regression with leave-one-year-out cross-validation was applied to shrink its scale towards the global estimate $s^* = 0.151$. The CV selected $\lambda \approx 0$ --- identical to plain OLS --- for every cell. The reason is instructive: the within-training fit of $s^* = 0.098$ is not a statistical artefact but a genuine property of the 2010--2019 data; the problem is that the OOS period 2019--2024 represents a different vol regime. Ridge regularises \emph{within-sample} variance, not \emph{temporal} regime shifts, so it cannot detect or correct this kind of extrapolation failure.

\paragraph{A direct test of the $\mathcal{K}$ mis-calibration.}
If the residual error were driven primarily by a directional drift bias in $\mathcal{K}$ --- i.e.\ $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}}[S_T] \neq F_0$ pulling rates systematically in one direction --- then pricing the \emph{straddle} $(V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}})/2$ instead of the payer alone should cancel the bias and improve fit. The straddle implied vol,
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{str}} = \frac{V_{\mathrm{pay}} + V_{\mathrm{rec}}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\mathcal{A}_0 \sqrt{T_e}},
\]
equals the payer vol under a correctly specified $\mathbb{Q}$ model, but under a biased model the averaging removes the directional component. The experiment was run: the OOS MAE jumped from $75$ bp (payer OLS) to $287$ bp (straddle). The reason is that the forward bias $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}}[S_T] - F_0$ is not uniformly positive --- it is strongly positive for some cells (e.g.\ $+239$ bp for 5Y$\times$1Y) and strongly negative for others (e.g.\ $-401$ bp for 1Y$\times$5Y). When the bias is negative, the receiver payoff is massively inflated and the straddle is far worse than the payer alone. This confirms that the residual error does not arise from a single-direction drift bias but from a more complex mis-calibration of $\mathcal{K}$ across the grid, and that the $\mathbb{Q}_\mathcal{A}$-martingale condition is violated inconsistently across cells. Table~\ref{tab:straddle_comparison} gives the cell-level breakdown.

\paragraph{Implications and the path to improvement.}
The $\mathcal{H}$ weights are fixed after reconstruction training and do not respond to changes in the market vol regime. When market vols nearly double during the 2022--23 hiking cycle, $\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}(1)$ barely moves, so any OLS scale estimate is regime-dependent. A 12-month rolling OLS and a scale conditioned on the latent state $z_t$ were both tried; both produced OOS MAEs above $95$ bp, substantially worse than the fixed expiry-level estimate: the scale signal is too noisy and too non-linear for short-window estimation to help.

Genuine improvement requires calibrating the dynamics to derivative prices, not swap curves. Two routes are conceivable. The first is to retrain $\mathcal{K}$ and $\mathcal{H}$ on a joint objective that includes swaption vols, enforcing the martingale condition for $S_T$; this is architecturally infeasible because both networks appear in the reconstruction ODE, so any gradient step on them corrupts the cross-sectional bond pricing. The second is to introduce a stochastic vol factor that allows $\mathcal{H}(z_t)$ to track the vol regime; this is a natural model extension but requires a separate calibration infrastructure outside the current framework.
