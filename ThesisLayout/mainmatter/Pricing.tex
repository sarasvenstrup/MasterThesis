%=========================================================
\chapter{Simulation-Based Swaption Pricing and Calibration}
\label{chap:pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

The previous chapter established that the encoder--decoder model produces stable latent dynamics suitable for scenario generation. We now ask a harder question: can the same model be used to price swaptions? This requires the \emph{risk-neutral} distribution of future swap rates, which is a fundamentally different object from the historical cross-section the model was trained on.

%=========================================================
\section{Monte Carlo Swaption Pricing}
\label{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}
%=========================================================

We consider European swaptions \citep{Filipovic2009} written on the single-curve swap rates introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:interest_rate_swaps}. The option expires at \(T_e\) and gives the holder the right to enter a swap running from \(T_e\) to \(T_e+n\), with annual fixed-leg payment dates \(T_e+1, T_e+2, \ldots, T_e+n\).

For an at-the-money (ATM) swaption the strike equals the time-zero forward swap rate. Given the discount curve \(P(0,T)\), the forward annuity and forward swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A_0
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}P(0,T_e+j),
\qquad
F_0
=
\frac{P(0,T_e)-P(0,T_e+n)}{\mathcal A_0},
\]
so the ATM strike is \(K = F_0\).

On each Monte Carlo path \(m=1,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{MC}}\), the latent state is simulated to the option expiry \(T_e\) using a fine time grid with step \(dt \ll 1\). The decoder then produces the time-\(T_e\) zero-coupon bond curve \(\tau\mapsto P^{(m)}(T_e,\tau)\), from which the pathwise annuity and swap rate are
\[
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}P^{(m)}(T_e,j),
\qquad
S^{(m)}(T_e)
=
\frac{1-P^{(m)}(T_e,n)}
{\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)}.
\]
The payer and receiver swaption payoffs are
\[
X^{(m),\mathrm{pay}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigl(S^{(m)}(T_e)-K\bigr)^+,
\qquad
X^{(m),\mathrm{rec}}_{T_e}
=
\mathcal A^{(m)}(T_e)\bigl(K-S^{(m)}(T_e)\bigr)^+,
\]
where we set notional \(\mathcal N = 1\). Each payoff is discounted by the pathwise money-market factor
\[
D^{(m)}_{T_e}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^{T_e}r_s^{(m)}\,ds\right),
\]
approximated on the simulation grid by the trapezoidal rule (Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}). The Monte Carlo price estimator \citep{Glasserman2004} is
\[
\widehat V_0
=
\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
\sum_{m=1}^{N_{\mathrm{MC}}}
D^{(m)}_{T_e}X^{(m)}_{T_e}.
\]

\subsection{Bachelier implied normal volatility}
\label{subsec:bachelier_formula}

Market swaption quotes are reported as normal \citep{Bachelier1900} implied volatilities \(\sigma_N\). Under the Bachelier model the forward swap rate satisfies \(dF(t)=\sigma_N\,dW^{\mathcal A}(t)\) under the annuity measure \citep[Ch.~26]{Bjork2020}, giving the ATM price
\[
V^{\mathrm{ATM}}
=
\mathcal A_0
\frac{\sigma_N\sqrt{T_e}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}.
\]
The normal convention is standard in low- and negative-rate environments because it permits negative rates \citep{Antonov2015}. To convert a Monte Carlo price into an implied vol we invert this relation.

To decouple the vol level from any directional bias, we price both a payer and a receiver at the ATM strike and average:
\[
\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}
=
\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
+
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
\right),
\qquad
\widehat{\sigma}_{N}
=
\frac{\widehat V_{\mathrm{str}}\sqrt{2\pi}}
{\mathcal A_0\sqrt{T_e}}.
\]
This \emph{straddle vol} eliminates the effect of the distribution being off-centre: if the model shifts the forward rate up or down, both payer and receiver prices move in opposite directions and the average is unchanged at first order. All vols are reported in basis points.

We also track the \emph{forward centering bias}
\[
\widehat b
=
\frac{
\widehat V^{\mathrm{pay}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
-
\widehat V^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathrm{MC}}
}
{\mathcal A_0},
\]
which should be close to zero at the ATM strike for a correctly centred model.

\section{Pricing with the Stable Model}
\label{sec:pricing_stable_model}
%=========================================================

The stable model from Chapter~\ref{chap:model} is written entirely under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\),
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
and the no-arbitrage condition is enforced by construction through the bond-pricing ODE. In principle this is all that is needed to price options: simulate latent paths under the stable dynamics, decode to bond curves at expiry, and compute the Monte Carlo estimator of Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}. There is no measure change to perform. The natural first experiment is therefore to ask whether the stable model, used directly, prices swaptions accurately.

\subsection{Empirical results}
\label{subsec:stable_pricing_results}

We price all available EUR ATM swaption cells using the stable model directly, without any adjustment to its dynamics. The model parameters --- the drift \(\mu\), diffusion \(\sigma\), short rate \(r\) and decoder \(G\) --- are held fixed at the values obtained from the reconstruction calibration in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}. We refer to this configuration as the \emph{frozen stable model}: only the stochastic Brownian increments vary across Monte Carlo paths, while all neural-network parameters remain constant. The simulation uses \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 512\) antithetic\footnote{Antithetic variates is a standard Monte Carlo variance-reduction technique \citep{Glasserman2004}. For each randomly drawn path with Brownian increment vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), a mirrored path with \(-\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) is also simulated. The two paths are then averaged. This eliminates the variance contribution from the linear part of the payoff and approximately halves the Monte Carlo standard error at unchanged computational cost.} paths per cell and the time grid described in Section~\ref{sec:swaption_pricing_methodology}.

A latent-state model that reconstructs curves accurately is therefore not, by itself, a swaption-pricing model.

\subsection{How could that be?}
\label{subsec:stable_pricing_issue}

The size of this error is striking. It is worth pausing to understand why an arbitrage-free model that fits swap curves to within tens of basis points produces option-pricing errors two orders of magnitude larger. The reason is a calibration mismatch. The parameters of the drift, diffusion and decoder networks --- \(\mu\), \(\sigma\) and \(G\) --- were chosen to minimise the curve reconstruction loss
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}
= \mathbb{E}\!\left[\bigl\|\mathbf{S} - \hat{\mathbf{S}}(z)\bigr\|^2\right],
\]
which measures how accurately the decoder reproduces today's observed swap curve. This objective contains no information about the \emph{distribution of future} swap rates and no information about option markets. A drift and diffusion that fit curve cross-sections well are not guaranteed to reproduce the option-implied risk-neutral distribution of \(F_{T_e}\).

Two distinct problems arise, both visible in the table above:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Wrong volatility level.} The diffusion \(\sigma(z)\) and drift \(\mu(z)\) together determine the spread of the distribution of \(F_{T_e}\) under \(\mathbb{Q}\). Since neither was calibrated against option prices, the resulting implied vol can differ substantially from market-observed levels. The large positive vol errors in the table are a direct expression of this mismatch.
    \item \textbf{Wrong forward centering.} The drift \(\mu(z)\) is calibrated to reconstruct the current curve, not to centre the simulated forward swap rate at \(F_0\). Paths drift away from the ATM forward, producing the strongly negative forward bias \(\widehat b\) reported in the table.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the chapter responds to these two problems in two steps. The first introduces a minimal trainable pricing layer that recalibrates the risk-neutral dynamics while keeping the stable model frozen. The second augments the pricing layer with information from the observed swaption volatility surface.

\section{Constant Market Price of Risk}
\label{sec:constant_mpr}
%=========================================================

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{subsec:cmpr_architecture}

We introduce a minimal trainable pricing layer on top of the frozen stable model. It recalibrates the risk-neutral drift \(\mu(z)\) by adding a learnable constant correction in the diffusion direction:
\[
\boxed{
\mu^*(z) = \mu(z) + \sigma(z)\,\lambda_0,
}
\]
where \(\lambda_0 \in \mathbb{R}^d\) is a learnable constant vector and \(\mu^*(z)\) is the option-calibrated drift. Since the model is already under \(\mathbb{Q}\), this is not a change of measure but a recalibration of the risk-neutral dynamics. The adjusted SDE is
\[
dz_t = \mu^*(z_t)\,dt + \mathrm{diag}(s)\,\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
with per-factor diffusion scales \(s \in \mathbb{R}^d_{>0}\) also learnable. All base model parameters are frozen. The model has \(2d = 8\) trainable parameters for \(d = 4\).

The vector \(\lambda_0\) has a natural interpretation as a \emph{market price of risk}: it quantifies the additional drift --- beyond what the reconstruction-calibrated \(\mu(z)\) provides --- that the risk-neutral measure requires in order for the simulated distribution of \(F_{T_e}\) to match option prices. The correction \(\sigma(z)\lambda_0\) rotates \(\lambda_0\) through the diffusion geometry so that the adjustment is aligned with the directions of uncertainty in the latent space.

The key design choice is that \(\lambda_0\) is \emph{constant}: it does not depend on the current latent state \(z_t\). A state-dependent correction \(\sigma(z)\Lambda z_t\) would grow as \(z_t\) deviates from its starting point under diffusion noise, creating a positive feedback loop through the decoder's Jacobian that produces explosive implied vols for short-expiry options. With a constant \(\lambda_0\) the correction is uniform across the latent space and no such amplification can occur.

\subsection{Calibration}
\label{subsec:cmpr_calibration}

Calibration minimises a straddle vol loss plus a forward-centering penalty:
\[
\mathcal{L}
= \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
\left(
\frac{\hat\sigma_{N}^{(i)} - \sigma_{\mathrm{mkt}}^{(i)}}{100}
\right)^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vol}}}
+\;
0.5\underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
\left(\frac{\hat b^{(i)}}{100}\right)^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bias}}}
+\;
10^{-3}\,\|\lambda_0\|^2,
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}\) is a mini-batch of randomly sampled EUR swaption observations. Optimisation uses Adam with linear warmup and cosine annealing over \(1000\) epochs, with \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 512\) antithetic paths per observation.

\label{subsec:cmpr_results}

Table~\ref{tab:constant_mpr_per_cell_all} reports per-cell errors. The overall MAE falls from \(414.7\) bp (base model) to \(\mathbf{40.1}\) bp — a reduction of over \(90\%\) using only \(8\) trainable parameters.

The very small train--test gap is the central observation about this model: \emph{Constant MPR does not overfit}. This suggests that the yield curve alone is approaching an information ceiling at this 40 bp level.

%=========================================================
\section{Summary}
\label{sec:pricing_summary}
%=========================================================

The pricing experiments establish two facts about the latent-state model when applied to swaption pricing:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The frozen stable model, used directly, gives implied-vol errors of order \(415\) bp — two orders of magnitude above bid--offer spreads. A latent model that reconstructs swap curves accurately is not by itself a pricing model.
    \item A minimal trainable pricing layer with only \(8\) parameters, the Constant MPR (\(\lambda_0\) drift correction and a per-factor diffusion scale), reduces this to \(40\) bp overall and \(42\) bp out-of-sample. This is the cleanest demonstration that the latent space supports a Q-recalibration.
    
\end{enumerate}
