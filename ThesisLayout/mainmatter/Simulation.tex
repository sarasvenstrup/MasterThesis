\chapter{Simulation and Stability Analysis}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter studies the behaviour of the trained neural encoder--decoder model when it is used beyond static curve reconstruction. The model maps observed swap curves into a low-dimensional latent representation and decodes latent states into arbitrage-free zero-coupon bond curves. A natural next question is therefore whether the learned latent dynamics can also be used to generate stable yield-curve scenarios.

This is not guaranteed by reconstruction accuracy alone. A model may reproduce observed swap curves well while the simulated latent process drifts away from the region represented in the training data. If this happens, the decoder, volatility network and short-rate network are evaluated outside the region where they were trained, and the resulting curves may become unstable or economically difficult to interpret.

The experiment therefore addresses an important practical question: is accurate reconstruction of observed curves sufficient for using the model as a scenario generator or as the basis for simulation-based pricing? To investigate this, we compare two model variants. The first is the baseline model, whose latent dynamics are learned without explicit stability constraints. The second is a stable parameterisation designed to improve the behaviour of simulated paths. The comparison focuses on whether the latent states remain in meaningful regions and whether the resulting short rates, zero-coupon bond curves and decoded swap-rate curves remain economically interpretable over the simulation horizon.

%========================================================
\section{Simulation of Latent Dynamics and Curve Reconstruction}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

We use the same swap-rate data as in the previous chapters. Let \(S_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote the observed market swap curve selected as the initial condition for the simulation, corresponding to the eight quoted maturities used in the model.

The trained encoder maps the initial swap curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = E(S_0) \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\). The simulated dynamics are therefore interpreted as risk-neutral dynamics generated by the trained model, rather than as historical real-world dynamics estimated from observed time-series changes.

In the implementation, the drift and short-rate functions are given by the trained networks
\[
\mu(z)=\mathcal{K}(z),
\qquad
\tilde r(z)=\mathcal{R}(z).
\]
The network \(\mathcal{H}\) returns state-dependent parameters from which the volatility levels and correlations are constructed. These quantities are then assembled into the volatility loading matrix \(\sigma(z)\), constructed as the lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the instantaneous covariance matrix as described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. This matrix enters directly in the latent simulation.

In the two-dimensional case, this corresponds to writing
\[
\sigma(z)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\sigma_1(z) & 0 \\
\rho(z)\sigma_2(z) & \sqrt{1-\rho(z)^2}\,\sigma_2(z)
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where \(\sigma_1(z)\) and \(\sigma_2(z)\) are the instantaneous volatility levels of the two latent factors, and \(\rho(z)\) is the instantaneous correlation between their stochastic innovations.

The latent dynamics are discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme. For a time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), the simulated paths satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
\sigma(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]

From the simulated latent path we obtain the corresponding short-rate path
\[
r_{t_n}=\tilde r(z_{t_n}),
\qquad n=0,\ldots,N.
\]
The associated bank account is
\[
B_t=\exp\!\left(\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right),
\]
and the pathwise discount factor is therefore
\[
D_t=B_t^{-1}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right).
\]
Since the short rate is only observed on the simulation grid, the integral is approximated numerically using the trapezoidal rule,
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]

For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the corresponding zero-coupon bond curve is obtained through the decoder described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. Conditional on this state, the functions \(A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) and \(B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) are obtained by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system numerically, and bond prices are recovered from
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]

Hence, each simulated path produces a latent-factor path, a short-rate path, pathwise discount factors, and a sequence of zero-coupon bond curves across maturities. These objects form the basis for assessing whether the trained model remains stable when it is used as a scenario generator.

%========================================================
\section{Stability Analysis of the Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The simulation procedure turns the trained encoder--decoder model into a forward scenario generator. Starting from an observed swap curve, the model first maps the curve to a latent initial state, then simulates the latent state forward in time, and finally decodes each simulated state into a zero-coupon bond curve. This requires more than accurate reconstruction of observed swap curves. The simulated latent paths must also remain sufficiently well behaved over the simulation horizon, since unstable latent dynamics may lead to short rates and bond curves that are economically difficult to interpret.

This section compares the baseline model with a stable parameterisation. The baseline model has an unconstrained affine drift and therefore does not guarantee mean reversion. In contrast, the stable model introduces a Hurwitz drift, bounded volatilities, positive-definite correlation matrices and a bounded short-rate parameterisation by construction.

%========================================================
\subsection{Well-Posedness and Simulation Stability}
\label{subsec:wellposedness_stability}
%========================================================

The latent simulation model is an SDE of the form
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}.
\]
As discussed in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, Lipschitz continuity and linear growth of the drift and diffusion coefficients are sufficient for the existence of a unique non-explosive solution.

In the numerical experiments in this chapter we consider the two-dimensional case \(\ell=2\). For the baseline model, the drift is affine and therefore globally Lipschitz. The volatility network \(\mathcal{H}\) uses bounded activation functions, so for fixed trained weights its raw outputs remain bounded. After applying the exponential and hyperbolic tangent transformations, the resulting volatility levels are positive and bounded, while the correlation parameter lies in \((-1,1)\). In two dimensions, this is sufficient to produce a valid \(2\times2\) correlation matrix. The stable model is constructed to satisfy the same type of regularity conditions.

However, mathematical well-posedness is weaker than practical simulation stability. A process may have a unique finite-time solution while still moving far outside the region covered by the encoded training data. In that case, the decoder, volatility network and short-rate network are forced to extrapolate beyond the latent states on which they were trained. The resulting short rates and zero-coupon bond curves may therefore be mathematically defined, but economically difficult to interpret.

The analysis below therefore examines whether the simulated paths remain stable enough to produce plausible model-generated yield-curve scenarios.

%========================================================
\subsection{Baseline Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}
%========================================================

When the baseline model is used for forward simulation, three related failure modes can arise. They all stem from the fact that the reconstruction objective does not by itself constrain the behaviour of the learned functions away from the encoded training data.

Before discussing the drift instability, we recall the following definition.

\begin{definition}[Hurwitz matrix]
A square matrix \(M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) is called Hurwitz if all
eigenvalues of \(M\) have strictly negative real parts, i.e.
\[
\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(M))<0,
\qquad i=1,\ldots,\ell.
\]
\end{definition}

Here \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(M))\) denotes the real part of the eigenvalue \(\lambda_i(M)\). This is relevant because a real matrix may have complex eigenvalues.

\begin{description}

\item[Unstable drift.]
The baseline drift is given by
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N,
\qquad
M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
N\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(M\) and \(N\) are learned parameters. The matrix \(M\) determines how the current latent state affects the deterministic drift of the process. Since \(M\) is learned without restrictions on its eigenvalues, the baseline model does not guarantee mean reversion.

To see this, consider the deterministic part of the latent dynamics,
\[
\dot z_t=Mz_t+N,
\]
where we temporarily omit the Brownian shock term in order to study the stability of the deterministic drift. If \(M\) is invertible, the fixed point \(z^*\) is the state where the deterministic drift is zero:
\[
Mz^*+N=0,
\qquad
z^*=-M^{-1}N.
\]
Writing the deviation from this fixed point as \(z_t-z^*\), we obtain
\[
z_t-z^*=e^{Mt}(z_0-z^*),
\]
where \(e^{Mt}\) denotes the matrix exponential. Thus the eigenvalues of \(M\) determine whether deviations from \(z^*\) decay or grow over time.

Mean reversion toward \(z^*\) requires all eigenvalues of \(M\) to have strictly negative real parts, i.e.\ \(M\) must be Hurwitz. If \(M\) has an eigenvalue with non-negative real part, then deviations in the corresponding latent direction do not decay. In a stochastic simulation, random shocks are then no longer systematically pulled back toward the fixed point and may instead be propagated along unstable latent directions, causing the latent state to move far away from the region covered by the encoded training data.

\item[Short-rate saturation.]
With zero bias terms, the baseline short-rate network has the form
\[
\tilde r(z)=W_2\psi(W_1z),
\qquad
\psi(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}-\frac12.
\]
Since each component of \(\psi(W_1z)\) lies in \((-1/2,1/2)\), the output satisfies
\[
|\tilde r(z)|
=
|W_2\psi(W_1z)|
\leq
\sum_j |(W_2)_j|\,|\psi((W_1z)_j)|
\leq
\frac12\|W_2\|_1
\qquad
\forall z.
\]

Thus the short rate remains bounded for all latent states. However, if the latent state becomes very large, then \(W_1z\) becomes large in magnitude and the centered soft-step activation is pushed close to its bounds \(\pm 1/2\). The derivative of the activation is then close to zero, so the short-rate network becomes almost insensitive to further changes in \(z\). The simulated short rate is therefore pinned near an artificial network-implied upper or lower level. It remains finite, but this boundedness is a saturation effect rather than evidence of stable dynamics.

\item[Decoder extrapolation.]
Once the latent state leaves the region covered by the encoded training data, the decoder \(\mathcal{G}\) is evaluated far away from the states on which it was trained. The no-arbitrage ODE system still produces zero-coupon bond prices, so the output remains mathematically well defined. However, the shape of the resulting bond curve is then driven by extrapolation of the neural network rather than by behaviour learned from observed market curves. The resulting bond prices and swap rates may therefore be economically difficult to interpret, even if the model reconstructs the training data accurately.

\end{description}

%========================================================
\subsection{Stable Parameterisation}
\label{subsec:sim_stable_solutions}
%========================================================

The failure modes described above motivate a stable variant of the model. The baseline and stable models share the same overall encoder--decoder framework and the same arbitrage-free bond-pricing construction. They differ only in the parameterisations of the drift network \(\mathcal{K}\), the diffusion network \(\mathcal{H}\), and the short-rate network \(\mathcal{R}\). The purpose of the stable parameterisation is to restrict the learned dynamics so that forward simulations remain better behaved.

\begin{description}

\item[Hurwitz drift.]
The stable model replaces the unconstrained drift matrix with
\[
M
=
-\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right),
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0,
\]
where \(V\) is a trainable matrix and \(\varepsilon\) is a fixed small positive constant. For any vector \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\),
\[
x^\top V^\top Vx
=
(Vx)^\top(Vx)
=
\|Vx\|^2
\geq 0.
\]
Hence \(V^\top V\) is positive semi-definite. Since \(\varepsilon I_\ell\) is strictly positive definite, the matrix \(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\) is strictly positive definite. Therefore all eigenvalues of
\[
M=-\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right)
\]
are strictly negative, with
\[
\lambda_i(M)\leq -\varepsilon<0.
\]
The drift matrix is therefore Hurwitz by construction. Since \(M\) is invertible, the deterministic drift has the equilibrium
\[
z^*=-M^{-1}N,
\]
and the latent factors are pulled back toward this point.

This parameterisation is sufficient but not necessary for mean reversion. A general Hurwitz matrix need not be symmetric, whereas the matrix above is symmetric negative definite. The restriction is nevertheless useful because it guarantees stable deterministic drift for any value of the trainable matrix \(V\).

\item[Bounded volatilities.]
The volatility levels enter the diffusion matrix \(\sigma(z)\) and therefore determine the scale of the random shocks in the latent simulation. In the baseline parameterisation, individual volatilities are recovered as
\[
\sigma_i(z)=\exp([\mathcal{H}(z)]_i).
\]
This ensures positivity. Moreover, for fixed trained weights and bounded activation functions, the network output is bounded. However, the resulting upper bound is implicit and depends on the learned parameters. Consequently, the baseline model does not provide an explicit numerical control on the diffusion scale when the latent state is evaluated away from the training region.

The stable model instead uses the sigmoid-bounded parameterisation
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
where \(h_i(z)\) is the \(i\)-th network output before the volatility transformation, \(b_i\) is a learnable offset, and
\[
0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}
\]
are prespecified numerical bounds. Since the sigmoid takes values in \((0,1)\), this ensures
\[
\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})
\qquad
\forall z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]
The diffusion scale therefore remains explicitly controlled even when the latent state is outside the region observed during training.

\item[Positive-definite correlations.]
The baseline network parameterises pairwise correlations as
\[
\rho_{ij}(z)=\tanh([\mathcal{H}(z)]_k),
\]
which ensures that each individual correlation lies in \((-1,1)\). For \(\ell=2\), this is sufficient to define a valid \(2\times2\) correlation matrix. For \(\ell\geq3\), however, specifying each pairwise correlation separately does not in general guarantee that the full correlation matrix is positive definite. Thus, in the two-dimensional simulations considered here, the hyperspherical construction is not needed for validity of the correlation matrix, but it becomes relevant in the general multi-factor formulation.

The stable model instead constructs the correlation matrix through a hyperspherical Cholesky parameterisation. Rather than predicting the pairwise correlations directly, the network outputs \(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) unconstrained values \(g_k(z)\), which are transformed into angles
\[
\theta_k(z)=\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z)).
\]
Hence \(\theta_k(z)\in(0,\pi)\) for all \(z\). These angles are used to construct a lower-triangular matrix \(L_R(z)\) with unit row norms and strictly positive diagonal entries, as described in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperspherical_cholesky}. The correlation matrix is then defined by
\[
R(z)=L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top.
\]
Since \(L_R(z)\) has full rank, \(R(z)\) is positive definite. Since the rows of \(L_R(z)\) have unit norm, the diagonal entries of \(R(z)\) are equal to one. Thus \(R(z)\) is a valid correlation matrix by construction.

\item[Bounded short-rate network.]
The short-rate network is also modified in the stable variant. Recall that the baseline short-rate network is bounded because of the centered soft-step activation, but its effective upper and lower limits are determined indirectly by the learned weights. If the latent state becomes large, the activation saturates and the short rate can be pushed to these network-implied limits.

The stable model makes the bounded range explicit by using
\[
r(z)
=
r_{\mathrm{center}}
+
r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh(f(z)),
\]
where \(f(z)\) is a neural network, \(r_{\mathrm{center}}\) is a learnable centre parameter, and \(r_{\mathrm{scale}}>0\) is a learnable scale parameter. In the implementation, positivity of the scale is enforced by
\[
r_{\mathrm{scale}}
=
\operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+r_{\min},
\]
where \(\eta\) is an unconstrained trainable parameter and \(r_{\min}>0\) is a small lower bound. Since \(\tanh(f(z))\in(-1,1)\), the short rate satisfies
\[
r(z)
\in
\bigl(
r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},
\;
r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}}
\bigr).
\]
Thus the stable short-rate network does not remove boundedness; instead, it makes the bound explicit and learnable. This avoids the situation where simulated rates are determined only by the accidental saturation limits of the baseline network.

\end{description}

Together, these modifications make the stable model more restrictive than the baseline model, but better suited for forward simulation. The Hurwitz drift controls the deterministic latent dynamics, the volatility functions remain within prespecified numerical bounds, the correlation matrix is positive definite by construction, and the short rate remains within a learnable bounded range. The empirical comparison below shows the practical effect of these changes on simulated paths.

The volatility bounds should be interpreted as stabilising hyperparameters rather than estimates of hard economic limits. They are chosen to be wide enough to contain the volatility values produced on the encoded training region, while still preventing extreme diffusion scales when the model is evaluated outside that region.

%========================================================
\subsection{Empirical Evidence from Simulated Paths}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To demonstrate the practical effect of the stable parameterisation, we run paired Euler--Maruyama simulations from the baseline and stable ep5000 checkpoints. Both models are initialised from the same observed EUR swap curve, using the first available date in the dataset. Both variants use the same encoder architecture, but the encoder weights are learned separately as part of each trained model. Hence, the same initial swap curve may be mapped to different latent initial states in the two checkpoints. The latent dynamics are then simulated using identical Brownian increments. This paired simulation design reduces Monte Carlo noise and makes the comparison primarily reflect the effect of the model parameterisation. All figures use \(N=500\) simulated paths, monthly time steps \(\Delta t=1/12\), a 10-year horizon and random seed 1234.

Because the two encoders are trained separately, the latent coordinates are not identified up to a common scale or rotation. The raw latent magnitudes should therefore be interpreted relative to each model's own encoded training cloud. In the present experiment, however, the baseline divergence is so large that this identifiability issue does not affect the qualitative conclusion.

\paragraph{Root cause: drift-matrix eigenvalues.}

The fundamental difference between the two variants is visible in the eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix \(M\), shown in Figure~\ref{fig:drift_eigenvalues}. Both eigenvalues of the baseline matrix are strictly positive,
\[
\lambda_1 \approx 5.571,
\qquad
\lambda_2 \approx 0.120,
\]
so deviations from the deterministic equilibrium \(z^*\) are amplified rather than dampened along both latent directions. The dominant eigenvalue determines the asymptotic divergence rate; with \(\lambda_1\approx5.6\), unstable modes grow on a timescale of roughly \(1/\lambda_1 \approx 0.18\) years, which is well within the simulation horizon. Both eigenvalues of the stable matrix are strictly negative,
\[
\lambda_1 \approx -1.327,
\qquad
\lambda_2 \approx -10.846,
\]
which implies mean reversion toward the equilibrium \(z^*\) along both directions. The two stable eigenvalues differ by an order of magnitude, corresponding to mean-reversion timescales of \(1/|\lambda_1|\approx 0.75\) and \(1/|\lambda_2|\approx 0.09\) years. The trained model has therefore learned two distinct timescales: a slow factor that explores the latent cloud on a roughly nine-month horizon, and a fast factor that reverts within weeks.

\paragraph{Rate of divergence.}

Figure~\ref{fig:log_growth} plots \(\log_{10}(\operatorname{mean}|z_d(t)|)\) over time for each latent dimension. This diagnostic is useful because exponential growth appears approximately linear on the logarithmic scale: a process growing like \(e^{ct}\) traces out a straight line with slope \(c/\ln 10\). The baseline curves are essentially linear over the full ten-year horizon, with both factors traversing roughly twenty decades of magnitude. This is consistent with exponential growth driven by the positive drift eigenvalues identified in Figure~\ref{fig:drift_eigenvalues}; the empirical slope is somewhat below the maximum possible rate \(\lambda_1/\ln 10\) because the diffusion takes time to populate the unstable mode.

The stable curves behave very differently. After a short initial widening driven by stochastic shocks, both curves flatten and remain on a controlled scale. Crucially, they plateau close to the dashed reference line that marks the training \(\pm 2\sigma\) level: the stable simulation has reached a stationary distribution whose magnitude is comparable to the encoded training cloud. This is the regime in which the decoder, volatility network and short-rate network were trained, so subsequent decoder outputs remain in a familiar region of latent space. The annotations report final values of \(10^{19}\) for the baseline against \(10^{-0.2}\) and \(10^{-1.0}\) for the stable model on the two factors, a discrepancy spanning roughly twenty orders of magnitude.

\paragraph{Latent-factor percentile fans.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans} shows percentile fan charts for each latent factor with eight individual sample paths overlaid. The fans summarise the distribution across the 500 simulated paths, while the sample paths reveal individual trajectories.

The top row shows the baseline model. Both the bands and the individual paths appear to hug the zero line for most of the horizon, only fanning out near the right edge of the plot. This is a scaling artefact: the vertical axis is forced into units of \(10^{19}\) to accommodate the terminal values, so the entire trajectory before the divergence becomes visually negligible. The figure is therefore most informative as a confirmation of the conclusion already drawn from Figures~\ref{fig:drift_eigenvalues} and \ref{fig:log_growth}: the divergence is not driven by isolated outliers but is a property of the bulk of the simulated distribution.

The bottom row shows the stable model. Both factors remain on a controlled scale throughout the horizon. The two factors operate at very different magnitudes --- the inner percentile band for \(z_1\) covers approximately \([-0.6,0.3]\), while the band for \(z_2\) covers approximately \([-0.1,0.1]\) --- consistent with the order-of-magnitude difference in the stable eigenvalues. The factor with stronger mean reversion has a tighter stationary distribution, as expected for an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck-type process under bounded diffusion. The sample paths confirm this: occasional excursions to \(z_1\approx 3\) appear, but the system always returns toward the median.

\paragraph{Short-rate fan and sample paths.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_short_rate_fan} shows the short-rate percentile fans with eight individual sample paths overlaid. The two panels are plotted on different vertical scales because the spread of the baseline rates would compress the stable panel into a single line.

The baseline short rate (left panel) departs from the initial level of approximately \(4\%\) almost immediately and saturates at the network output limits within roughly one year. The median traces a sharp transition followed by an essentially flat segment near \(+125\%\), while the percentile bands span \([-125\%,+125\%]\) for the rest of the horizon. This is the saturation pattern predicted in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}: as the latent state becomes large, the centered soft-step activation in the baseline short-rate network is pushed against its bounds and the output is pinned at \(\pm\tfrac12\|W_2\|_1\). The behaviour is bounded but not stable; it is a property of the network's saturation rather than of the underlying dynamics.

The stable short rate (right panel) remains within a learned bounded range of approximately \([-11\%,+11\%]\) over the full horizon. The median is roughly flat near \(+4\%\), and the inner percentile band fluctuates around this level. Individual sample paths exhibit substantial volatility but always return toward the median. The contrast with the baseline is qualitative as well as quantitative: the stable short rate exhibits a continuous distribution of plausible values, whereas the baseline distribution is degenerate at its saturation limits.

\paragraph{Volatility bounds.}

Figure~\ref{fig:sigma_bounds} compares the individual volatility functions \(\sigma_i(z)\), evaluated both on the encoded training cloud (solid histograms) and on an expanded grid extending three training standard deviations beyond the training mean (dashed histograms). This diagnostic does not describe simulated paths directly; rather, it illustrates how the learned volatility functions behave when extrapolated.

On the training cloud, the baseline volatilities are unimodal: \(\sigma_1\) is concentrated around \(0.7\)--\(1.1\) and \(\sigma_2\) around \(0.5\)--\(1.0\). The stable volatilities are markedly different: \(\sigma_1\) is bimodal, with a sharp peak near \(\sigma_{\min}\) and a broader peak around \(1.6\), while \(\sigma_2\) is concentrated in a narrow range \([0.26,0.35]\). The bimodality of stable \(\sigma_1\) suggests that the trained model has learned a state-dependent volatility regime: certain regions of latent space generate very low diffusion, while others generate substantial diffusion. This is a feature of the trained weights, not a constraint imposed by the parameterisation.

The expanded-grid histograms reveal what happens off the training distribution. The baseline volatilities take values substantially outside the training range, with \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\) reaching beyond \(4\) and \(5\) respectively. Such large diffusion coefficients amplify shocks well beyond what the model encountered during training, contributing directly to the divergence observed in earlier figures. The stable volatilities, in contrast, accumulate at the prescribed bounds \(\sigma_{\min}\) and \(\sigma_{\max}\) by construction. The bounds are doing visible work: they prevent the extrapolation pathology even though the network outputs are unrestricted.

\paragraph{Decoded swap-rate curves at simulation horizons.}

The diagnostics above describe what the simulator produces inside the model. Figure~\ref{fig:yield_curves} translates these dynamics back into the observable space by decoding the simulated latent states into swap-rate curves at horizons \(t=1\), \(5\) and \(10\) years. Each panel shows the cross-sectional mean curve across the 500 simulated paths together with the 25--75 and 5--95 percentile bands, with five sample curves overlaid. This is the most direct evidence that simulation stability matters for the model's intended use.

The baseline panels (top row) are clipped to \(\pm 50\%\) for readability. At the one-year horizon, only \(12\%\) of paths produce decoded swap rates that fit within the clipped range; the remaining \(88\%\) are outside it. By the five- and ten-year horizons, \(100\%\) of paths produce numerically finite curves, but those curves lie almost entirely outside any economically plausible interest-rate range, which is why the panels appear as nearly uniform shaded bands. The ``valid paths'' annotation should be interpreted carefully: it measures only numerical finiteness of the decoded swap rates, not economic plausibility. The fact that finiteness is preserved is a consequence of the saturation behaviour described earlier --- the decoder is being evaluated at extreme latent states, but the saturation of the short-rate network keeps the resulting bond prices within float range.

The stable panels (bottom row) show curves that remain on a controlled and economically interpretable scale. At the one-year horizon, the mean curve slopes upward from approximately \(4\%\) at the short end to a similar level at the long end, with a percentile band of roughly \([-2\%,+8\%]\). At later horizons the bands widen as expected for a stochastic interest-rate model: the five-year band covers approximately \([-15\%,+15\%]\) and the ten-year band a similar range, while the mean curve continues to slope gently upward. This widening reflects genuine uncertainty about future levels rather than divergence; the percentile bands are wide but bounded, and the mean curves remain interpretable.

The diagnostics confirm what the figures show. The baseline model remains finite in floating-point arithmetic, but it is not stable as a forward scenario generator in this experiment. The latent state reaches values of order \(10^{20}\), the drift matrix has positive eigenvalues, and the short rate saturates at the network output limits. The in-sample reconstruction RMSE row should be interpreted together with the simulation results. In the present experiment, the stable checkpoint does not sacrifice reconstruction accuracy: it achieves a lower in-sample error (\(8.49\) bps versus \(17.61\) bps) while also generating much better-behaved simulated paths. The comparison should therefore not be read as a necessary fit-versus-stability trade-off, but rather as evidence that structural stability constraints can materially improve simulation behaviour without harming the fit on observed curves.

The volatility-range rows quantify a phenomenon that was already visible in Figure~\ref{fig:sigma_bounds}: on the encoded training cloud, the two models produce volatility values of comparable order, but on the expanded grid the baseline range expands by roughly an order of magnitude in both factors, while the stable range is constrained to remain within \((\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\). The structural bound on diffusion is therefore the proximate cause of the controlled simulation behaviour observed elsewhere in the table.

The central conclusion is that reconstruction accuracy is not sufficient on its own for simulation-based pricing. A model may reconstruct observed curves accurately while learning latent dynamics that behave poorly when iterated forward. In the present experiment, the baseline checkpoint has unstable drift eigenvalues, simulated latent states leave the encoded training region, and the decoded swap-rate curves become economically uninterpretable. The stable parameterisation materially changes this behaviour by imposing mean-reverting deterministic dynamics, explicit volatility bounds, positive-definite correlations and a learnable bounded short-rate network. These restrictions lead to simulated paths and decoded swap-rate curves that are much more suitable for model-based pricing.

%========================================================
\subsection{Out-of-Sample Generalisation}
\label{subsec:oos_generalisation}
%========================================================

The analysis up to this point has examined a single in-sample window and assessed stability through forward simulation. This isolates the effect of the structural parameterisation on the simulated paths, but it does not directly answer whether the same stability properties also benefit reconstruction of swap curves the model has not seen during training. The forward simulation in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_evidence} relies on extrapolation of the decoder, the volatility network and the short-rate network into latent regions visited only after long simulation horizons. Out-of-sample reconstruction relies on extrapolation along a different axis, namely the encoded latent states of test-period swap curves drawn from a future market regime. The aim of this subsection is to test whether the stability properties identified earlier translate into more accurate out-of-sample reconstructions across multiple time periods, currencies and latent dimensions.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We re-run the rolling-window evaluation protocol of Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} for both variants. The protocol --- five-year training period, six-month test period, advanced six months at a time, yielding eighteen non-overlapping test windows from January~2015 to December~2023 --- and the per-currency RMSE metric are unchanged from there. The only modification is that both the baseline and the stable variant are estimated on every window at three latent dimensions \(\ell\in\{2,3,4\}\), giving \(18\times 2\times 3=108\) trained models in total. Each is trained for \(3{,}500\) epochs, in line with the convergence analysis underlying the evaluation chapter.

\paragraph{Aggregate results.}
Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} reports the across-window mean and maximum of the rolling out-of-sample RMSE for each \((\ell,\text{variant})\) pair, with the corresponding mean in-sample RMSE for reference. The dim-4 stable model is the strongest configuration, with a mean rolling out-of-sample RMSE of \(12.1\) bps and a maximum of \(24.2\) bps. The improvement over the baseline is substantial at \(\ell=4\), where the average error falls from \(100.5\) to \(12.1\) bps and the worst-window error from \(691.8\) to \(24.2\) bps; the improvement at \(\ell=3\) is similar in direction although smaller in magnitude. The pattern reverses at \(\ell=2\), where the stable variant produces a higher mean out-of-sample RMSE than the baseline. The reversal is driven by a single window (2016-07-01) on which the no-arbitrage ODE used for decoding fails on eight test rows; we return to this case below. The mean in-sample errors are similar across configurations (between \(8\) and \(12\) bps) for five of the six rows, so in-sample fit alone does not predict the out-of-sample ranking.

\paragraph{Interpretation: the role of the eigenvalue spectrum.}
The ranking in Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} is consistent with the structure of the learned drift. For the baseline model, the maximum real eigenvalue \(\lambda_{\max}\) of the drift matrix \(M\) is positive in 18 of 18 windows at \(\ell=2\), 13 of 18 at \(\ell=3\), and 18 of 18 at \(\ell=4\); the corresponding ranges across windows are \([-2.86,5.31]\), \([-4.76,3.30]\) and \([-3.58,2.68]\). Every catastrophic baseline failure (rolling RMSE above \(50\) bps) occurs in a window with \(\lambda_{\max}>0\), so the explosive-drift mechanism identified theoretically in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_baseline_problems} is also responsible for the largest baseline errors out of sample. A positive maximum eigenvalue is, however, a necessary rather than sufficient condition for catastrophic failure: the dim-2 baseline has \(\lambda_{\max}>0\) in all eighteen windows but produces a rolling RMSE above \(50\) bps in only one of them. The interpretation is that explosive drift causes large reconstruction errors only when the encoded test states are also far enough from the training cloud for the local explosive component of \(\mathcal{K}\) to amplify the displacement; in benign windows the encoded test states remain inside the training cloud and reconstruction is accurate despite the unstable spectrum. The eigenvalue spectrum is therefore a leading indicator of failure risk rather than a guarantee of failure.

For the stable variant, all eigenvalues are non-positive by construction. The observed ranges are \([-14.04,-0.001]\), \([-8.65,-0.004]\) and \([-17.75,-0.002]\) for \(\ell=2,3,4\) respectively. The presence of eigenvalues very close to zero indicates that the trained model has learned a near-unit-root mode in the latent dynamics. While this is consistent with the Hurwitz constraint, it produces a slowly mean-reverting (almost integrated) factor that is sensitive to small displacements of the latent state. This is the proximate cause of the dim-2 stable failure on the 2016-07-01 window, where the near-unit-root behaviour combines with the limited dynamical headroom of a two-dimensional latent space to make the decoder ODE numerically fragile.

\paragraph{Interpretation: behaviour in the 2022 rate-hike cycle.}
A single test period dominates the worst-case behaviour: the second half of 2022, during which the European Central Bank, the Federal Reserve and most other major central banks raised policy rates rapidly. Models trained on the preceding five-year window had been fitted almost entirely to a low-rate environment, so the encoded 2022 test states fall well outside the training cloud. The window functions as a natural stress test for the encoder/decoder pair, and it separates the variants cleanly. The dim-3 and dim-4 baselines fail with rolling RMSEs of \(296\) and \(395\) bps respectively, the latter additionally producing ten test rows of non-finite ODE output. The dim-2 stable also struggles, at \(266\) bps, and the dim-3 stable reaches \(146\) bps. Only the dim-4 stable model handles the period cleanly, with a rolling RMSE of \(23.7\) bps and no decoder failures. The pattern is consistent with the interpretation that the lower-dimensional stable models lack the representational capacity to extrapolate through this regime, while the higher-dimensional stable model has enough latent flexibility to reconstruct test curves outside the training distribution without the explosive baseline pathology. Outside the 2022 period and a small number of structural-shift windows, the two variants perform comparably across the eighteen rolling windows, with errors typically in the \(7\)--\(25\) bps range. The stable parameterisation therefore does not sacrifice accuracy on benign windows in order to better handle stress periods.

\paragraph{Summary and chapter conclusion.}
The rolling-window evidence reinforces the in-sample stability conclusions of this chapter. In-sample reconstruction loss alone does not predict out-of-sample behaviour: both variants achieve similar in-sample RMSE while their out-of-sample errors differ by orders of magnitude. The eigenvalue spectrum of the trained drift matrix, by contrast, is informative: a positive maximum eigenvalue is a leading indicator of catastrophic out-of-sample failure, while eigenvalues clustered very close to zero in the stable variant flag configurations that may remain numerically fragile in stress periods. Taken together with the in-sample evidence in Sections~\ref{subsec:sim_evidence} and~\ref{subsec:sim_summary}, the results show that the stable parameterisation materially improves both the forward-simulation behaviour of the model and its out-of-sample reconstruction accuracy, without sacrificing in-sample fit. Among the configurations considered, the dim-4 stable model is the most robust on both criteria, and we adopt it as the reference configuration for the application chapters that follow.
