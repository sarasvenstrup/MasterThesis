\chapter{Simulation and Stability Analysis}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter studies the behaviour of the trained neural encoder--decoder model when it is used for simulation. Whereas the previous chapters focused on reconstructing observed swap curves, the purpose here is to examine whether the learned model can also generate stable future yield-curve scenarios. This requires simulating the latent state forward in time and decoding each simulated state into a full zero-coupon bond curve.

The experiment addresses an important practical question: is accurate curve reconstruction sufficient for simulation-based pricing? To answer this, we compare two model variants. The first is the baseline model, whose latent dynamics are learned without explicit stability constraints. The second is a stable parameterisation designed to produce well-behaved simulated paths. The comparison focuses on whether the simulated latent states remain in meaningful regions and whether the resulting short rates, zero-coupon bond curves and decoded swap-rate curves are suitable for pricing applications.

%========================================================
\section{Simulation of Latent Dynamics and Curve Reconstruction}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================
We use the same swap-rate data as in the previous chapters. Let \(S_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote the observed market swap curve selected as the initial condition for the simulation, corresponding to the eight quoted maturities used in the model.
The trained encoder maps the initial swap curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = E(S_0) \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\). The simulated dynamics are therefore interpreted as risk-neutral dynamics generated by the trained model, rather than as historical real-world dynamics estimated from observed time-series changes.
In the implementation, the drift and short-rate functions are given by the trained networks
\[
\mu(z)=\mathcal{K}(z),
\qquad
\tilde r(z)=\mathcal{R}(z).
\]
The network \(\mathcal{H}\) returns state-dependent parameters from which the volatility levels and correlations are constructed. These quantities are then assembled into the volatility loading matrix \(\sigma(z)\), constructed as the lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the instantaneous covariance matrix as described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. This matrix enters directly in the latent simulation.
In the two-dimensional case, this corresponds to writing
\[
\sigma(z)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\sigma_1(z) & 0 \\
\rho(z)\sigma_2(z) & \sqrt{1-\rho(z)^2}\,\sigma_2(z)
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where \(\sigma_1(z)\) and \(\sigma_2(z)\) are the instantaneous volatility levels of the two latent factors, and \(\rho(z)\) is the instantaneous correlation between their stochastic innovations.
%where \(\sigma_1(z)\) and \(\sigma_2(z)\) are the instantaneous volatility levels of the two latent factors, and \(\rho(z)\) determines the instantaneous correlation between the random changes in the two latent factors.
The latent dynamics are discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme. For a time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), the simulated paths satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
\sigma(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]

From the simulated short-rate path \(r_{t_0},r_{t_1},\ldots,r_{t_N}\), the corresponding money-market discount process is constructed. In continuous time,
\[
D_t=B_t^{-1}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right).
\]
Since the short rate is only observed on the simulation grid, the integral is approximated using the trapezoidal rule:
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]

For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the corresponding zero-coupon bond curve is obtained through the decoder described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. Bond prices are recovered from
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr),
\]
where \(A\) and \(B\) are obtained by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system. Each simulated path therefore produces a short-rate path, pathwise discount factors, and a full sequence of decoded swap-rate curves.

\section{Stability Analysis of the Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The simulation procedure turns the trained encoder--decoder model into a forward scenario generator. Starting from an observed swap curve, the model first maps the curve to a latent initial state, then simulates the latent state forward in time, and finally decodes each simulated state into a zero-coupon bond curve. This requires more than accurate reconstruction of observed swap curves. The simulated latent paths must also remain stable over the simulation horizon, since unstable latent dynamics may lead to short rates and bond curves that are economically difficult to interpret. 

This section compares the baseline model with a stable parameterisation. The baseline model has an unconstrained affine drift and therefore does not guarantee mean reversion. In contrast, the stable model introduces a Hurwitz drift, bounded volatilities, positive-definite correlation matrices and a bounded short-rate parameterisation by construction.

%========================================================
\subsection{Well-Posedness and Simulation Stability}
\label{subsec:wellposedness_stability}
%========================================================

The latent simulation model is an SDE of the form \(dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\). As discussed in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, Lipschitz continuity and linear growth of the drift and diffusion coefficients are sufficient for the existence of a unique non-explosive solution. In the two-dimensional simulations considered here, both variants satisfy these regularity conditions under the learned parameter values.

However, mathematical well-posedness is weaker than practical simulation stability. A process may have a unique finite-time solution while still moving far outside the region covered by the encoded training data. In that case, the decoder and short-rate network are evaluated out of distribution, and the resulting zero-coupon bond curves may no longer be economically meaningful.

%========================================================
\subsection{Baseline Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}
%========================================================

When the baseline model is used for forward simulation, three related failure modes can arise. They all stem from the fact that the reconstruction objective does not by itself constrain the behaviour of the learned functions away from the encoded training data.

Before discussing the drift instability, we recall the following definition.

\begin{definition}[Hurwitz matrix]
A square matrix \(M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) is called Hurwitz if all
eigenvalues of \(M\) have strictly negative real parts, i.e.\
\(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(M))<0\) for \(i=1,\ldots,\ell\).
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Unstable drift.}
The baseline drift is \(\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N\) with \(M\) learned without eigenvalue constraints. The deviation from the fixed point \(z^*=-M^{-1}N\) satisfies \(z_t-z^*=e^{Mt}(z_0-z^*)\), so mean reversion requires \(M\) to be Hurwitz. A positive eigenvalue implies exponential divergence in the corresponding latent direction.

\paragraph{Short-rate saturation.}
The baseline short-rate network has \(\tilde r(z)=W_2\psi(W_1z)\) with \(\psi(x)=\operatorname{sigmoid}(x)-\tfrac12\in(-\tfrac12,\tfrac12)\), so \(|\tilde r(z)|\leq\tfrac12\|W_2\|_1\). When the latent state becomes large, the activation saturates and the short rate is effectively pinned near an artificial limit. It remains finite, but the resulting path is no longer economically meaningful.

\paragraph{Decoder extrapolation.}
Once the latent state leaves the training support, the decoder \(\mathcal{G}\) is evaluated out of distribution. The resulting swap-rate curves may bear no relation to observed market behaviour even if the model reconstructs the training data accurately.

%========================================================
\subsection{Stable Parameterisation}
\label{subsec:sim_stable_solutions}
%========================================================

\paragraph{Hurwitz drift.}
The stable model uses \(M=-\!\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right)\), \(V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\), \(\varepsilon>0\). Since \(V^\top V\succeq 0\) and \(\varepsilon I_\ell\succ 0\), all eigenvalues satisfy \(\lambda_i\leq-\varepsilon<0\) for any trainable \(V\).

\paragraph{Bounded volatilities.}
The stable model uses
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
ensuring \(\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\) for all \(z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\).

\paragraph{Positive-definite correlations.}
The stable model uses a hyperspherical Cholesky parameterisation \citep{Pinheiro1996} with angles \(\theta_k(z)=\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z))\in(0,\pi)\), producing a lower triangular factor \(L_R\) with strictly positive diagonal. Hence \(R=L_RL_R^\top\) is positive definite by construction for any \(\ell\).

\paragraph{Bounded short-rate network.}
The stable model uses \(r(z)=r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh(f(z))\) with \(r_{\mathrm{scale}}=\operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+r_{\min}>0\), ensuring \(r(z)\in(r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},\,r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}})\).

\subsection{Empirical Evidence from Simulated Paths}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To demonstrate the practical effect of the stable parameterisation, we run paired Euler--Maruyama simulations from the baseline and stable ep5000 checkpoints. Both models are started from the same observed EUR swap curve (first available date), use their own encoders to obtain their initial latent states, and then use identical Brownian increments. All figures use \(N=500\) paths, monthly steps \(\Delta t=1/12\), a 10-year horizon and shared seed 1234.

\paragraph{Root cause: drift-matrix eigenvalues.}

Figure~\ref{fig:drift_eigenvalues} shows the eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix \(M\). Both baseline eigenvalues are positive (\(\lambda_1\approx5.571\), \(\lambda_2\approx0.120\)), so the deterministic part of the baseline dynamics contains no restoring direction. Both stable eigenvalues are strictly negative (\(\lambda_1\approx-1.327\), \(\lambda_2\approx-10.846\)), guaranteeing mean reversion.

Figure~\ref{fig:log_growth} plots \(\log_{10}(\operatorname{mean}|z_d(t)|)\) over time. The stable curve flattens after the initial diffusion phase, consistent with mean-reverting dynamics.

The diagnostics confirm what the figures show. The baseline model remains finite in floating-point arithmetic but is not usable for pricing: the latent state reaches order \(10^{20}\), the drift matrix has positive eigenvalues, and zero paths remain bounded (\(|z_T|<10\)). The stable model keeps the latent state compact, has strictly negative drift eigenvalues, and 100\,\% of paths satisfy \(|z_T|<10\). The in-sample reconstruction RMSE row quantifies the fit-versus-stability trade-off: the stable model pays a small RMSE cost in exchange for these stability guarantees.

The central conclusion is that the structural constraints of the stable parameterisation --- Hurwitz drift, sigmoid-bounded volatilities, hyperspherical positive-definite correlations and a learnable bounded short-rate network --- are not merely theoretical refinements. They are necessary for producing simulated paths that are usable for derivative pricing, as demonstrated directly by the decoded swap-rate curves in Figure~\ref{fig:yield_curves}. Good reconstruction accuracy is therefore a necessary but far from sufficient condition for a simulation-based pricing model.
