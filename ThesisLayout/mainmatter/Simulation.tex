\chapter{Simulation and Stability}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter studies the behaviour of the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model when it is used beyond curve reconstruction. In particular, we ask whether the learned latent dynamics can be used to generate interest rate curve scenarios, comprising zero-coupon bond price curves and swap-rate curves, by simulating latent paths under the model's risk-neutral dynamics.

To investigate this, we consider two model variants. The first is the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model from the previous chapter, referred to here as the \textit{baseline}. The second is a \textit{stable} variant proposed in this thesis, which extends the baseline with modifications to the latent dynamics designed to produce more controlled simulated paths. These modifications are described in Section~\ref{sec:stability_analysis}, after which the two variants are compared empirically to assess whether the stable specification achieves more controlled simulated scenarios without sacrificing reconstruction accuracy.


%========================================================
\section{Simulation Setup}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

This section describes the simulation procedure common to both model variants. Starting from an observed swap-rate curve on an arbitrary initial date, the encoder maps the curve to a latent initial state, which is then simulated forward under the model's learned risk-neutral dynamics. At each time step, the resulting latent state is decoded to produce a zero coupon bond price curve and the corresponding swap-rate curve.



Let \(\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote the observed market swap curve on a chosen initial date. The trained encoder \eqref{eq:linear_encoder} maps this curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = A_1\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics \eqref{latentdynamics},
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(\mu(z_t)\) is the learned drift vector and \(\sigma(z_t)\) is the volatility matrix defined in Section~\ref{subsec:neural-modelling-dynamics}.

\textcolor{red}{Under the regularity conditions in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, the latent SDE therefore has a unique non-explosive solution. This ensures that the simulated process is mathematically well defined.[Vi skal lige snakke om hvordan vi skal have dette med.]}

The latent SDE is discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme \citep{Glasserman2004}. For a time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), \(n=0,1,\ldots,N\), where $N$ is the total number of steps and \(T=N\Delta t\) is the terminal horizon, the simulated paths satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
\sigma(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]
From the simulated latent path, the short-rate path is obtained using the short-rate specification \eqref{shortratedynamic},
\[
r_{t_n}=\tilde r(z_{t_n}),
\qquad n=0,1,\ldots,N,
\]
The pathwise discount factor is then approximated using the trapezoidal rule \citep{Glasserman2004}:
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]
For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the zero-coupon bond curve is recovered through the decoder by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system \eqref{ODEB}--\eqref{ODEA} numerically,
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]
Hence, each simulated path yields a time series of latent states, short rates, discount factors, and zero-coupon bond curves, from which the corresponding swap-rate curves are obtained using \eqref{estimatedswaprates}.

%========================================================
\section{The Stable Variant}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

When the learned latent dynamics are iterated over a multi-year simulation horizon, a mathematically well-defined SDE does not by itself ensure that the simulated paths remain on a controlled scale. Several stability issues may arise, stemming from the drift, diffusion, and short rate components of the latent model. The following identifies a set of such issues and the corresponding modifications introduced in the stable variant.


\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]
\item[Drift stability.] The baseline drift is affine,
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N,
\qquad
M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
N\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(M\) and \(N\) are learned parameters. Mean reversion is a natural property to impose on the drift, as it ensures the latent state fluctuates around a long-run equilibrium rather than drifting without bound. The baseline model does not guarantee this, since \(M\) is unconstrained.

Whether the drift is mean-reverting is determined by the eigenvalues 
of $M$, as the following result establishes.

\begin{proposition}[Mean reversion condition]
The deterministic system \(\dot{z}_t = Mz_t + N\) with fixed point 
\(z^* = -M^{-1}N \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell}\) is globally asymptotically 
stable about \(z^*\) if and only if all eigenvalues of \(M\) have 
strictly negative real parts.
\label{prop:meanreversion}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(y_t = z_t - z^*\). Since \(Mz^* + N = 0\), we obtain
\[
\dot{z}_t = Mz_t + N = M(y_t + z^*) + N = My_t + Mz^* + N = My_t.
\]
Moreover, since \(z^*\) is constant, \(\dot{y}_t = \dot{z}_t\), so 
the deviation satisfies
\[
\dot{y}_t = My_t,
\]
with unique solution \(y_t = e^{Mt}y_0\), where \(y_0 = z_0 - z^*\), 
or equivalently,
\[
z_t - z^* = e^{Mt}(z_0 - z^*).
\]
Suppose \(M\) is diagonalisable with eigenvalues 
\(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_\ell \in \mathbb{C}\) and corresponding 
eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_\ell\). Writing 
\(y_0 = \sum_{i=1}^\ell c_i v_i\), linearity of \(e^{Mt}\) gives
\[
y_t = \sum_{i=1}^\ell c_i e^{\lambda_i t} v_i.
\]
Since \(|e^{\lambda_i t}| = e^{\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i)t}\),
\[
\|y_t\| \leq \sum_{i=1}^\ell |c_i|\, 
e^{\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i)t} \|v_i\|.
\]
If \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0\) for all \(i\), each term 
decays to zero and hence \(y_t \to 0\) as \(t \to \infty\). 
Conversely, if \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_j) \geq 0\) for some \(j\), 
choosing \(y_0 = v_j\) gives \(y_t = e^{\lambda_j t} v_j\), which 
does not decay to zero. Since \(y_t \to 0\) is equivalent to 
\(z_t \to z^*\), the result follows; see 
\citep[Theorem~4.5]{Khalil2002}.
\end{proof}


To enforce this, the stable variant replaces the unconstrained drift matrix with the parametrisation  
\[
M = -\underbrace{\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right)}_{=:\,A},
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0.
\]

For any non-zero vector \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\),
\[
x^\top A x
=
\|Vx\|^2+\varepsilon\|x\|^2
>
0.
\]
so \(A\) is symmetric positive definite with eigenvalues satisfying $\lambda_i(A)\geq\varepsilon$. Since \(M=-A\), it follows that
\[
\lambda_i(M)=-\lambda_i(A)\leq -\varepsilon<0.
\]

By Proposition \ref{prop:meanreversion}, the stable drift matrix therefore guarantees global asymptotic stability about $z^*$ by construction.

%\begin{remark}
%    The scalar analogue of the stable parametrisation is the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process, where $M=-\kappa$ for some $\kappa>0$, so that $\dot{z}_t=-\kappa(z_t-\mu)$ is mean-reverting with speed $\kappa$. The stable variant generalises this to the multivariate setting by replacing the scalar $-\kappa$ with the matrix $M=-(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell),$ which plays the role of the mean reversion speed matrix.
%\end{remark}


\item[Diffusion scale.] The baseline volatility network \(\mathcal{H}\) recovers individual volatility levels as
\[
\sigma_i(z)=\exp([\mathcal{H}(z)]_i).
\]
which ensures strict positivity, but places no upper bound on \(\sigma_i(z)\). Large volatility values amplify the Brownian shock term, and even a mean-reverting drift may fail to keep simulated paths on a controlled scale if the diffusion term is sufficiently large.



The stable variant therefore bounds the volatility levels \textit{explicitly}. Instead of using an unrestricted exponential transformation, it sets
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
where \(h_i(z)\) is the \(i\)-th raw network output before the volatility transformation, \(b_i\) is a learnable bias term, and
\[
0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}
\]
are fixed numerical bounds. Since the sigmoid function takes values in \((0,1)\), we have
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
\in
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\min},\log\sigma_{\max}\bigr),
\]
and therefore
\[
\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})
\qquad
\forall z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]

The bounds $\sigma_{\min}$ and $\sigma_{\max}$ are fixed numerical hyperparameters, not learned from data.



\item[Correlation structure.] The volatility levels alone do not determine the full diffusion matrix. In the baseline model, the network \(\mathcal{H}\) also outputs raw pairwise correlation parameters, which are transformed as
\[
\rho_{ij}(z)=\tanh([\mathcal{H}(z)]_{ij}).
\]
This ensures that each individual correlation lies in \((-1,1)\). For \(\ell=2\), this is sufficient to define a valid \(2\times2\) correlation matrix. For \(\ell\geq3\), however, specifying pairwise correlations separately does not in general guarantee that the full correlation matrix is positive definite. Hence the resulting covariance matrix may fail to be valid in higher dimensions.

The stable variant therefore constructs the correlation matrix through a hyperspherical Cholesky representation. \textcolor{red}{reference!} Instead of predicting pairwise correlations directly, the network outputs \(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) unconstrained values \(g_k(z)\), which are transformed into angles
\[
\theta_k(z)
=
\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z)).
\]
Since the sigmoid function takes values in \((0,1)\), the angles satisfy
\[
\theta_k(z)\in(0,\pi).
\]
These angles are used to construct a lower-triangular matrix \(L_R(z)\) with unit row norms and strictly positive diagonal entries, as described in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperspherical_cholesky}. The correlation matrix is then defined by
\[
R(z)=L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top.
\]
Because \(L_R(z)\) has full rank, \(R(z)\) is positive definite. Moreover, since the rows of \(L_R(z)\) have unit norm, the diagonal entries of \(R(z)\) are equal to one. Thus \(R(z)\) is a valid correlation matrix by construction for any latent dimension \(\ell\).


\item[Short-rate behaviour.] In the baseline model, the short-rate network uses the centered soft-step activation from \eqref{eq:activation}:
\[
\tilde r(z)
=
W_2\psi(W_1z),
\qquad
\psi(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}-\frac12.
\]
Since each component of \(\psi(W_1z)\) lies in \((-1/2,1/2)\), the output is bounded:
\[
|\tilde r(z)|
=
|W_2\psi(W_1z)|
\leq
\sum_j |(W_2)_j|\,|\psi((W_1z)_j)|
\leq
\frac12\|W_2\|_1.
\]
Thus the baseline short rate remains bounded for all latent states, but the bound is implicit and set by the learned weights. When the simulated latent state becomes large, \(W_1z\) grows in magnitude and the activation is pushed toward its limiting values \(\pm 1/2\), pinning the short rate near a network-implied ceiling or floor. The rate stays finite, but only because the activation saturates, not because the latent dynamics are stable.

The stable variant instead parametrises the short rate as
\[
r(z)
=
r_{\mathrm{center}}
+
r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh(f(z)),
\]
where \(f(z)\) is a neural network, \(r_{\mathrm{center}}\) is a learnable parameter, and \(r_{\mathrm{scale}}>0\) is a learnable scale parameter. To ensure positivity of the scale,
\[
r_{\mathrm{scale}}
=
\operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+s_{\min},
\]
where \(\eta\) is an unconstrained trainable parameter and \(s_{\min}>0\) is a small lower bound on the scale, which applies to the width of the interval, rather than to the short rate itself. Since \(\tanh(f(z))\in(-1,1)\), the short rate satisfies
\[
r(z)
\in
\bigl(
r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},
\;
r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}}
\bigr).
\]
The range is now set by trainable parameters rather than by activation saturation. 

\end{description}

Together, these restrictions define the stable variant considered in the simulation experiment. 

%Together, these restrictions define the stable variant considered in the simulation experiment. Relative to the baseline model, the drift restriction imposes mean reversion in the deterministic latent dynamics, the volatility bounds control the diffusion scale, the correlation construction ensures a valid covariance matrix, and the short-rate specification makes the rate range explicit and learnable. The empirical comparison below investigates how these changes affect the simulated latent paths and the resulting model-generated swap-rate curve scenarios.


%========================================================
\subsection{A Simulation Experiment}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To investigate the practical effect of the stability restrictions introduced in the previous section, we run a simulation experiment comparing the baseline and stable models. We simulate latent paths from both models and compare their behaviour over a $10$-year horizon. The simulation follows the Euler--Maruyama procedure described in Section \ref{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}. Both models use the latent dimension \(\ell=2\), are trained for 5000 epochs, and are initialised from the observed EUR swap curve on 1 January 2010. Although both variants share the same encoder architecture, the encoder weights are trained separately, so the same initial curve may map to different initial states. We generate $M=500$ paths with monthly time steps $\Delta t=1/12$, drawing identical Brownian increments for both models using random seed 1234, so that differences between the simulated paths reflect differences in the model specifications rather than Monte Carlo noise.

Larger volatility values therefore lead to larger random movements in the simulated latent state.

\end{description}

The empirical evidence supports the stability mechanisms discussed above. In the baseline model, the learned drift matrix has positive eigenvalues, the simulated latent states grow along an unstable direction, and the short-rate network is pushed toward its activation-induced output limits. These effects carry through to the decoded swap-rate curves.

The stable model behaves differently. The drift eigenvalues are negative, the volatility functions are explicitly bounded, the correlation matrix is valid by construction, and the short-rate range is explicit and learnable. In the simulation experiment, these restrictions keep the latent factors, short rates and decoded swap-rate curves on a much more controlled scale.

The main conclusion is that reconstruction accuracy alone is not sufficient for using the learned dynamics as a scenario generator. A model may reconstruct observed curves accurately while still producing unstable paths when its latent dynamics are iterated forward. The next subsection therefore examines whether the improved simulation behaviour of the stable variant comes at the cost of reconstruction accuracy.

%========================================================
\subsection{Reconstruction Accuracy}
\label{subsec:sim_reconstruction_accuracy}
%========================================================

The simulation results above show that the stable variant produces more controlled simulated paths than the baseline model. A natural concern is that this behaviour may come at the cost of reconstruction accuracy. The stability restrictions reduce the flexibility of the drift, volatility, correlation and short-rate specifications, so it is important to check whether the stable model can still reconstruct observed swap curves accurately.

We first compare the in-sample reconstruction errors for the two models used in the simulation experiment. Both models are trained on the same EUR swap-rate data, use latent dimension \(\ell=2\), and are trained for \(5000\) epochs. Reconstruction accuracy is measured by the root mean squared error between observed and reconstructed swap rates, reported in basis points.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\input{Figures/Simulation/sim_insample_rmse_table}
\caption{In-sample reconstruction RMSE for the baseline and stable models used in the simulation experiment. Both models are trained on EUR swap-rate data with latent dimension \(\ell=2\) for \(5000\) epochs. RMSE is reported in basis points.}
\label{tab:sim_insample_rmse}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:sim_insample_rmse} shows that the stable variant does not sacrifice in-sample reconstruction accuracy in this experiment. It achieves a lower in-sample RMSE than the baseline model, with \(8.49\) bps compared to \(17.61\) bps. Thus, in this experiment, the stable simulation behaviour is not obtained by giving up reconstruction accuracy.

We also compare the two variants out of sample using the rolling-window evaluation protocol from Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}. The experiment is repeated for latent dimensions \(\ell\in\{2,3,4\}\), with each model trained for \(3500\) epochs. Reconstruction accuracy is measured by the rolling out-of-sample RMSE.

Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} shows that the effect of the stability restrictions depends strongly on the latent dimension. For the baseline model, the mean out-of-sample RMSE worsens as the latent dimension increases, even though the mean in-sample RMSE remains low. This indicates that a good in-sample fit does not necessarily translate into robust out-of-sample reconstruction.

The stable variant shows the opposite pattern. At \(\ell=2\), the restrictions appear too strong relative to the limited latent dimension, leading to both high in-sample and out-of-sample RMSE. As the latent dimension increases, the stable model gains more flexibility while retaining the same stability restrictions. Its reconstruction performance therefore improves substantially, and at \(\ell=4\) it achieves the lowest mean out-of-sample RMSE, the lowest worst-window RMSE, and the lowest mean in-sample RMSE among all configurations in Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary}. This suggests that the stability restrictions are not inherently harmful for reconstruction, but require enough latent flexibility to represent the observed curve movements.

To better understand where the out-of-sample errors arise, we next inspect the rolling-window errors over time. This is useful because the average and maximum RMSE values in Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} may be driven by a small number of stressed market periods rather than by persistent differences across all windows.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Simulation/fig_rolling_rmse.png}
    \caption{Rolling out-of-sample RMSE across test windows for the baseline and stable variants at \(\ell\in\{2,3,4\}\).}
    \label{fig:rolling_oos_rmse}
\end{figure}

The rolling-window errors show that a large part of the worst-case behaviour is concentrated in the second half of 2022. This period is a natural stress test for the model, since the European Central Bank, the Federal Reserve and other major central banks raised policy rates rapidly after several years of low interest rates. Models trained on the preceding five-year window were therefore fitted mostly to a low-rate environment, while the test curves came from a substantially different market regime.

The 2022 window separates the variants clearly. The dim-3 and dim-4 baseline models produce large rolling RMSEs of \(296\) and \(395\) bps, respectively, with the dim-4 baseline additionally producing ten test rows with non-finite ODE output. The lower-dimensional stable variants also struggle: the dim-2 stable model reaches \(266\) bps and the dim-3 stable model reaches \(146\) bps. By contrast, the dim-4 stable model reconstructs this period much more accurately, with a rolling RMSE of \(23.7\) bps and no decoder failures.

This pattern suggests that the stable restrictions are most useful when combined with sufficient latent flexibility. In lower dimensions, the restrictions may leave too little capacity to represent a stressed rate environment. With \(\ell=4\), however, the stable model has enough latent degrees of freedom to reconstruct curves from a regime that is far from the training window, while still avoiding the unstable behaviour seen in the baseline simulations. Outside the 2022 stress period and a small number of structural-shift windows, the two variants are more similar, with errors typically in the \(7\)--\(25\) bps range.

The main conclusion is therefore that the preferred specification depends on the intended use. For benign reconstruction windows, the baseline and stable variants can perform similarly. However, when the model is intended for simulation or for reconstruction under stressed market regimes, the stable variant becomes more attractive, provided the latent dimension is sufficiently large. Among the configurations considered here, the stable model with \(\ell=4\) provides the strongest combination of reconstruction accuracy, simulation stability and robustness in the 2022 stress window.
