\chapter{Simulation and Stability}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter studies the behaviour of the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model when it is used beyond curve reconstruction. In particular, we ask whether the learned latent dynamics can be used to generate yiield curve scenarios, comprising zero-coupon bond price curves and swap-rate curves, by simulating latent paths under the model's risk-neutral dynamics.

To investigate this, we consider two model variants. The first is the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model from the previous chapter, referred to here as the \textit{baseline}. The second is a \textit{stable} variant proposed in this thesis, which extends the baseline with modifications to the latent dynamics designed to produce more controlled simulated paths. These modifications are described in Section~\ref{sec:stability_analysis}, after which the two variants are compared empirically to assess whether the stable specification achieves more controlled simulated scenarios without sacrificing reconstruction accuracy.


%========================================================
\section{Simulation Setup}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

This section describes the simulation procedure common to both model variants. Starting from an observed swap-rate curve on an arbitrary initial date, the encoder maps the curve to a latent initial state, which is then simulated forward under the model's learned risk-neutral dynamics. At each time step, the resulting latent state is decoded to produce a zero coupon bond price curve and the corresponding swap-rate curve.



Let \(\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote the observed market swap curve on a chosen initial date. The trained encoder \eqref{eq:linear_encoder} maps this curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = A_1\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics \eqref{latentdynamics},
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(\mu(z_t)\) is the learned drift vector and \(\sigma(z_t)\) is the volatility matrix defined in Section~\ref{subsec:neural-modelling-dynamics}.


Under the regularity conditions established in Appendix~\ref{app:???}, the latent SDE admits a unique non-explosive solution for both models considered in this chapter, ensuring that the simulated processes are mathematically well defined.


The latent SDE is discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme \citep{Glasserman2004}. For a time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), \(n=0,1,\ldots,N\), where $N$ is the total number of steps and \(T=N\Delta t\) is the terminal horizon, the simulated paths satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
\sigma(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]
From the simulated latent path, the short-rate path is obtained using the short-rate specification \eqref{shortratedynamic},
\[
r_{t_n}=\tilde r(z_{t_n}),
\qquad n=0,1,\ldots,N,
\]
The pathwise discount factor is then approximated using the trapezoidal rule \citep{Glasserman2004}:
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]
For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the zero-coupon bond curve is recovered through the decoder by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system \eqref{ODEB}--\eqref{ODEA} numerically,
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]
Hence, each simulated path yields a time series of latent states, short rates, discount factors, and zero-coupon bond curves, from which the corresponding swap-rate curves are obtained using \eqref{estimatedswaprates}.

\section{The Stable Variant}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The baseline latent dynamics admit a unique non-explosive solution under the regularity conditions verified in Appendix~\ref{app:???}, establishing that the model is mathematically well-defined independently of any simulation scheme. When iterated over a multi-year horizon, however, this does not by itself ensure that simulated paths remain on a controlled scale. Several stability issues may arise, stemming from the drift, diffusion, and short rate components of the latent model. The following identifies these issues and the corresponding modifications introduced in the stable variant.

\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]
\item[Drift stability.] The baseline drift is affine,
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N,
\qquad
M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
N\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(M\) and \(N\) are learned parameters. Mean reversion is a natural property to impose on the drift, as it ensures the latent state fluctuates around a long-run equilibrium rather than drifting without bound. The baseline model does not guarantee this, since \(M\) is unconstrained.

Whether the drift is mean-reverting is determined by the eigenvalues 
of $M$, as the following result establishes.

\begin{proposition}[Mean reversion condition]
The deterministic system \(\dot{z}_t = Mz_t + N\) with fixed point 
\(z^* = -M^{-1}N \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell}\) is globally asymptotically 
stable about \(z^*\) if and only if all eigenvalues of \(M\) have 
strictly negative real parts.
\label{prop:meanreversion}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(y_t = z_t - z^*\). Since \(Mz^* + N = 0\), we obtain
\[
\dot{z}_t = Mz_t + N = M(y_t + z^*) + N = My_t + Mz^* + N = My_t.
\]
Moreover, since \(z^*\) is constant, \(\dot{y}_t = \dot{z}_t\), so 
the deviation satisfies
\[
\dot{y}_t = My_t,
\]
with unique solution \(y_t = e^{Mt}y_0\), where \(y_0 = z_0 - z^*\), 
or equivalently,
\[
z_t - z^* = e^{Mt}(z_0 - z^*).
\]
Suppose \(M\) is diagonalisable with eigenvalues 
\(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_\ell \in \mathbb{C}\) and corresponding 
eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_\ell\). Writing 
\(y_0 = \sum_{i=1}^\ell c_i v_i\), linearity of \(e^{Mt}\) gives
\[
y_t = \sum_{i=1}^\ell c_i e^{\lambda_i t} v_i.
\]
Since \(|e^{\lambda_i t}| = e^{\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i)t}\),
\[
\|y_t\| \leq \sum_{i=1}^\ell |c_i|\, 
e^{\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i)t} \|v_i\|.
\]
If \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0\) for all \(i\), each term 
decays to zero and hence \(y_t \to 0\) as \(t \to \infty\). 
Conversely, if \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_j) \geq 0\) for some \(j\), 
choosing \(y_0 = v_j\) gives \(y_t = e^{\lambda_j t} v_j\), which 
does not decay to zero. Since \(y_t \to 0\) is equivalent to 
\(z_t \to z^*\), the result follows; see 
\citep[Theorem~4.5]{Khalil2002}.
\end{proof}


To enforce this, the stable variant replaces the unconstrained drift matrix with the parametrisation  
\[
M = -\underbrace{\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right)}_{=:\,A},
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0.
\]

For any non-zero vector \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\),
\[
x^\top A x
=
\|Vx\|^2+\varepsilon\|x\|^2
>
0.
\]
so \(A\) is symmetric positive definite with eigenvalues satisfying $\lambda_i(A)\geq\varepsilon$. Since \(M=-A\), it follows that
\[
\lambda_i(M)=-\lambda_i(A)\leq -\varepsilon<0.
\]

By Proposition \ref{prop:meanreversion}, the stable drift matrix therefore guarantees global asymptotic stability about $z^*$ by construction.

%\begin{remark}
%    The scalar analogue of the stable parametrisation is the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process, where $M=-\kappa$ for some $\kappa>0$, so that $\dot{z}_t=-\kappa(z_t-\mu)$ is mean-reverting with speed $\kappa$. The stable variant generalises this to the multivariate setting by replacing the scalar $-\kappa$ with the matrix $M=-(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell),$ which plays the role of the mean reversion speed matrix.
%\end{remark}


\item[Diffusion scale.] The baseline volatility network \(\mathcal{H}\) recovers individual volatility levels as
\[
\sigma_i(z)=\exp([\mathcal{H}(z)]_i).
\]
which ensures strict positivity, but places no upper bound on \(\sigma_i(z)\). Large volatility values amplify the Brownian shock term, and even a mean-reverting drift may fail to keep simulated paths on a controlled scale if the diffusion term is sufficiently large.



The stable variant therefore bounds the volatility levels \textit{explicitly}. Instead of using an unrestricted exponential transformation, it sets
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
where \(h_i(z)\) is the \(i\)-th raw network output before the volatility transformation, \(b_i\) is a learnable bias term, and
\[
0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}
\]
are fixed numerical bounds. Since the sigmoid function takes values in \((0,1)\), we have
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
\in
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\min},\log\sigma_{\max}\bigr),
\]
and therefore
\[
\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})
\qquad
\forall z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]

The bounds $\sigma_{\min}$ and $\sigma_{\max}$ are fixed numerical hyperparameters, not learned from data.


\item[Correlation structure.] The volatility levels alone do not determine the full diffusion matrix. In the baseline model, the last $\ell(\ell-1)/2$ outputs of $\mathcal{H}(z)$ are used as raw parameters for the pairwise correlations, and are denoted
\[
g_{ij}(z) := [\mathcal{H}(z)]_{\ell+k}, \qquad k = 1,\ldots,\ell(\ell-1)/2,
\]
where $k$ enumerates the pairs $(i,j)$ with $i=2,\ldots,\ell$ and $j=1,\ldots,i-1$ in row-wise order. Each raw parameter is mapped to a pairwise correlation via
\[
\rho_{ij}(z) = \tanh \bigl(g_{ij}(z)\bigr) \in (-1,1).
\]
While each $\rho_{ij}$ lies in $(-1,1)$, specifying pairwise correlations  independently does not guarantee that the full correlation matrix is  positive definite for $\ell \geq 3$.

The stable variant therefore constructs the correlation matrix through a 
hyperspherical Cholesky representation \citep{PourahmadiWang2015}. The outputs $g_{ij}(z)$ are instead transformed into angles via
\[
\theta_{ij}(z) = \pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid} \bigl(g_{ij}(z)\bigr) \in (0,\pi),
\]
which are used to construct $L_R(z)$ as described in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperspherical_cholesky}. Since $L_R(z)$ has full rank and unit row norms, $R(z) = L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top$ is symmetric, positive definite with unit diagonal, and hence a valid correlation matrix by construction for any $\ell$.

\item[Short-rate behaviour.] In the baseline model, the short-rate network uses the centered soft-step activation from \eqref{eq:activation}:
\[
\tilde r(z)
=
W_2\psi(W_1z),
\qquad
\psi(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}-\frac12.
\]
Since each component of \(\psi(W_1z)\) lies in \((-1/2,1/2)\), the output is bounded:
\[
|\tilde r(z)|
=
|W_2\psi(W_1z)|
\leq
\sum_j |(W_2)_j|\,|\psi((W_1z)_j)|
\leq
\frac12\|W_2\|_1.
\]
Thus the baseline short rate remains bounded for all latent states, but the bound is implicit and set by the learned weights. When the simulated latent state becomes large, \(W_1z\) grows in magnitude and the activation is pushed toward its limiting values \(\pm 1/2\), pinning the short rate near a network-implied ceiling or floor. The rate stays finite, but only because the activation saturates, not because the latent dynamics are stable.

The stable variant instead parametrises the short rate as
\[
r(z)
=
r_{\mathrm{center}}
+
r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh(f(z)),
\]
where \(f(z)\) is a neural network, \(r_{\mathrm{center}}\) is a learnable parameter, and \(r_{\mathrm{scale}}>0\) is a learnable scale parameter. To ensure positivity of the scale,
\[
r_{\mathrm{scale}}
=
\operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+s_{\min},
\]
where \(\eta\) is an unconstrained trainable parameter and \(s_{\min}>0\) is a small lower bound on the scale, which applies to the width of the interval, rather than to the short rate itself. Since \(\tanh(f(z))\in(-1,1)\), the short rate satisfies
\[
r(z)
\in
\bigl(
r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},
\;
r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}}
\bigr).
\]
The range is now set by trainable parameters rather than by activation saturation. 
\end{description}



Together, these restrictions define the stable variant. Section~\ref{sec:sim_evidence} compares it against the baseline through a simulation experiment, and Section~\ref{sec:sim_reconstruction_accuracy} then examines whether the restrictions affect reconstruction accuracy.

\section{A Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

We compare the baseline and stable models by simulating latent paths over a $10$-year horizon. Both models use the latent dimension \(\ell=2\), are trained for 5{,}000 epochs on swap curves from all nine currencies jointly, and are initialised from the observed EUR swap curve on 29 January 2010. The simulation follows the Euler--Maruyama procedure described in Section \ref{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}. Although both variants share the same encoder architecture, the encoder weights are trained separately, so the same initial curve may map to different initial latent states. We generate $M=500$ paths with monthly time steps $\Delta t=1/12$, drawing identical Brownian increments for both models using random seed 1234, so that differences between the simulated paths reflect differences in the model specifications rather than Monte Carlo noise. \textcolor{red}{(add a reference to appendix with a table of all hyperparameters chosen, like the sigma bounds r scale and etc.)} A full list of architecture, training and simulation hyperparameters is

The eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix $M$ explain why. By Proposition \ref{prop:meanreversion} in Section \ref{sec:stability_analysis}, mean reversion requires all eigenvalues of $M$ to have negative real parts. The learned eigenvalues are:
\[
\lambda_1^{\mathrm{base}} \approx 5.571,
\qquad
\lambda_2^{\mathrm{base}} \approx 0.120,
\]
and
\[
\lambda_1^{\mathrm{stable}} \approx -1.327,
\qquad
\lambda_2^{\mathrm{stable}} \approx -10.846.
\]

Both baseline eigenvalues are positive, so deviations from the fixed point grow rather than decay, consistent with the \(10^{19}\) scale in Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans}. For the stable model, however, both eigenvalues are negative, so deviations revert toward the fixed point. The faster reversion of $z_2$, with $|\lambda_2^{\mathrm{stable}}| \approx 10.846$ versus \(|\lambda_1^{\mathrm{stable}}| \approx 1.327\), is reflected in its tighter range in Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans}.


For each latent factor \(d=1,2\), Figure~\ref{fig:log_growth} gives a complementary view by plotting

\log_{10}\left(
\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}|z_d^{(m)}(t)|
\right),
\]
where \(z_d^{(m)}(t)\) denotes latent factor \(d\) on simulated path \(m\). Exponential growth appears approximately linear on this scale, with slope \(c/\ln(10)\) for a quantity growing as \(e^{ct}\). The baseline curves follow this pattern closely, while the stable curves level off.

The stable variant prevents this, but the simulated $z_1$ paths still reach roughly $\pm 2$--$3$, well beyond the training cloud of $[-0.30,0.02]$ visble in Figure \ref{fig:training_cloud}. The stability restrictions therefore keep the paths bounded, but do not confine them to the region seen during training.

The stable short rate, by contrast, stays within its learned bounded range of approximately \([-11\%,+11\%]\) throughout the horizon, as the mean-reverting latent paths never push the activation to its limits.

Because this eigenvalue is positive, any component along it grows without bound, and after ten years all paths are dominated by that single mode. The short histogram reflects this degeneracy, with virtually all mass concentrated at extreme values near \(\pm125\%\).

The experiment shows that the stability restrictions turn out to be a practical requirement, \textcolor{red}{not only a theoretical one}. Constraining the drift, diffusion scale and the short-rate output together is what keeps the paths and decoded curves on a interpretable scale throughout the ten-year horizon.

Whether the stability restrictions improve reconstruction accuracy depends strongly on the latent dimension. At $\ell=2$, the stable model struggles severely, with an average IS RMSE of $138.7$ bps against $10.5$ bps for the baseline, suggesting the restrictions are too constraining relative to the available latent degrees of freedom. From $\ell=3$ onward, the stable model improves substantially. 

The baseline, as established in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}, overfits increasingly with latent dimension, and this is reflected here in its out-of-sample RMSE increasing from $19.0$ bps at $\ell=2$ to $100.5$ bps at $\ell=4$. The stable model moves in the opposite direction, and at $\ell=4$ achieves the lowest average and maximum out-of-sample RMSE across all configurations. At $12.1$ bps out-of-sample, it improves upon the best baseline configuration, $\ell=2$ at $19.0$ bps. The stability restrictions therefore do not come at the cost of reconstruction accuracy. With sufficient latent dimensions, they improve it.

The aggregate results in Table \ref{tab:oos_summary_sim}, however, do not reveal whether the stable model addresses the difficulties with specific curve variants identified for the baseline in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}.
