\chapter{Simulation and Stability Analysis}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter studies the behaviour of the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model when it is used beyond curve reconstruction. A natural next question is whether the learned latent dynamics can also be used to generate stable yield-curve scenarios by simulating latent paths forward in time.

The chapter therefore addresses the following practical question: is accurate reconstruction of observed curves sufficient for using the learned latent dynamics as a scenario generator? To investigate this, we compare two variants of the model. The first is the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, which we refer to in this chapter as the baseline model. In this version, the latent dynamics are learned without explicit stability restrictions. The second is a stable variant designed to control the behaviour of simulated paths.

%========================================================
\section{Simulation of Latent Dynamics and Curve Reconstruction}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

Using the same swap-rate data as in the previous chapters, let \(\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote the observed market swap curve selected as the initial condition for the simulation.

As in the linear encoder \eqref{eq:linear_encoder} of Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, the trained encoder maps the initial swap curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = A_1\mathbf{S}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics given in \eqref{latentdynamics},
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + \sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\). Here \(\mu(z_t)\) is the learned drift vector and \(\sigma(z_t)\) is the volatility loading matrix defined by the neural dynamics in Section~\ref{subsec:neural-modelling-dynamics}.

Under the regularity conditions in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, the latent SDE therefore has a unique non-explosive solution. This ensures that the simulated process is mathematically well defined. 

We simulate the latent dynamics by discretising the diffusion process above using the Euler--Maruyama scheme, a standard method for approximating sample paths of continuous-time diffusion models; see \citep{Glasserman2004}. For a time step \(\Delta t>0\), terminal horizon \(T=N\Delta t\), and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), \(n=0,\ldots,N\), the simulated paths satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
\sigma(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]
From the simulated latent path we obtain the corresponding short-rate path using the short-rate specification \eqref{shortratedynamic},
\[
r_{t_n}=\tilde r(z_{t_n}),
\qquad n=0,\ldots,N,
\]
where \(N\) is the number of time steps in the simulation.

Consistent with the risk-neutral pricing setup leading to the bond-pricing PDE \eqref{eq:bond_pricing_pde}, the associated bank account is
\[
B_t=\exp\!\left(\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right),
\]
and the pathwise discount factor is therefore
\[
D_t=B_t^{-1}
=
\exp\!\left(-\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right).
\]
Since the short rate is only observed on the simulation grid, we approximate the integral using the trapezoidal rule, a standard numerical integration method \citep{Glasserman2004}:
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]
For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the corresponding zero-coupon bond curve is obtained through the decoder described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. Conditional on this state, the functions \(A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) and \(B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) are obtained by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system \eqref{ODEB}--\eqref{ODEA} numerically, and bond prices are recovered from the affine-inspired representation \eqref{affineinspiredzcbprices},
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]
Hence, each simulated path produces a latent-factor path, a short-rate path, pathwise discount factors, and a sequence of zero-coupon bond curves across maturities, which can be converted into decoded swap-rate curves using \eqref{estimatedswaprates}.

%========================================================
\section{Stability Analysis of the Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The previous section describes how the arbitrage-free encoder--decoder model is turned into a scenario generator. The latent SDE is mathematically well defined under the regularity conditions stated above, but this does not by itself ensure stable simulated paths. When the learned dynamics are iterated over a simulation horizon, several stability issues may arise.

The discussion below presents these issues together with the corresponding restrictions introduced in the stable variant. 

\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]

\item[Drift stability.] The baseline drift is affine,
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N,
\qquad
M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
N\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(M\) and \(N\) are learned parameters. For simulation purposes, it is desirable that the deterministic drift is mean-reverting, since shocks to the latent state should be pulled back toward a central region rather than propagated away from the encoded training data. The baseline model does not guarantee this, since \(M\) is learned without any restrictions.

To see the role of \(M\), consider the deterministic dynamics obtained by omitting the Brownian shock term,
\[
\dot z_t = Mz_t+N,
\]
where \(\dot z_t\) denotes the derivative with respect to time. If \(M\) is invertible, the fixed point \(z^*\) is the state where the deterministic drift is zero:
\[
Mz^*+N=0,
\qquad
z^*=-M^{-1}N.
\]
Define the deviation from equilibrium by
\[
y_t=z_t-z^*,
\qquad
\text{so that}
\qquad
z_t=y_t+z^*.
\]
Since \(Mz^*+N=0\), we obtain
\[
\dot z_t
=
Mz_t+N
=
M(y_t+z^*)+N
=
My_t+Mz^*+N
=
My_t.
\]
Moreover, since \(z^*\) is constant,
\[
\dot y_t
=
\frac{d}{dt}(z_t-z^*)
=
\dot z_t.
\]
Hence the deviation from equilibrium satisfies
\[
\dot y_t=My_t,
\]
with solution
\[
y_t=e^{Mt}y_0,
\qquad
y_0=z_0-z^*.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
z_t-z^*=e^{Mt}(z_0-z^*).
\]

Thus the matrix exponential \(e^{Mt}\) determines whether deviations from equilibrium decay or grow. In an eigenvector direction \(v\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\), an initial deviation \(y_0=cv\) evolves as
\[
y_t=c e^{\lambda t}v.
\]
Hence deviations in this direction decay only if \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)<0\). Mean reversion therefore requires all eigenvalues of \(M\) to have negative real parts.

The stable variant enforces this by replacing the unconstrained drift matrix with
\[
M
=
-\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right),
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0.
\]
Let
\[
A=V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell.
\]
For any non-zero vector \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\),
\[
x^\top A x
=
\|Vx\|^2+\varepsilon\|x\|^2
>
0.
\]
Thus \(A\) is symmetric positive definite, so all eigenvalues of \(A\) are positive and satisfy
\[
\lambda_i(A)\geq \varepsilon.
\]
Since \(M=-A\), the eigenvalues of \(M\) satisfy
\[
\lambda_i(M)=-\lambda_i(A)\leq -\varepsilon<0.
\]
All eigenvalues of the stable drift matrix therefore have negative real parts by construction. Consequently, the deterministic part of the stable latent dynamics is mean-reverting: deviations from \(z^*\) decay over time instead of growing.

\item[Diffusion scale.] In the baseline model, the volatility network \(\mathcal{H}\) outputs raw log-volatility values, and the individual volatility levels are recovered as
\[
\sigma_i(z)=\exp([\mathcal{H}(z)]_i).
\]
This guarantees strictly positive volatilities. However, the upper bound on \(\sigma_i(z)\) is only implicit and depends on the trained network weights. This matters in simulation because large volatility values increase the size of the Brownian shock term in
\[
dz_t=\mu(z_t)\,dt+\sigma(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q}.
\]
Even if the deterministic drift is stable, large diffusion terms can push simulated paths away from the region where the networks were trained.

The stable variant therefore bounds the volatility levels explicitly. Instead of using an unrestricted exponential transformation, it sets
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
where \(h_i(z)\) is the \(i\)-th raw network output before the volatility transformation, \(b_i\) is a learnable bias term, and
\[
0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}
\]
are fixed numerical bounds. Since the sigmoid function takes values in \((0,1)\), we have
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
\in
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\min},\log\sigma_{\max}\bigr),
\]
and therefore
\[
\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})
\qquad
\forall z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]

The stable variant thus keeps the diffusion scale explicitly controlled for all latent states, including states outside the encoded training region. The bounds \(\sigma_{\min}\) and \(\sigma_{\max}\) should be interpreted as stabilising numerical hyperparameters rather than hard economic limits. They are chosen wide enough to contain the volatility values observed on the encoded training region, while preventing extreme diffusion values when the model is evaluated outside this region.

\item[Correlation structure.] The volatility levels alone do not determine the full diffusion matrix. In the baseline model, the network \(\mathcal{H}\) also outputs raw pairwise correlation parameters, which are transformed as
\[
\rho_{ij}(z)=\tanh([\mathcal{H}(z)]_k).
\]
This ensures that each individual correlation lies in \((-1,1)\). For \(\ell=2\), this is sufficient to define a valid \(2\times2\) correlation matrix. For \(\ell\geq3\), however, specifying pairwise correlations separately does not in general guarantee that the full correlation matrix is positive definite. Hence the resulting covariance matrix may fail to be valid in higher dimensions.

The stable variant therefore constructs the correlation matrix through a hyperspherical Cholesky representation. Instead of predicting pairwise correlations directly, the network outputs \(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) unconstrained values \(g_k(z)\), which are transformed into angles
\[
\theta_k(z)
=
\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z)).
\]
Since the sigmoid function takes values in \((0,1)\), the angles satisfy
\[
\theta_k(z)\in(0,\pi).
\]
These angles are used to construct a lower-triangular matrix \(L_R(z)\) with unit row norms and strictly positive diagonal entries, as described in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperspherical_cholesky}. The correlation matrix is then defined by
\[
R(z)=L_R(z)L_R(z)^\top.
\]
Because \(L_R(z)\) has full rank, \(R(z)\) is positive definite. Moreover, since the rows of \(L_R(z)\) have unit norm, the diagonal entries of \(R(z)\) are equal to one. Thus \(R(z)\) is a valid correlation matrix by construction for any latent dimension \(\ell\).


\item[Short-rate behaviour.] In the baseline model, the short-rate network uses the centered soft-step activation from \eqref{eq:activation}:
\[
\tilde r(z)
=
W_2\psi(W_1z),
\qquad
\psi(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}-\frac12.
\]
Since each component of \(\psi(W_1z)\) lies in \((-1/2,1/2)\), the output is bounded:
\[
|\tilde r(z)|
=
|W_2\psi(W_1z)|
\leq
\sum_j |(W_2)_j|\,|\psi((W_1z)_j)|
\leq
\frac12\|W_2\|_1.
\]
Thus the baseline short rate remains bounded, and therefore finite, for all latent states. However, this boundedness is implicit and determined by the learned weights. If the latent state becomes large, then \(W_1z\) also becomes large in magnitude, and the centered soft-step activation is pushed close to its limiting values \(\pm 1/2\). The simulated short rate is therefore pinned near a network-implied upper or lower level. The rate remains finite, but this reflects the activation function flattening near its bounds rather than stability of the simulated latent state.

The stable variant makes the bounded range explicit by using
\[
r(z)
=
r_{\mathrm{center}}
+
r_{\mathrm{scale}}\tanh(f(z)),
\]
where \(f(z)\) is a neural network, \(r_{\mathrm{center}}\) is a learnable centre parameter, and \(r_{\mathrm{scale}}>0\) is a learnable scale parameter. Positivity of the scale is enforced by
\[
r_{\mathrm{scale}}
=
\operatorname{softplus}(\eta)+s_{\min},
\]
where \(\eta\) is an unconstrained trainable parameter and \(s_{\min}>0\) is a small lower bound on the scale. This lower bound applies only to the width of the short-rate interval, not to the short rate itself. Since \(\tanh(f(z))\in(-1,1)\), the short rate satisfies
\[
r(z)
\in
\bigl(
r_{\mathrm{center}}-r_{\mathrm{scale}},
\;
r_{\mathrm{center}}+r_{\mathrm{scale}}
\bigr).
\]
The stable short-rate network therefore remains bounded by construction, but the range is explicit and learnable rather than an accidental consequence of the activation function flattening near its bounds.

\end{description}

Together, these restrictions define the stable variant considered in the simulation experiment. Relative to the baseline model, the drift restriction imposes mean reversion in the deterministic latent dynamics, the volatility bounds control the diffusion scale, the correlation construction ensures a valid covariance matrix, and the short-rate specification makes the rate range explicit and learnable. The empirical comparison below investigates how these changes affect the simulated latent paths and the resulting model-generated yield-curve scenarios.


%========================================================
\subsection{Empirical Evidence from Simulated Paths}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To investigate the practical effect of the stability restrictions, we simulate latent paths from the baseline and stable models trained for \(5000\) epochs. Both models use the latent dimension \(\ell=2\) and are initialised from the same observed EUR swap curve on 1 January 2010. The two variants use the same encoder architecture, but the encoder weights are learned separately as part of each trained model. Hence, the same initial swap curve may be mapped to different latent initial states in the two models. The latent dynamics are then simulated using the Euler--Maruyama scheme.

The simulations use identical Brownian increments for the two models. This paired design reduces Monte Carlo noise and makes differences between the simulated paths primarily reflect differences between the baseline and stable specifications. We generate \(M=500\) simulated paths, using monthly time steps \(\Delta t=1/12\), a 10-year horizon, and random seed 1234.

\begin{description}[
    style=unboxed,
    leftmargin=0pt,
    labelindent=0pt,
    labelsep=0.5em
]


\item[Latent-factor percentile fans and growth.]

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans} provides a visual comparison of the simulated latent paths. The purpose is to assess whether the simulated latent states remain on a controlled scale over the ten-year horizon, or whether they move rapidly away from the region where the model was trained.

The figure shows percentile fan charts for the simulated latent factors \(z_1(t)\) and \(z_2(t)\) under the baseline and stable models, with eight individual sample paths overlaid. At each simulation time \(t_n\), percentiles are computed across the \(M=500\) simulated paths. The shaded bands therefore describe the distribution of simulated latent states over time, while the individual sample paths show single simulated trajectories.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Simulation/fig_latent_fans.png}
    \caption{Percentile fan charts for the simulated latent factors \(z_1(t)\)
    and \(z_2(t)\). At each time step, percentiles are computed across the
    \(M=500\) simulated paths. The dark line shows the median, the shaded
    regions show the 25--75 and 5--95 percentile ranges, and eight individual
    sample paths are overlaid. Top row: baseline. Note the vertical-axis units
    of \(10^{19}\), which compress most of the early trajectory toward zero.
    Bottom row: stable.}
    \label{fig:comp_fans}
\end{figure}

The top row shows the baseline model. The latent factors are plotted in units of \(10^{19}\) because the terminal values become extremely large. As a result, most of the earlier part of the trajectories is compressed visually toward zero. The sharp widening near the end of the horizon therefore reflects the very large scale reached by the simulated baseline paths. Since both the percentile bands and the overlaid sample paths expand, the instability is not caused only by a few isolated paths, but affects a substantial part of the simulated distribution.

The bottom row shows the stable model. Both latent factors remain on a controlled scale throughout the ten-year horizon. The two factors operate at different magnitudes: the variation in \(z_1\) is substantially larger than the variation in \(z_2\). Individual paths may still move away from the median temporarily, but they remain on a much smaller scale over the simulation horizon.

To understand this difference, we inspect the learned drift matrix \(M\). In Section~\ref{sec:stability_analysis}, we established that the deterministic deviations from the fixed point are governed by
\[
z_t-z^*=e^{Mt}(z_0-z^*).
\]
Hence, for the deterministic latent dynamics to be mean-reverting, all eigenvalues of \(M\) must have negative real parts. If this condition fails, deviations in the corresponding directions are not pulled back toward the fixed point and may instead grow over time.

We calculate the eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix for both variants:
\[
\lambda_1^{\mathrm{base}} \approx 5.571,
\qquad
\lambda_2^{\mathrm{base}} \approx 0.120,
\]
and
\[
\lambda_1^{\mathrm{stable}} \approx -1.327,
\qquad
\lambda_2^{\mathrm{stable}} \approx -10.846.
\]
The baseline eigenvalues are both positive. This means that the deterministic part of the baseline dynamics contains directions in which deviations from the fixed point grow. To see this, recall that a deviation in an eigenvector direction evolves as
\[
y_t=c e^{\lambda t}v.
\]
When \(\lambda>0\), the factor \(e^{\lambda t}\) increases with time. Hence a shock in such a direction is not pulled back by the deterministic drift, but is instead reinforced as the simulation is iterated forward. This is consistent with the sharp increase in the scale of the baseline latent paths in Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans}.

For the stable model, both eigenvalues are negative. When \(\lambda<0\), the factor \(e^{\lambda t}\) decreases with time. Deviations in both deterministic eigenvector directions are therefore pulled back toward the fixed point. The two stable eigenvalues have different magnitudes, so the two latent directions revert at different speeds, but both are mean-reverting.

Figure~\ref{fig:log_growth} gives a complementary view of the same phenomenon. For each latent factor \(d=1,2\), it plots
\[
\log_{10}\left(
\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}|z_d^{(m)}(t)|
\right),
\]
where \(z_d^{(m)}(t)\) denotes latent factor \(d\) on simulated path \(m\). This diagnostic is useful because exponential growth appears approximately linear on a logarithmic scale. If a quantity grows like \(e^{ct}\), then its base-10 logarithm grows approximately linearly with slope \(c/\ln(10)\).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Simulation/fig_log_growth.png}
    \caption{\(\log_{10}(\operatorname{mean}|z_d(t)|)\) for the baseline and
    stable models. The baseline curves grow approximately linearly on the log
    scale, indicating exponential growth in the simulated latent factors. The
    stable curves remain on a controlled scale and are shown together with a
    dashed reference line based on the encoded training cloud.}
    \label{fig:log_growth}
\end{figure}

The baseline curves increase almost linearly on the log scale, showing that the latent factors grow approximately exponentially over the simulation horizon. This agrees with the positive drift eigenvalues reported above. The stable curves, by contrast, rise initially and then remain on a controlled scale. This is consistent with negative drift eigenvalues, where deterministic deviations are pulled back rather than reinforced.

Together, Figures~\ref{fig:comp_fans} and~\ref{fig:log_growth} show both the distributional behaviour and the growth rate of the simulated latent factors. The baseline model generates latent paths that grow by many orders of magnitude, while the stable model keeps the latent factors on a substantially smaller scale over the same ten-year horizon.


\paragraph{Short-rate fan and sample paths.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_short_rate_fan} shows the short-rate percentile fans with eight individual sample paths overlaid. The two panels are plotted on different vertical scales because the spread of the baseline rates would compress the stable panel into a single line.

The baseline short rate (left panel) departs from the initial level of approximately \(4\%\) almost immediately and saturates at the network output limits within roughly one year. The median traces a sharp transition followed by an essentially flat segment near \(+125\%\), while the percentile bands span \([-125\%,+125\%]\) for the rest of the horizon. This is the saturation pattern predicted in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}: as the latent state becomes large, the centered soft-step activation in the baseline short-rate network is pushed against its bounds and the output is pinned at \(\pm\tfrac12\|W_2\|_1\). The behaviour is bounded but not stable; it is a property of the network's saturation rather than of the underlying dynamics.

The stable short rate (right panel) remains within a learned bounded range of approximately \([-11\%,+11\%]\) over the full horizon. The median is roughly flat near \(+4\%\), and the inner percentile band fluctuates around this level. Individual sample paths exhibit substantial volatility but always return toward the median. The contrast with the baseline is qualitative as well as quantitative: the stable short rate exhibits a continuous distribution of plausible values, whereas the baseline distribution is degenerate at its saturation limits.

\paragraph{Volatility bounds.}

Figure~\ref{fig:sigma_bounds} compares the individual volatility functions \(\sigma_i(z)\), evaluated both on the encoded training cloud (solid histograms) and on an expanded grid extending three training standard deviations beyond the training mean (dashed histograms). This diagnostic does not describe simulated paths directly; rather, it illustrates how the learned volatility functions behave when extrapolated.

On the training cloud, the baseline volatilities are unimodal: \(\sigma_1\) is concentrated around \(0.7\)--\(1.1\) and \(\sigma_2\) around \(0.5\)--\(1.0\). The stable volatilities are markedly different: \(\sigma_1\) is bimodal, with a sharp peak near \(\sigma_{\min}\) and a broader peak around \(1.6\), while \(\sigma_2\) is concentrated in a narrow range \([0.26,0.35]\). The bimodality of stable \(\sigma_1\) suggests that the trained model has learned a state-dependent volatility regime: certain regions of latent space generate very low diffusion, while others generate substantial diffusion. This is a feature of the trained weights, not a constraint imposed by the parameterisation.

The expanded-grid histograms reveal what happens off the training distribution. The baseline volatilities take values substantially outside the training range, with \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\) reaching beyond \(4\) and \(5\) respectively. Such large diffusion coefficients amplify shocks well beyond what the model encountered during training, contributing directly to the divergence observed in earlier figures. The stable volatilities, in contrast, accumulate at the prescribed bounds \(\sigma_{\min}\) and \(\sigma_{\max}\) by construction. The bounds are doing visible work: they prevent the extrapolation pathology even though the network outputs are unrestricted.

\paragraph{Decoded swap-rate curves at simulation horizons.}

The diagnostics above describe what the simulator produces inside the model. Figure~\ref{fig:yield_curves} translates these dynamics back into the observable space by decoding the simulated latent states into swap-rate curves at horizons \(t=1\), \(5\) and \(10\) years. Each panel shows the cross-sectional mean curve across the 500 simulated paths together with the 25--75 and 5--95 percentile bands, with five sample curves overlaid. This is the most direct evidence that simulation stability matters for the model's intended use.

The baseline panels (top row) are clipped to \(\pm 50\%\) for readability. At the one-year horizon, only \(12\%\) of paths produce decoded swap rates that fit within the clipped range; the remaining \(88\%\) are outside it. By the five- and ten-year horizons, \(100\%\) of paths produce numerically finite curves, but those curves lie almost entirely outside any economically plausible interest-rate range, which is why the panels appear as nearly uniform shaded bands. The ``valid paths'' annotation should be interpreted carefully: it measures only numerical finiteness of the decoded swap rates, not economic plausibility. The fact that finiteness is preserved is a consequence of the saturation behaviour described earlier --- the decoder is being evaluated at extreme latent states, but the saturation of the short-rate network keeps the resulting bond prices within float range.

The stable panels (bottom row) show curves that remain on a controlled and economically interpretable scale. At the one-year horizon, the mean curve slopes upward from approximately \(4\%\) at the short end to a similar level at the long end, with a percentile band of roughly \([-2\%,+8\%]\). At later horizons the bands widen as expected for a stochastic interest-rate model: the five-year band covers approximately \([-15\%,+15\%]\) and the ten-year band a similar range, while the mean curve continues to slope gently upward. This widening reflects genuine uncertainty about future levels rather than divergence; the percentile bands are wide but bounded, and the mean curves remain interpretable.

The diagnostics confirm what the figures show. The baseline model remains finite in floating-point arithmetic, but it is not stable as a forward scenario generator in this experiment. The latent state reaches values of order \(10^{20}\), the drift matrix has positive eigenvalues, and the short rate saturates at the network output limits. The stable model keeps the latent state on a controlled scale, has strictly negative drift eigenvalues, and produces short-rate paths that remain within a much narrower learned range.

The in-sample reconstruction RMSE row should be interpreted together with the simulation results. In the present experiment, the stable checkpoint does not sacrifice reconstruction accuracy: it achieves a lower in-sample error (\(8.49\) bps versus \(17.61\) bps) while also generating much better-behaved simulated paths. The comparison should therefore not be read as a necessary fit-versus-stability trade-off, but rather as evidence that structural stability constraints can materially improve simulation behaviour without harming the fit on observed curves.

The volatility-range rows quantify a phenomenon that was already visible in Figure~\ref{fig:sigma_bounds}: on the encoded training cloud, the two models produce volatility values of comparable order, but on the expanded grid the baseline range expands by roughly an order of magnitude in both factors, while the stable range is constrained to remain within \((\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\). The structural bound on diffusion is therefore the proximate cause of the controlled simulation behaviour observed elsewhere in the table.

The central conclusion is that reconstruction accuracy is not sufficient on its own for simulation-based pricing. A model may reconstruct observed curves accurately while learning latent dynamics that behave poorly when iterated forward. In the present experiment, the baseline checkpoint has unstable drift eigenvalues, simulated latent states leave the encoded training region, and the decoded swap-rate curves become economically uninterpretable. The stable parameterisation materially changes this behaviour by imposing mean-reverting deterministic dynamics, explicit volatility bounds, positive-definite correlations and a learnable bounded short-rate network. These restrictions lead to simulated paths and decoded swap-rate curves that are much more suitable for model-based pricing.

%========================================================
\subsection{Out-of-Sample Generalisation}
\label{subsec:oos_generalisation}
%========================================================

The analysis up to this point has examined a single in-sample window and assessed stability through forward simulation. This isolates the effect of the structural parameterisation on the simulated paths, but it does not directly answer whether the same stability properties also benefit reconstruction of swap curves the model has not seen during training. The forward simulation in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_evidence} relies on extrapolation of the decoder, the volatility network and the short-rate network into latent regions visited only after long simulation horizons. Out-of-sample reconstruction relies on extrapolation along a different axis, namely the encoded latent states of test-period swap curves drawn from a future market regime. The aim of this subsection is to test whether the stability properties identified earlier translate into more accurate out-of-sample reconstructions across multiple time periods, currencies and latent dimensions.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We re-run the rolling-window evaluation protocol of Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} for both variants. The protocol --- five-year training period, six-month test period, advanced six months at a time, yielding eighteen non-overlapping test windows from January~2015 to December~2023 --- and the per-currency RMSE metric are unchanged from there. The only modification is that both the baseline and the stable variant are estimated on every window at three latent dimensions \(\ell\in\{2,3,4\}\), giving \(18\times 2\times 3=108\) trained models in total. Each is trained for \(3{,}500\) epochs, in line with the convergence analysis underlying the evaluation chapter.

\paragraph{Aggregate results.}
Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} reports the across-window mean and maximum of the rolling out-of-sample RMSE for each \((\ell,\text{variant})\) pair, with the corresponding mean in-sample RMSE for reference. The dim-4 stable model is the strongest configuration, with a mean rolling out-of-sample RMSE of \(12.1\) bps and a maximum of \(24.2\) bps. The improvement over the baseline is substantial at \(\ell=4\), where the average error falls from \(100.5\) to \(12.1\) bps and the worst-window error from \(691.8\) to \(24.2\) bps; the improvement at \(\ell=3\) is similar in direction although smaller in magnitude. The pattern reverses at \(\ell=2\), where the stable variant produces a higher mean out-of-sample RMSE than the baseline. The reversal is driven by a single window (2016-07-01) on which the no-arbitrage ODE used for decoding fails on eight test rows; we return to this case below. The mean in-sample errors are similar across configurations (between \(8\) and \(12\) bps) for five of the six rows, so in-sample fit alone does not predict the out-of-sample ranking.

\paragraph{Interpretation: the role of the eigenvalue spectrum.}
The ranking in Table~\ref{tab:oos_summary} is consistent with the structure of the learned drift. For the baseline model, the maximum real eigenvalue \(\lambda_{\max}\) of the drift matrix \(M\) is positive in 18 of 18 windows at \(\ell=2\), 13 of 18 at \(\ell=3\), and 18 of 18 at \(\ell=4\); the corresponding ranges across windows are \([-2.86,5.31]\), \([-4.76,3.30]\) and \([-3.58,2.68]\). Every catastrophic baseline failure (rolling RMSE above \(50\) bps) occurs in a window with \(\lambda_{\max}>0\), so the explosive-drift mechanism identified theoretically in Section~\ref{subsec:sim_baseline_problems} is also responsible for the largest baseline errors out of sample. A positive maximum eigenvalue is, however, a necessary rather than sufficient condition for catastrophic failure: the dim-2 baseline has \(\lambda_{\max}>0\) in all eighteen windows but produces a rolling RMSE above \(50\) bps in only one of them. The interpretation is that explosive drift causes large reconstruction errors only when the encoded test states are also far enough from the training cloud for the local explosive component of \(\mathcal{K}\) to amplify the displacement; in benign windows the encoded test states remain inside the training cloud and reconstruction is accurate despite the unstable spectrum. The eigenvalue spectrum is therefore a leading indicator of failure risk rather than a guarantee of failure.

For the stable variant, all eigenvalues are non-positive by construction. The observed ranges are \([-14.04,-0.001]\), \([-8.65,-0.004]\) and \([-17.75,-0.002]\) for \(\ell=2,3,4\) respectively. The presence of eigenvalues very close to zero indicates that the trained model has learned a near-unit-root mode in the latent dynamics. While this is consistent with the Hurwitz constraint, it produces a slowly mean-reverting (almost integrated) factor that is sensitive to small displacements of the latent state. This is the proximate cause of the dim-2 stable failure on the 2016-07-01 window, where the near-unit-root behaviour combines with the limited dynamical headroom of a two-dimensional latent space to make the decoder ODE numerically fragile.

\paragraph{Interpretation: behaviour in the 2022 rate-hike cycle.}
A single test period dominates the worst-case behaviour: the second half of 2022, during which the European Central Bank, the Federal Reserve and most other major central banks raised policy rates rapidly. Models trained on the preceding five-year window had been fitted almost entirely to a low-rate environment, so the encoded 2022 test states fall well outside the training cloud. The window functions as a natural stress test for the encoder/decoder pair, and it separates the variants cleanly. The dim-3 and dim-4 baselines fail with rolling RMSEs of \(296\) and \(395\) bps respectively, the latter additionally producing ten test rows of non-finite ODE output. The dim-2 stable also struggles, at \(266\) bps, and the dim-3 stable reaches \(146\) bps. Only the dim-4 stable model handles the period cleanly, with a rolling RMSE of \(23.7\) bps and no decoder failures. The pattern is consistent with the interpretation that the lower-dimensional stable models lack the representational capacity to extrapolate through this regime, while the higher-dimensional stable model has enough latent flexibility to reconstruct test curves outside the training distribution without the explosive baseline pathology. Outside the 2022 period and a small number of structural-shift windows, the two variants perform comparably across the eighteen rolling windows, with errors typically in the \(7\)--\(25\) bps range. The stable parameterisation therefore does not sacrifice accuracy on benign windows in order to better handle stress periods.

\paragraph{Summary and chapter conclusion.}
The rolling-window evidence reinforces the in-sample stability conclusions of this chapter. In-sample reconstruction loss alone does not predict out-of-sample behaviour: both variants achieve similar in-sample RMSE while their out-of-sample errors differ by orders of magnitude. The eigenvalue spectrum of the trained drift matrix, by contrast, is informative: a positive maximum eigenvalue is a leading indicator of catastrophic out-of-sample failure, while eigenvalues clustered very close to zero in the stable variant flag configurations that may remain numerically fragile in stress periods. Taken together with the in-sample evidence in Sections~\ref{subsec:sim_evidence} and~\ref{subsec:sim_summary}, the results show that the stable parameterisation materially improves both the forward-simulation behaviour of the model and its out-of-sample reconstruction accuracy, without sacrificing in-sample fit. Among the configurations considered, the dim-4 stable model is the most robust on both criteria, and we adopt it as the reference configuration for the application chapters that follow.
