\chapter{Simulation and Stability Analysis}
\label{chap:simulation}

This chapter conducts a simulation experiment based on the trained neural encoder--decoder model. Whereas the previous chapters focused on reconstructing observed swap curves, the purpose here is to examine whether the learned model can also generate stable future yield-curve scenarios. This requires simulating the latent state forward in time and decoding each simulated state into a full zero-coupon bond curve.

The experiment addresses an important practical question: is accurate curve reconstruction sufficient for simulation-based pricing? To answer this, we compare two model variants. The first is the baseline model, whose latent dynamics are learned without explicit stability constraints. The second is a stable parameterisation designed to produce well-behaved simulated paths. The comparison focuses on whether the simulated latent states remain in meaningful regions and whether the resulting short rates and zero-coupon bond curves are suitable for pricing applications.

%========================================================
\section{Simulation of Latent Dynamics and Curve Reconstruction}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

We use the same swap data as in the previous chapters. Let
\(S_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote an observed market swap curve at the initial
date, corresponding to the eight quoted maturities used in the model. The
initial curve \(S_0\) is selected from the observed dataset. In the numerical
experiments below, this is either the first available observation or the
observation corresponding to a specified calendar date.

The trained encoder maps the initial swap curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = E(S_0) \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent
state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the
risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\). The simulated dynamics are therefore
interpreted as risk-neutral dynamics implied by the trained model, rather than
as historical real-world dynamics estimated from time-series changes.

In the implementation, the drift and short-rate functions are given by the
trained networks
\[
\mu(z)=\mathcal{K}(z),
\qquad
\tilde r(z)=\mathcal{R}(z),
\]
while the network \(\mathcal{H}\) returns state-dependent volatilities and
correlations. These are transformed into a diffusion matrix \(L(z)\), which
enters directly in the latent simulation.

The latent dynamics are discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme. For a
time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), the simulated paths
satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
L(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]

From the simulated short-rate path
\(r_{t_0},r_{t_1},\ldots,r_{t_N}\),
the corresponding money-market discount process is constructed. In continuous
time,
\(D_t=\exp\!\left(-\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right)\).
Since the short rate is only observed on the simulation grid, the integral is
approximated using the trapezoidal rule, giving
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\!\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\,\Delta t
\right).
\]

For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the corresponding zero-coupon bond
curve is obtained through the decoder described in
Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. For each realised state
\(z_{t_n}\), the functions \(A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) and \(B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) are
obtained by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system numerically, and bond prices are
recovered from
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]

Hence, each simulated path produces not only a short-rate path and pathwise
discount factors, but also a full sequence of zero-coupon bond curves across
maturities. These are the dynamic objects used in the pricing analysis in the
following chapter.

%========================================================
\section{Stability Analysis of the Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The simulation procedure turns the trained encoder--decoder model into a
forward scenario generator. This requires more than accurate reconstruction of
observed swap curves: the simulated latent paths must also remain stable over
the pricing horizon.

This section compares the baseline model with a stable parameterisation. The
baseline model has an unconstrained affine drift and therefore does not
guarantee mean reversion. In contrast, the stable model imposes a Hurwitz drift,
bounded volatilities and positive-definite correlation matrices by construction.

%========================================================
\subsection{Well-Posedness and Simulation Stability}
\label{subsec:wellposedness_stability}
%========================================================

The latent simulation model is an SDE of the form
\(dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\).
As discussed in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, Lipschitz continuity and
linear growth of the drift and diffusion coefficients are sufficient for the
existence of a unique non-explosive solution. Both model variants satisfy these
conditions: the affine drift is globally Lipschitz, and the bounded activation
functions in \(\mathcal{H}\) confine the diffusion outputs to a compact range.

However, mathematical well-posedness is weaker than practical simulation
stability. A process may have a unique finite-time solution while still moving
far outside the region covered by the encoded training data. In that case, the
decoder and short-rate network are evaluated out of distribution, and the
resulting zero-coupon bond curves may no longer be economically meaningful.
The analysis below therefore focuses not only on existence of the latent SDE,
but on whether simulated paths remain usable for pricing.

%========================================================
\subsection{Baseline Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}
%========================================================

When the baseline model is used for forward simulation, three related failure
modes can arise.

\paragraph{Unstable drift.}
The baseline drift is given by
\(\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N\),
where \(M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) is learned without restrictions on
its eigenvalues. Mean reversion toward the fixed point \(z^*=-M^{-1}N\)
requires \(M\) to be Hurwitz. If \(M\) has an eigenvalue with non-negative real
part, the corresponding latent direction does not revert to the fixed point, and
a forward simulation will push the latent state far away from the region covered
by the encoded training data.

\begin{definition}[Hurwitz matrix]
A square matrix \(M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) is called Hurwitz if all
eigenvalues of \(M\) have strictly negative real parts, i.e.\
\(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(M))<0\) for all \(i=1,\ldots,\ell\).
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Short-rate saturation.}
The short-rate network \(\mathcal{R}(z)=W_2\psi(W_1z)\) uses the centered
soft-step activation \(\psi(x)=\operatorname{sigmoid}(x)-\tfrac12\), which is
bounded in \((-\tfrac12,\tfrac12)\). The output therefore satisfies
\(|\tilde r(z)|\leq\tfrac12\|W_2\|_1\) for all \(z\).
Thus the short rate cannot become infinite at any single time step. However,
when the latent state becomes very large, the activation saturates and the
short rate is pinned close to artificial output limits. The resulting paths are
bounded but economically uninformative.

\paragraph{Decoder extrapolation.}
Once the latent state leaves the region covered by the encoded training data,
the decoder \(\mathcal{G}\) is evaluated out of distribution. The resulting
bond prices and swap rates may bear little relation to observed market behaviour,
even if the model reconstructs the training data accurately.

%========================================================
\subsection{Stable Parameterisation}
\label{subsec:sim_stable_solutions}
%========================================================

The two variants share the same overall encoder--decoder framework but differ
in the parameterisations of the drift \(\mathcal{K}\), the diffusion network
\(\mathcal{H}\), and the short-rate network \(\mathcal{R}\).

\paragraph{Hurwitz drift.}
The stable model replaces the unconstrained drift matrix with
\[
M
=
-\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right),
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0.
\]
Since \(V^\top V\) is positive semi-definite and \(\varepsilon I_\ell\) is
strictly positive definite, all eigenvalues of \(M\) satisfy
\(\lambda_i\leq -\varepsilon<0\).
The drift is therefore Hurwitz by construction for any trainable matrix \(V\),
and the latent factors are pulled back toward the equilibrium \(z^*=-M^{-1}N\).

\paragraph{Bounded volatilities.}
The stable model replaces the \(\exp\)-link with a sigmoid-bounded parameterisation,
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)\,
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
ensuring \(\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\) for all
\(z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\), with user-specified bounds
\(0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}\).

\paragraph{Positive-definite correlations.}
The stable model uses a hyperspherical Cholesky parameterisation
\citep{Pinheiro1996}, mapping \(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) free angles
\(\theta_k(z)=\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z))\in(0,\pi)\)
into a lower triangular factor \(L_R\) such that
\(R=L_RL_R^\top\)
is positive definite by construction for any input \(z\).

Together, these modifications ensure that the drift always pulls the latent
state back toward equilibrium, the diffusion scale remains within a controlled
range, and the correlation matrix is always well-defined. The empirical
comparison below shows the practical effect on simulated paths.

%========================================================
\subsection{Empirical Evidence from Simulated Paths}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To demonstrate the practical effect of the stable parameterisation, we run
paired Euler--Maruyama simulations from the baseline and stable ep5000
checkpoints. Both models are started from the same observed EUR swap curve
(first available date). Each model encodes this curve using its own encoder to
obtain \(z_0\), and the two simulations then use identical Brownian increments.
This isolates the effect of the network architecture from Monte Carlo variation.
All figures use \(N=500\) paths, monthly steps \(\Delta t=1/12\), a 10-year
horizon and shared seed 1234.

\paragraph{Root cause: drift-matrix eigenvalues.}

The fundamental difference between the two variants is visible in the
eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix \(M\), shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:drift_eigenvalues}. Both eigenvalues of the baseline matrix
\(M\) are positive (\(\lambda_1\approx+5.57\), \(\lambda_2\approx+0.12\)),
meaning no eigenvalue provides a restoring force. In contrast, both eigenvalues
of the stable matrix are strictly negative
(\(\lambda_1\approx-1.33\), \(\lambda_2\approx-10.85\)),
guaranteeing mean reversion toward \(z^*\).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Simulation/fig_eigenvalues.png}
    \caption{Eigenvalues of the learned drift matrix \(M\) for the baseline
    (blue) and stable (red) variants (\(\ell=2\), ep5000). The baseline has
    two positive eigenvalues, making the drift explosive. The stable
    parameterisation guarantees all eigenvalues are strictly negative.}
    \label{fig:drift_eigenvalues}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Consequence on individual latent paths.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_latent_paths} shows 30 individual simulated paths for
both latent factors. The most immediate signal is the scale of the vertical
axis in the top row. The baseline paths reach values of order \(10^{20}\) within
the 10-year horizon, while the stable paths (bottom row) remain within a compact
range comparable to the encoded training data, shown as the grey \(\pm2\sigma\)
band. The baseline paths look flat only because the axis must accommodate the
extreme divergence; in reality every path has diverged far beyond the training
region.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Simulation/fig1_latent_paths.png}
    \caption{Individual Euler--Maruyama paths for \(z_1(t)\) (left) and
    \(z_2(t)\) (right) over 10 years. \textbf{Top row}: baseline (blue), on
    its own diverging scale; each panel annotates the maximum absolute value
    reached. \textbf{Bottom row}: stable (red), with the grey band showing the
    \(\pm2\sigma\) training support. The stable paths revert toward the
    training cloud throughout the horizon.}
    \label{fig:comp_latent_paths}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Rate of divergence: exponential vs.\ bounded growth.}

Figure~\ref{fig:log_growth} makes the divergence quantitative by plotting
\(\log_{10}(\text{mean}\,|z_d(t)|)\) over time. Under the baseline model, the
curve is nearly linear on the log scale, consistent with exponential growth at
rate \(\lambda_{\max}\approx5.6\). After 10 years, the mean absolute value
exceeds \(10^{20}\). Under the stable model, the curve flattens after the first
year and stabilises close to the dashed training-support level, consistent with
convergence to the stationary distribution of the SDE.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

    \caption{Mean \(|z_d(t)|\) on a \(\log_{10}\) scale for baseline (blue)

    growth), while the stable model flattens near the training-support level
    (dashed grey), indicating convergence to stationarity.}
    \label{fig:log_growth}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Percentile fan charts for the latent factors.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_fans} shows percentile fan charts (5/25/50/75/95) for
each latent factor. The top row (baseline) shows fans that diverge
exponentially. The 5--95 band grows without bound, reflecting paths pulled in
opposite directions by the unstable drift directions. The bottom row (stable)
displays the expected pattern of a mean-reverting process: the uncertainty grows
briefly and then stabilises as the Hurwitz drift dominates. With
state-dependent diffusion, the long-run distribution is not exactly Gaussian,
but the stabilising effect of the negative eigenvalues is clearly visible.

\paragraph{Short-rate paths and fan.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_short_rate_paths} shows individual short-rate paths.
Because the short-rate network is bounded, the baseline short rate does not
diverge numerically. Instead, once \(\|z\|\) grows large, the centered
soft-step activation saturates and the short rate is pinned near \(\pm123\%\),
the hard output limits of the network. The stable short rate, by contrast,
starts near the observed initial level and evolves smoothly within a range that
is economically interpretable.

\paragraph{Terminal distributions.}

Figure~\ref{fig:comp_terminal} compares the distributions at the 10-year
terminal date. The stable terminal cloud (left panel) forms a compact ellipse
within the training support, reflecting the mean-reverting drift. The baseline
latent cloud is entirely off any reasonable axis scale; its mean values are of
order \(10^{16}\) and are indicated by the text annotation. The right two panels
show the terminal short-rate histograms separately. The stable model produces
a unimodal, approximately Gaussian distribution centred near positive rates,
while the baseline produces a bimodal distribution concentrated at the
saturation limits \(\pm123\%\).

\paragraph{Volatility bounds.}

Figure~\ref{fig:sigma_bounds} compares the distribution of the individual
volatilities \(\sigma_i(z)\) evaluated both on the training latent cloud
(solid) and on an expanded grid sampled at \(\pm3\sigma\) beyond the training
range (dashed). Within the training cloud the two variants produce similar
volatility levels. On the expanded grid, the baseline volatilities can reach
substantially larger values, while the stable volatilities remain within the
prescribed bounds \((\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})\) regardless of the latent
state. This confirms that the sigmoid-bounded parameterisation provides a
meaningful safety margin for out-of-distribution inputs.

The diagnostics confirm what the figures show. The baseline model is
mathematically finite but economically unusable: the latent factors diverge to
\(10^{20}\), the eigenvalues of \(M\) are positive, and the short rate saturates
at its network output limits. The stable model keeps the latent state in a
compact region, the eigenvalues are both negative, and the short-rate
distribution is centred near realistic levels throughout the horizon.

The central conclusion is that the three structural constraints of the stable
parameterisation --- Hurwitz drift, sigmoid-bounded volatilities and
hyperspherical positive-definite correlations --- are not merely theoretical
refinements. They are necessary for producing simulated paths that are usable
for derivative pricing. Good reconstruction accuracy is a necessary but far from
sufficient condition for a simulation-based pricing model.

For this reason, the stable checkpoint is used as the starting point for the
simulation-based pricing analysis in the following chapter.
