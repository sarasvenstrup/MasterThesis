\chapter{Simulation and Stability Analysis}
\label{chap:simulation}

TEST 12344

This chapter conducts a simulation experiment based on the trained neural encoder--decoder model. Whereas the previous chapters focused on reconstructing observed swap curves, the purpose here is to examine whether the learned model can also generate stable future yield-curve scenarios. This requires simulating the latent state forward in time and decoding each simulated state into a full zero-coupon bond curve.

The experiment addresses an important practical question: is accurate curve reconstruction sufficient for simulation-based pricing? To answer this, we compare two model variants. The first is the baseline model, whose latent dynamics are learned without explicit stability constraints. The second is a stable parameterisation designed to produce well-behaved simulated paths. The comparison focuses on whether the simulated latent states remain in meaningful regions and whether the resulting short rates and zero-coupon bond curves are suitable for pricing applications.

%========================================================
\section{Simulation of Latent Dynamics and Curve Reconstruction}
\label{sec:simulation-latent-dynamics}
%========================================================

We use the same swap data as in the previous chapters. Let
\(S_0 \in \mathbb{R}^8\) denote an observed market swap curve at the initial
date, corresponding to the eight quoted maturities used in the model. The
initial curve \(S_0\) is selected from the observed dataset. In the numerical
experiments below, this is either the first available observation or the
observation corresponding to a specified calendar date.

The trained encoder maps the initial swap curve to a latent initial state,
\[
z_0 = E(S_0) \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell},
\]
where \(\ell\) denotes the latent dimension. Starting from \(z_0\), the latent
state is simulated forward under the learned risk-neutral dynamics
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
where \(W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) is an \(\ell\)-dimensional Brownian motion under the
risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\). The simulated dynamics are therefore
interpreted as risk-neutral dynamics implied by the trained model, rather than
as historical real-world dynamics estimated from time-series changes.

In the implementation, the drift and short-rate functions are given by the
trained networks
\[
\mu(z)=\mathcal{K}(z),
\qquad
\tilde r(z)=\mathcal{R}(z),
\]
while the network \(\mathcal{H}\) returns state-dependent volatilities and
correlations. These are transformed into a diffusion matrix \(L(z)\), which
enters directly in the latent simulation.

The latent dynamics are discretised using the Euler--Maruyama scheme. For a
time step \(\Delta t>0\) and grid points \(t_n=n\Delta t\), the simulated paths
satisfy
\[
z_{t_{n+1}}
=
z_{t_n}
+
\mu(z_{t_n})\,\Delta t
+
L(z_{t_n})\,\Delta W_n,
\qquad
\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t\,I_{\ell}).
\]

From the simulated short-rate path
\[
r_{t_0},r_{t_1},\ldots,r_{t_N},
\]
the corresponding money-market discount process is constructed. In continuous
time,
\[
D_t=\exp\left(-\int_0^t r_s\,ds\right).
\]
Since the short rate is only observed on the simulation grid, the integral is
approximated using the trapezoidal rule,
\[
\int_0^{t_n} r_s\,ds
\approx
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\Delta t,
\]
which gives
\[
D_{t_n}
\approx
\exp\left(
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{r_{t_k}+r_{t_{k+1}}}{2}\Delta t
\right).
\]

For each simulated latent state \(z_{t_n}\), the corresponding zero-coupon bond
curve is obtained through the decoder described in
Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}. More precisely, for each realised state
\(z_{t_n}\), the functions \(A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) and \(B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\) are
obtained by solving the no-arbitrage ODE system numerically. Bond prices are
then recovered from
\[
P(z_{t_n},\tau)
=
\exp\bigl(
A_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)
-
B_{z_{t_n}}(\tau)\mathcal{G}(z_{t_n},\tau)
\bigr).
\]

Hence, each simulated path produces not only a short-rate path and pathwise
discount factors, but also a full sequence of zero-coupon bond curves across
maturities. These are the dynamic objects used in the pricing analysis in the
following chapter.

\section{Stability Analysis of the Simulation Experiment}
\label{sec:stability_analysis}
%========================================================

The simulation procedure turns the trained encoder--decoder model into a
forward scenario generator. This requires more than accurate reconstruction of
observed swap curves: the simulated latent paths must also remain stable over
the pricing horizon.

This section compares the baseline model with a stable parameterisation. The
baseline model has an unconstrained affine drift and therefore does not
guarantee mean reversion. In contrast, the stable model imposes a Hurwitz drift,
bounded volatilities and positive-definite correlation matrices by construction.

The analysis first relates the latent SDE to the well-posedness conditions from
Chapter~\ref{chap:finance_theory}, then explains why well-posedness alone is
not enough for practical simulation, and finally evaluates the effect of the
stable parameterisation on simulated paths.

%========================================================
\subsection{Well-Posedness and Simulation Stability}
\label{subsec:wellposedness_stability}
%========================================================

The latent simulation model is an SDE of the form
\[
dz_t = \mu(z_t)\,dt + L(z_t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}.
\]
As discussed in Proposition~\ref{prop:sde_existence}, Lipschitz continuity and
linear growth of the drift and diffusion coefficients are sufficient for the
existence of a unique non-explosive solution.

For the baseline model, the drift is affine,
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N.
\]
This map satisfies both linear growth and Lipschitz continuity. The diffusion
coefficient is generated by the network \(\mathcal{H}\). Since the baseline
network uses bounded activation functions, its pre-transformation outputs are
bounded for fixed trained weights. The resulting volatility functions are
therefore bounded and Lipschitz. Provided that the generated correlation matrix
remains uniformly positive definite, the Cholesky map is well-defined and the
full diffusion matrix \(L(z)\) also satisfies the regularity conditions needed
for well-posedness.

However, mathematical well-posedness is weaker than practical simulation
stability. A process may have a unique finite-time solution while still moving
far outside the region covered by the encoded training data. In that case, the
decoder and short-rate network are evaluated out of distribution, and the
resulting zero-coupon bond curves may no longer be economically meaningful.
The stability analysis below therefore focuses not only on existence of the
latent SDE, but on whether simulated paths remain usable for pricing.

%========================================================
\subsection{Baseline Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:sim_baseline_problems}
%========================================================

When the baseline model is used for forward simulation, three related failure modes can arise.

\paragraph{Unstable drift}
The baseline drift is given by
\[
\mathcal{K}(z)=Mz+N,
\qquad
M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}.
\]
The matrix \(M\) is learned without restrictions on its eigenvalues. If \(M\) is
invertible, the fixed point of the deterministic drift is
\[
z^*=-M^{-1}N.
\]
Mean reversion toward this point requires \(M\) to be Hurwitz. If \(M\) has an
eigenvalue with non-negative real part, the corresponding latent direction does
not revert to the fixed point. In a forward simulation, this can push the latent
state far away from the region covered by the encoded training data.

\begin{definition}[Hurwitz matrix]
A square matrix \(M\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell}\) is called Hurwitz if all
eigenvalues of \(M\) have strictly negative real parts, i.e.
\[
\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(M))<0,
\qquad i=1,\ldots,\ell.
\]
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Short-rate saturation}
The short-rate network \(\mathcal{R}(z)\) maps the latent state to a scalar using the centered soft-step activation
\[
\psi(x)=\operatorname{sigmoid}(x)-\frac12
\in
\left(-\frac12,\frac12\right).
\]
With no bias terms, the network has the form
\[
\tilde r(z)=W_2\psi(W_1z),
\qquad
W_1\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times\ell},
\quad
W_2\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times h}.
\]
Because \(\psi\) is componentwise bounded, the output satisfies
\[
|\tilde r(z)|\leq \frac12\|W_2\|_1
\qquad
\forall z.
\]
Thus the short rate cannot become infinite at any single time step. However, when the latent state becomes very large, the activation saturates and the short rate becomes pinned close to artificial output limits. The resulting short-rate paths are bounded, but no longer economically informative.

\paragraph{Decoder extrapolation}
Once the latent state leaves the region covered by the encoded training data, the decoder \(\mathcal{G}\) is evaluated out of distribution. The resulting bond prices and swap rates may therefore bear little relation to observed market behaviour, even if the model reconstructs the training data accurately.


%========================================================
\subsection{Stable Parameterisation}
\label{subsec:sim_stable_solutions}
%========================================================

The two variants use the same overall encoder--decoder framework. However, the stable variant modifies the parameterisations of the latent drift \(\mathcal{K}\), the diffusion network \(\mathcal{H}\), and the short-rate network \(\mathcal{R}\) in order to improve simulation stability.

\paragraph{Hurwitz drift.}
The stable model replaces the unconstrained drift matrix with
\[
M
=
-\left(V^\top V+\varepsilon I_\ell\right),
\qquad
V\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell\times\ell},
\quad
\varepsilon>0.
\]
Since \(V^\top V\) is positive semi-definite and \(\varepsilon I_\ell\) is
strictly positive definite, the matrix \(M\) is negative definite for every
choice of \(V\). Hence all eigenvalues satisfy
\[
\lambda_i\leq -\varepsilon<0.
\]
The drift is therefore Hurwitz by construction, and the latent factors are
pulled back toward the equilibrium
\[
z^*=-M^{-1}N.
\]
This parameterisation is sufficient but not necessary for mean reversion: a
general Hurwitz matrix need not be symmetric. The restriction is nevertheless
useful because it guarantees stability for any value of the trainable matrix
\(V\).

\paragraph{Bounded volatilities.}
In the baseline parameterisation, individual volatilities are recovered as
\[
\sigma_i(z)=\exp([\mathcal{H}(z)]_i).
\]
Although the bounded activation function implies that these volatilities are
theoretically bounded for fixed trained weights, the practical upper bound
depends on the learned parameters and may be large.

The stable model replaces this link with a sigmoid-bounded parameterisation,
\[
\log\sigma_i(z)
=
\log\sigma_{\min}
+
\bigl(\log\sigma_{\max}-\log\sigma_{\min}\bigr)
\operatorname{sigmoid}\bigl(h_i(z)+b_i\bigr),
\]
where \(h_i(z)\) denotes the \(i\)-th network output before the volatility
transformation, \(b_i\) is a learnable offset, and
\[
0<\sigma_{\min}<\sigma_{\max}
\]
are user-specified bounds. Since the sigmoid takes values in \((0,1)\), this
ensures
\[
\sigma_i(z)\in(\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max})
\qquad
\forall z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}.
\]
The diffusion scale therefore remains controlled even when the latent state is
outside the region observed during training.

\paragraph{Positive-definite correlations.}
The baseline network parameterises pairwise correlations as
\[
\rho_{ij}(z)=\tanh([\mathcal{H}(z)]_k),
\]
which ensures that each individual correlation lies in \((-1,1)\). For
\(\ell=2\), this is sufficient to define a valid \(2\times2\) correlation
matrix. For \(\ell\geq3\), however, valid pairwise correlations do not
necessarily form a positive-definite correlation matrix.

The stable model instead uses a hyperspherical Cholesky parameterisation
\citep{Pinheiro1996}. It maps \(\ell(\ell-1)/2\) free angles into a lower
triangular matrix \(L_R\), such that
\[
R=L_RL_R^\top
\]
is a valid correlation matrix. The angles are generated by
\[
\theta_k(z)
=
\pi\,\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z)),
\]
where \(g_k(z)\) is a network output. Since
\(\operatorname{sigmoid}(g_k(z))\in(0,1)\), the angles satisfy
\[
\theta_k(z)\in(0,\pi)
\qquad
\forall z.
\]
The resulting Cholesky factor has strictly positive diagonal entries, and
therefore \(R=L_RL_R^\top\) is positive definite by construction.

If the output-layer weights generating the angles are initialised at zero, then
\(g_k(z)=0\) and \(\theta_k(z)=\pi/2\). Since \(\cos(\pi/2)=0\), the
off-diagonal entries of \(L_R\) vanish and \(L_R=I\). The model therefore starts
from an uncorrelated prior and learns non-trivial correlation structure during
training.

Together, these modifications make the stable model more restrictive than the
baseline model, but better suited for forward simulation. The drift pulls the
latent state back toward its equilibrium, the volatility functions remain within
user-specified bounds, the correlation matrix is positive definite by
construction, and the short rate remains within a learnable bounded range. The
empirical comparison below shows the practical effect of these changes on
simulated paths.

%========================================================
\subsection{Empirical Evidence from Simulated Paths}
\label{subsec:sim_evidence}
%========================================================

To demonstrate the practical effect of the stable parameterisation, we run paired Euler--Maruyama simulations from the baseline and stable checkpoints. Both models are started from the same observed market swap curve. The curve is encoded using each model's own encoder, and the resulting latent states are then simulated using identical Brownian increments. This isolates the effect of the model architecture from Monte Carlo variation.

All figures in this section are generated using \(N=500\) paths, monthly time steps \(\Delta t=1/12\), a 10-year simulation horizon and shared Brownian increments with seed 1234. The results reported below use the ep3500 checkpoints.

\paragraph{Individual latent paths.}
Figure~\ref{fig:comp_latent_paths} shows individual Euler--Maruyama paths for the two latent factors. The most immediate visual signal is the scale of the vertical axis. The baseline paths occupy a range of order \(10^{20}\), while the stable paths remain within a range comparable to the encoded training data. This reflects the unstable eigenvalue structure of the baseline drift matrix. Even with monthly time steps, one positive drift direction is sufficient for the simulated process to diverge over a 10-year horizon.

    \textbf{Blue}: baseline ep3500. \textbf{Red}: stable ep3500. The baseline
    paths diverge to \(|z|\sim10^{20}\), while the stable paths remain in a

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Short-rate paths.}
Figure~\ref{fig:comp_short_rate_paths} shows the consequence of the divergent
latent state on the short rate. Since the short-rate network is bounded, the
baseline short rate does not become infinite. However, once \(\|z\|\) becomes
very large, the centered soft-step activation saturates componentwise. The
short rate is then pinned close to the network's artificial output limits.

The stable short rate, by contrast, starts near the initial observed level and
evolves smoothly within a range that is economically interpretable relative to
the low-rate EUR environment in the training sample.

\paragraph{Terminal distributions.}
Figure~\ref{fig:comp_terminal} compares the terminal distributions at the
10-year horizon. The stable terminal cloud forms a compact region reflecting
the mean-reverting drift. The baseline terminal cloud is spread over an
enormous region, far outside the scale on which the decoder was trained.

The right panel confirms the short-rate saturation effect. The baseline model
produces a distribution concentrated close to the saturation limits of the
short-rate network, while the stable model produces a smoother and more
interpretable terminal distribution.

\paragraph{Percentile fan charts.}
Figures~\ref{fig:comp_fans} and~\ref{fig:comp_short_rate_fan} summarise the
path behaviour across all simulated trajectories. For the stable model, the
fan charts display the expected pattern of a mean-reverting process:
uncertainty grows initially as paths diffuse away from the common starting
point, but the Hurwitz drift keeps the process in a stable region.

If the diffusion is approximated locally by its value near the equilibrium
\(z^*\), the long-run behaviour resembles that of an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck
process, with covariance determined by the Lyapunov equation
\[
M C_\infty + C_\infty M^\top + L(z^*)L(z^*)^\top = 0.
\]
With state-dependent diffusion, this Gaussian approximation is only local, but
it still explains the stabilising effect of the Hurwitz drift.

For the baseline model, the fan charts show unbounded growth in the latent
factors and immediate saturation in the short rate. This is exactly the
behaviour predicted by the drift-eigenvalue analysis: without a Hurwitz
constraint, a positive eigenvalue can dominate the simulation even if the model
reconstructs observed curves accurately.

The diagnostics show that the baseline model is mathematically finite in the
reported simulation, but economically unusable. The latent factors diverge far
outside the training region, and the short-rate network saturates at its output
limits. The stable model, by contrast, keeps the latent state in a compact
region and produces short-rate paths that remain interpretable over the full
10-year horizon.

The central conclusion is that the three structural constraints introduced by
the stable parameterisation --- Hurwitz drift, sigmoid-bounded volatilities and
hyperspherical positive-definite correlations --- are not merely theoretical
refinements. They are necessary for producing paths that are usable for
simulation-based derivative pricing.

The baseline model may achieve good reconstruction accuracy, but once simulated
forward it can generate latent states far outside the region covered by the
training data. The stable variant addresses this problem directly. For this
reason, the stable checkpoint is used as the starting point for the
simulation-based pricing analysis in the following chapter.
