\chapter{Results}

This chapter presents the empirical results for the arbitrage-free autoencoder model fitted to swap rate curves across nine currencies. The analysis is structured around four themes. We begin by examining in-sample reconstruction accuracy as a function of the number of latent factors $\ell \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, using models trained for 5\,000 epochs, and assess convergence through training loss curves. We then evaluate out-of-sample performance in both a fixed-window and a rolling scheme, benchmarking against the Extended Kalman Filter Dynamic Nelson--Siegel (EKF-DNS) model. Next, we present arbitrage diagnostics based on the Sharpe-ratio criterion implied by the risk-neutral drift, and examine how the no-arbitrage constraint is satisfied across maturities. Finally, we interpret the extracted latent factors through their correlation with classical yield curve drivers---level, slope, and curvature---and inspect the time series of the risk-neutral parameters $\mu$, $\sigma$, $\varrho$, and $\tilde{r}$ to assess economic plausibility.

\section{Latent Factor Interpretation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

% ── 1. Opening ───────────────────────────────────────────────

The yield curve is observed at eight maturities, but its variation may be governed by far few underlying factors. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) identifies these by finding the directions of maximum variance, revealing how many dimensions are needed to summarize the data without meaningful loss. Since the encoder is linear, its learned directions can be directly compared to the PCA eigenvectors, telling us whether a model trained purely on reconstruction error recovers the same structure as a method designed explicitly to capture variance.

When performing PCA on our data, we see in \ref{weight_projection_bar} that the first two principle components explain the variance, and the rest are neglible motivating the choice of
$\ell = 2$.


\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/AutoencoderPerformance/Q5b_weight_projection_barplot.png}
  \caption{Relative weights of the eigenvalues $\rho_j = \lambda_j /
    \mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ for the eight principal components of
    the in-sample swap rate curve, sorted in decreasing order. The weights
    are negligible from PC4 onwards, motivating the use of a low-dimensional
    latent representation with $\ell \in \{2, 3, 4\}$ factors. Following
    \citet{Poulsen2024}, Figure~4.}
  \label{fig:weight_projection_bar}
\end{figure}

Let $\textbf{a}_k\in\mathbb{R}^M$ denote the $k$-th row of the encoder weight matrix $A_1$, representing the direction along which the $k$-th latent factor compresses the input. To assess how much this direction aligns with the $j$-th principal component $\mathbf{v}_j$ obtained from PCA, we follow \cite{MatinWanPoulsen2025} and decompose each $\textbf{a}_k$ in the PCA basis, defining the projection weight
$$w_{kj}=(\textbf{a}_k\cdot \textbf{v}_j)^2$$
which is the dot product between the encoder row and the principal component. And further we normalize in order to get this a percentage, to understand how much of the same direction is the encoder row pointing in the eigen vector.
\begin{equation}
    w_{kj} = \frac{(\mathbf{a}_k^\top \mathbf{v}_j)^2}{\|\mathbf{a}_k\|^2}
    \times 100, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_{kj} = 100.
    \label{eq:weight_proj}
\end{equation}

The projection weights for the 3 different models across all $M=8$ PC, are presented in Table \ref{tab:weight_projection} where we see \textcolor{red}{Comment when we obtain our correct results}

However the projection weights $w_{ij}$ are purely geometric, in the sense that they compare directions statically. They say nothing abut whether the latent factors actually move with the PCA score through time.

In order to retrieve this insight, we compute Pearson correlation between factor $z_k$ and the $j$-th PC score
$\mathrm{PC}_{j,t} = \mathbf{v}_j^\top \mathbf{S}_t$ over in-sample observations for each currency and repeat for all models $\ell\in\{2,3,4\}$. suchh that the standard pearson correlation is computed across all $n$ IS observations:

$$\rho_{kj}=\frac{\sum_{t=1}^n(z_k(t)-\bar{z}_k)(PC_{j,t}-\bar{PC}_j)}{\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n(z_k(t)-\bar{z}_k)^2\sum_{t=1}^n(PC_{j,t}-\bar{PC}_j)^2}}$$

where $\bar{z}_k$ and $\bar{PC}_j$ is the ... for latent factor $z$ and principle component $j$ respectively.

The results are as wee see here in \ref{fig:q5b_heatmap} where we see the correlations between them. \textcolor{red}{Note that the sign of $z_k$ is arbitrary: any rotation $\mathbf{R}\in O(\ell)$ applied to $z_t$ leaves the reconstruction loss unchanged.}

\textcolour{red}{Comment: Here it is important to now that a model could correlate strongly with PCA scores simply because it has overfit the training data, not because it has found genuine structure, why it would be a good idea, to draw from the conclusions we end up making in the Out-of-sample test section.}

If it does not aling: Third, PCA finds linear directions of maximum variance. If the autoencoder is capturing genuinely nonlinear structure in the data, its factors might deliberately not align with PCA eigenvectors — and that would be a feature, not a problem. Using PCA as the benchmark implicitly assumes the relevant structure is linear, which favours the autoencoder's linear encoder by construction and is not a neutral comparison.

Beyond that, you could also report the correlation on a holdout period specifically — fitting PCA on the training data and then computing scores on the test data, then correlating those with the latent factors from each model evaluated on the same test period. This breaks the circularity of comparing two things both estimated on the same data.
