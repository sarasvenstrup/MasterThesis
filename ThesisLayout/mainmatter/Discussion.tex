\section{Summary}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The evaluation yields three main findings. First, the autoencoder achieves reasonable in-sample reconstruction accuracy once at least two latent factors are used, while the one-factor specification is too restrictive. Increasing the number of latent factors improves the in-sample fit, but with diminishing marginal gains and higher computational cost.

Second, the no-arbitrage diagnostic confirms that the generated zero-coupon bond prices satisfy the bond-pricing PDE up to numerical approximation error. The residuals are close to zero across maturities and currencies, indicating that the arbitrage-free construction is preserved in practice.

Third, the rolling-window analysis reveals a substantial generalization gap. Although the higher-dimensional specifications fit the training windows well, their out-of-sample performance deteriorates markedly, especially for $\ell=3$ and $\ell=4$. The $\ell=2$ model is less accurate in-sample but more stable on unseen data. This suggests that additional latent flexibility improves reconstruction within the estimation sample, but weakens out-of-sample robustness in the rolling-window setting.

* What latent dimension tells us
- In-sample, more dimensions improve fit; out-of-sample, the baseline becomes less robust; with stability constraints, higher dimensions become useful again.

The comparison across latent dimensions evaluates how many factors are needed for this representation. In-sample, reconstruction improves as the number of latent factors increases, suggesting that additional dimensions provide useful flexibility. However, the rolling out-of-sample results show that this flexibility can come at the cost of robustness. For the baseline model, the higher-dimensional specifications are more sensitive to regime shifts, while the two-factor model gives the most stable out-of-sample performance.

The stable variant changes this conclusion. Once the latent dynamics are constrained through mean reversion, bounded volatility, valid correlations and bounded short-rate behaviour, the additional dimensions become useful rather than destabilising. The four-factor stable model performs best both in-sample and out-of-sample, suggesting that complexity is beneficial when it is combined with appropriate stability restrictions.

* Why the decoder is more than a fitted vector
-It learns a state-to-curve map, producing zero-coupon bond prices/yields/forwards on a richer maturity grid.

The reconstruction task is also useful because the decoder learns a state-to-curve map rather than only a mapping to the observed maturities. Although the model takes swap rates at eight maturities as input, the decoder generates zero-coupon bond prices on a richer maturity grid. The latent state can therefore be used to generate a model-implied term structure, including bond prices, yields, forward rates and swap rates at maturities beyond the original input set.

- this state-to-curve mapping is exactly the mapping benth is talking about, and shows /proofs can actually be learned. 

A natural question is why we should expect such a state-to-curve map to be learnable by a neural network in the first place. The decoder is not only asked to fit individual maturities, but to learn a structured map from a latent state to an entire pricing function. For each latent state \(z\), it produces a bond-price curve
\[
    \tau \mapsto P_\theta(z,\tau).
\]
Thus, the object being learned is closer to a pricing operator than to a collection of unrelated scalar predictions.

This perspective is conceptually related to \citet{Benth2024}, who study neural approximation of solution operators for backward parabolic Cauchy problems. This is relevant because Markovian arbitrage-free bond-pricing equations are themselves backward parabolic PDEs. Their results therefore provide a mathematical argument that, under suitable regularity and continuity assumptions, pricing-type solution maps are suitable objects for neural approximation.

The connection is not exact. Benth's framework concerns operators between infinite-dimensional function spaces, where the input may be a coefficient or terminal condition and the output is a PDE solution function. In contrast, the encoder--decoder used here takes a finite-dimensional latent state \(z\in\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\) as input and is trained on swap rates observed at finitely many maturities. For fixed \(z\), however, the output remains function-valued, since the decoder generates a maturity curve. The model can therefore be viewed as learning a map
\[
    D_\theta:\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\to C([0,T]),
    \qquad
    z\mapsto P_\theta(z,\cdot),
\]
rather than a full function-to-function operator. In this sense, the theory of \citet{Benth2024} does not prove that the specific encoder--decoder architecture used in this thesis will succeed, but it supports the broader viewpoint that pricing functions arising from parabolic PDEs can be treated as learnable objects.

* Why no-arbitrage matters
- The model reconstructs curves through zero-coupon bonds and swap-pricing identities, so outputs are financially structured.

In this thesis, the state-to-curve representation is financially structured. The decoder maps the latent state into zero-coupon bond prices before converting them into swap rates. A successful reconstruction therefore indicates not only that the curve can be compressed, but also that it can be regenerated within an arbitrage-free term-structure framework.

This matters because an unconstrained neural network could fit the observed swap rates while producing curves that violate basic pricing relations. The no-arbitrage decoder restricts the output to curves that are consistent with zero-coupon bond prices and swap-pricing identities. The empirical results suggest that imposing this structure does not prevent accurate reconstruction, while the PDE residual diagnostic confirms that the no-arbitrage restriction is satisfied numerically.

* What reconstruction does not guarantee
- It does not guarantee stable simulation or good swaption pricing.

Successful reconstruction is only one requirement for a useful term-structure model. Reconstruction accuracy measures whether the current curve can be represented by the latent state, but it does not guarantee that the latent dynamics are stable when simulated forward. Nor does it guarantee accurate derivative pricing, since swaption prices depend on the risk-neutral distribution of future swap rates rather than only the current curve.

The main implication is that arbitrage-free reconstruction, stable simulation and derivative pricing are related but distinct objectives.
