\section{Neural Approximation of State-to-Curve Maps}
\label{sec:state_to_curve_approximation}

In the encoder--decoder framework studied in this thesis, the generation of a continuous term structures are performed by the decoder. Let \(\theta\) denote the learned model parameters of the decoder. Once these parameters have been learned during training, the decoder defines the map
\[
    D_\theta:\mathbb{R}^{\ell}\to C([0,\tau_{\max}]),
    \qquad
    z\mapsto P_\theta(z,\cdot),
\]
where \(P_\theta(z,\cdot)\) is the maturity-dependent bond-price curve generated from the latent state \(z\), and \(C([0,\tau_{\max}])\) denotes the space of continuous real-valued functions on some prespecified time-to-maturity interval. We refer to \(D_\theta\) as a state-to-curve map.

A natural question is whether a state-to-curve map of this form can be approximated by a neural network. The standard universal approximation theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:UAT} in Chapter \ref{chap:ml_theory}) guarantees that a sufficiently wide neural network with one layer can approximate continuous functions between finite-dimensional spaces. It therefore does not directly provide an approximation result in the encoder-decoder framework, where the codomain is a function space. A relevant extension, however, is provided by \citet{Benth2023}, who establish a universal approximation result for continuous maps with function-valued inputs or outputs.

Building on this result, \citet{Benth2024} study the solution operator associated with a class of backward parabolic Cauchy problems. They show, under suitable regularity assumptions, that this solution operator is continuous and therefore meets the conditions of the extended universal approximation theorem. Consequently, it can be approximated on compact sets by a neural network. This is relevant here because the no-arbitrage zero-coupon bond-pricing PDE in \eqref{eq:bond_pricing_pde} (Chapter \ref{chap:evaluation}) is a special case of the backward parabolic Cauchy problems considered by \citet{Benth2024}.\footnote{In the notation of \citet{Benth2024}, the zero-coupon bond-pricing problem corresponds to the special case with terminal condition \(\phi\equiv 1\), forcing term \(f\equiv 0\) and zeroth-order coefficient $c=r$, with $r$ being the short rate.} 

However, the result of \citet{Benth2024} is not directly applicable to the state-to-curve map $D_\theta$. A direct application of their result would concern a map from a short-rate specification to the corresponding bond-price curve. In the present framework, by contrast, the short-rate specification is learned as part of the model. The decoder then takes the current finite-dimensional latent state as input, not the short rate, and returns the bond-price curve. Their results therefore do not provide a direct approximation guarantee for the specific encoder--decoder framework used in this thesis.

Nevertheless, the result of \citet{Benth2024} is of theoretical interest as it suggests that neural networks are in principle capable of approximating bond-price curves, even if it does not directly apply to the decoder used in this thesis. A more detailed comparison is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:operator_learning_relation}.

\section{Comparing Curve-Shape Augmentation and Stability Restrictions}
\label{sec:comparing_model_modifications}

We now turn to the empirical comparison of the models considered throughout this thesis, focusing on two approaches to improve upon the baseline reconstruction accuracy. The first is the augumented model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:augmentedmodel}, which makes slope and curvature information explicit in the model input and training objective. The second is the stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, which imposes stability restrictions on the neural networks governing the latent dynamics. To complete the comparison, we also construct an \textit{augmented stable} model, which combines the input augmentation with the stable architecture. The four model specifications form a $2\times 2$ design summarised in Table~\ref{tab:variants_2x2}.

Before comparing the performance of the models, we first establish the optimal latent dimension for each model. For each model, this is selected as the smallest number of factors that captures the variation in the swap curves without overfitting out-of-sample. This yields two latent factors for the baseline and augmented models, four for the stable model, and three for the augmented stable model. See Table~\ref{tab:all_models_dim_comparison} in Appendix~\ref{app:modelselectiontable} for details.




We begin the empirical comparison by examining reconstruction quality across four curve observations chosen to span the range of shapes in the data, from a normal upward-sloping curve to the most challenging negative and crossing configurations, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:all_models_fitted_vs_actual}.

For the normal EUR curve from 2015, all four models reconstruct the overall 
shape well, as expected for a curve type that is well represented in the training data. The three remaining panels are more revealing. Across the deeply negative EUR curve, the crossing JPY curve, and the inverted CAD curve, the stable model consistently outperforms its unrestricted counterpart, with baseline errors ranging from 19.7 to 50.0 bps reduced to between 5.4 and 
19.9 bps. Augmentation alone does not replicate this improvement. On the crossing and inverted panels, the augmented model produces errors close to 
those of the baseline, showing that the explicit slope and curvature inputs do not compensate for the absence of architectural stability constraints. The augmented stable model achieves the lowest errors across the three challenging panels, suggesting that the two modifications are complementary rather than 
substitutes.





This qualitative picture is confirmed quantitatively in Table~\ref{tab:all_models_is_oos_summary}, which reports the reconstruction accuracy of all four models under the rolling-window scheme. Considering first the in-sample results, the models with the stable architecture perform better than those with the unrestricted architecture, reducing the average RMSE by approximately \(2\) bps.

Turning to the out-of-sample results, the stable model achieves an average RMSE of \(11.61\) bps, compared with \(16.42\) bps for the baseline model and \(17.06\) bps for the augmented model. The improvement is particularly pronounced in the maximum out-of-sample error, which stands at \(24.22\) bps for the stable model, compared with \(77.00\) bps for the baseline and \(70.79\) bps for the augmented model, suggesting that the stability restrictions not only reduce average reconstruction errors but also substantially limit extreme reconstruction failures across the rolling evaluation windows. The median corroborates this, and since it is less sensitive to the few curve shapes that are difficult to reconstruct, it further confirms that the stable model performs better across the full range of curve shapes encountered in the evaluation windows.

While summary statistics can obscure how reconstruction errors are distributed across individual curves, Figure~\ref{fig:oos_regime_scatter_all_models} shows a scatter plot of the out-of-sample RMSE for each reconstructed curve, colored by curve type, where bars represent curves with errors above \(215\) bps. All models struggle during the 2015-2016 period, when the rolling windows contained very few negative-rate curves in the training data, with the stable model exhibiting the lowest errors during this period.


In particular, the augmented stable model produces the largest reconstruction errors during this period, and struggles more than the other models not only here but across the other difficult regions of the sample. This suggests that imposing the stable architecture restrictions and the slope and curvature augmentation simultaneously over-constrains the model, resulting in overfitting. This is further evidenced by the large generalisation gap in Table~\ref{tab:all_models_is_oos_summary}, where the augmented stable model achieves an in-sample average of 8.70 bps but an out-of-sample RMSE of 20.7 bps.



The comparison reveals that improved out-of-sample generalisation is not obtained simply by providing the model with additional curve-shape information. Despite making slope and curvature explicit in the input and training criterion, the augmented model does not improve upon the baseline, and adding the same augmentation to the stable architecture does not yield further gains. The stable architecture alone, by contrast, prove more effective, suggesting that constraining how the latent dynamic components are learned has better effect than augmenting the input. In the baseline and augmented models, these components are learned freely, whereas the stable architecture imposes explicit restrictions that appear to steer the model towards more reliable latent representations, and thus handling underrepresented curves better in the process, \textcolor{red}{this is seen clearly in Figure \ref{fig:prevalence_baseline_vs_stable}(overgang!)}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:model_aug} in Chapter \ref{chap:augmentedmodel}, the baseline model reconstructs whichever curve type dominates the training set and produces elevated error for the underrepresented type, which is clearly visible at the 50\% crossover point in Figure~\ref{fig:prevalence_baseline_vs_stable}.

The stable model tells a different story. Negative-rate RMSE decreases as the share of negative curves in training increases, indicating that the model learns to reconstruct these curves better as it sees more of them during training. Unlike the baseline, the two error curves seem more independent of each other - improving the reconstruction of negative curves does not come at the cost of normal-curve reconstruction, suggesting that the stable model can learn multiple curve types at the same time rather than trading one off against the other. 

The roughly constant normal-rate RMSE may reflect that normal-rate curves are well-covered even with a small share in training, since they are relatively homogenous and the model quickly learns their structure. Negative curves, by contrast, span a wider range of shapes, being deeply negative, slightly negative, or crossing at zero at different maturities. The model requires more examples to learn their structure, which is consistent with reconstruction continues to improve. This behaviour may reflect that the stable model's architecture prevents one curve type from dominating the latent representation, allowing both to coexist once enough examples of each have been seen during training.

This suggests that the relevant issue is not only whether slope and curvature information is supplied to the model, but also how the resulting flexibility is controlled through the latent-dynamics architecture.
