\chapter{Structural versus Objective-Based Model Extensions}
\label{chap:model_comparison}

This chapter introduces two model extensions and evaluates all four model 
specifications considered in this thesis. The baseline model, assessed in 
Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}, is our implementation of the encoder-decoder framework of \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}. The stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, extends it by imposing restrictions on the neural networks to improve simulation behaviour and out-of-sample generalisation. Both models, however, exhibit difficulty with negative-rate curves. We hypothesise that this difficulty may be rooted in properties of the linear encoder, and therefore propose the \textit{augmented model} to address it. Specifically, by augmenting the input vector with slope and curvature features. Combining the augmentation with the stability restrictions yields a fourth specification, the \textit{augmented stable model}. All four models are then evaluated jointly on in-sample fit and out-of-sample generalisation to assess how each modification improves upon the baseline.

\section{Introducing the Augmented Model}
The evaluation in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} establishes that the baseline model struggles with negative-rate curves. Two distinct failure patterns emerge. For curves that cross from negative short-end rates to positive long-end rates, the baseline reconstructs a curve sloping in the wrong direction entirely. For curves where all or most tenors are negative, the baseline reconstructs a flat curve near zero. Both patterns are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_fit_failure_modes}, which contrasts a well-reconstructed positive-rate curve with one example of each failure pattern.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/AutoencoderPerformanceAugmented/baseline_fit_failure_modes.png}
    \caption{Baseline ($\ell=2$) reconstruction for three representative EUR and JPY swap curves. The positive-rate EUR curve on 2015-03-31 is reconstructed accurately. The crossing EUR curve on 2020-10-30 and the deeply negative JPY curve on 2016-09-30 illustrate the two distinct failure patterns.}
    \label{fig:baseline_fit_failure_modes}
\end{figure}

As established in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}, both failure patterns persist regardless of the number of latent factors, suggesting that the issue lies in how shape information is represented in the input rather than in the expressive capacity of the model. We hypothesise that this is linked to the linearity of the encoder: since $z = A_1\mathbf{S}$ with no bias and no activation function, the latent representation is driven by the sign and magnitude of the raw rates rather than by their shape, as illustrated in Example~\ref{ex:level}.

\begin{example}\label{ex:level}
Consider two upward-sloping EUR swap curves: a positive-rate curve and a 
negative-rate curve, both visible in Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_fit_failure_modes}. 
Expressed in decimal terms,
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{S}^{+} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0.0008 & 0.0008 & 0.0012 & 0.0024 & 
0.0056 & 0.0072 & 0.0078 & 0.0080 \end{pmatrix}^\top, \\
\mathbf{S}^{-} &= \begin{pmatrix} -0.0053 & -0.0054 & -0.0054 & -0.0049 & 
-0.0028 & -0.0009 & -0.0002 & -0.0005 \end{pmatrix}^\top.
\end{align*}
Both curves slope upward, as reflected in their short-end slope features,
$$f_1^{+} = 0.0056 - 0.0008 = 0.0048, \qquad 
f_1^{-} = -0.0028 - (-0.0053) = 0.0025,$$
which are positive and of similar magnitude. 

The learned encoder matrix for the $\ell=2$ baseline model is
$$A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0.46 & 0.37 & 0.00 & 0.40 & 0.26 & -0.23 & -0.66 & -0.10 \\ 
-1.12 & -0.63 & -0.66 & -0.83 & -0.50 & -0.29 & -0.22 & 0.13 \end{pmatrix}.$$ 
which was trained on data from 2010 -- which largely consists of positive curves. Applying this yields the latent factors
$$z^{+} = A_1\mathbf{S}^{+}=\begin{pmatrix} -0.0045 \\ -0.0098 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
z^{-} = A_1\mathbf{S}^{-}=\begin{pmatrix} -0.0068 \\ +0.0186 \end{pmatrix},$$
where the important observation is that the second factors have opposite signs. For $\mathbf{S}^{+}$, the small positive rates produce small negative products with the negative weights of $a_2$, yielding $z_2^{+} = -0.0098$. For $\mathbf{S}^{-}$, the negative rates interact with the negative weights to produce positive products that dominate, yielding $z_2^{-} = +0.0186$. The second factor is therefore seems to be driven by the sign \textcolor{red}{and magnitude} of the raw rates rather than by the slope of the curve.\;\;\circ
\end{example}

While this does not constitute a formal proof, the figure and example together suggest that the absence of explicit shape information in the input may be a contributing factor to both failure patterns. We therefore propose the augmented model specified in the following subsection.


\subsection{Model Specification}

Motivated by the above, we augment the input vector with derived features. We add a short-end slope $f_1 = S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}}$, a long-end slope $f_2 = S_{30\text{Y}} - S_{10\text{Y}}$, and a curvature measure $f_3 = 2S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}} - S_{30\text{Y}}$. The augmented input vector is then
$$\mathbf{S}^{\text{aug}} = \bigl(S^{(1)}, \ldots, S^{(M)}, f_1, f_2, 
f_3\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{M+3}.$$
The features are constructed as differences between tenors and therefore capture the slope and curvature of the curve more directly than the raw rates alone. However, since the features depend on absolute rate differences, two curves with different rate levels but similar shapes will not necessarily produce the same feature values.

\begin{example}
Consider three upward-sloping curves with the same slope shape but different 
rate levels. A positive-rate curve with $S_{1\text{Y}} = 2\%$ and 
$S_{10\text{Y}} = 4\%$, a crossing curve with $S_{1\text{Y}} = -2\%$ and 
$S_{10\text{Y}} = 2\%$, and a negative-rate curve with $S_{1\text{Y}} = -4\%$ 
and $S_{10\text{Y}} = -2\%$ give
$$f_1^{\text{pos}} = 4\% - 2\% = 2\%, \qquad f_1^{\text{cross}} = 2\% - (-2\%) 
= 4\%, \qquad f_1^{\text{neg}} = -2\% - (-4\%) = 2\%.$$
The features therefore do not fully decouple shape from level, as the crossing 
curve produces a different $f_1$ despite having a similar upward slope.
\end{example}

The features nevertheless provide more explicit shape information than the raw 
rates alone, particularly for curves with a pronounced slope or curvature at 
low or negative rate levels. This argument does not apply to approximately flat 
curves near zero, where the features are themselves near zero.


The augmented model follows the same encoder-decoder architecture described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, with two modifications. The encoder receives the 11-dimensional augmented input rather than the raw 8-dimensional swap curve, and the loss is evaluated over the eight reconstructed rates plus the three derived features computed from them, giving eleven terms in total. The decoder output remains an 8-dimensional swap curve $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}$.

We therefore evaluate four models in total: baseline ($\ell=3$), stable ($\ell=4$), augmented ($\ell=3$), and augmented stable ($\ell=3$). The latent dimension for each new model is selected based on its own best out-of-sample performance.

The conclusion of this is going to be about the way of how to make the encoder-decoder model to reconstruct swap curves absorb information in the best way. Baseline is completley freely, the augmented is consttraining how this is learned freely based on the training error penalizing when curves are matching on curvature and slope infromation as well, where the stable has none of these restictions in the training objective, but the neural networks have been restricted in some way so the stable model learns more effectively in some areas as so it seems. Putting these to ways of doing so together does not seem to have any positive impact, and just worsens the overeal reconstruction results.

%The comparison highlights an important distinction. The augmented model restricts learning through the reconstruction objective by forcing the model to match slope and curvature features. This improves some difficult in-sample fits, but it does not produce a more robust out-of-sample model. The stable model instead restricts the architecture of the latent dynamics and decoder. This gives the lowest average out-of-sample RMSE and, more importantly, avoids the extreme rolling-window failures observed for the other variants.

%Combining the two approaches does not improve performance. The augmented-stable model has a higher average and maximum out-of-sample error than the stable model alone. The evidence therefore suggests that, for this encoder--decoder setting, robustness is better achieved through structural restrictions on the model than through additional reconstruction penalties. The stable model is therefore the preferred specification for the pricing analysis in the next chapter.
