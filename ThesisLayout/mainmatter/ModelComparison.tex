\section{Introducing the Augmented Model}


The evaluation in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} identifies a structural limitation of the baseline model. For negative-rate curves, the reconstructed curve captures the approximate level but fails to reproduce the slope and curvature, a pattern that persists regardless of the number of latent factors. This section proposes a 
modification to the encoder input and training objective aimed at addressing this limitation, and assesses whether it improves reconstruction of the problematic curve variants.


%During the evaluation of the baseline model, we observe empirically that the model finds the approximate level of negative-rate curves but fails to reconstruct their slope and curvature. 




\subsection{Model Specification}

We hypothesise that this is related to the structure of the linear encoder. Since the encoder computes  \textcolor{red}{reference}
$$z = A_1\mathbf{S}, \qquad \mathbf{S}=(S^{(1)},...,S^{(M)})^\top$$ 
with no bias and no activation function, the magnitude of $z$ scales proportionally with $|\mathbf{S}|$, so near-zero curves push $z$ toward the origin of $\mathbb{R}^\ell$. If the training data consisted predominantly of positive-rate environments, the origin may lie outside the support of the training distribution of $z$.


\begin{example}
To illustrate the compression effect, let $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{S}^* \in \mathbb{R}^8$ 
denote the EUR swap curves observed on 2014-08-29 and 2020-03-31 respectively, 
expressed in decimal terms,
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{S} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0.0025 & 0.0027 & 0.0032 & 0.0050 & 0.0109 & 
0.0146 & 0.0164 & 0.0176 \end{pmatrix}^\top, \\
\mathbf{S}^* &= \begin{pmatrix} -0.0032 & -0.0032 & -0.0031 & -0.0023 & -0.0002 
& 0.0016 & 0.0023 & 0.0018 \end{pmatrix}^\top,
\end{align*}
where $\mathbf{S}$ is a normal upward-sloping positive-rate curve and $\mathbf{S}^*$ 
is a negative-rate curve with a pronounced upward slope. The learned encoder matrix 
for the $\ell=2$ baseline model is
$$A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0.46 & 0.37 & 0.00 & 0.40 & 0.26 & -0.23 & -0.66 & -0.10 \\ 
-1.12 & -0.63 & -0.66 & -0.83 & -0.50 & -0.29 & -0.22 & 0.13 \end{pmatrix}.$$
For each curve, construct a synthetic flat counterpart at the same mean rate level,
$$\mathbf{S}_{\text{flat}} = \bar{S}\cdot\mathbf{1}, \quad 
\mathbf{S}^*_{\text{flat}} = \bar{S}^*\cdot\mathbf{1},$$
where $\bar{S} = 0.00911$ and $\bar{S}^* = -0.000788$, so each pair shares the 
same mean level but differs only in slope and curvature. Applying $A_1$ gives
\begin{align*}
z = A_1\mathbf{S} &= \begin{pmatrix} -0.0088 \\ -0.0217 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
z_{\text{flat}} = A_1\mathbf{S}_{\text{flat}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.0046 \\ 
-0.0375 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \|z - z_{\text{flat}}\| = 0.0208, \\
z^* = A_1\mathbf{S}^* &= \begin{pmatrix} -0.0057 \\ 0.0089 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
z^*_{\text{flat}} = A_1\mathbf{S}^*_{\text{flat}} = \begin{pmatrix} -0.0004 \\ 
0.0032 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \|z^* - z^*_{\text{flat}}\| = 0.0078.
\end{align*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/AutoencoderPerformanceAugmented/augmented_latent_space_regime.png}
    \caption{Latent space representation of all in-sample swap rate curves under 
    the baseline encoder ($\ell = 2$), coloured by curve variant. Each point 
    corresponds to one curve observation projected onto the two-dimensional latent 
    space $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^2$. Normal curves are shown in teal, inverted 
    curves in black, and negative-rate curves in red. Four specific observations

    denote the corresponding representations for the EUR 2020-03-31 negative-rate 
    curve, with distance $d = 0.0078$.}
    \label{fig:latent_space_regime}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:latent_space_regime} plots these four points against the full in-sample training distribution. The normal curve and its flat counterpart are well separated within a densely populated region of the latent space, where the decoder has ample training exposure to distinguish between different curve shapes. The negative-rate curve and its flat counterpart, by contrast, both fall in a sparse region near the origin with a separation of only $0.0078$, roughly one third of the normal case. In this region, the decoder has little basis for 
recovering slope and curvature, which is consistent with the empirical finding that reconstructed negative-rate curves capture the level but fail to reproduce their shape.

\end{example}







Motivated by this hypothesis, we augment the input vector with 
derived features. We add a short-end slope $f_1 = S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}}$, a long-end slope $f_2 = S_{30\text{Y}} - S_{10\text{Y}}$, and a curvature measure $f_3 = 2S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}} - S_{30\text{Y}}$. The augmented input vector is then
$$\mathbf{S}^{\text{aug}} = \bigl(S^{(1)}, \ldots, S^{(M)}, f_1, f_2, f_3\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{M+3}$$

All three features are invariant to parallel level shifts in the rate level, meaning that replacing 
$\mathbf{S}$ by $\mathbf{S} + c\mathbf{1}$ leaves $f_1, f_2, f_3$ unchanged. For curves with \textcolor{red}{non-trivial} slope or curvature \textcolor{red}{but a low} or negative level, the derived features therefore carry shape information that the raw rates do not.  However, this argument does not apply to approximately flat curves near zero, 
where the features are themselves near zero. 


A second motivation is the training objective. Without augmentation, the loss $\lVert \mathbf{S} - \tilde{\mathbf{S}} \rVert^2$ provides no explicit incentive to match slope 
or curvature. Including the derived features in both the input and the 
loss directly penalises shape mismatch. Whether these modifications are sufficient to resolve the reconstruction failure for negative-rate curves is an empirical question, assessed in this following section.


The augmented model follows the same encoder-decoder architecture described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, with two modifications. The encoder receives the 11-dimensional augmented input rather than the raw 8-dimensional swap curve, and the loss is evaluated over the eight reconstructed rates plus the three derived features computed from them, giving eleven terms in total. The decoder output remains an 8-dimensional swap curve $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}$.

We therefore evaluate four models in total: baseline ($\ell=3$), stable ($\ell=4$), augmented ($\ell=3$), and augmented stable ($\ell=3$). The latent dimension for each new model is selected based on its own best out-of-sample performance.
