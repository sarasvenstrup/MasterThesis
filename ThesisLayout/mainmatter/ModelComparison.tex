\chapter{Structural versus Objective-Based Model Extensions}
\label{chap:model_comparison}


This chapter introduces two model extensions and evaluates all four model 
specifications considered in this thesis. The baseline model, assessed in 
Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation}, is our implementation of the encoder-decoder framework of \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}. The stable model, introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}, extends it by imposing restrictions on the neural networks to improve simulation behaviour and out-of-sample generalisation. Both models, however, exhibit difficulty with negative-rate curves. We hypothesise that this difficulty may be rooted in properties of the linear encoder, and therefore propose the \textit{augmented model} to address it by augmenting the input vector with slope and curvature features. Combining the augmentation with the stability restrictions yields a fourth specification, the \textit{augmented stable model}. All four models are then evaluated jointly on in-sample fit and out-of-sample generalisation to assess how each modification improves upon the baseline.

\subsection{Motivation}



The evaluation in Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} establishes that the baseline 
model struggles with negative-rate curves. Examining the worst-fit curves more 
closely reveals two distinct failure patterns. For curves where most tenors are 
negative, the baseline reconstructs a flat curve near zero, capturing the 
approximate level but losing all shape information. For curves that cross from 
negative short-end rates to positive long-end rates, the baseline reconstructs 
a curve sloping in the wrong direction entirely. Both patterns are visible in 
Figure~\ref{fig:worst_fit_curves}, which shows the baseline fitted curves for 
the most severely affected observations.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/thesis_results/AutoencoderPerformanceAugmented/augmented_worst_fit_curves.png}
    \caption{Worst-fit curves for the baseline ($\ell=2$), augmented ($\ell=3$), 
    and stable ($\ell=4$) models, restricted to curves with at least 7 out of 8 
    negative tenors or with the first 5 tenors negative and a positive long end, 
    ranked by baseline RMSE. The baseline consistently reconstructs a flat curve 
    for deeply negative curves and a downward-sloping curve for upward-sloping 
    negative-rate curves.}
    \label{fig:worst_fit_curves}
\end{figure}

We hypothesise that both failure patterns are linked to the linearity of the 
encoder, though what follows is speculative rather than a formal result. The 
following two examples illustrate the proposed mechanism numerically using the 
learned encoder matrix of the $\ell=2$ baseline model.

\begin{example}[Mostly negative curve]\label{ex:compression1}
Let $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^8$ denote the normal EUR swap curve observed on 
2014-08-29, and let $\mathbf{S}^- = \mathbf{S} - 0.015\cdot\mathbf{1}$ denote 
a stylised counterpart shifted down by 150 bps, so that six out of eight tenors 
become negative. Expressed in decimal terms,
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{S} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0.0025 & 0.0027 & 0.0032 & 0.0050 & 0.0109 & 
0.0146 & 0.0164 & 0.0176 \end{pmatrix}^\top, \\
\mathbf{S}^- &= \begin{pmatrix} -0.0125 & -0.0123 & -0.0118 & -0.0100 & -0.0041 
& -0.0004 & 0.0014 & 0.0026 \end{pmatrix}^\top.
\end{align*}
The learned encoder matrix for the $\ell=2$ baseline model is
$$A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0.46 & 0.37 & 0.00 & 0.40 & 0.26 & -0.23 & -0.66 & -0.10 \\ 
-1.12 & -0.63 & -0.66 & -0.83 & -0.50 & -0.29 & -0.22 & 0.13 \end{pmatrix}.$$
The second row $a_2^\top$ of $A_1$ loads negatively across almost all tenors. 
For $\mathbf{S}^-$, six tenors are negative, so the products of the negative 
weights with the negative rates flip sign and become positive contributions,
\begin{align*}
a_2^\top \mathbf{S}^- &= 
\underbrace{(-1.12)(-0.0125) + (-0.63)(-0.0123) + (-0.66)(-0.0118) + 
(-0.83)(-0.0100)}_{\text{1Y--5Y: }+0.0388} \\
&\quad + \underbrace{(-0.50)(-0.0041) + (-0.29)(-0.0004)}_{\text{10Y--15Y: }+0.0021} 
+ \underbrace{(-0.22)(0.0014)}_{20\text{Y: }-0.0003}
+ \underbrace{(0.13)(0.0026)}_{30\text{Y: }+0.0003} \\
&= +0.0403 - 0.0003 = +0.0400,
\end{align*}
compared to $a_2^\top \mathbf{S} = -0.0217$ for the normal curve. The full 
latent representations are
$$z = A_1\mathbf{S} = \begin{pmatrix} -0.0088 \\ -0.0217 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
z^- = A_1\mathbf{S}^- = \begin{pmatrix} -0.0164 \\ +0.0400 \end{pmatrix}.$$
The second factor flips from large and negative to large and positive. The 
decoder has been trained predominantly on curves with $z_2 < 0$, and has 
virtually no training exposure in the region $z_2 = +0.04$. With no reliable 
basis for reconstruction, it defaults to a flat curve near the mean rate level.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Upward-sloping negative-rate curve]\label{ex:compression2}
Let $\mathbf{S}^- = \mathbf{S} - 0.006\cdot\mathbf{1}$ denote a stylised 
counterpart shifted down by 60 bps, so that the short-to-mid tenors become 
negative while the long end remains positive, producing an upward-sloping curve,
$$\mathbf{S}^- = \begin{pmatrix} -0.0035 & -0.0033 & -0.0028 & -0.0010 & 0.0049 
& 0.0086 & 0.0104 & 0.0116 \end{pmatrix}^\top.$$
For this curve, the negative short-to-mid rates produce positive contributions 
to the second latent factor while the positive long-end rates produce negative 
contributions,
\begin{align*}
a_2^\top \mathbf{S}^- &= 
\underbrace{(-1.12)(-0.0035) + (-0.63)(-0.0033) + (-0.66)(-0.0028) + 
(-0.83)(-0.0010)}_{\text{1Y--5Y: }+0.0102} \\
&\quad + \underbrace{(-0.50)(0.0049) + (-0.29)(0.0086) + 
(-0.22)(0.0104)}_{\text{10Y--20Y: }-0.0072}
+ \underbrace{(0.13)(0.0116)}_{30\text{Y: }+0.0015} \\
&= +0.0102 - 0.0072 + 0.0015 = +0.0030,
\end{align*}
giving
$$z^- = A_1\mathbf{S}^- = \begin{pmatrix} -0.0119 \\ +0.0030 \end{pmatrix}.$$
The positive and negative contributions nearly cancel, collapsing the second 
factor from $-0.0217$ to $+0.0030$. The decoder, having learned that a large 
negative second factor encodes an upward slope for positive-rate curves, now 
receives $z^-_2 \approx 0$ and has no reliable basis for associating it with 
an upward-sloping curve. It therefore reconstructs the slope in the wrong 
direction, consistent with the empirical pattern observed in 
Figure~\ref{fig:worst_fit_curves}.
\end{example}

\subsection{Model Specification}
Motivated by this hypothesis, we augment the input vector with 
derived features. We add a short-end slope $f_1 = S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}}$, a long-end slope $f_2 = S_{30\text{Y}} - S_{10\text{Y}}$, and a curvature measure $f_3 = 2S_{10\text{Y}} - S_{1\text{Y}} - S_{30\text{Y}}$. The augmented input vector is then
$$\mathbf{S}^{\text{aug}} = \bigl(S^{(1)}, \ldots, S^{(M)}, f_1, f_2, f_3\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{M+3}$$

All three features are invariant to parallel level shifts in the rate level, meaning that replacing 
$\mathbf{S}$ by $\mathbf{S} + c\mathbf{1}$ leaves $f_1, f_2, f_3$ unchanged. For curves with \textcolor{red}{non-trivial} slope or curvature \textcolor{red}{but a low} or negative level, the derived features therefore carry shape information that the raw rates do not.  However, this argument does not apply to approximately flat curves near zero, 
where the features are themselves near zero. 


A second motivation is the training objective. Without augmentation, the loss $\lVert \mathbf{S} - \tilde{\mathbf{S}} \rVert^2$ provides no explicit incentive to match slope 
or curvature. Including the derived features in both the input and the 
loss directly penalises shape mismatch. Whether these modifications are sufficient to resolve the reconstruction failure for negative-rate curves is an empirical question, assessed in this following section.


The augmented model follows the same encoder-decoder architecture described in Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel}, with two modifications. The encoder receives the 11-dimensional augmented input rather than the raw 8-dimensional swap curve, and the loss is evaluated over the eight reconstructed rates plus the three derived features computed from them, giving eleven terms in total. The decoder output remains an 8-dimensional swap curve $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}$.

%The comparison highlights an important distinction. The augmented model restricts learning through the reconstruction objective by forcing the model to match slope and curvature features. This improves some difficult in-sample fits, but it does not produce a more robust out-of-sample model. The stable model instead restricts the architecture of the latent dynamics and decoder. This gives the lowest average out-of-sample RMSE and, more importantly, avoids the extreme rolling-window failures observed for the other variants.

%Combining the two approaches does not improve performance. The augmented-stable model has a higher average and maximum out-of-sample error than the stable model alone. The stable model is therefore the preferred specification for the pricing analysis in the next chapter.
