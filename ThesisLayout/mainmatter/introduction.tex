\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:intro}

The term structure of interest rates is a central object in fixed-income modelling. It describes how interest rates vary across maturities and underlies the valuation of bonds, swaps and interest-rate derivatives. Modelling the term structure is challenging for two reasons. First, the object of interest is not a single rate, but an entire curve across maturities, observed only at finitely many points. Second, the curve is a pricing object, so any representation should respect the financial relations between zero-coupon bond prices, yields, forward rates and swap rates. These two requirements, low-dimensional representation and consistency with arbitrage-free pricing, are non-trivial to combine.

Classical term-structure models address this problem by specifying a small number of state variables and imposing structure through the dynamics and pricing equations. Statistical factor models instead represent curve movements through a few dominant empirical factors. More recently, neural-network methods have been used to learn low-dimensional representations directly from data. The challenge for such models is that flexible function approximation alone does not guarantee financial consistency. A neural network may fit observed swap rates accurately while producing curves that violate basic pricing relations.

This thesis studies this problem through the affine-inspired encoder--decoder framework of \citet{MatinWanPoulsen2025}. The model combines a data-driven latent representation with a no-arbitrage decoder. The encoder maps observed swap rates into a low-dimensional latent state, while the decoder maps this state into zero-coupon bond prices before converting them into swap rates. The model therefore learns a state-to-curve map rather than only a fitted vector of rates, and the reconstructed swap curves are arbitrage-free by construction.

Building on this architecture, the thesis asks three related questions. First, can the model represent par swap-rate curves accurately across nine major currencies and different market regimes, and how robust is its performance out of sample? Second, can the learned latent dynamics be used not only for cross-sectional reconstruction, but also for forward simulation of interest-rate scenarios? Third, can the same framework be adapted to price interest-rate derivatives, specifically European swaptions? These questions correspond to three increasingly demanding uses of the model: reconstruction, simulation and pricing.

The thesis makes three main contributions.

First, the in-sample and rolling out-of-sample evaluation shows that the baseline arbitrage-free encoder--decoder reconstructs swap-rate curves accurately in-sample once at least two latent factors are used. However, the rolling-window analysis reveals a flexibility--robustness trade-off. Higher latent dimensions improve the in-sample fit but lead to weaker out-of-sample performance, especially during regime changes such as the negative-rate period and the post-2022 tightening cycle. The results also show that atypical curve shapes, including negative-rate and inverted curves, are more difficult to reconstruct.

Second, the thesis proposes a stable variant of the model. The stable specification restricts the latent dynamics by imposing mean reversion in the drift, bounded volatility, positive-definite correlation matrices and bounded short-rate behaviour. These restrictions make the simulated latent process more controlled and improve out-of-sample robustness. In contrast to the baseline model, the four-factor stable specification performs best both in-sample and out-of-sample among the variants considered. The simulation experiment further shows that the stable model can generate forward scenarios on an interpretable scale, while the baseline dynamics may produce explosive paths.

Third, the thesis investigates whether the learned latent representation can be used for swaption pricing. The unmodified reconstruction model performs poorly when applied directly to ATM swaption straddles, showing that arbitrage-free curve reconstruction does not by itself identify the option-implied distribution of future swap rates. A small pricing recalibration layer substantially reduces the pricing error, indicating that the latent state contains useful information for pricing. However, more flexible yield-curve-only pricing layers overfit and do not improve out-of-sample performance. When contemporaneous option-market information is included through leave-one-cell-out volatility-surface features, pricing accuracy improves further. The improvement relative to the yield-curve-only pricing layer can be interpreted as the value of contemporaneous option-market volatility information that is not contained in the yield curve.

The remainder of the thesis is organised as follows. Chapters~\ref{chap:finance_theory} and~\ref{chap:ml_theory} introduce the financial and machine-learning theory used throughout the thesis. Chapter~\ref{ch:model-background} places the affine-inspired encoder--decoder in the term-structure modelling literature, while Chapter~\ref{chap:encoderdecodermodel} describes the model architecture in detail. Chapter~\ref{chap:datadescription} presents the Bloomberg swap-rate dataset. Chapter~\ref{chap:evaluation} evaluates reconstruction accuracy and rolling out-of-sample robustness. Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation} introduces the stable variant and studies simulation of the latent dynamics. Chapter~\ref{chap:pricing_methodology} applies the model to swaption pricing. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the findings and limitations, and Chapter~\ref{chap:con} concludes.
